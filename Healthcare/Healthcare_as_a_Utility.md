# **Problem**

Healthcare behaves like a fragmented toll system: prices are opaque, intermediate actors extract spread via contracts and rebates, and the public sector pays a large share of the bill without receiving commensurate control, accountability, or returns. The result is persistently high costs, uneven access, and incentives that prioritize revenue optimization over health outcomes.

# **Policy Objective**

Make healthcare legible, governable, and outcomes-driven by applying public-utility principles (universal service, fair rates, reliability) while preserving operational pluralism in care delivery (multiple private options in any one area). Convert public spending from a pure outflow into durable public value through standardized rules, disciplined margins where appropriate, and reinvestment aligned to health outcomes.

# **Theory of Change**

First, force end-to-end price and cost transparency so policymakers and the public can see where spreads and denials occur. Second, impose targeted ceilings and public-service obligations on the worst extraction points (not blanket price controls everywhere). Third, simplify financing into a single payer rail to remove administrative waste and align incentives. Fourth, restructure delivery networks toward private entities with partial co-op/community governance so surplus is recycled into capacity and prevention. Finally, create regulated competition on risk-adjusted outcomes with prosperity-linked rewards and strict anti-gaming enforcement.

# **Policy Design**

## **Phase 1: Utility-Style Transparency and Public-Service Obligations**

Establish a Health Utility Commission model (federal baseline with regional counterparts) empowered to require standardized “utility filings” and audit them. Covered entities include insurers, PBMs, hospital systems, clinic networks, device manufacturers/distributors, pharmaceutical manufacturers/wholesalers, and major labs/imaging chains.

Transparency requirements include net prices and transfers, not sticker prices. Entities must disclose true unit economics and contract flows: acquisition cost, list price, net price after rebates/chargebacks, rebate recipients, admin and marketing overhead, denial/prior auth volumes, average time-to-authorization, claim reversal rates, facility fees, related-party transactions, and margin by product/service line. Contracts funded materially by public dollars move to open-contracting norms (disclosed pricing terms and performance obligations, with narrow trade-secret carveouts for manufacturing process details rather than pricing).

Public-service obligations attach to essential service providers (ER coverage, trauma, maternity, primary care adequacy, rural access) with explicit reliability and access standards (wait-time targets, network adequacy, continuity-of-care protections).

## **Phase 2: Targeted Markup Ceilings and Rate Discipline**

Using Phase 1 data, regulators set ceilings where spreads are structurally unjustified and non-competitive. This is implemented as category-based “allowable margin bands” (by risk class and service type) rather than one blunt cap. Examples include limits on facility-fee markups for routine services, caps on out-of-network multipliers, bounded spreads for commodity generics, and regulated pass-through rules for devices where procurement is concentrated.

Rate-setting is introduced selectively: global budget pilots for hospitals in high-uncompensated-care markets; standardized reference pricing for shoppable services; and “cost-plus with allowed margin” for essential inputs where market structure prevents real competition. Antitrust enforcement is paired with rate discipline so consolidation does not turn into regulated monopoly rents.

## **Phase 3: Single Payer Rail with Pluralistic Delivery**

Migrate core benefits to a single public payer rail for the defined essential package (with optional supplemental private coverage for upgrades that do not gate essential care). Claims, coding, medical-necessity rules, and appeals become uniform. The payment model shifts away from fee-for-service toward a blended structure: global budgets for hospitals, capitation/shared savings for primary and chronic care, bundled payments for predictable episodes, and outcomes-based contracts for high-cost therapies with independent measurement.

Administrative simplification is treated as a primary savings lever: one enrollment, one set of rules, one prior-authorization regime (or sharply limited prior auth), and standardized clinical documentation.

## **Phase 4: Ownership and Governance Transition to Private + Partial Co-op Networks**

Delivery networks transition toward private entities with partial cooperative/community governance. The default target is regional non-profit/co-op systems with clinician governance and community representation, with reinvestment requirements and limits on profit extraction for essential services. For-profit operators can exist but must operate under public-benefit charters for essential lines of business (utility conditions: transparency, service obligations, reinvestment floors, and margin bounds).

Where public procurement or public R&D materially creates private upside (e.g., breakthrough drugs/devices), the payer can receive standardized upside participation (warrants, revenue share, or price-linked rebates into a Health Trust Fund), with a strict firewall between investing functions and regulatory enforcement.

# **Outcomes Competition and Prosperity Incentives**

## **Prosperity Health Index**

Create a risk-adjusted, auditable index at the delivery-network level, including: avoidable mortality, chronic disease control, preventable hospitalizations, readmissions, maternal outcomes, behavioral health access, wait times, patient-reported outcomes, and equity metrics. All metrics are risk-adjusted and audited to prevent “cream skimming” and under-service.

## **Incentive Mechanisms**

High performers earn predictable upside through prosperity-linked tax relief, capital access (fast-track approvals for capacity expansions), and shared-savings pools. Underperformers face mandatory improvement plans, governance intervention, management review, or contracting restrictions. Fraudulent reporting triggers severe penalties plus automatic disqualification from incentives.

# **Implementation Pathways**

What an Executive Can Do (Fast Start)

An administration can pilot utility-like behavior through federal purchasing power and existing agency rulemaking: Medicare/Medicaid/VA/DoD contract terms requiring the transparency schema, pass-through rules, and outcome reporting; enforcement prioritization against deceptive practices and anticompetitive conduct; and demonstration projects for global budgets and standardized benefits. This creates de facto utility conditions in the publicly financed portion of the market.

What Requires Congress (Durable Change)

Permanent utility designation, universal transparency mandates across all private actors, national rate-setting authority, and prosperity-linked tax code changes require legislation. A “Healthcare Utility & Prosperity Act” would authorize the commission structure, define essential services, set the disclosure regime, establish margin/rate authority, and create the single payer rail and governance standards.

Safeguards and Risk Controls

This design anticipates predictable failure modes: provider exit, politicized rate-setting, innovation slowdowns, and metric gaming. Mitigations include phased rollout, independent audits, regional cost-of-living adjustments, explicit rural-service subsidies, competitive procurement, protected clinical autonomy, outcome measures focused on long-term function, and strict penalties for fraud plus whistleblower rewards for verified misconduct.

Milestones and Deliverables

Phase 1 delivers a national transparency schema, audited filings, and public dashboards within 12–18 months of authorization. Phase 2 delivers targeted ceilings and global budget pilots by year 2. Phase 3 migrates essential benefits to a unified payer rail over years 2–5. Phase 4 completes governance transition and prosperity-index incentives over years 3–7, with annual recalibration based on measured outcomes and cost trends.

Appendix

Minimum Transparency Schema (Core Fields)

Entities report standardized fields for: unit price (list/net), acquisition cost, rebates/chargebacks (amount and recipient), admin overhead, denial/prior-auth volumes and turnaround times, claim reversal rates, provider payment rates by service line, facility fees, ownership/related-party payments, and margins by product/service category. Data is auditable, machine-readable, and published with privacy protections for patients, not for pricing.

Whistleblower Integrity Dividend

Create a standing reward program tied to recovered funds and proven deception/fraud in essential industries, with strong anti-retaliation protections and evidentiary standards. This increases enforcement leverage and makes deliberate deception economically self-defeating.

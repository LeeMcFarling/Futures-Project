# Equipment, Right-to-Repair & Farm Data Rights

## 1. Problem Statement — What’s Broken

Farm equipment has become one of the largest and fastest-growing sources of cost pressure in American agriculture. Modern tractors and implements combine high upfront capital costs with restrictive software controls, proprietary diagnostics, and exclusive service agreements that limit farmers’ ability to maintain, or repair equipment they own.

Additionally, manufacturers increasingly lock essential functions behind software permissions, require authorized dealer servicing, and impose recurring subscription fees for basic operation, diagnostics, or performance features. These practices shift control away from farmers and independent repair providers, raising lifetime equipment costs and increasing downtime during critical planting and harvest windows.

At the same time, operational data generated by farm equipment—yield maps, soil conditions, machine performance—are often captured and controlled by manufacturers rather than the farmers who generate it. This data asymmetry limits farmers’ ability to use their own information for decision-making, negotiation, or integration with independent tools.

The result is an extractive equipment ecosystem in which farmers pay more, wait longer for repairs, lose control over their own assets, and face reduced competition in both service and technology markets.

---

## 2. Policy Objective — What This Brief Optimizes For

The objective of this policy is to **restore farmer control over owned equipment and farm-generated data while preserving innovation and competition in equipment markets**.

Farmers who purchase equipment should have the practical ability to repair, service, and operate it without being locked into exclusive vendor relationships. Equipment markets should compete on quality, durability, and performance—not on artificial restrictions.

Data generated on farms should be accessible to farmers in usable formats, enabling better decision-making, interoperability with independent tools, and fairer negotiation with buyers and lenders.

Success is defined by lower lifetime equipment costs, reduced downtime, increased repair competition, improved data access, and continued innovation in agricultural machinery.

---

## 3. Core Mechanisms — How the System Works

This policy establishes clear ownership, access, and interoperability rights while maintaining room for private innovation.

### Right-to-Repair for Agricultural Equipment

Manufacturers are required to provide farmers and independent repair providers with access to diagnostic tools, repair manuals, firmware updates, and replacement parts on fair and reasonable terms.

Software locks may not prevent lawful repair, maintenance, or restoration of equipment functionality. Safety-critical systems remain protected, but repair access cannot be withheld for purely commercial reasons.

### Software Transparency and Functionality Access

Essential operational features may not be gated behind recurring subscription fees when those features are necessary for basic equipment use. Optional premium services remain allowed, but baseline functionality must remain accessible to owners.

Any software restrictions affecting equipment operability must be clearly disclosed at point of sale.

### Farm Data Ownership and Access Rights

Data generated by farm equipment during normal operation is recognized as farmer-owned. Manufacturers may collect and process this data only under disclosed terms and must provide farmers with timely access in standardized, machine-readable formats.

Farmers retain the right to share their data with third-party tools, cooperatives, lenders, or advisors of their choosing.

### Interoperability and Open Standards

To prevent new forms of vendor lock-in, equipment data formats and interfaces are subject to interoperability standards that allow integration with independent software and analytics platforms.

Standards focus on data access and portability, not design uniformity, preserving room for competitive differentiation.

### Enforcement Through Market Access

Compliance is enforced primarily through market access rules tied to federal procurement, financing programs, and eligibility for agricultural support programs, rather than heavy-handed penalties.

---

## 4. What This Policy Is Not

This policy is not a ban on proprietary technology or intellectual property. Manufacturers retain full rights to innovate, patent, and differentiate products.

It does not require companies to disclose trade secrets or source code beyond what is necessary for lawful repair and data access.

It is not a price control regime for equipment or parts.

It does not prevent manufacturers from offering premium software services or advanced features on optional terms.

It is not anti-technology. It is pro-ownership and pro-competition.

---

## 5. Farmer Impact — What Changes at the Field Level

Farmers gain the ability to repair equipment locally and quickly, reducing downtime during critical seasons.

Lifetime equipment costs decline as repair markets become more competitive and subscription creep is limited.

Access to farm-generated data improves decision-making, supports integration with market-signal tools, and strengthens negotiating position with buyers and lenders.

Farmers regain practical ownership of their equipment—not just legal title—restoring independence and resilience at the field level.

---

## 6. Governance & Safeguards

Implementation is phased and pilot-based, beginning with specific equipment categories and model years.

Safety and emissions compliance remain protected through narrowly tailored safeguards.

Independent oversight ensures interoperability standards remain open and are not captured by any single vendor or consortium.

Periodic review assesses impacts on costs, repair access, innovation, and market competition.

---

## 7. Pilot Strategy

Initial pilots focus on high-impact equipment classes with the greatest repair delays and cost concentration.

Pilots run in opt-in states and regions, with clear metrics on downtime, repair costs, farmer satisfaction, and secondary market activity.

Scaling occurs only after demonstrated improvements in cost, access, and resilience without adverse safety or innovation impacts.

# PUBLIC FACING DRAFT: Let Farmers Fix What They Own

## What’s the Problem?

Farm equipment is expensive. Tractors and harvesters can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars. But even after paying that much, many farmers still don’t fully control their own machines.

Today, many repairs can only be done by approved dealers. Software locks prevent basic fixes. Some features only work if farmers pay ongoing subscription fees. When something breaks during planting or harvest, farmers may have to wait days for service—while crops are at risk.

On top of that, modern equipment collects a lot of data about fields and crops. In many cases, this data is controlled by the manufacturer, not the farmer who created it.

This drives up costs, wastes time, and leaves farmers dependent on a small number of companies.

---

## What Is the Goal?

The goal is simple: **if farmers buy equipment, they should be able to use it, fix it, and access their own data**.

This policy does not ban technology, or stop innovation but rather makes sure farmers actually own what they pay for, which means that markets stay competitive.

---

## How Would This Work?

First, farmers and local repair shops would have access to the tools and manuals needed to fix equipment safely. Manufacturers could still protect safety systems, but they couldn’t block repairs just to control the market.

Second, basic equipment functions could not be locked behind surprise subscription fees. Optional upgrades are fine. Basic operation should work without ongoing payments.

Third, data created on the farm would belong to the farmer. Farmers could download it in usable formats and share it with tools, advisors, or cooperatives they choose.

Finally, equipment data would work across systems. Farmers wouldn’t be forced into one company’s software just because of file formats.

---

## What This Is *Not*

This is not anti-technology.

It does not force companies to share trade secrets.

It does not set prices for equipment or parts.

It does not stop companies from offering premium services.

It does not tell farmers how to farm.

---

## How This Helps Farmers

Farmers can fix equipment faster and closer to home.

Repair costs go down when there is real competition.

Downtime during planting and harvest is reduced.

Farmers can actually use their own data to make better decisions.

Over time, this means lower costs, more independence, and less pressure to sell out or scale up just to survive.

Farmers stay in charge. The equipment finally works for them again.

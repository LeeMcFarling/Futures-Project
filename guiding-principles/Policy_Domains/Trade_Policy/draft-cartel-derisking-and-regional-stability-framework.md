# Cartel De-Risking & Regional Stability Framework

## Executive Summary

This framework defines a **security-linked industrial investment system** that allocates capital based on regional stability conditions.

The objective is not to directly eliminate cartel activity through enforcement.

The objective is:

> **To reduce exposure to instability while directing industrial investment toward regions that demonstrate reliable governance and security**

Over time, this creates a reinforcing system where:

- Stable regions attract capital and jobs  
- Unstable regions face reduced investment  
- Economic incentives align with security outcomes  

Mexico serves as the initial deployment environment, with long-term expansion across Latin America.

---

## 1. Strategic Principle — Capital Follows Stability

Industrial investment is guided by:

> **Measured, observable risk conditions at the sub-national level**

Rather than treating countries as uniform units, the system evaluates:

- States  
- Regions  
- Industrial corridors  

This allows:

- Precision capital allocation  
- Reduced exposure to instability  
- Faster scaling in viable regions  

---

## 2. Investment Risk Mapping System

DoDA maintains a **Regional Investment Risk Map** across Mexico and, over time, Latin America.

### Classification Tiers

**Tier 1 — Low-Risk / Stabilized Regions**
- Low cartel activity  
- Reliable governance  
- Eligible for full industrial deployment  

**Tier 2 — Transitional Regions**
- Moderate risk  
- Eligible for partial or monitored investment  

**Tier 3 — High-Risk Regions**
- High cartel activity  
- Not eligible for strategic manufacturing deployment  

---

### System Characteristics

The map is:

- Continuously updated  
- Data-driven (violence, governance, infrastructure metrics)  
- Publicly visible  
- Used directly in investment decision-making  

---

## 3. Deployment Logic

Industrial capital is allocated according to risk tier:

- Tier 1 regions receive concentrated investment  
- Tier 2 regions receive selective, conditional investment  
- Tier 3 regions are excluded from strategic deployment  

This ensures:

> Industrial systems are built in environments where they can operate reliably

---

## 4. Expansion Mechanism — Growing the Map

The system is designed to expand over time.

Regions can move between tiers based on:

- Sustained reduction in cartel activity  
- Improved rule-of-law indicators  
- Infrastructure and governance improvements  

Reclassification triggers:

- Increased investment eligibility  
- Integration into supplier systems  
- Inclusion in corridor development  

---

## 5. Economic Signal Design

The framework creates a visible economic gradient:

- Stable regions receive factories, jobs, and infrastructure  
- Unstable regions receive limited or no strategic investment  

This generates:

> Internal economic pressure for stability and governance improvement

The mechanism operates through:

- Labor market incentives  
- Local business pressure  
- State-level political competition  

---

## 6. Mexico as Initial Anchor

Mexico serves as the first deployment environment due to:

- Geographic proximity  
- Existing industrial base  
- Integration with U.S. supply chains  
- Scale potential comparable to historical manufacturing hubs  

The objective is to:

> Establish Mexico as a high-capacity, regionally integrated manufacturing base

---

## 7. Latin America Expansion Strategy

Over a 10-year horizon, the framework expands beyond Mexico.

### Phase 1 — Mexico Investment
- Build Tier 1 industrial zones  
- Scale supplier ecosystems  
- Establish investment credibility  

### Phase 2 — Regional Extension
- Identify similar low-risk regions in Latin America  
- Extend investment mapping system  
- Begin selective industrial deployment  

### Phase 3 — Networked Industrial System
- Create interconnected production zones across the region  
- Enable intra-regional investment flows  
- Allow Mexico to act as a capital and industrial bridge  

---

## 8. Second-Order Effects (Implicit)

As the system scales:

- Labor demand increases in stable regions  
- Informal and cartel-linked economic activity becomes less competitive  
- Regional migration pressures shift toward formal labor markets  

These outcomes are not direct policy goals, but emerge from:

> Sustained alignment of economic opportunity with stability

---

## 9. Integration with Manufacturing Stack

This framework integrates directly with:

### Demand Coordination System
Ensures suppliers in low-risk regions receive visible demand signals

### NORAM Manufacturing Alignment
Provides geographic structure for nearshoring  

### Execution Corps
Deploys industrial capacity into qualified regions  

### Tariff Governance
Reinforces geographic production alignment  

---

## 10. Guardrails

To prevent unintended consequences:

- Investment withdrawal is gradual, not sudden  
- Transitional regions retain partial access  
- Re-entry pathways are clearly defined  
- Capital is not fully withdrawn from high-risk regions in non-critical sectors  

This ensures:

> The system remains incentive-based rather than punitive

---

## 11. Strategic Outcome

Over time, the framework produces:

- Expansion of stable industrial zones  
- Reduced reliance on high-risk regions  
- Alignment of economic growth with governance quality  
- Emergence of a regionally integrated manufacturing system  

The system does not attempt to directly solve security challenges.

It ensures that:

> Industrial capital flows reinforce stability wherever it emerges

---
## 12. Regional Stability Scoring Model & Investment Response System

### Purpose

This section defines the **quantitative scoring model and capital allocation response curve** used to operationalize the Cartel De-Risking Framework.

The system converts:

> Security, governance, and infrastructure conditions → into → capital allocation decisions

---

## 12.1 Stability Score (S-Score)

Each region (state or sub-state corridor) is assigned a **Stability Score (S-Score)** on a 0–100 scale.

The score is composed of five weighted components:

### A. Security Conditions (30%)

- Violent crime rate (normalized)  
- Cartel activity indicators (intelligence + incident reports)  
- Trend direction (improving vs deteriorating)  

---

### B. Rule of Law & Governance (20%)

- Law enforcement effectiveness  
- Judicial reliability  
- Corruption indicators  
- Contract enforcement outcomes  

---

### C. Infrastructure & Industrial Readiness (20%)

- Power reliability  
- Logistics access (road, rail, port proximity)  
- Industrial zoning availability  
- Telecommunications capacity  

---

### D. Workforce & Labor Stability (15%)

- Skilled labor availability  
- Training pipeline capacity  
- Labor participation rates  
- Workforce retention stability  

---

### E. Economic Formalization (15%)

- Share of formal vs informal economy  
- Tax base stability  
- Business registration rates  
- SME ecosystem density  

---

### S-Score Calculation

S-Score = Weighted composite of all five categories

Scores are:

- Updated quarterly  
- Based on rolling 12-month data  
- Published publicly  

---

## 12.2 Tier Classification

Regions are classified based on S-Score:

| Tier | Score Range | Classification |
|------|------------|----------------|
| Tier 1 | 70–100 | Low-Risk / Stabilized |
| Tier 2 | 50–69  | Transitional |
| Tier 3 | 0–49   | High-Risk |

---

## 12.3 Investment Response Curve

Capital allocation follows a **non-linear response curve**.

### Tier 1 (70–100)

- Full industrial deployment  
- Corridor-level investment  
- Supplier ecosystem activation  
- Priority nearshoring destination  

---

### Tier 2 (50–69)

- Selective sector deployment  
- Smaller-scale manufacturing  
- Monitoring + conditional expansion  
- Workforce pipeline activation  

---

### Tier 3 (0–49)

- No strategic manufacturing deployment  
- Limited non-critical investment allowed  
- Monitoring only  

---

## 12.4 Gradient-Based Allocation (Within Tiers)

Within each tier, capital allocation scales continuously.

Example:

- S-Score 72 → baseline Tier 1 deployment  
- S-Score 88 → priority Tier 1 (accelerated investment)  

This prevents:

> Cliff effects and abrupt investment discontinuities

---

## 12.5 Transition Rules

### Upward Movement (Reward Acceleration)

If a region improves:

- +5 point increase sustained over 2 quarters → early access to next-tier benefits  
- +10 point increase → accelerated investment deployment  

---

### Downward Movement (Controlled Withdrawal)

If a region deteriorates:

- −5 point drop → pause new investment  
- −10 point drop → gradual capital reallocation  

Critical rule:

> Withdrawal is slower than expansion to avoid destabilization shocks

---

## 12.6 Investment Flow Dynamics

### Positive Flow (Stabilization)

Higher S-Score → Increased:

- Capital investment  
- Job creation  
- Supplier density  
- Infrastructure buildout  

---

### Negative Flow (Risk Containment)

Lower S-Score → Reduced:

- New project approvals  
- Strategic sector participation  
- Capital intensity  

---

## 12.7 Time Horizon Dynamics

The system operates across three timeframes:

### Short-Term (0–2 Years)
- Stabilize investment in Tier 1 regions  
- Avoid exposure to Tier 3 regions  

---

### Mid-Term (2–5 Years)
- Expand Tier 1 footprint  
- Upgrade Tier 2 regions  

---

### Long-Term (5–10 Years)
- Expand total stabilized geographic area  
- Enable regional industrial network formation  

---

## 12.8 Feedback Loop Integration

The system continuously updates based on:

- Real investment outcomes  
- Security condition changes  
- Workforce and infrastructure development  

This creates a **closed feedback loop**:

Security → Investment → Jobs → Local Pressure → Security

---

## 12.9 Anti-Gaming Safeguards

To maintain integrity:

- Independent data verification required  
- No self-reported scoring by local authorities  
- Lagged scoring to prevent short-term manipulation  
- Multi-source data inputs (public + intelligence + private sector)  

---

## 12.10 System-Level Outcome

Over time, the scoring system produces:

- Expansion of Tier 1 regions  
- Contraction of high-risk zones  
- Alignment of capital flows with stability  

The system does not force outcomes.

It ensures that:

> Stability is consistently rewarded with economic growth

# PUBLIC FACING DRAFT: Invest Where It Works

Not every place is equally ready for investment.

Some regions are stable and growing. Others face challenges that make it hard for businesses to operate.

This plan focuses on investing where it works.

We prioritize areas that are safe, stable, and ready to grow.  
Over time, more areas can qualify as conditions improve.

That means:

- More jobs in stable regions  
- Stronger supply chains close to home  
- Clear incentives for improvement

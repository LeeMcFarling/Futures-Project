# Construction Throughput Mapping Initiative (CTMI)  
## Pre-Mapping & De-Risking Building Materials Bottlenecks

## Purpose

The Construction Throughput Mapping Initiative (CTMI) establishes a preventative, rule-based framework to identify and de-risk supply constraints that could undermine scalable housing expansion under the Special Transit Zone (STZ) model. Its purpose is not to direct industrial production or mandate specific materials, but to ensure that successful housing reform does not trigger avoidable bottlenecks in critical construction inputs.

CTMI recognizes that recent supply chain disruptions exposed structural fragility across glazing systems, electrical components, mechanical equipment, and structural assemblies. If STZ-enabled re-urbanization scales as designed, demand for standardized mid-rise assemblies will increase in concentrated corridors. Without advance capacity mapping, that surge could produce inflationary pressure, extended lead times, and schedule instability that would undermine affordability goals.

CTMI therefore functions as industrial due diligence. It maps constraints before they destabilize housing throughput.

---

## Problem Statement

The United States has experienced repeated material volatility and lead-time shocks over the past decade, revealing uneven domestic surge capacity and concentrated import exposure in key construction sectors. These disruptions were not isolated anomalies but indicators of systemic vulnerability in high-leverage building inputs.

At the same time, the STZ framework and modular re-urbanization strategy are explicitly designed to unlock sustained housing supply at scale. If successful, these reforms will generate predictable, repeatable demand for standardized assemblies embedded within Regional Architectural Pattern Books. Concentrated growth without prior industrial readiness risks recreating the very inflation and delay dynamics the policy intends to resolve.

CTMI addresses this coordination failure by pre-auditing capacity, quantifying volatility risk, and activating time-bound stabilization tools only when statistically verified thresholds are breached. It does not regulate price formation or dictate supplier choice. It strengthens market function by improving visibility and reducing avoidable variance.

---

## Scope

CTMI applies exclusively to construction inputs formally designated within standardized pattern-book assemblies used in STZ-aligned mid-rise development. It does not monitor all construction materials and does not expand national building codes.

Eligible components must meet all of the following conditions:

1. They are critical to mid-rise residential or mixed-use development within STZ corridors.  
2. They represent meaningful cost or schedule leverage.  
3. They exhibit measurable cost volatility, import concentration, or limited domestic surge capacity.  
4. They pose documented risk under projected STZ rollout scenarios. 

Illustrative categories include insulated glazing units, exterior wall systems, structural framing assemblies, heat pumps and ventilation equipment, electrical distribution gear, fire protection systems, elevators, and insulation systems. CTMI does not evaluate general commodity markets outside this defined envelope.

---

## Core Principle

CTMI is diagnostic first and intervention-conditional. Monitoring does not equal mandate. Publication of a bottleneck index does not equal activation of subsidy.

Intervention tools may activate only when predefined statistical thresholds are met and delivery risk is verified. All activation criteria, methodologies, and review cadences are published in advance. No discretionary override is permitted outside defined trigger pathways.

The objective is throughput stability, not industrial steering.

---

## Construction Bottleneck Index (CBI)

CTMI operationalizes risk monitoring through the Construction Bottleneck Index (CBI), a composite metric that measures supply stress across monitored standardized assemblies.

Each component receives a normalized score derived from four empirically weighted factors:

- Cost Deviation Index (CDI)  
- Lead Time Index (LTI)  
- Capacity Coverage Ratio (CCR)  
- Concentration & Import Exposure Index (CIEI)

Weights are derived using regression back-testing on historical construction cycles. DoDa constructs a multi-year dataset including project cost overruns, schedule delays, financing extensions, component price volatility, and STZ-aligned demand modeling. Out-of-sample predictive contribution determines weight proportionality. Weights are normalized and published annually in a Weight Calibration Memo.

Weights may be recalibrated once per fiscal year on a fixed calendar date. No recalibration may occur during an active intervention cycle. No factor weight may shift more than ±0.05 annually absent documented structural break conditions. Methodology and confidence intervals are publicly archived and independently audited biennially.

Risk bands are defined as follows:

- 0–30: Stable  
- 31–50: Watchlist  
- 51–70: Elevated Risk  
- 71+: Critical Risk  

Two consecutive quarters above 51 initiate formal review. Critical Risk classification requires sustained breach above 70.

---

## Escalation Framework

Escalation under CTMI proceeds through tiered, time-bound instruments.

Tier I tools activate under sustained Elevated Risk classification and include demand forecasting publication, workforce development support, and reinforcement of open multi-vendor specification pathways within pattern books. These tools reduce uncertainty without fiscal exposure.

Tier II tools activate when risk remains elevated across review cycles and may include federal credit enhancement for plant expansion, accelerated depreciation allowances, and conditional volume purchase commitments aligned to STZ rollout projections.All such tools sunset automatically when risk bands normalize.

Tier III tools activate only under sustained Critical Risk classification and documented delivery disruption to STZ projects. These may include temporary capacity grants, limited strategic procurement stabilization, or short-term import diversification mechanisms. All Tier III measures carry strict sunset provisions and exit plans tied to domestic capacity restoration.

---

## Executive Backstop

If Tier III measures fail to stabilize supply and domestic surge capacity cannot restore equilibrium within defined windows, executive trade coordination mechanisms may be temporarily deployed. Such measures are limited to monitored components and must include automatic sunset clauses and congressional reporting.

Executive authority operates as a stabilizing backstop, not a permanent industrial instrument.

---

## Guardrails

CTMI includes the following structural safeguards:

- No price controls or mandated cost targets.  
- No exclusive supplier designation.  
- No single firm may receive more than 25% of total support under any activation cycle.  
- All standardized assemblies must maintain multi-vendor compliance eligibility.  
- All capital tools sunset automatically unless reauthorized under sustained statistical breach.  

CTMI cannot expand beyond its defined scope without statutory amendment.

---

## Strategic Outcome

CTMI ensures that a housing expansion under STZ scaling does not generate self-inflicted inflation or supply shock. It strengthens the credibility of rule-based infrastructure deployment by aligning industrial readiness with projected demand.

By publishing demand signals early, stabilizing volatility before crisis thresholds, and preserving open-market competition, CTMI supports a housing boom without introducing central planning or permanent industrial mandates.

The initiative converts industrial uncertainty into measurable risk and transforms risk into predictable response. Housing throughput becomes scalable because its supply chain is pre-audited rather than assumed frictionless.

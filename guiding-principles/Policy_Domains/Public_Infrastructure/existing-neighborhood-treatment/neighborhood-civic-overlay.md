## Purpose

The Neighborhood Civic Overlay (NCO) is a rule-based, opt-in zoning overlay designed to retrofit existing subdivisions and traditional neighborhoods with walkable, daily-life amenities while preserving their residential character. Its purpose is to allow a small set of useful civic and neighborhood-scale services—by right—so people can meet everyday needs within walking distance of their homes.

The NCO is not a density mandate and does not broadly rezone neighborhoods. It is a targeted tool to make walking practical first, and to let higher-order transit and density follow only where demand is clearly demonstrated.

---

## Problem Statement

Many American neighborhoods are physically capable of walkability but legally blocked from hosting the places that make walking worthwhile. Zoning rules separate housing from basic services, forcing short trips onto roads and increasing infrastructure costs without increasing local revenue.

When small walkable amenities are approved informally, they often arrive without a plan for long-term maintenance. This leaves cities with growing costs and no stable way to pay for them. The NCO addresses both problems at the same time.

---

## Core Principles

The overlay is voluntary, additive, and administrative. It does not change base residential zoning, lot sizes, or neighborhood form. If a project meets the published standards, it is approved.

Each project must pass a simple test: will the new shops or services bring in enough local tax revenue to help cover the extra wear and maintenance they add to roads, utilities, lighting, and public spaces? The overlay only looks at new impacts and does not ask neighborhoods to pay for old or existing infrastructure.

The overlay is designed to work across neighborhood boundaries. Adjacent neighborhoods are encouraged to adopt compatible overlays in nearby locations so shared amenities can serve both areas and naturally form small local hubs.

---

## Eligible Uses

Permitted uses are intentionally limited and small in scale. Examples include small food markets and bakeries, childcare and early learning centers, primary care and physical therapy clinics, neighborhood cafés, community pools or gyms, shared workspaces, tutoring centers, and small professional offices serving local residents.

Uses that attract large crowds, late-night activity, or regional traffic are not allowed.

---

## Form & Compatibility Standards

All projects must fit the neighborhood. Buildings must stay within residential or missing-middle scale, follow strict size limits tied to the local population, meet noise and vibration standards, operate during controlled hours, and prioritize pedestrian access.

Architectural appearance follows locally adopted architectural pattern books so new buildings match the character of the surrounding community. Subjective, case-by-case aesthetic judgment is avoided.

---

## Administrative Review

Projects are reviewed administratively using a simple checklist. Approval is based on compliance with local standards and pattern books.

Because individual veto power has historically blocked modest and widely supported improvements, no single property owner or small group may block a compliant project. If the jurisdiction does not act within the review window, approval is automatic.

---

## Infrastructure Solvency Test

Each project must answer one question:

“Will the local tax revenue generated by these new shops or services help cover the cost of maintaining roads and other infrastructure in your area?”

If yes, the project proceeds. If no, it does not.

The test applies only to new impacts and does not require funding for existing infrastructure.

---

## Seam Coordination

When neighboring areas adopt compatible overlays, the city may designate a shared civic seam. These seams are ideal places to cluster amenities, create higher-quality public spaces, and eventually support transit connections.

This does not broadly upzone neighborhoods. It simply places useful, everyday services where people already walk, so daily needs can be met without changing the rest of the neighborhood.

---

## Transit Integration Path

Transit follows demonstrated use. First comes walkability. Then local bus or shuttle service. Fixed rail is considered only after clear demand is shown through real usage, not forecasts.

---

## Relationship to STZs and Nodes

Neighborhood Civic Overlays generate early, local demand. Coordinated seams surface candidate nodes. Those nodes may later qualify for Special Transit Zones, which unlock larger infrastructure investments.

This sequencing keeps growth predictable and grounded in real behavior.

---

## Incentives & Capital Alignment

Projects that comply may qualify for better financing terms, faster permitting, or infrastructure support. Incentives are automatic and rule-based, not negotiated.

---

## Outcomes & Decision Rules

Success is measured by more walking for daily trips, positive local revenue for infrastructure maintenance, stable or improving neighborhood satisfaction, and sufficient demand to justify transit connections.

Failure remains local and does not spread system-wide.

---

## Why This Works

The Neighborhood Civic Overlay restores everyday walkability without forcing large-scale change. It gives residents nearby amenities they will actually use, helps cities maintain infrastructure responsibly, and allows transit to grow naturally where it makes sense.

Walkability comes first. Demand proves itself. Infrastructure follows.

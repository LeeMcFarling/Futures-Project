# Continuous Competitive Procurement  
## Department of Defense Pilot Framework

## Purpose

This pilot establishes a **continuous competitive procurement architecture** for high-demand defense systems.

The objective is to prevent vendor lock-in, maintain cost discipline, accelerate engineering improvement, and ensure sustained surge production capacity.

Traditional defense procurement awards a single prime contractor long-term production authority. This structure often results in:

- cost escalation  
- industrial bottlenecks  
- limited innovation pressure  
- dependence on single production nodes

The Continuous Competitive Procurement Framework replaces single-prime lifecycle contracts with **contestable production portfolios**.

Multiple qualified manufacturers produce subsystems simultaneously while competing for future production share.

Competition continues throughout the production lifecycle rather than ending at contract award.

---

# I. Core Principle: Continuous Contestability

No contractor retains permanent production authority for designated systems.

Production allocation is periodically reassessed based on measurable performance outcomes.

Manufacturers maintain or expand production share only by demonstrating superior performance relative to competitors.

Competition becomes a continuous industrial process rather than a one-time procurement event.

---

# II. Portfolio Production Model

Designated systems are produced through **multi-vendor portfolio allocation**.

At program entry, several manufacturers are certified to produce the system or its subsystems.

Initial production shares are distributed across vendors to establish manufacturing capacity and industrial redundancy.

Example structure:

Vendor A — 50%  
Vendor B — 30%  
Vendor C — 20%

Production share is subject to periodic reallocation based on performance evaluation.

All vendors remain active competitors during production.

---

# III. Evaluation Metrics

Production allocation is adjusted through standardized performance metrics.

Primary evaluation criteria include:

### Cost Efficiency

Cost per subsystem or unit produced at operational scale.

### Operational Reliability

Observed reliability of components under real-world conditions.

### Manufacturing Throughput

Baseline production capacity during steady-state conditions.

### Surge Production Capability

Ability to expand production rapidly during crisis conditions.

### Supply Chain Resilience

Redundancy of critical upstream inputs and manufacturing nodes.

Evaluation metrics rely heavily on operational testing data generated through the **Continuous Test & Certification Infrastructure**.

---

# IV. Evaluation Cycles

Production allocation is reassessed on fixed intervals.

Typical evaluation cycles occur every **24–36 months** depending on system complexity.

During evaluation cycles:

- vendor performance metrics are reviewed
- subsystem reliability data is assessed
- surge testing results are incorporated

Production share adjusts automatically according to predefined rules.

Persistent underperformance may trigger decertification.

---

# V. Modular Architecture Requirement

Continuous competition requires modular subsystem architecture.

Designated systems must adhere to **modular open interface standards** that allow subsystems to be replaced without redesigning the entire platform.

Interface standards define:

- mechanical interfaces
- electrical interfaces
- thermal tolerances
- data protocols
- maintenance diagnostics

Sensitive system specifications remain classified. Only integration interfaces are standardized.

---

# VI. Industrial Redundancy

Systems participating in the pilot must maintain **multiple certified production nodes**.

Redundancy requirements include:

- multiple subsystem manufacturers
- geographically distributed production facilities
- diversified upstream suppliers

Industrial redundancy is treated as a core national security requirement rather than a byproduct of procurement.

---

# VII. Integration with Testing Infrastructure

Certification for production eligibility is determined through the **Continuous Test & Certification Infrastructure**.

Subsystems must demonstrate operational performance during formal evaluation cycles.

Production allocation decisions rely directly on observed test performance rather than documentation compliance.

Testing outcomes therefore function as procurement inputs.

---

# VIII. Missile Interceptor Platform Example

Missile interceptors provide a clear example of how the framework operates.

Interceptor systems consist of modular subsystems including:

- propulsion stages
- seeker assemblies
- guidance electronics
- maneuvering thrusters
- warhead components
- launch interface systems

Under continuous competitive procurement, multiple manufacturers produce these subsystems simultaneously.

Subsystems must comply with standardized interface specifications ensuring compatibility with the interceptor architecture.

---

## Example: Propulsion Module Competition

Three manufacturers are certified to produce interceptor propulsion modules.

Initial allocation:

Vendor A — 50%  
Vendor B — 30%  
Vendor C — 20%

After the evaluation cycle:

Vendor B demonstrates lower cost and improved surge manufacturing capacity.

Production allocation shifts:

Vendor B — 45%  
Vendor A — 35%  
Vendor C — 20%

The interceptor system itself remains unchanged.

Only production allocation adjusts.

---

## Example: Seeker System Development

Multiple manufacturers develop seeker assemblies meeting identical interface specifications.

Vendors iterate prototypes through continuous testing ranges.

During certification events:

- seeker accuracy
- thermal resilience
- electronic interference resistance

are evaluated.

Manufacturers demonstrating superior performance gain production share in the next procurement cycle.

---

# IX. Surge Production Benefits

Maintaining multiple active production lines dramatically improves surge capacity.

In traditional procurement systems, scaling production requires expanding a single prime contractor's facilities.

Under the portfolio model, surge production expands across multiple certified manufacturers simultaneously.

This significantly shortens the timeline required to scale interceptor production during crises.

---

# X. Strategic Effect

The Continuous Competitive Procurement Framework transforms defense acquisition from a static contract model into a **dynamic industrial ecosystem**.

Manufacturers compete continuously to maintain production share.

Engineering improvements translate directly into increased market participation.

Industrial redundancy improves resilience against supply chain disruption.

---

# Bottom Line

Defense procurement should not select a single winner for decades.

It should maintain a competitive industrial ecosystem where manufacturers must **continually earn their place in production**.

Competition becomes a permanent feature of the defense industrial base rather than an occasional procurement event.

# Alliance-Wide Critical Materials & Energy Resilience Framework

## Purpose

This framework establishes a binding alliance standard for identifying, governing,
and protecting critical materials and energy inputs whose disruption would
materially degrade defense readiness, economic stability, or escalation control.

The objective is **resilience under correlated failure**, not efficiency under
normal conditions. The framework constrains coercion, reduces incentives for
national hedging, and preserves alliance freedom of action across peacetime,
gray-zone pressure, and conflict.

This framework does not mandate any specific ownership model. It defines
qualification, redundancy, triggers, and continuity rules that apply regardless
of whether capacity is privately operated, state-owned, or hybrid, and that
operate primarily through markets until activation thresholds are met.

---

## 1. Authority: What Constitutes an Alliance-Critical Material

A material or energy input is designated **Alliance-Critical** if it meets **any two**
of the following criteria, or **any one** at elevated threat posture:

1. Loss of supply would impair alliance military operations, defense industrial
   output, or strategic deterrence within 12 months.
2. Loss of supply would cause cascading failure across multiple civilian sectors
   essential to wartime sustainment.
3. Replacement time, including qualification, permitting, and logistics, exceeds
   24 months under disrupted conditions.
4. Processing, refinement, or fabrication is concentrated in fewer than three
   independent jurisdictions or firms.
5. The material is subject to coercive leverage, export restriction, cartel
   behavior, or chokepoint control **by any actor**, including allied states,
   allied firms, or alliance-internal jurisdictions.
6. The material is required for nuclear energy, medical isotopes, or
   treaty-governed fuel cycles where interruption creates disproportionate risk.

Alliance designation explicitly accounts for intra-alliance coercion and asymmetric
dependency. Alliance membership does not exempt a supply chain from resilience
requirements.

---

## 2. Covered Material Classes

At minimum, this framework applies to:

- Oil, refined fuels, and strategic petroleum products  
- Rare earth elements and critical minerals  
- Energetics, propellants, explosives precursors, and specialty chemicals  
- Semiconductor inputs, process gases, wafers, and fabrication-critical materials  
- Nuclear fuel, enrichment services, fuel fabrication, waste handling, and
  essential medical and industrial isotopes  

Civilian and military supply chains are treated as interdependent unless proven
otherwise.

---

## 3. Default N+2 Resilience Standard

Unless explicitly waived, the alliance sets **N = 3**.

Each Alliance-Critical input must maintain:

- 3 active suppliers  
- 3 active processors / refiners / fabricators  
- +2 fully qualified alternates  

All distributed across **at least two independent theaters**.

This is a floor, not a target.

---

## 4. Independence and Theater Separation

Suppliers or processors count as distinct only if failure of one does not cascade
through shared ownership, jurisdiction, energy inputs, transport routes, insurance,
regulatory authority, or labor pools.

At least one supplier–processor pair must sit outside the primary contingency
theater relevant to that domain. Resilience planning assumes simultaneous loss of
one primary node and one theater-level node.

---

## 5. Dual Sourcing, Routing, and Finance

Each compliant supply chain must demonstrate:

- Dual sourcing across independent jurisdictions  
- Dual routing across non-shared maritime, land, air, and digital logistics  
- Dual insurance and financing pathways not reliant on a single market or
  clearing system  

Dependence on a single chokepoint constitutes non-compliance absent waiver.

---

## 6. Stockpile and Buffer Doctrine

Stockpiles are sized to **time-to-replace**, not consumption alone, and are:

- Distributed rather than centralized  
- Category-specific  
- Held under clear custodial responsibility  

Stockpiles exist to buy decision time and prevent coercion.

---

## 7. Offtake Contracts and Triggers

The alliance pre-negotiates conditional offtake agreements that activate only
under objective triggers, including:

- Physical disruption or seizure  
- Transport denial or blockade  
- Insurance withdrawal  
- Treaty-linked defense activation  
- Coercive export restriction by any actor  

Political disagreement alone is not a trigger.

---

## 8. Processing and Refinement Chokepoints

Processing and refinement are treated as primary strategic bottlenecks. The
alliance maintains minimum viable capacity across multiple jurisdictions even
when not cost-optimal in peacetime.

---

## 9. Transport, Insurance, and Legal Continuity

The alliance pre-establishes contingent insurance backstops, shared risk pools,
and legal safe harbors to ensure continuity of transport and contracting under
stress.

Deterrence credibility depends on robust, diversified availability of critical
materials and energy inputs.

---

## 10. Nuclear Materials, Fuel-Cycle Resilience, and Deterrence Assurance

Nuclear materials and fuel-cycle services are explicitly included due to their
civilian and strategic importance.

This framework operates within existing non-proliferation regimes and does not
authorize new weapons pathways.

The purpose of alliance nuclear fuel assurance and deterrence posture is to
**reduce incentives for national nuclear hedging** by ensuring continuity and
credible security guarantees.

Under verified hostile threat, the alliance may employ forward deployment of
alliance-controlled capabilities within the territory of consenting member
states, under existing legal frameworks and without conferring national control.

Continuity guarantees do not apply to actors in material breach of
non-proliferation obligations.

---

## 11. Governance, Compliance, and Anti-Coercion Safeguards

Governance is designed to prevent adversarial and intra-alliance coercion.

- Symmetric choke protection applies to all members, regardless of size.
- Allegations of alliance-internal coercion trigger an independent review panel
  with authority to impose interim procedural constraints.
- Under verified threat, affected members receive assured access to
  defense-sustaining inputs that cannot be subordinated to GDP weighting or
  political bargaining.
- Forward deployment requires explicit host-nation consent and may be terminated
  by the host under predefined conditions.
- Members asked to accelerate extraction or transit receive standardized
  value-sharing packages.
- Crisis allocation schedules are pre-published, with a non-discrimination
  minimum sustainment floor.

---

## 12. Phase Alignment

- Phase 1: Constraint definition and designation authority  
- Phase 2: Asset mapping, chokepoint analysis, contract scaffolding  
- Phase 3: Locked triggers, decision rules, and governance  

---

## Design Principle

The alliance plans for **correlated failure, not bad luck**.  
Resilience replaces improvisation. Rules replace leverage. Assurance replaces
proliferation.

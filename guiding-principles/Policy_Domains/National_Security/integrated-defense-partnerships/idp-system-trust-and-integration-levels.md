# IDP Governance — System Trust and Integration Level Framework

## Purpose

This brief defines how the alliance maintains system integrity when member-state behavior introduces risk into shared production, logistics, or security systems. It establishes a continuous, rule-based classification framework that determines the degree to which any partner can safely operate within tightly coupled alliance architecture — and adjusts that classification automatically when observed behavior changes the calculation.

Participation level reflects system compatibility, not political judgment. Movement between levels is continuous reclassification based on observed behavior, not punishment. The system applies to all members including the United States. No member's size or strategic importance creates immunity from reclassification.

The design objective is simple: misbehavior becomes self-penalizing. Compliance becomes the only reliable path to economic and strategic advantage within the alliance. The system does the disciplining. Political actors do not.

---

## Core Principle: System Compatibility, Not Regime Type

The alliance does not evaluate partners based on political system, ideology, or governance model. The question is never what kind of government a country has. The question is whether a partner's current behavior makes the shared system safer or more fragile.

This distinction is what makes the framework defensible, consistent, and hard to weaponize politically. A partner that governs very differently from its allies but operates with full integrity within shared industrial and security systems participates at full integration level. A partner whose behavior — regardless of governance model — introduces systemic risk gets reclassified automatically. The metric is observable behavior inside the system. Nothing else.

---

## Integration Levels

All partners are continuously classified into one of four integration levels based on their current System Trust Score. Classification is a current state, not a permanent designation. It moves in either direction as behavior changes.

**Level 1 — Full Industrial Integration.** Full participation in EPU certification, production allocation, shared interface standards, and the competitive procurement system. Maximum allocation share eligibility. Full depth of intelligence sharing, joint logistics planning, and industrial cooperation within the IDP framework.

**Level 2 — Conditional Industrial Integration.** Restricted participation in specified system classes. Allocation share capped below Level 1 maximum. Enhanced audit requirements apply. EPUs operated by Level 2 partners carry elevated ERS correlation penalties reflecting the additional uncertainty their current status introduces into the network. Level 2 is a constrained operating state under active evaluation, not a steady-state target. Partners either demonstrate restored compatibility and return to Level 1 or continue degrading toward Level 3. Indefinite operation at Level 2 is treated as sustained non-compliance and will trigger continued score evaluation accordingly.

**Level 3 — Limited Industrial Integration.** No participation in shared production systems. EPUs are decertified from the IDP pool and production share is reallocated across remaining certified EPUs. Security coordination continues under applicable frameworks where it serves alliance stability. Industrial integration resumes only after reclassification to Level 2 or above.

**Level 4 — Security Coordination Only.** No industrial integration. Minimal coordination limited to defined channels where continued engagement serves alliance stability. Treated as external to the alliance production network for all EPU and allocation purposes.

Entry at any level is normal. Some partners enter at Level 1, others at Level 2 or Level 3, depending on current system compatibility. Entry level carries no stigma. Trajectory is what matters.

---

## System Trust Score

Each partner is continuously evaluated across three dimensions.

**Industrial Integrity Score (IIS)** measures reliability and transparency within shared production systems: procurement integrity, EPU compliance, audit cooperation, supply chain transparency, and adherence to interface standards and allocation rules.

**Security Compliance Score (SCS)** measures compatibility with shared alliance security commitments: non-aggression toward other members, adherence to escalation frameworks, non-interference in alliance coordination mechanisms, and avoidance of actions that entangle the alliance in conflict without appropriate coordination.

**System Stability Score (SSS)** measures whether internal conditions create predictable risk to the shared network: production continuity, infrastructure reliability, logistics dependability, and the absence of state actions that generate foreseeable supply chain disruption across the alliance.

These three scores produce a composite **System Trust Score (STS)** computed continuously against pre-published weights and data inputs. The weights and methodology are published, auditable, and not subject to discretionary adjustment by any single member or political authority. Data inputs are sourced from a combination of alliance audit bodies, verified reporting systems, and cross-member validation mechanisms. No single member state controls the input layer.

### Classification Thresholds

| STS Range | Integration Level |
|---|---|
| ≥ 0.8 | Level 1 — Full Industrial Integration |
| 0.6 – 0.8 | Level 2 — Conditional Industrial Integration |
| 0.4 – 0.6 | Level 3 — Limited Industrial Integration |
| < 0.4 | Level 4 — Security Coordination Only |


### Update Cadence

Score updates operate on three timescales. Trigger inputs are assessed in near real-time as observable events occur and are reported through alliance audit channels. Score recalculation occurs on a rolling weekly window, ensuring that score movement reflects sustained behavior rather than single-event noise. Integration level and allocation adjustments take effect at the next quarterly evaluation cycle following a threshold crossing, except in cases of severe acute violations where immediate reclassification is warranted. This cadence ensures the system responds to trajectory rather than reacting to isolated incidents while remaining fast enough to matter when drift becomes sustained.

Threshold calibration is a technical exercise requiring validation across industrial, security, and operational domains. The thresholds above are illustrative. The enforcement architecture — that classification follows score automatically — is the policy decision.

## Trigger Categories

Triggers are pre-defined, observable conditions that feed directly into score components. They are not political determinations. They are inputs to a scoring system.

**Industrial integrity triggers** directly affect IDP systems: corruption or manipulation in defense procurement, interference with certified EPUs or supply chains, refusal to comply with audit or transparency requirements, unauthorized export or diversion of IDP systems. Effect: immediate IIS degradation, EPU decertification, automatic ERS penalties across affected nodes.

**Alliance security triggers** undermine network trust between members: aggressive action toward alliance members or partners, gray-zone coercion affecting alliance stability, material violation of non-aggression commitments, actions that risk entangling the alliance in conflict without coordination. Effect: immediate SCS degradation, suspension of affected EPUs, transition toward Level 3 if threshold is exceeded.

**System stability triggers** create spillover risk into shared systems: large-scale internal instability affecting production continuity, state actions that disrupt labor, infrastructure, or logistics reliability, policies that create predictable alliance-level supply chain risk. Effect: SSS penalties applied across all EPUs in the affected jurisdiction, level adjustment depending on severity and duration.

Triggers accumulate. A partner experiencing multiple minor triggers across categories may reach a reclassification threshold that no single trigger would have crossed alone. The composite score captures drift that individual trigger reviews might miss.

---

## Enforcement Mechanics

**Automatic and continuous.** When a trigger condition is met, the relevant score component updates, the STS is recalculated, and the integration level adjusts immediately if the new STS crosses a threshold. No vote is required. No political consensus is required. No discretionary authority intervenes between trigger and consequence.

**Industrial consequences are the primary lever.** Reclassification triggers EPU decertification or suspension, ERS penalties across affected nodes, automatic reduction in allocation share, and supply chain reweighting away from the degraded member. Loss of trust produces loss of production share. Reclassification directly reduces a member's share of alliance production, associated industrial investment, and downstream economic benefits. The economic consequence is immediate and mechanical.

**No certified-once status.** Scores are recalculated continuously. A partner that achieved Level 1 certification last year and has since drifted on any dimension will see its STS reflect that drift in real time. There is no grace period between score degradation and consequence activation.

**Anti-gaming safeguards.** Shared ownership or control between nominally separate EPUs introduces correlation penalties. Artificial corporate restructuring designed to bypass trigger conditions is treated as non-compliant. Selective data withholding triggers automatic score penalties. Political appeals, diplomatic pressure, and bilateral negotiations do not pause enforcement while a review is pending.

## The Drift Logic

Most reclassification will not be sudden. It will be gradual drift — a partner whose industrial integrity score erodes slowly over multiple procurement cycles, or whose security compliance score declines across a series of gray-zone incidents that individually fall below hard trigger thresholds but cumulatively move the STS toward a level boundary.

This gradual drift logic is a feature, not a bug. It means the system responds proportionally. A single incident produces a small score adjustment. Sustained misbehavior produces sustained drift. Severe or acute violations produce large immediate adjustments. The response is calibrated to the reality rather than being binary.

It also means partners can see their trajectory before a level change occurs. A partner whose STS is declining has visibility into the direction of travel and the magnitude of the drift. That visibility creates an ongoing incentive to correct before a level threshold is crossed — which is the preferred outcome. The system is not designed to trap partners in lower levels. It is designed to make the consequences of drift clear enough that correction happens before reclassification does.

At the extreme end of drift — sustained behavior that drives STS well below Level 4 threshold, or acute violations severe enough to collapse the score rapidly — the practical outcome is effective expulsion from the industrial network. The brief does not prescribe a formal expulsion mechanism because the industrial consequences of sustained Level 4 status accomplish the same result without requiring a political decision that would inevitably become contentious. The system does not need to expel. It simply stops integrating.

---

## Re-Entry Framework

Re-entry to a higher integration level is performance-based, not negotiated. Commitments and declarations do not move the score. Demonstrated behavior does.

Re-entry requires verified cessation of the triggering behavior, score recovery above the target level threshold sustained over a defined evaluation window, EPU re-certification including full independence and surge validation where applicable, and a probationary period at the target level under enhanced audit requirements before full reinstatement.

The probationary period exists because score recovery can be engineered temporarily. The evaluation window and probationary period together require that improved behavior is sustained rather than performed. A partner that cleans up its procurement practices for one quarter and then reverts will not complete the probationary window at the higher level. Its score will drift again before reinstatement is confirmed.

Re-entry is not penalized beyond what the scoring system requires. A partner that genuinely corrects its behavior and sustains that correction through the evaluation and probationary window returns to the level its score supports. The system does not hold grudges.

## Symmetry and Self-Application

This framework applies to all members. The United States is not exempt. A United States administration that manipulates alliance procurement for domestic political benefit, interferes with EPU certification processes, or takes security actions that entangle the alliance without coordination generates the same trigger inputs as any other member.

This symmetry is not idealistic — it is strategically necessary. An alliance integrity framework that exempts its largest member provides no real integrity guarantee. Partners will not accept a system that disciplines them but not the anchor member. And a system that cannot discipline the anchor member when it misbehaves is exactly the kind of hub-and-spoke dependency structure this architecture is designed to replace.

The scoring system's published methodology, independent audit function, and automatic enforcement are the mechanisms that make symmetry real rather than rhetorical. No member — including the largest — can override a score that reflects its own observed behavior.

## Worked Example: Coercive Territorial Pressure on an Alliance Member

The following illustrates how the scoring system responds to sustained behavior that individually stays below acute violation thresholds but cumulatively produces meaningful drift.

A major alliance member begins making repeated public statements asserting territorial claims over a geographic area administered by a fellow alliance member. The claims are not backed by military action. No shots are fired. No formal annexation is declared. The behavior sits in a gray zone: politically provocative, strategically destabilizing, but deniable as mere rhetoric.

**Weeks 1–4:** The statements generate diplomatic friction but no trigger inputs. STS unchanged.

**Weeks 5–8:** The member begins making procurement decisions that slow joint logistics planning with the affected alliance partner. Audit cooperation on two shared EPU nodes becomes intermittent. IIS begins a small decline. SCS registers the coercive posture as a non-aggression signal degradation. Weekly recalculation shows STS moving from 0.87 to 0.83 — still comfortably in Level 1, but the direction is visible to all members through the published score dashboard.

**Weeks 9–16:** Public statements escalate. The member's government begins making bilateral trade threats toward the affected partner. SSS registers supply chain uncertainty as regional logistics partners begin hedging. A second EPU audit response is delayed past the compliance window. IIS drops further. STS reaches 0.81. Still Level 1, but the trajectory is now clearly visible and documented.

**Weeks 17–24:** The member formally delays participation in a joint production allocation cycle, citing "ongoing bilateral discussions." This constitutes interference with alliance coordination mechanisms — a SCS trigger input. Score recalculation drops STS to 0.76. At the next quarterly evaluation cycle, the member is automatically reclassified to Level 2. No vote is taken. No political decision is made. The score crossed the threshold and the classification followed.

**Consequences at Level 2:** Allocation share is capped. Two EPU nodes operated by the member are placed under elevated ERS audit. Production share begins shifting toward EPUs in other theaters. The member's industrial partners within the alliance begin experiencing reduced order flow. The economic signal is immediate and visible.

**The correction incentive:** The member now faces a choice between continuing the behavior and watching its industrial integration — and the economic benefits attached to it — continue to drift downward, or demonstrating restored system compatibility through audit cooperation, cessation of bilateral coercion, and re-engagement with alliance coordination mechanisms. The system does not negotiate that choice. It simply prices it.

**If behavior continues:** Each additional trigger input moves the STS further toward the Level 3 threshold. The member's EPU share continues to shrink. Other certified EPUs in other theaters absorb the redistributed production. By the time the behavior has been sustained long enough to cross the Level 3 threshold, the practical industrial decoupling is already substantially complete. The formal reclassification confirms what the market has already registered.

**If behavior corrects:** Trigger inputs cease. Score components begin recovering on the weekly recalculation cycle. The member must sustain improved behavior through the evaluation window and probationary period before returning to Level 1. Declarations of intent do not move the score. Observed behavior does.

This is the system working as designed. No emergency summit. No political confrontation. No need for any member to formally accuse another of bad faith. The score drifted. The classification followed. The consequences accumulated. The incentive to correct was clear throughout.

## Strategic Effect

An alliance with a functioning system trust framework changes the strategic calculus for misbehavior in a fundamental way. The cost of defection, coercion, or industrial manipulation is not a diplomatic consequence that can be negotiated away. It is an automatic reduction in production share, an automatic loss of EPU certification, and an automatic reweighting of the alliance industrial network away from the degrading member.

That makes the system self-reinforcing. Partners that maintain full system compatibility capture more production share, more allocation, more industrial integration, and more of the economic and security benefits the network produces. Partners that degrade capture less — automatically and continuously. The incentive gradient runs in the right direction without requiring any political actor to enforce it.

The framework also makes the alliance more credible to potential partners considering entry. A system with visible, mechanical, symmetric enforcement is a system that can be trusted. A system that disciplines some members politically while exempting others cannot. The integrity of the framework is itself an alliance asset.

---

## Bottom Line

Participation in the IDP framework is not a political designation. It is a continuously earned certification of system compatibility. When a member's behavior introduces systemic risk — through industrial manipulation, security violations, or stability degradation — the network responds by reducing that member's integration automatically, proportionally, and without requiring political consensus to act.

The system does not moralize. It does not politicize. It measures observable behavior, scores it against published criteria, and adjusts integration level accordingly. Drift in behavior produces drift in classification. Correction produces restoration. Sustained defection produces effective exclusion — not through a political decision, but through the accumulated mechanical consequence of a score that reflects what a partner actually does inside the shared system.

That is how an alliance maintains integrity at scale across political cycles, leadership changes, and the full range of conditions that real alliances face over time.

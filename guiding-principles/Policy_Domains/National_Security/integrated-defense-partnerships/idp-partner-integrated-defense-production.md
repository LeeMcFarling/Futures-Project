# Integrated Defense Production (IDP)  
## Industrial Capacity & Defense Production

## Purpose

This brief defines how IDP partners collectively generate, sustain, and regenerate
**weapons stockpile capacity** under peacetime competition and sustained conflict.
It responds directly to observed failure modes in European rearmament and Ukraine-era
industrial warfare: slow scaling, bespoke over-engineering, single-node dependence,
prime capture, and procurement disconnected from battlefield reality.

This brief explicitly excludes civilian industrial policy, energy systems, and raw
materials. Its scope is **weapons, munitions, sustainment, and repair systems**
intended to be consumed at scale.

These outcomes require binding authorities, and decision rules. If these mechanisms
remain norm-bound, the architecture is assumed to fail under political stress.

---

## I. Arsenal Bifurcation (Foundational Principle)

IDP explicitly bifurcates alliance arsenals into two categories.

**Tier A: IDP-Standard Stockpile Systems**  
High-expenditure, consumable systems required in large volumes under combat
conditions. These include munitions, sensors, sustainment components, logistics
enablers, and scalable platforms. They are designed for fungibility, modularity,
multi-node production, and rapid regeneration.

**Tier B: Sovereign Crown-Jewel Systems**  
Nationally controlled, scarce capabilities whose value lies in asymmetry and
technical edge. These systems remain fully sovereign. No alliance replication,
IP transfer, or substitution is assumed or required.

Constraint or failure in Tier B must not collapse Tier A. Tier A exists to ensure
wars are not lost due to preventable industrial or sustainment failure.

---

## II. Stockpile Selection Principle (Binding Doctrine)

IDP-standard systems are selected based on **expected consumption under sustained
combat conditions**, not on predicted war-winning platforms or national doctrine.

The governing question is:

*If alliance forces were to fight in a given theater, which consumable systems would
need to scale immediately and continuously to sustain operations?*

Selection is theater-agnostic at entry, theater-specific in execution, and adaptive
over time as operational evidence evolves. Crown-jewel systems are explicitly
excluded from this regime.

This doctrine does not attempt to predict future wars. It institutionalizes the
assumption that attrition, logistics friction, and repair demand recur across
conflicts even when tactics differ.

---

## III. Industrial Doctrine: Fungible Modular Production

IDP rejects brittle, bespoke, single-prime production as a national-security risk.

IDP-standard systems are designed around modular architectures governed by strict
interface contracts. Capability improvements occur at the module level rather than
through whole-platform redesigns. Industrial resilience is achieved through
**tooling replication**, not preservation of specific factories.

Replicable tooling packages, test rigs, software configuration controls, and
manufacturing instructions are digitally escrowed under alliance custody to enable
rapid instantiation across secure facilities.

Where security permits, components and processes deliberately overlap with secure
civilian manufacturing to expand surge capacity. Innovation proceeds through
bounded, competitive increments with explicit exit ramps. Blank-check concurrency
and speculative “make-the-future” systems are disallowed for IDP-standard stockpiles.

---

## IV. Surge Capacity as a Network Property

A system is eligible for IDP-standard designation only if alliance output can expand
by at least threefold within six to nine months **without new greenfield builds**,
assuming the loss of at least one production node.

Surge capacity must be exercised regularly. Nominal or paper capacity does not
qualify.

---

## V. Multi-Node Executability (Hard Requirement)

All IDP-standard systems must have **N+2 executable production nodes** across **at
least two theaters**, with certified tooling, trained labor, quality-assurance
equivalence, and software and configuration control.

This requirement structurally prevents coercion, embargo leverage, and single-partner
veto over alliance production.

---

## VI. Aggregated Demand and Guaranteed Offtake (Treaty-Bound)

IDP establishes an **Alliance Offtake Compact** for defined stockpile classes. The
compact commits to multi-year minimum purchase volumes, learning-curve price
adjustments, automatic option-lot expansion under contingency triggers, and buy-out
guarantees if political conditions change.

Participation is opt-in by system class rather than total force structure.
Enforcement is procurement-native, including volume reallocation, loss of preferred
access, and suspension from IDP-standard eligibility.

---

## VII. Anti-Capture Rules for Primes

To prevent political engineering and cost inflation, IDP-standard programs require
sustainment and continuity data escrow, transparent cost structures for follow-on
lots, competitive re-bidding across IDP primes, and caps on proprietary lock-in.

Allied primes are explicitly used to apply price and performance discipline to
dominant suppliers.

---

## VIII. Sovereign Right-to-Repair (Treaty-Bound)

For IDP-standard systems only, partners retain on-soil repair, overhaul, and
sustainment rights, including full access to diagnostics, telemetry, and maintenance
data integrated into alliance logistics systems.

Control mechanisms that undermine command confidence are prohibited. Crown-jewel
systems are exempt.

---

## IX. Sustainment Under Attack

IDP planning assumes contested logistics, degraded communications, and cyber and
kinetic disruption. Cross-border sustainment, mobile repair, dispersed depots,
cannibalization, emergency substitution, and fast-track re-certification are
pre-authorized and exercised.

---

## X. Munitions Scalability Gate

Systems that require years to replenish months of expenditure are disqualified from
IDP-standard designation. Manufacturability, modularity, and line replication are
first-order design constraints rather than secondary considerations.

---

## XI. Alliance Testing and Operational Evidence (Treaty-Bound)

Alliance testing applies **only to IDP-standard stockpile systems**, not crown-jewel
capabilities. Testing evaluates reliability at tempo, maintainability, repair cycle
time, manufacturability at scale, and substitution tolerance under disruption.

Results gate scale procurement and pricing. A formal operational evidence channel
incorporates partner and Ukraine-derived lessons while avoiding disclosure of
operational plans.

---

## XII. Governance and Enforcement

A standing IDP Production Board certifies executability, publishes pass/fail outcomes,
reallocates volume on non-performance, revokes waivers, and maintains escrowed
industrial continuity packages.

Core metrics are time-to-surge, cost-at-scale, repair turnaround, and node-loss
resilience.

---

## XIII. Sovereignty Assurance Clause (Treaty Text)

Participation in IDP does not require transfer, disclosure, or joint production of
sovereign crown-jewel weapons systems or doctrines. Each state retains full national
authority over such systems.

IDP obligations apply only to voluntarily designated IDP-standard stockpile systems
and create no supranational command authority. No single state may control, veto, or
disable alliance production. The framework reduces—not increases—strategic dependence
by distributing production across sovereign nodes.

Withdrawal from specific programs remains a national right; access to pooled demand,
surge capacity, and certification reciprocity is contingent on continued compliance
for those programs.

---

## XIV. Bottom Line

The alliance does not attempt to predict the next war’s decisive technology.  
It ensures wars are not lost due to preventable industrial collapse.

Crown jewels deter.  
Stockpiles fight.  
Industry must survive contact with reality.

# Alliance Interface Standards and N+2 Theater Sourcing Doctrine

## Purpose

This brief defines two foundational requirements for IDP-standard shared weapons systems: alliance-owned interface standards that make those systems genuinely interoperable across the alliance industrial base, and a theater-based N+2 sourcing requirement that ensures production continuity even when an entire geographic theater is degraded or denied.

This brief is the integration layer that ties together the industrial capacity, procurement reform, and critical materials frameworks already established in the stack. It adds three things those briefs do not fully address: the Executable Production Unit as the only valid unit of analysis for compliance, theater sourcing as distinct from node counting, and the binding of N+2 compliance directly to competitive allocation dynamics so that resilience becomes a market-shaping constraint rather than a compliance rule.

The N+2 requirement floors at N+2. In its lowest configuration (N=1), this requires a minimum of three certified EPUs within a single theater. This is the minimum viable redundancy for any IDP-standard system. It has no fixed ceiling. The alliance sets the designation level for each system based on strategic risk, consumption rate, and production lead time, scaling as high as the system requires. The intent throughout is to source from different companies across different IDP member countries, competing on cost, speed, and production velocity. That competition is not incidental — it is the mechanism through which the alliance industrial base improves continuously rather than stagnating under protected incumbency.

The one-line doctrine this brief encodes: **the alliance must be able to lose an entire theater and still increase output.**

---

## The Problem: Incompatibility and Cosmetic Redundancy

Allied forces that cannot share ammunition, cannot perform maintenance on each other's equipment, cannot integrate logistics chains, and cannot operate through common software protocols do not translate nominal military alliance into actual operational capability. This is an alliance-wide structural failure that applies wherever parallel national procurement has produced nominally allied but operationally incompatible systems. The United States is not exempt. Systems procured primarily for domestic political geography rather than operational interoperability contribute to the same fragility.

The redundancy problem is equally alliance-wide. A production base that passes a facility count on paper but fails under correlated stress is not redundant. Two facilities in different countries that share semiconductor foundries, share a maritime logistics corridor, share energy grid infrastructure, or sit under common ownership fail together when that shared constraint is disrupted. Geographic separation without independence at the constraint layer is cosmetic resilience. This brief defines what genuine resilience requires and makes that definition the enforcement mechanism.

## Alliance Interface Standards

### The Standard as Alliance Commons

For every designated IDP-standard shared weapons system, the alliance defines and owns a complete interface standard. The standard belongs to the alliance — not to any company, prime contractor, or member state. No firm can hold the interoperability layer hostage through proprietary specification control.

The standard covers the full interoperability stack:

**Mechanical and electrical interfaces:** Physical connection geometry, dimensional tolerances, power delivery specifications, connector standards, and electromagnetic compatibility requirements at every subsystem boundary.

**Data and software protocols:** Communication standards, targeting data formats, fire control integration interfaces, diagnostic telemetry formats, and maintenance data protocols. Software is increasingly the real interoperability constraint. Hardware sourced from multiple vendors achieves genuine interoperability only when the integration layer is common.

**Maintenance and logistics standards:** Tool requirements, inspection intervals, replacement part specifications, and depot maintenance procedures. A certified maintenance facility anywhere in the alliance can service any certified unit regardless of manufacturer.

**Training standards:** Common operator qualification requirements and simulation interface specifications so that training developed in one member state transfers directly to operators in any other.

The standard defines what must be common. Internal component design, materials choices, manufacturing processes, and performance optimization above the interface floor are where companies compete. Innovation within the standard is encouraged. Interface divergence is not permitted.

### Crown Jewel Carve-Out

The interface standard applies only to voluntarily designated IDP-standard shared systems. Sovereign crown jewel systems, advanced classified platforms, and any system a member state chooses not to designate as IDP-standard remain fully under national authority. The IDP arsenal bifurcation brief governs which systems fall into which category.

### New Standards and Generational Advancement

The standard does not freeze technology. A company that develops a capability warranting a generational update can demonstrate it through the test range, trigger a formal alliance standard revision process, and if the capability meets the bar, advance the standard. Better is always welcome. Better-but-incompatible is not. Revisions are managed to preserve backward compatibility where possible and to give the industrial base adequate transition time where it is not.

## The Executable Production Unit

The only valid unit of analysis for N+2 theater sourcing is the **Executable Production Unit (EPU)**. All N+2 compliance, theater distribution, and allocation scoring are evaluated exclusively at the EPU level. Facilities, firms, and nodes have no independent standing outside EPU certification.

An EPU is the minimum complete combination of inputs, energy, logistics, workforce, digital control systems, financing pathways, and manufacturing capacity required to produce a certified quantity of an IDP-standard system to specification, independently and under stress conditions.

**Constraint-layer independence** is the defining attribute of a certified EPU. Constraint-layer independence encompasses: production inputs and upstream suppliers, energy sourcing and backup generation, logistics and transport routing, workforce and labor pools, digital control and manufacturing execution systems, and financing and insurance pathways. An EPU that shares any of these with another EPU in a way that would cause both to fail under the same disruption is not independent at the constraint layer.

**Surge-certified capacity is a required attribute of EPU qualification, not a separate evaluation dimension.** A production unit that meets steady-state output requirements but cannot demonstrate surge to the required multiplier within the required window is not a certified EPU. There is no partial credit for surge capability. An EPU either qualifies or it does not.

EPU certification is not evaluated against normal operating conditions. It is evaluated against stress conditions: the EPU must demonstrate that it can operate independently when its theater's shared infrastructure is degraded — port closures, grid disruption, communications blackout, supply route interdiction. A facility that functions under peacetime conditions but depends on theater-level shared infrastructure for sustained operation does not satisfy EPU certification.

**Structural configurations designed to satisfy nominal EPU counts while preserving correlated failure modes are treated as non-compliant under ERS evaluation.** The test is independence at the constraint layer, not geographic separation or corporate distinctness.

## N+2 Theater Sourcing Standard

### The Requirement

Every IDP-standard shared weapons system must maintain certified EPU capacity across a minimum of N independent geographic theaters, with a minimum of N+2 certified EPUs in total, where each EPU meets the full independence criteria above and each theater contains at least one EPU capable of independent operation under full theater stress conditions.

**Certification requires both N+2 EPU count and ERS at or above the system-class threshold. Failure on either condition constitutes non-compliance.** Node count alone cannot satisfy certification. ERS alone cannot satisfy certification. Both must be met simultaneously and continuously.

N is the number of independent theaters required to sustain production if any single theater is completely denied. The +2 minimum total EPU count provides within-theater and cross-theater redundancy above the bare minimum. The designation floors at 2N+2 and scales upward based on system-level strategic risk assessment. There is no fixed ceiling — the alliance sets the designation as high as the system requires.

### Multi-Company, Multi-Country Competition

Diversification across firms and member states is enforced through ERS scoring. Common ownership or jurisdictional concentration introduces correlation penalties that reduce effective EPU contribution to the network resilience score. EPUs operated by different companies from different IDP member countries produce higher effective ERS than nominally separate EPUs with shared ownership, shared management, or shared jurisdictional exposure. This is not a preference — it is the structural intent. Competition among companies across the alliance on cost, production speed, and manufacturing velocity is the mechanism through which the alliance industrial base improves. A network of EPUs operated by a single prime contractor across multiple countries achieves geographic distribution but eliminates the competitive pressure that drives improvement. A network of EPUs operated by genuinely competing firms from genuinely different member states achieves both resilience and continuous performance pressure simultaneously.

Member states benefit from hosting certified EPUs: production employment, industrial capability development, and allocation share that grows with demonstrated performance. Companies benefit from certification: access to the pooled alliance procurement market and allocation share that increases with ERS contribution and competitive performance. The system is designed so that the incentives of member states, companies, and the alliance as a whole are aligned rather than in tension.

### Reading the N Notation

The notation must be read precisely because ambiguity in implementation produces exactly the cosmetic compliance problem this doctrine exists to prevent.

**N** is the number of independent theaters required. The minimum total EPU count is N plus two. Every theater must contain at least one certified EPU. The additional EPUs provide within-theater and cross-theater redundancy above the bare minimum.

**2N+2** requires production capacity across at least 2 independent theaters and a minimum of 4 certified EPUs total, with at least 1 EPU in each theater. Example: an interceptor propulsion module designated at 2N+2 requires certified EPUs in two independent theaters — such as the Indo-Pacific and European theaters — with at least one EPU in each and two additional EPUs providing redundancy within or across those theaters. If the entire Indo-Pacific theater production capacity is lost, the remaining EPUs must sustain baseline production and initiate surge. Two EPUs both located in the Indo-Pacific theater do not satisfy 2N+2 regardless of their geographic separation within the theater.

**3N+3** requires production capacity across at least 3 independent theaters and a minimum of 6 certified EPUs total, with at least 1 EPU in each theater. Example: a high-criticality munitions component designated at 3N+3 requires certified EPUs in three independent theaters — such as Indo-Pacific, European, and North American — with at least one EPU in each and three additional EPUs providing depth. Under the stress test scenario, the remaining EPUs must sustain and surge without the lost theater's capacity.

Higher designations follow the same logic. The floor is 2N+2. The ceiling is whatever the strategic risk assessment requires.

### What Does Not Satisfy the Notation

The following configurations do not satisfy the N notation regardless of how they appear on paper:

A single prime contractor with multiple facilities in different countries within the same theater does not satisfy the theater distribution requirement. Theater count is determined by independent geographic theater, not by the number of facilities a single firm operates or the number of countries those facilities are located in.

Multiple EPUs that share semiconductor suppliers, maritime logistics corridors, energy infrastructure, workforce pools, digital control systems, or financing pathways count as fewer EPUs than their raw number suggests. The ERS correlation penalty reduces their effective contribution to network resilience proportionally.

Facilities that meet steady-state production requirements but cannot demonstrate surge capacity to the required multiplier within the required window are not certified EPUs. Surge is a certification requirement embedded in EPU qualification, not a separate metric.

Facilities that cannot operate independently if their theater's shared infrastructure is disrupted fail the EPU independence test even if they are owned by different companies and located in different countries within the same theater.

### The Stress Test as Certification Condition

**The stress test scenario constitutes the formal certification condition. Systems that fail the scenario are not certifiable regardless of their architecture on paper.**

The formal compliance scenario is: full loss of the largest single theater's production capacity, plus partial degradation of one additional EPU in a remaining theater. Under that scenario the alliance must sustain production at baseline and initiate surge to above-baseline output using the remaining certified EPUs. A system whose remaining EPUs cannot meet this scenario does not achieve N+2 certification regardless of its designation-level EPU count or nominal geographic distribution.

Paper architecture that has not been validated against the stress test does not count.

## Panic Threshold and Deterrence Threshold

The N+2 architecture defines two distinct strategic levels that planners and member states should internalize explicitly.

**The panic threshold** is the minimum configuration below which the system becomes unstable under node loss. A system at exactly its designation floor — minimum EPU count, minimum ERS — is at the panic threshold. Loss of any single EPU at this level risks dropping below certification compliance, triggering the fragility scenario described above: scrambled production, political pressure to accept non-compliant supply, and the loss of deterrence credibility that comes from visible industrial fragility.

**The deterrence threshold** is the level at which the system remains non-collapsible under adversarial stress — the point at which the alliance can absorb theater-level loss and still increase output without any EPU operating outside its certified parameters. Systems operating well above their designation floor, with genuine theater redundancy that does not depend on any single member's participation, are operating at or above the deterrence threshold.

The certification floor defines compliance. The deterrence threshold defines strategic credibility. An alliance that builds only to the certification floor has met the minimum. An alliance that builds to the deterrence threshold has built something an adversary cannot plan around.

## ERS as Continuous Gate

**ERS is continuously recalculated. Degradation below the system-class threshold — regardless of cause — triggers automatic share reduction and remediation requirements.** There is no grace period, no negotiation, and no grandfather clause for EPUs that were compliant at the time of certification but have since drifted below threshold.

Causes of ERS degradation that trigger automatic consequences include: loss of an EPU through facility failure, discovery of a previously unidentified shared dependency that increases correlation between EPUs, a supplier failure that concentrates upstream inputs, a logistics route denial that increases shared routing dependence, and any ownership or financing change that introduces correlation across nominally independent EPUs.

Restoration of ERS above threshold restores eligibility for full allocation at the next evaluation cycle. Remediation plans are required within a defined window following any degradation event. Failure to submit or execute a remediation plan within that window triggers further share reduction.

## Binding N+2 to Competitive Allocation

N+2 is a continuous market-shaping constraint, not a one-time compliance gate.

**Certification eligibility:** An EPU that does not meet the full independence criteria, surge certification, and ERS threshold is not eligible for production share in that system class. Non-certified EPUs do not compete.

**Allocation weight:** Allocation weight is a function of performance metrics and EPU ERS contribution. Marginal ERS improvements produce proportional allocation gains at each cycle. Higher ERS contribution — more genuine independence, more verified surge capacity, more resilient constraint-layer sourcing — produces higher allocation share over a manufacturer that meets the minimum but does not exceed it. This is not discretionary. It is mechanically tied to the ERS score.

**Automatic penalties:** Discovery of a shared dependency, failure of a surge validation exercise, or loss of input source independence triggers automatic share reduction proportional to the ERS degradation caused. The penalty activates automatically. It is not negotiated and does not require a finding by any discretionary authority.

**Political engineering is self-defeating:** Fragmenting production across political geographies to maximize congressional or parliamentary sponsorship while degrading EPU independence and surge capacity produces a lower ERS, a lower allocation share, and automatic penalties when the correlation is identified. The metrics measure resilience directly. Political production footprint cannot substitute for genuine independence at the constraint layer.

## Test Ranges as Competitive Innovation Infrastructure

Test ranges in the IDP framework are standing competitive infrastructure — continuously available environments where companies bring new designs, iterate rapidly, fail early, fix, and prove performance before the allocation cycle where it counts. They are not one-time certification gates.

Companies are explicitly encouraged to use the range continuously and competitively. A manufacturer that arrives at a demonstration event with more validated iterations, more discovered-and-fixed failure modes, and more demonstrated surge performance than a competitor has a direct allocation advantage. The range is available. Using it aggressively is how companies improve their ERS contribution, their surge certification, and their allocation share simultaneously.

Periodic live demonstration events set the competitive allocation moments. Continuous range access is where the engineering work happens. Failure during development at the range is expected and acceptable — it is structurally preferable to failure discovered after full-rate production is underway. New entrants and manufacturers from allied member states use the range to establish certification and compete for share without requiring a pre-existing prime contractor relationship or political sponsorship.

## Full-Stack Deterrence

The N+2 architecture is not only a resilience mechanism. It is a deterrence signal encoded into industrial topology.

An adversary assessing the cost of conflict with the alliance must plan against a production base that is genuinely distributed across independent theaters, genuinely surge-capable at each node, and genuinely competitive across multiple companies and member states. That is a fundamentally different planning problem than one presented by a concentrated single-theater industrial base dependent on a handful of primes. The former can absorb strikes, redistribute production, and increase output under stress. The latter cannot. The difference is not rhetorical — it is structural, visible, and verifiable by any serious industrial intelligence assessment.

This is what credible deterrence looks like without hegemony or imperial overreach. It does not threaten conquest. It makes the cost of conflict with the alliance so structurally prohibitive — in the production math alone, before any other military consideration — that the calculation never looks favorable. An adversary that knows the alliance can lose an entire theater and still increase output has no viable theory of victory that depends on industrial attrition. An adversary facing a concentrated, brittle industrial base does.

The deterrence value requires that the architecture be real, not declared. Paper EPUs, cosmetically compliant theater distribution, and surge capacity that exists only in certification documents provide no deterrence because a serious adversary's industrial intelligence will identify the gap between the claim and the reality. Genuine independence at the constraint layer, genuine surge validation at the test range, and genuine multi-company competition across member states are not just resilience requirements — they are the conditions under which the deterrence signal is credible rather than performative.

This is also why the multi-company, multi-country competition requirement matters beyond economics. An alliance industrial base where companies from Japan, Poland, Australia, and the United States are genuinely competing to produce the same interceptor component to the same standard signals something different to an adversary than one where a single American prime contractor holds the production monopoly with nominal allied participation. The former is a genuinely distributed alliance capability. The latter is American industrial capacity with allied labeling.

## Node Loss and the Fragility of the Minimum Floor

The 2N+2 designation is the minimum certification threshold. It is not the target, and the alliance should not treat sitting at exactly the minimum floor as an acceptable steady state.

The reason is straightforward. At exactly 2N+2 — two theaters, four EPUs — the loss of any single EPU leaves the system at 2N+1. Depending on which EPU is lost, that may or may not still satisfy the theater distribution requirement. If the lost EPU was the sole certified EPU in one of the two required theaters, the system drops below theater distribution compliance simultaneously with losing the EPU count. The alliance is suddenly in a position where one of its required production theaters has no certified capacity, the remaining EPUs are being asked to surge to compensate, and the political and industrial pressure to accept non-compliant production to fill the gap is intense.

That is the panic scenario. It is not hypothetical. EPUs can be lost through conflict damage, political departure from the alliance, demotion for rules violations under the alliance conduct framework, industrial failure, or supply chain collapse. Any of those causes can happen without warning. A system sitting at the minimum floor has no buffer against any of them.

The alliance treats operation above the designation floor as the default steady-state condition. Systems operating at the floor are considered minimally compliant but strategically fragile. The alliance therefore actively incentivizes systems to operate above their designation floor. The allocation mechanism already creates this incentive partially — additional certified EPUs above the minimum contribute to ERS and therefore to allocation share for the companies that operate them. But the brief should be explicit that the designation floor is a certification threshold, not a strategic comfort level.

**Systems operating above their designation floor receive an explicit resilience buffer designation.** A system designated at 2N+2 but operating with 3 theaters and 6 certified EPUs has a full-theater buffer — it can lose an entire theater without dropping below certification threshold. That buffer has strategic value that should be recognized in the system's deterrence posture and in the alliance's production planning.

**The demotion mechanism interacts directly with this.** When an alliance member is demoted for conduct violations under the alliance behavioral framework, any EPUs operated by that member are suspended from the certified pool. If those EPUs were the sole certified capacity in a required theater, the affected systems drop below theater distribution compliance immediately. This is not a theoretical edge case — it is a foreseeable consequence of the demotion architecture that the production planning framework must account for. Systems that depend on a single member state's EPUs for theater compliance are structurally exposed to that member's political and behavioral trajectory. The solution is the same as for any other fragility: operate above the minimum floor with genuine theater redundancy that does not depend on any single member's continued participation. This fragility is precisely why N+2 defines certification, while operation at or above higher effective resilience levels is the strategic objective.

## Strategic Effect

An alliance industrial base organized around certified EPUs, genuine theater independence, ERS-gated allocation, multi-company competition across member states, and continuous test range performance evaluation changes the adversary's calculus in three ways.

Targeting a single production facility, a single prime contractor, or even a single theater's production capacity does not degrade alliance output in a way that can be strategically exploited. The network redistributes and surges. To meaningfully degrade production the adversary would need to simultaneously attack multiple genuinely independent EPUs across multiple theaters with uncorrelated constraint layers — a materially higher threshold of effort and escalation than the current concentrated industrial base requires.

Operational interoperability that currently exists on paper becomes real at the field level. Allied forces sustain joint operations using shared systems, shared maintenance, and shared logistics without the coordination friction that nominally allied but actually incompatible procurement systems produce.

Continuous competitive pressure across companies from multiple member states produces a shared systems industrial base that improves faster than one organized around protected incumbents. The adversary faces not only a resilient production network but one whose shared systems are continuously improving through genuine performance competition that no single national industrial base could sustain alone.

## Bottom Line

Alliance interoperability is not achieved through declarations. It is engineered through binding interface standards, certified through EPU independence testing at the constraint layer, and sustained through distributed production capacity that no adversary can degrade with a targeted strike on a single node or theater.

The N+2 theater sourcing requirement — flooring at 2N+2, scaling without ceiling, evaluated exclusively at the EPU level, ERS-gated, surge-integrated, continuously recalculated, and tied mechanically to competitive allocation — is not a redundancy rule. It is a certification system, a scoring system, a competitive allocation driver, and a deterrence mechanism encoded into industrial topology.

Different companies from different IDP member countries competing on cost, speed, and velocity within a common interface standard is not a side effect of this architecture. It is the point. The alliance gets the industrial base it needs not through mandate alone but through a market structured so that genuine resilience and genuine competition are the same thing.

## Purpose

Security Backstop Partnerships support regional stability not only through defensive coordination but also through mechanisms that strengthen economic survivability during periods of instability.

This framework establishes structured pathways through which participating states can coordinate responses to economic disruption arising from security crises, political shocks, coercive pressure, or transnational instability.

The objective is to preserve strategic decision-making capacity by reducing the risk that sudden economic deterioration forces reactive or destabilizing policy choices.

---

## Economic Survivability as a Security Function

Modern security risks frequently generate economic effects that undermine state resilience even in the absence of large-scale military conflict.

These effects may include:

- capital flight or liquidity contraction  
- logistics or shipping disruption  
- energy supply instability  
- insurance withdrawal  
- infrastructure damage or sabotage  
- labor displacement or migration shocks  

Regional coordination mechanisms help participating states manage these pressures more predictably and reduce incentives for unilateral responses that may amplify instability.

---

## Regional Stabilization Coordination

Regional Security Councils may support structured consultation on economic continuity measures during periods of elevated risk.

Coordination may include:

- information-sharing regarding market conditions and critical supply vulnerabilities  
- alignment of contingency planning for trade and logistics continuity  
- dialogue on energy or infrastructure resilience initiatives  
- preparation of crisis communication strategies to stabilize investor confidence  

These processes remain advisory and do not mandate national economic policy actions.

---

## Crisis Liquidity and Financial Coordination Pathways

Participating states may explore cooperative approaches to managing sudden liquidity constraints or financial market disruption.

This may include:

- consultation regarding access to multilateral financial facilities  
- coordination of regional stabilization messaging  
- dialogue on temporary trade or capital flow management strategies  
- preparation of joint engagement with international financial institutions  

Such coordination aims to reduce fragmentation in crisis responses and improve regional credibility.

---

## Infrastructure and Supply Chain Continuity

SBP economic frameworks may support coordination on protecting and restoring critical infrastructure and trade corridors.

Potential areas of focus include:

- port and customs resilience  
- energy transmission or fuel distribution continuity  
- digital and communications infrastructure stability  
- humanitarian logistics coordination  

Regional dialogue can help ensure that recovery efforts are mutually reinforcing rather than competitive.

---

## External Stabilization Engagement

Where regional economic disruption exceeds local response capacity, councils may collectively consider engagement with external partners.

This may involve:

- support from international financial institutions  
- infrastructure protection cooperation  
- coordinated economic assistance initiatives  
- stabilization guarantees or investment confidence measures  

External engagement follows the broader SBP principle of regionally informed sequencing.

---

## Sovereignty Safeguards

Participation in economic stabilization coordination does not obligate states to adopt specific fiscal, monetary, trade, or investment policies.

Councils facilitate consultation and preparedness rather than economic governance.

All national economic decision-making authority remains intact.

---

## Strategic Effect

By strengthening regional economic survivability, SBPs help participating states maintain policy autonomy during periods of instability and reduce incentives for escalatory or destabilizing responses driven by financial stress.

Economic coordination complements defensive cooperation by addressing the broader systemic pressures that shape regional security outcomes.

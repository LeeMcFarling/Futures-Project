## Security Backstop Partnerships (SBP)

Security Backstop Partnerships are regional security coordination frameworks that organize existing defense relationships with the United States into structured regional consultation mechanisms for managing shared threats.

### Reasons for Implementation

Many contemporary security challenges — including gray-zone coercion, transnational criminal networks, maritime disruption, infrastructure vulnerability, political instability cycles, and economic shock transmission — operate across borders and affect multiple states simultaneously. While bilateral defense arrangements remain essential, they are often insufficient on their own to support timely coordination, shared situational awareness, or stabilization planning in regions where risks are interconnected. 

Additionally, traditional frameworks often fall back on hub-and-spoke bilateral US agreements instead of regional coordination which produces two failures: 

1) Any action is delayed and subject to coordination failures, including by the United Nations (and especially if 'great power competition' plays a role

2) Any intervention could be unwanted and / or destabilizing to the reagion; could be read as neo-colonialism by regional powers, and could devolve into great power conflict.

### Regional and Local Sovereignty 

As a result, SBPs are organized into local councils to coordinate action, veto any actions that would not be supported locally, and serve as a proto-united-nations body for future regional stability. 

SBPs provide a standing institutional environment in which participating sovereign governments can align defensive priorities, improve crisis communication, and coordinate responses to developments that carry regional consequences. They formalize patterns of cooperation that already exist by creating predictable forums for consultation and coordination among states facing common threat environments.

### Security Backstop Partnerships are not Alliances

Participation in an SBP does not replace treaty alliances, bilateral defense agreements, or national security institutions. States retain full authority over the organization, deployment, and use of their armed forces, as well as over broader defense and foreign policy decisions. SBPs do not create automatic military obligations among members and do not require deeper alliance integration. Their purpose is to improve coordination and preparedness while preserving national autonomy.

By organizing security consultation at the regional level, SBPs help reduce uncertainty during crises, support more coherent defensive alignment, and enable participating states to collectively shape approaches to managing instability that affects their shared environment. This can contribute to more effective burden distribution, clearer escalation signaling, and stronger legitimacy for stabilization efforts undertaken in response to regional risks.

### Adaptability, not One-Size-Fits-All 

SBP frameworks are intentionally adaptable. While the governance logic remains consistent, coordination priorities may vary significantly across regions depending on the nature of prevailing threats. In some regions, cooperation may focus primarily on maritime security, infrastructure resilience, or escalation containment. In others, emphasis may fall on countering illicit logistics networks, managing migration pressures, strengthening institutional continuity, or protecting critical trade corridors.

External partners, including the United States, may continue to provide deterrence support, technical assistance, intelligence cooperation, or economic stabilization tools under existing relationships. Within an SBP structure, however, regional governments collectively shape coordination priorities and consultation processes. This approach supports greater regional ownership of security management while maintaining established partnerships.

Over time, structured regional coordination mechanisms may contribute to broader international security processes by providing organized venues through which regional perspectives can inform multilateral crisis response and stabilization efforts.

### Summary 

In essence, Security Backstop Partnerships strengthen regional preparedness and defensive coherence by transforming fragmented consultation patterns into standing coordination frameworks. They enable sovereign states to manage interconnected risks more effectively without altering national command authority or requiring deeper alliance commitments.

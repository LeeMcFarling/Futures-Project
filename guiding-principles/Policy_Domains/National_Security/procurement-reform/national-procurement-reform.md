## Problem Statement

The U.S. defense procurement system contains a structural weakness: political engineering of industrial geography has been embedded into acquisition practice. Production is frequently fragmented across districts, transport complexity is artificially inflated, and lower-tier supplier concentration remains opaque. This creates systemic exposure in surge scenarios and prioritizes political durability over operational readiness.

Cost and schedule are monitored. Structural resilience is not treated as a first-order national security variable. There is no statutory instrument dedicated to measuring and enforcing supply-chain readiness. The result is overexposure in procurement architecture during adversarial conditions.

This brief establishes a constrained-delegation acquisition framework and a statutory supply-chain readiness instrument aligned with military necessity rather than political geography.

---

## Statutory Performance Constraints

Congress retains appropriations authority and defines mission envelopes, funding ceilings, and industrial performance thresholds.

### Surge Capacity Benchmarks

Programs must demonstrate verified surge capability under simulated wartime conditions.

Benchmarks are defined upstream and may include:

- Ability to increase production by a defined percentage within a fixed time window  
- Verified access to critical inputs under degraded logistics conditions  
- Demonstrated alternate routing and substitution pathways  
- Environmental durability validation across relevant operational theaters  

Surge validation is conducted through structured stress exercises. Where applicable, these exercises are coordinated with IDP industrial partners.  

For sovereign, non-shared weapons systems, surge philosophy remains internal: production must be fungible across certified domestic facilities under adversarial constraints. Alliance integration is additive, not substitutive.

---

### Industrial Redundancy Minimums (N+2 Model)

Critical components must meet minimum redundancy thresholds designed specifically for manufacturing continuity under adversarial conditions. These thresholds include measures like: 

- At least N+2 qualified production nodes (different facilities and in different regions to avoid single points of failure.)
- Geographic diversification across IDP jurisdictions where feasible (spread production across multiple theaters if needed)
- Tier-2 and tier-3 supplier mapping with exposure scoring  (ensure stability in sub-componenets, semi-conductors, etc.)

Redundancy is evaluated at the component level, not merely at the prime contractor level. IDP co-production eligibility counts toward redundancy compliance where continuity agreements exist.

Note: Bleeding-edge systems in early development are not required to meet full N+2 thresholds during prototyping or low-rate initial production. However, transition to full-rate production requires a published industrial hardening plan with defined milestones toward redundancy certification. Under this philosophy, innovation tolerates concentration temporarily. Scaling does not.

---

### Modular Open-Architecture Requirements

“Open” refers to interface standardization and modularity, not declassification.

Requirements include:

- Interchangeable certified subsystems across qualified manufacturers  
- Defined interface standards enabling rapid supplier substitution  
- Cross-sector manufacturability where feasible, including surge certification for compatible heavy-industry facilities  

Sensitive technical specifications remain classified. The objective is production fungibility, not transparency of system design.

---

### IDP Co-Production Integration Standards

Where strategically appropriate, acquisition frameworks must incorporate:

- Multi-node production rights within the IDP industrial perimeter  
- Shared certification and quality assurance standards  
- Reciprocal surge commitments  
- Allied benchmarking for cost and industrial efficiency  

Domestic primes compete within an alliance industrial topology rather than a politically fragmented national network.

---

## Execution Corps: Supply-Chain Readiness Authority

A rotating, statutorily independent Execution Corps functions as the industrial readiness instrument.

Mandate:

- Map full supplier graph exposure across tiers  
- Quantify transport complexity and fragmentation  
- Validate N+2 compliance  
- Certify modular interface adherence  
- Conduct surge simulations (domestic and IDP-integrated where applicable)  
- Publish resilience and exposure indices  

The Corps does not award contracts or direct siting decisions. It certifies compliance against statutory industrial criteria.

### Governance, Security, and Anti-Capture Safeguards

The Execution Corps operates under strict statutory independence and integrity requirements.

All members must hold appropriate security clearances, including Top Secret and compartmented access where required by program sensitivity. Clearance eligibility is continuously maintained and subject to periodic reinvestigation.

To prevent financial or professional capture:

- Mandatory comprehensive financial disclosure is required upon entry and updated annually  
- Direct holdings in defense contractors, major sub-tier suppliers, or relevant industrial equities must be divested or placed in qualified blind trusts  
- Outside compensated advisory roles are prohibited during service  
- Post-service employment with prime contractors or designated critical suppliers is restricted for a defined cooling-off period  

Members serve fixed, rotating terms to reduce long-term entrenchment.  

The Corps does not set strategy, select contractors, or allocate funds. Its authority is limited to industrial readiness certification and statutory compliance validation. Independence is designed to protect the integrity of its technical judgments, not to create an autonomous procurement authority.

### Automatic Triggers

To prevent institutional stagnation, certification outcomes carry automatic consequences:

- Failure to meet surge benchmarks pauses production expansion  
- Failure to remediate redundancy gaps within defined window triggers partial recompete of affected components  
- Chronic cost or schedule variance linked to supply fragility opens certification to alternate suppliers  
- Non-compliance with modular standards prevents scale authorization  

Override requires explicit congressional action.

---

## Structural Outcome

Procurement shifts from geographically distributed political optimization to rule-bound industrial performance. Congress legislates objectives and resilience thresholds. The Pentagon executes within bounded authority. The Execution Corps verifies structural readiness.

Industrial topology becomes optimized for adversarial continuity, alliance-integrated surge, and measurable resilience rather than district dispersion.

# Appendix A: Example Triggers & Threshold Models (Illustrative Artifact)

The following models are illustrative examples of how industrial resilience metrics may be structured.  
They are not final statutory values. Threshold calibration requires expert validation across acquisition, logistics, materials science, and operational communities.

These examples demonstrate the type of measurable triggers required for rule-bound enforcement.

---

### A1. Surge Multiplier (SM)

SM = Maximum sustainable production rate within 180 days ÷ Baseline steady-state production rate

Illustrative thresholds:

- Strategic munitions: SM ≥ 2.5  
- Major subsystems: SM ≥ 1.75  
- Complex platforms: SM ≥ 1.5  

Failure to meet required SM at Full-Rate Production review pauses scale expansion pending certified remediation plan.

---

### A2. Effective Redundancy Score (ERS)

ERS = N × (1 − CF)

Where:

- N = Number of certified production nodes  
- CF = Correlation Factor measuring shared upstream risk exposure  

Illustrative minimums:

- ERS ≥ 3.0 for designated strategic components  
- ERS ≥ 2.0 for major subsystems  

Correlation analysis incorporates shared precursor materials, shared semiconductor foundries, shared energy infrastructure, and transport chokepoints.

Failure to meet ERS threshold requires industrial diversification plan within defined remediation window.

---

### A3. Supplier Concentration Threshold

Modified HHI applied to critical upstream inputs.

Illustrative constraints:

- No single upstream node >40% share for designated strategic component  
- Aggregate concentration index below defined ceiling  

Exceeding concentration threshold triggers mandatory diversification pathway or partial subsystem recompete.

---

### A4. Modular Interoperability Certification (MIC)

Certification requires:

- Defined and standardized subsystem interfaces  
- At least two qualified manufacturers capable of certified integration  
- Successful swap validation under operational simulation  

Failure to maintain dual-source modular compliance prevents production expansion beyond baseline.

---

### A5. Environmental Stress Validation

Systems must demonstrate performance certification under relevant operational theaters (e.g., arctic, desert, maritime, high-humidity environments) prior to full deployment authorization.

Failure in required stress environments requires remediation prior to full-rate authorization.

---

### Expert Validation Requirement

Final threshold values, index methodologies, and remediation timelines must be developed in consultation with:

- Acquisition and logistics experts  
- Materials and semiconductor specialists  
- Operational test authorities  
- IDP industrial integration partners  
- Congressional oversight committees  

This appendix establishes structural intent: industrial resilience metrics must be measurable, enforceable, and tied to automatic consequence.

Threshold calibration is a technical exercise. The enforcement architecture is the policy decision.

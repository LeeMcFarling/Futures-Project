# Defense Posture: Integrated Partnerships, Anchored Deterrence, No World Policing

As stated in our foreign policy orientation, the United States under this administration rejects the pursuit of global hegemony, the role of world police, as well as any attempt to revert the post–World War II order into rigid spheres of influence that historically resulted in abject humanitarian disaster.

While we recognize the reality of a multipolar world and adjust our alliance structures accordingly, we assert that any civilization serious about its own survival must privilege multilateralism, diplomacy, and international rules. Any global order that does not root itself in these principles is implicitly planning for its own collapse. For that reason, the United States supports the reform of international institutions so they not only articulate human rights and international norms, but possess the legitimacy, capacity, and enforcement mechanisms necessary to uphold them in practice.

In the meantime, the United States operates in an unstable, multipolar world. Whatever the contributions of previous administrations to this outcome, today we must confront conditions on the ground as they exist rather than as we wish they might have been.

Alliance structures that treated the United States as a single point of failure or as the sole backstop against violence proved to be structural weaknesses, not strengths, for all partners involved. They encouraged complacency, constrained partner sovereignty, and produced brittle arrangements ill-suited to contemporary security challenges.

Meeting present realities requires collective capacity-building. The United States and its partners must strengthen military capability, industrial and economic resilience, and stability across critical domains—including energy, supply chains, critical materials, infrastructure, and governance so all partners can stand on their own two feet.

In this, the United States maintains defense partnerships across regions under clear, explicit frameworks designed to remove ambiguous open-ended commitments, and de-risk political variance across partner (and U.S.) administrations. 

## Partnership Frameworks

**Integrated Defense Partnerships (IDPs)** apply to allies with durable constitutional alignment, interoperable forces, and demonstrated capacity to operate within shared deterrence and escalation frameworks. With these partners, the United States closely integrates planning, intelligence, logistics, and industrial capacity. For IDP partners, the United States anchors deterrence while regional allies lead conventional operations.

**Security Backstop Partnerships** apply where U.S. support is strategically important but does not constitute mutual defense. These arrangements provide bounded deterrence, missile and air defense cooperation, intelligence sharing at defined levels, sustainment support, and crisis coordination without implying automatic escalation or permanent U.S. operational control.

**Aligned Defense Partnerships** support cooperation through training, exercises, arms cooperation, maritime security, and information sharing without implied defense guarantees.

Across all partnership types, U.S. commitments are explicit, conditional, and durable across political cycles.

## Core Defense Obligations

The core defense strategy of this administration is that the United States will defend its territory, its citizens, its treaty commitments, and the integrity of global trade routes and commons essential to its economic stability in that order.

Beyond core U.S. defense obligations, regional actors are responsible for their own security, with the United States acting as a deterrence anchor and defined backstop. This posture emphasizes joint capacity-building—military, industrial, and economic—so allies can sustain security independently while reducing reliance on any single U.S. political cycle.

The United States does not assume permanent responsibility for enforcing internal political outcomes or indefinitely managing regional disputes where capable local leadership exists. Its role is to support stability and prevent catastrophic escalation alongside partners, not to police the world.

## Multilateral Institutional Reform

As the United States steps away from unilateral enforcement, it will work with international institutions to reform global governance so it functions credibly without reliance on U.S. military policing. Reform priorities include legitimacy, enforceability, burden-sharing, and institutional resilience rather than symbolic authority.

## Bottom Line

This posture preserves deterrence, reduces single-point-of-failure risk created by U.S. political volatility, and aligns incentives so partners build lasting capacity rather than dependency.

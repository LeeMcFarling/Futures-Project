# Foreign Policy — Internal Stability and External Reorientation

## Core Directive

The number one foreign-policy objective of this administration is internal stability.

The United States cannot maintain a credible, constructive, or durable relationship with the world while its own institutions remain vulnerable to extreme political oscillation, democratic erosion, administrative incoherence, and recurring swings in strategic worldview between administrations. These oscillations have imposed real costs on the American people and on the wider world. Allies have been forced to hedge against American unpredictability. Adversaries have been encouraged to test boundaries. International institutions have been weakened by dependence on a power whose own commitments can shift radically from one election to the next.

For that reason, this administration treats internal stabilization not as separate from foreign policy, but as its first condition. A country that cannot govern itself coherently cannot be a stable force in the external world.

## Alliance Reorientation

Whether as an intentional choice or an unintended side-effect, post WWII American foreign policy has largely organized alliances around permanent dependency on U.S. defense guarantees as their sole functional backbone. Our alliance objective is to help build structures in which partners can defend themselves, sustain themselves, and coordinate both with the United States and with one another without assuming that America will always serve as the single operational backstop.

This does not mean abandonment. It means reorientation. The United States remains engaged, but engagement is aimed at functional and diplomatic resilience rather than dependence. Alliances should become more sovereign, more distributed, and less vulnerable to political volatility in Washington. The measure of success is not how many states depend on American discretion. It is how many can remain stable, aligned, and capable even when American politics are unstable or American capacity is constrained.

## Regional Deference and Security Backstop Logic

In regions where the United States is not the primary lived authority, it should stop pretending to understand local legitimacy, escalation dynamics, and political constraints better than the states exposed to them every day.

This administration therefore supports regional security coordination through Security Backstop Partnership–style institutions that allow affected states to interpret shared threats, articulate regional legitimacy, and sequence escalation with greater authority than distant outside actors. The United States should work through such structures where possible rather than defaulting to unilateral regional management or treating every crisis as a problem to be solved first in Washington.

This is not disengagement from the world. It is a recognition that stable order is more likely when regions are treated as political communities with agency rather than as theaters to be managed from abroad.

## United Nations and Global Institutional Reform

The United States will pursue reforms to the United Nations and related multilateral institutions with a simple objective: help build international governance that works for the world.

That requires institutions capable of functioning under disagreement, institutions less dependent on unilateral great-power discretion, and institutions more grounded in regional legitimacy and procedural integrity. The objective is not to preserve the postwar order unchanged, nor to abandon it to great-power competition. It is to modernize it so that it can actually perform the coordinating, constraining, and stabilizing role it claims to perform.

For the United States, this means participating in reform not as a hegemon seeking better tools of control, but as one state among others trying to help construct a system more resilient to coercion, paralysis, and belligerence from any major power, including itself.

## Outreach and External Support

When the United States engages in external support, stabilization, or economic outreach, it should prefer mechanisms that reach people directly rather than systems that are overly mediated through NGO layers, patronage networks, or institutional intermediaries detached from the populations they claim to serve.

Direct-to-populace monetary injections, where feasible and lawful, should be preferred over aid architectures that are highly leaky, administratively opaque, or easily captured by local elites and intermediaries. The objective is to increase legitimacy, reduce extraction, and make support visibly real to the people it is intended to help.

This does not eliminate the need for institutions, civil society, or implementation partners. It means the default bias should be toward directness, transparency, and population-level benefit rather than toward thick layers of mediation that too often become ends in themselves.

## What This Foreign Policy Is Trying to Build

This administration seeks to build resilience at home and reduce fragility abroad.

At home, that means institutions capable of absorbing political stress without exporting volatility to the rest of the world.

Abroad, that means alliance systems that do not collapse without American primacy, regional structures that can articulate legitimacy more credibly than outside powers, and multilateral institutions capable of functioning as real public authorities rather than symbolic shells or instruments of selective power.

The guiding principle is straightforward: we build institutions that are resilient at home, and we reach outward where we can in ways that strengthen rather than hollow out the legitimacy and agency of others.

## Bottom Line

The United States should pursue a foreign policy that begins with internal stability, reorients alliances toward self-defense and resilience, works through regional legitimacy structures where possible, supports reform of the United Nations into a system that works for the world, and favors direct, population-level outreach over overly mediated intervention models.

The United States is changing its relationship with the world in a way intended to benefit both the American people and the broader international order.

# China Strategy
## Managed Competition, Phase-Gated Diplomacy, and Systemic Resilience

## Purpose

This brief defines the public-facing structure of the United States' China strategy.

China is the most consequential strategic competitor facing the United States and its partners. The relationship spans military risk, trade, finance, technology, industrial capacity, maritime security, regional stability, climate, public health, and global institutional governance. It cannot be managed through slogans, personalistic diplomacy, tariff improvisation, or public escalation theater.

The United States will compete with China where interests diverge, negotiate with China where risk reduction is possible, and cooperate with China where shared problems require functional coordination.

The objective is not regime change, humiliation, containment for its own sake, or rhetorical confrontation. The objective is to build a durable international operating system in which coercion is less effective, partners are harder to isolate, trade and technology risks are managed deliberately, and de-escalation remains available through verified behavior.

## Public Doctrine and Strategic Non-Disclosure

This document does not disclose the full extent of the United States' China strategy.

That omission is intentional.

A responsible China strategy cannot be conducted entirely in public. Specific thresholds, intelligence assessments, contingency plans, negotiation sequencing, response curves, and cross-domain escalation assumptions may require internal, classified, or partner-restricted treatment.

The United States will not negotiate with China by publicly posturing through social media, campaign rhetoric, or performative confrontation. Serious powers negotiate through disciplined channels, clear commitments, private signaling, and observable behavior.

Public doctrine should define principles, institutional architecture, and strategic intent. It should not pre-negotiate sensitive concessions, reveal escalation thresholds, or publish operational assumptions for adversarial exploitation.

## Strategic Baseline

The United States begins from the world as it exists.

It recognizes that China is a major power with substantial military, economic, technological, and diplomatic capacity. It also recognizes that China's conduct in several domains creates direct risks to U.S. interests, allied security, regional sovereignty, market integrity, and international institutional legitimacy.

The United States will neither pretend that strategic competition can be wished away nor treat conflict as inevitable.

The governing posture is managed competition under conditions of low trust.

## Operating Model: Low-Trust Phase-Gated Engagement

The United States does not need to trust China to negotiate with China. It needs a system in which verified behavior determines consequences.

Engagement with China proceeds through multiple issue-specific phase gates rather than a single relationship-wide reset. Progress in one domain does not imply progress in all domains. Regression in one domain does not automatically collapse all channels unless priority security thresholds are crossed. Hostile actions trigger automatic cost imposition through pre-negotiated response curves without halting all other diplomatic tracks unless defined escalation ceilings are crossed.

The United States may maintain separate phase-gated tracks for:

- military deconfliction and crisis communication
- maritime and airspace safety
- Taiwan Strait stability
- trade and market access
- export controls and advanced technology
- financial integrity and sanctions enforcement
- climate, public health, and transnational risk
- fentanyl and illicit supply-chain disruption
- cyber and infrastructure interference
- international institutional conduct

Each track operates through observable behavior, verification, bounded incentives, and reversible concessions.

## Day 0 Baseline Rule

China policy begins from the inherited real-world state of the relationship at the start of an administration.

The United States will not pre-negotiate concessions during election cycles, reset the relationship rhetorically for domestic political effect, or re-litigate every prior arrangement absent material breach.

This rule prevents China, domestic factions, or future administrations from turning U.S. political cycles into recurring leverage points.

China engages a continuous system, not a rotating personality.

## Alliance and Regional Coordination

China strategy is not a purely bilateral U.S.–China matter.

U.S. actions must be coordinated with relevant allies and partners through Integrated Defense Partnerships, Security Backstop Partnerships, aligned defense relationships, and regional consultation structures.

No U.S. concession, rollback, trade adjustment, or security posture change should undermine existing commitments, degrade partner security, fragment regional coordination, or create asymmetric economic exposure for exposed partners.

This is especially important in the Indo-Pacific, where regional states face different combinations of military pressure, trade dependence, maritime exposure, supply-chain risk, and domestic political constraint. Many of these states cannot afford to be forced into binary alignment choices. Regional Security Councils provide structured consultation venues through which affected states can interpret shared threats, articulate regional legitimacy, and sequence external engagement on their own terms — without defaulting to hub-and-spoke U.S. management or improvised coalition-building under crisis pressure. As these councils mature through successive phases of security coordination, economic resilience, and political governance, they create the conditions under which regional states can sustain strategic autonomy even under sustained coercive pressure.

The United States retains sovereign execution authority, but later-phase engagement with China must be increasingly alliance-consistent. Bilateral diplomacy cannot become a wedge that weakens regional resilience.

## IDP Layer: Deterrence Through Engineered Resilience

Integrated Defense Partnerships provide the hard-power and industrial resilience layer of China strategy.

The United States and IDP partners will strengthen military interoperability, logistics, sustainment, critical-materials access, defense production, digital infrastructure, financial continuity, and gray-zone response capacity.

The purpose is not to encircle China rhetorically. The purpose is to make coercion less attractive by reducing the payoff from pressure.

A partner that can sustain itself militarily, economically, digitally, and logistically is harder to isolate. An alliance that can absorb disruption without improvisation is harder to intimidate.

## SBP Layer: Regional Legitimacy and Stabilization

Security Backstop Partnerships and Regional Security Councils provide the regional legitimacy layer of China strategy.

Not every regional state will want or qualify for deep IDP integration. Many will still need structured consultation, crisis coordination, economic stabilization pathways, and mechanisms for managing coercion without being forced into binary alignment.

SBPs allow regional states to coordinate around shared risks while preserving sovereign control over military and diplomatic decisions. This matters because regional legitimacy cannot be manufactured in Washington. It must be generated through institutions that affected states can recognize as their own.

The United States should support regional coordination without converting every regional problem into a U.S.–China confrontation.

## Trade and Economic Strategy

Trade policy toward China should be phase-gated, sector-specific, and coordinated with allies.

The United States should avoid both naïve liberalization and indiscriminate economic decoupling. The relevant question is not whether trade with China exists. The relevant question is which forms of trade increase coercive leverage, strategic dependency, industrial hollowing, or military risk.

Trade access should be conditioned by sector, behavior, and systemic exposure. Critical dependencies are reduced over structured timelines, with domestic and allied alternatives built before restrictions escalate rather than after. Export controls advance through observable behavioral triggers, not political discretion, and carry explicit off-ramps tied to verified compliance.

Sectors tied to defense, surveillance, coercive infrastructure, forced dependency, critical inputs, or advanced dual-use capability require stricter controls. Sectors with lower security sensitivity may remain open where doing so supports stability, consumer welfare, and diplomatic channels.

Economic policy should create a controlled incentive gradient: compliance and risk reduction allow limited expansion; coercion, evasion, or destabilizing conduct trigger automatic rollback.

## Technology and Export Controls

Technology policy toward China should distinguish between ordinary commerce, dual-use systems, strategic compute, surveillance infrastructure, military-relevant platforms, and foundational industrial inputs.

The United States should not attempt to freeze all technological exchange. It should prevent the transfer of capabilities that materially improve coercion, surveillance, military modernization, cyber operations, or strategic dependency.

Export controls should be coordinated with allies wherever possible to avoid leakage, arbitrage, and symbolic restrictions that impose cost without strategic effect.

The objective is not technological autarky. The objective is controlled exposure.

## Gray-Zone Conflict and Automatic Costs

China strategy must assume that competition will often occur below the threshold of open conflict.

Cyber disruption, infrastructure interference, economic coercion, maritime pressure, information operations, and unsafe military encounters should be managed through predefined response curves, not ad hoc outrage cycles. A critical feature of these curves is aggregation: repeated low-level actions accumulate toward activation thresholds even when each individual incident appears minor. This removes the ability to probe indefinitely without consequence.

Where behavior crosses defined thresholds, cost imposition activates through rules already coordinated with partners. This removes drama from escalation management and reduces incentives for repeated probing.

China strategy instantiates that logic without publicly disclosing all thresholds.

## Taiwan Strait Stability

### Make Coercion Fail, Invasion Unattractive, and Crisis Communication Functional

Taiwan is a priority security domain within China strategy.

The United States should preserve deterrence, oppose unilateral changes to the status quo by force or coercion, and maintain credible capacity to support Taiwan's resilience consistent with existing legal and strategic commitments.

At the same time, Taiwan policy should not be reduced to public provocation theater. Strategic clarity in capability and consequence can coexist with disciplined diplomacy and careful signaling.

Taiwan-related military coercion should constrain progress in other tracks. China should not be able to escalate around Taiwan while receiving broader economic or diplomatic normalization elsewhere.

## International Institutions and UN Reform

China strategy is not only an Indo-Pacific strategy. It is also an international-institutional strategy.

The United States should pursue UN reform — including Security Council restructuring, appellate review architecture, conflict screening and selective recusal, and a graduated enforcement ladder — that reduces the ability of major powers to paralyze collective action while claiming procedural legitimacy. These reforms are explicitly designed to prevent any major power, including the United States, from using institutional machinery to immunize itself or coerce others. That anti-capture logic is the same structural principle operating at the bilateral level: rules determine behavior consequences, not discretionary great-power management.

The point is not to exclude China from international institutions. The point is to make institutions harder for any major power to capture, freeze, or weaponize.

A stronger international system reduces the need for unilateral U.S. enforcement and creates more legitimate pathways for managing disputes.

## Decolonization, Sovereignty, and Legitimacy Repair

China's influence often grows where Western policy is viewed as hypocritical, extractive, or selectively committed to sovereignty.

A serious China strategy must therefore include legitimacy repair at two levels.

At the institutional level, the United States must narrow the gap between the standards it invokes abroad and the standards it accepts for itself. This means participating in international legal institutions rather than selectively undermining them, and pursuing institutional reform from inside a framework of compliance rather than from outside a framework of hostility. A United States that holds itself to the rules it asks others to obey is harder to portray as an instrument of great-power hypocrisy.

At the material level, the United States and its partners should support fairer development finance alternatives to coercive dependency models, infrastructure investment with transparent terms, debt transparency, anti-corruption tools, regional voice in international institutions, and representation for states historically treated as objects of great-power competition rather than sovereign participants.

This is not charity. It is strategy.

If the United States wants countries to resist coercive dependency, it must help make non-coercive partnership materially better, politically safer, and institutionally more respectful.

## Public Communications Discipline

The United States should negotiate with China through professional channels, not through performative public escalation.

Public communication should be firm, clear, and disciplined. It should state principles, defend partners, explain consequences, and preserve domestic legitimacy. It should not turn every dispute into a social-media confrontation or every negotiation into a public loyalty test.

Public screaming produces three costs: it hardens the other side's domestic position, reduces room for face-saving compromise, and turns policy into entertainment.

A serious China strategy requires strategic composure.

## Strategic Non-Goals

This strategy does not seek:

- regime change in China
- permanent economic severance
- humiliation of China as a civilization or state
- military confrontation as a desired outcome
- unilateral U.S. control over regional politics
- forcing every country into a binary alignment structure
- public disclosure of sensitive operational thresholds

The United States will compete where necessary, negotiate where possible, and build systems that reduce coercive leverage over time.

## Strategic Effect

This strategy shifts China policy from personality-driven confrontation to system-level management.

It does so by combining:

- phase-gated low-trust diplomacy governed by observable behavior, not political cycles
- alliance and regional resilience engineered to absorb pressure without improvisation
- regional legitimacy mechanisms that give Indo-Pacific states structured agency without forcing binary alignment
- trade and export-control discipline sequenced to build alternatives before imposing restrictions
- gray-zone response curves with cross-domain aggregation that prices repeated probing automatically
- international institutional reform designed to make capture harder for any major power
- legal compliance restoration that narrows the hypocrisy gap between U.S. rhetoric and U.S. conduct
- material legitimacy repair that makes non-coercive partnership a better offer than dependency

The United States does not need to announce every move in advance. It needs to build a world in which coercion is less profitable, partners are less vulnerable, and negotiation remains possible without weakness.

## Bottom Line

The United States will not manage China policy by panic, personality, or public performance.

It will build resilience, coordinate with partners, protect critical interests, negotiate through disciplined channels, and preserve pathways for de-escalation where verified behavior permits.

China strategy is not a speech.

It is an operating system.

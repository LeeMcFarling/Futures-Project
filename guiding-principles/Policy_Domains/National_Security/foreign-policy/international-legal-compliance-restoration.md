# International Legal Compliance Restoration Doctrine

## Purpose

This brief establishes a governing doctrine for the relationship between the presidency of the United States and international law. Its central claim is simple: compliance with international law is not an optional moral gesture or a matter of presidential preference. It is a core executive duty of the office of president.

The United States cannot credibly advocate for a rules-based order while treating international legal accountability as something that applies only to others. Nor can it claim to support institutional reform while sanctioning, threatening, or operationally undermining the very institutions through which that reform must occur. A durable foreign policy requires the United States to narrow the gap between the legal standards it invokes abroad and the standards it is willing to recognize for itself.

This doctrine does not require blind acceptance of existing institutions in their present form. It requires a different starting point: lawful participation, operational cooperation, and reform pursued from inside a framework of compliance rather than from outside a framework of hostility.

## Core Principle

This administration holds that compliance with international law is a core executive duty of the presidency.

That principle applies not only to adversaries and weaker states, but to the United States itself. It means the executive branch should not treat international legal institutions as illegitimate merely because they may scrutinize American personnel, American partners, or American conduct. It means the president should not use executive power to exempt the United States from legal standards the country expects others to obey. And it means the office of president should be understood as constrained not only by domestic law, but by the international legal obligations the United States has undertaken or seeks to undertake.

The objective is not national humiliation. It is institutional honesty. A country that wants international law to matter must behave as though it matters even when doing so imposes constraint.

## Strategic Rationale

For decades, the United States has too often approached international legal institutions through a posture of selective legitimacy. When institutions align with American preferences, they are treated as valuable. When they threaten American freedom of action, they are treated as hostile, naive, or illegitimate. This approach has produced predictable results: weakened credibility, a wider hypocrisy gap between rhetoric and conduct, and a global environment in which other major powers feel increasingly licensed to treat international law as optional whenever power permits.

This is not sustainable in a contested multipolar world. If the United States wants a more lawful and more stable international system, it must help normalize a different standard: powerful states do not stand above international law simply because they possess the means to resist it. They should instead be expected to participate in legal institutions, contest them lawfully where appropriate, reform them where necessary, and comply with them where obligated.

This administration therefore rejects the doctrine that sovereign power is diminished by legal constraint. The opposite is closer to the truth. Lawful self-binding is one of the conditions under which power becomes durable, legitimate, and strategically credible.

## Day-One Executive Actions

On the first day in office, the president should issue a formal directive establishing that this administration recognizes lawful compliance with international legal obligations as a core executive responsibility.

That directive should be accompanied by immediate operational actions.

First, the president should revoke sanctions and any similar punitive measures directed at International Criminal Court personnel or their immediate institutional support structures. The United States should not treat legal review itself as a hostile act.

Second, the administration should terminate formal executive hostility toward the ICC as an institution. This includes ending policies whose purpose is to intimidate, obstruct, or delegitimize the court as such.

Third, the administration should restore lawful channels of communication and operational cooperation with relevant international legal bodies where such cooperation is within executive authority.

Fourth, the president should state publicly and unambiguously that this administration will no longer treat accountability under international law as something that binds only other nations.

These first-day steps matter because doctrine without visible action will be dismissed as rhetoric. The point of day-one action is to make clear that the United States is changing its legal posture immediately, not merely promising to think differently later.

## Executive Compliance Policy

This administration should adopt a standing executive policy of lawful cooperation with applicable international legal processes to the maximum extent permitted under existing domestic authority.

That means the executive branch should treat international warrants, requests, investigations, and evidentiary processes as matters requiring legal evaluation and good-faith response, not reflexive political defiance. It should not default to obstruction where lawful cooperation is possible. It should not construct ad hoc immunity doctrines merely because the subject of review is American or aligned with the United States.

Where domestic law limits immediate executive compliance, the administration should state those limits openly rather than disguising political choice as legal inevitability. Where executive authority does exist, it should be used in favor of lawful cooperation rather than executive evasion.

The doctrine here is straightforward: the executive should comply where it lawfully can, seek legislative alignment where it cannot, and stop treating legal accountability as presumptively hostile.

## Congressional and Treaty Objectives

Day-one executive action is not enough. Durable compliance cannot rest on the goodwill of one administration. It requires longer-horizon institutional alignment through Congress and, where necessary, formal treaty process.

This administration should therefore pursue congressional pathways toward U.S. accession to the International Criminal Court and toward domestic statutory alignment with the obligations that accession would entail. That effort should be approached honestly. It is not a symbolic gesture. It is a legal and constitutional project that would require serious debate over jurisdiction, due process, service-member protections, cooperation standards, and the relationship between domestic and international judicial authority.

The administration should also pursue legislative reforms that reduce the ability of future presidents to treat hostility to international legal institutions as an ordinary instrument of executive power. Sanctioning courts, threatening personnel, or operationally sabotaging accountability systems should not remain a routine presidential option.

The broader objective is simple: move from temporary executive discretion toward durable legal commitment.

## Compliance and Reform Together

This doctrine does not treat the ICC or the broader multilateral legal order as above criticism. Institutions can be inconsistent, selective, procedurally weak, or politically distorted. But the answer to institutional weakness is not executive hostility paired with selective exemption. The answer is compliance paired with reform.

This administration will therefore work with other major powers and institutional partners to improve the legitimacy, consistency, transparency, and procedural integrity of the ICC and related multilateral bodies. That includes supporting reforms that reduce selectivity, improve due-process confidence, strengthen impartiality standards, and better integrate legal accountability with the broader UN reform agenda.

The United States should participate in this process not as a hegemon demanding exemption, but as a major state arguing for institutions strong enough to constrain all powerful actors more credibly over time.

## Relationship to UN Reform

This doctrine fits directly within the broader institutional-reform architecture.

The UN reform stack already argues that the international order must be able to function under conditions of great-power disagreement, must distribute legitimacy across levels, must screen conflicted actors, and must operate through a bounded ladder of consequence rather than theatrical condemnation. International legal compliance is part of that same logic. A system in which powerful states sabotage legal accountability from the outside cannot mature into a system of durable, legitimate order.

For the United States, this means ICC posture and UN posture cannot be treated as separate questions. If the administration is serious about helping build institutions that work for the world, it must begin by ceasing to undermine the institutions through which accountability is supposed to operate.

## What This Doctrine Is Not

This doctrine is not a renunciation of American sovereignty.

It is not blind deference to every institutional decision in every circumstance.

It is not a demand that the executive ignore domestic constitutional limits or statutory constraints.

It is not a declaration that international institutions are already sufficient in their current form.

And it is not a politics of apology for its own sake.

It is a doctrine of lawful self-binding. The United States should stop treating international legal accountability as a special threat to American power and start treating it as part of the infrastructure of a stable order the country claims to want.

## Strategic Effect

This doctrine changes the posture of the United States in four important ways.

First, it narrows the hypocrisy gap between American rhetoric about rules and American willingness to accept rules for itself.

Second, it lowers the legitimacy cost of U.S. participation in institutional reform by demonstrating that reform is being pursued from a position of legal engagement rather than strategic exemption.

Third, it makes American foreign policy more predictable by tying executive conduct to stated legal principles rather than shifting theories of discretionary immunity.

Fourth, it strengthens the long-run possibility of an international order capable of surviving belligerence or lawbreaking from any major power, including the United States, because it helps normalize the principle that powerful states are not outside the systems they ask others to obey.

## Bottom Line

This administration will treat compliance with international law as a core executive duty of the office of president.

On day one, it will end hostile sanctions and punitive measures directed at ICC personnel, restore lawful cooperation with international legal institutions, and state clearly that the United States will no longer treat legal accountability as something that applies only to others.

Beyond day one, it will pursue congressional and treaty pathways toward durable U.S. participation in the ICC and related legal frameworks, while working with other major powers to improve the legitimacy, integrity, and effectiveness of those institutions as part of broader UN reform.

The United States should not seek exemption from the lawful order it claims to defend. It should help build that order by participating in it.

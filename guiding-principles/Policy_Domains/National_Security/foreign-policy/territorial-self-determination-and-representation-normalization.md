# Territorial Self-Determination and Representation Normalization Act

## Purpose

This brief establishes a simple constitutional legitimacy principle: no permanently governed U.S. population should remain indefinitely in democratic limbo. If a jurisdiction is populated, subject to U.S. law, and expected to bear durable federal obligations, it must be offered a real pathway to full democratic representation, sovereign separation, or continued territorial status through explicit popular consent rather than inherited ambiguity.

The United States should therefore offer each of the five populated overseas territories, as well as the residential population of the District of Columbia, a binding plebiscitary pathway to determine its long-term political status. The available options should be clearly defined in advance, legally actionable, and accompanied by transparent transition compacts rather than vague symbolic promises.

This is not a one-sided act of federal benevolence. It is a correction to an institutional contradiction. A republic that claims representative legitimacy should not preserve semi-permanent populations under its authority without either full representation or a genuine route out of the arrangement.

## Core Principle

If you pay taxes, you get representation.

More broadly: if a population is durably governed, obligated, and incorporated into the federal order, it should not remain in a subordinate political category indefinitely. Taxation without representation is the clearest version of the problem, but not the only one. Military service, federal law, economic dependence, and executive control all matter as well. The deeper issue is not only revenue. It is durable rule without full democratic standing.

This administration therefore rejects indefinite territorial ambiguity as a normal condition of governance.

## Covered Jurisdictions

This framework applies to the five populated U.S. overseas territories:

- Puerto Rico
- Guam
- U.S. Virgin Islands
- American Samoa
- Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands

It also applies to the residential population of the District of Columbia, with the federal core carved out as constitutionally required for the seat of government.

The purpose is not to erase differences among these jurisdictions. Their constitutional histories, legal arrangements, and public preferences differ. The purpose is to establish a common legitimacy rule: each governed population must be given a real and binding status choice.

## The Plebiscite Framework

Each covered jurisdiction receives a federally recognized, locally binding plebiscite offering three options:

**Statehood.** Full and permanent incorporation into the Union, with equal representation, equal constitutional standing, and equal obligations under federal law.

**Sovereignty / Independence or Free Association.** Exit from territorial status through a negotiated sovereign compact, with terms for citizenship transition, economic support, treaty relations, and defense arrangements defined in advance.

**Status Quo Continuation.** Retention of the present territorial or district arrangement for a defined period, after which the issue returns on a scheduled review cycle unless a permanent status has been otherwise chosen.

The key design rule is that each option must be published in concrete legal and fiscal terms before any vote occurs. Voters must know what they are choosing.

## Statehood as a Permanent Option

Where a covered jurisdiction votes for statehood under the defined threshold, the United States should treat that choice as an application to join the Union on equal and permanent terms. Statehood is not a probationary category. It is full membership in the constitutional order.

This means that a vote for statehood is a vote to join the Union as an equal state for the indefinite future, subject to the same constitutional structure as every other state. It is not territorial management under another name, and it is not temporary partial incorporation.

The legitimacy of the offer depends on its seriousness. Statehood cannot be presented as one option among many and then quietly denied when chosen.

## Sovereignty and Free Association

Where a jurisdiction chooses independence or free association, the United States should negotiate a transition compact openly and lawfully. That compact should address citizenship, migration, economic support, debt and asset allocation, federal program transition, legal continuity, defense arrangements, and treaty obligations.

This option must be real as well. A vote for sovereignty cannot mean abandonment, nor can it mean pseudo-sovereignty while the United States retains unilateral control through informal means. If the United States offers an exit, it must offer one honestly.

## Status Quo as a Time-Bounded Choice

Status quo continuation may remain on the ballot, but it should no longer function as a permanent mechanism for avoiding resolution. If voters choose continuation, that decision should remain valid only for a fixed period before a new plebiscite is automatically scheduled.

The point is not to force immediate change against local will. It is to end indefinite ambiguity as the default constitutional condition. A population may choose continuity, but the federal government should not treat one vote for continuity as a permanent waiver of future self-determination.

## District of Columbia

The same legitimacy principle applies to the residential population of the District of Columbia. The federal seat of government may remain constitutionally protected as a distinct federal core, but that core does not justify maintaining a large permanent population in a condition of incomplete representation.

Residents of Washington, DC should therefore be offered a binding pathway to full democratic representation through statehood for the residential portions of the district, with the federal core separately preserved.

The principle here is straightforward: the capital cannot remain a permanent democratic exception.

## Military Basing and Strategic Carve-Outs

Where a covered jurisdiction chooses sovereignty or free association, U.S. military basing and strategic access should not continue automatically as an implied colonial residue. They should instead be governed through explicit, compensated, reviewable agreements.

This means any basing carve-outs must be negotiated openly as part of the transition compact. They may be long-term if mutually agreed. They may include defense guarantees, rent, infrastructure support, or joint-use arrangements. But they must be treaty-like in character rather than silently preserved through unequal status.

The purpose is to preserve strategic continuity where necessary without hollowing out the self-determination offer.

## Thresholds and Process

Each plebiscite should be federally recognized, locally administered under uniform integrity standards, and preceded by a published explanatory compact for each option.

Threshold rules should be clear enough to produce legitimacy without making change impossible. The most defensible structure is likely a majority vote with a turnout floor or equivalent legitimacy safeguard, though exact calibration can be refined by jurisdiction.

The federal government should commit in advance to honoring the winning option if the threshold is met. A plebiscite that does not bind the metropole is not a real self-determination mechanism.

## Why This Matters

This proposal is about more than territorial administration. It is about constitutional honesty.

A United States that asks people to live under its authority, serve in its institutions, contribute to its public finances, and remain subject to its laws while withholding either full representation or a real path to exit is preserving a contradiction at the center of its own democratic legitimacy.

That contradiction also weakens the country externally. A state that speaks in the language of self-government while maintaining indefinite subordinate statuses at home undermines its own credibility abroad. Ending territorial limbo is therefore both a domestic democratic reform and a foreign-policy legitimacy reform.

## What This Is Not

This is not an attempt to force statehood on unwilling populations.

It is not an attempt to force independence on populations that prefer continued union.

It is not a symbolic exercise in consultation without legal consequence.

And it is not a covert effort to preserve strategic control while pretending to offer self-determination.

It is a normalization framework. The United States offers real options, defines them honestly, and commits to honoring the result.

## Strategic Effect

This framework does three things at once.

First, it restores constitutional legitimacy by aligning durable federal rule with either representation or genuine consent.

Second, it reduces the democratic contradiction of permanently governed populations held in ambiguous status.

Third, it strengthens American credibility by applying at home the same basic principle the broader project seeks abroad: political communities should not live indefinitely inside structures that exercise power over them without clear, representative, and reviewable authority.

## Bottom Line

The United States should end indefinite territorial ambiguity.

All five populated overseas territories and the residential population of the District of Columbia should receive a binding plebiscitary pathway to choose among statehood, sovereignty/free association, or time-bounded status quo continuation. Where populations choose statehood, they should be admitted on equal and permanent terms. Where they choose sovereignty, the transition should be governed through honest and reviewable compacts, including any military basing arrangements. Where they choose continuation, the question should return on a fixed democratic cadence rather than disappear indefinitely.

If you pay taxes, you get representation. If you do not want representation, you get a real path out. What should end is the limbo.

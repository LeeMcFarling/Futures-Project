# Industrial Siting Compact

## Purpose

The Industrial Siting Compact defines the conditions under which an industrial corridor is activated and deployed.

It ensures that industrial development is:

- coordinated across systems  
- sequenced correctly  
- supported by necessary infrastructure  
- resilient and scalable  

This compact transforms industrial policy from:

> project-by-project approval

into:

> system-level deployment

---

## Core Principle

Industrial capacity must be deployed as an integrated system.

> no corridor is activated unless labor, inputs, logistics, housing, and regulatory capacity are aligned

---

## System Role

The compact functions as the **activation framework** for industrial corridors.

It:

- establishes readiness conditions  
- synchronizes deployment across systems  
- defines responsibilities across institutions  
- ensures continuous coordination during execution  

---

## Corridor Designation

Corridors are designated based on the **Industrial Corridor Viability Criteria Framework**.

Designation requires:

- sufficient logistics connectivity  
- workforce accessibility and scalability  
- housing and land-use capacity  
- supplier ecosystem potential  
- regulatory feasibility  

Corridor designation is:

> the trigger for full system activation

---

## Deployment Activation (Execution Corps Bundle)

At corridor designation, the **Execution Corps deployment bundle is automatically activated**, including:

- Execution Corps (project delivery)  
- Public Capital Authority (PCA)  
- Regulatory Modernization Corps (RMC)  
- Workforce Coordination Authority (WCA)  
- Immigration Services and Administration (ISA)  
- Department of Data and Accountability (DoDA) support  

No system is deployed independently.

---

## Readiness Framework

Corridor activation depends on alignment across four readiness dimensions:

---

### I. Workforce Readiness (WCA)

Defined as:

- Workforce Activation Contracts established  
- training pipelines initiated  
- domestic workforce routing active  

Training begins at designation, including long-lead roles.

---

### II. Supplier & Input Readiness

Defined as:

- supplier ecosystems mapped  
- critical supply chain gaps identified  
- input routing systems configured  

Domestic priority and circuit breaker systems are active.

---

### III. Logistics Readiness

Defined as:

- throughput requirements modeled  
- co-location opportunities identified against corridor viability scores  
- logistics capacity expansion initiated  

Supplier proximity and transport efficiency are aligned.

---

### IV. Urban & Housing Readiness

Defined as:

- housing expansion pathways activated through corridor-linked density overlays  
- land-use alignment established through the Land Use Stabilization & Inclusive Growth framework  
- community stabilization mechanisms deployed (including Dutch disease mitigation measures)  

This includes:

- pre-sequenced housing supply expansion aligned to workforce inflows  
- mixed-income and inclusionary development pathways  
- land value stabilization and resident participation mechanisms  

Urban readiness is achieved when:

> housing supply, land-use policy, and stabilization mechanisms are activated and aligned with projected population inflows

Operational mechanisms are defined in:

- Land Use Stabilization & Inclusive Growth Framework  
- Localized Industrial Boom Stabilization (Dutch Disease Mitigation)

---

## Deployment Sequencing

All systems activate at corridor designation and proceed in parallel:

### Phase 0 — Designation
- corridor selected  
- Execution Corps bundle activated  

### Phase 1 — Early Activation
- WCA initiates training pipelines  
- supplier gaps identified and financing initiated  
- logistics constraints mapped  
- housing expansion triggered  

### Phase 2 — Buildout
- construction begins  
- supplier capacity scales  
- logistics infrastructure expands  

### Phase 3 — Pre-Operations
- workforce pipelines mature  
- input systems stabilize  
- logistics systems operational  

### Phase 4 — Operations
- full production begins with aligned systems  

---

## Co-Location & Spatial Design

Corridors are structured to prioritize:

- co-location of suppliers and manufacturers  
- integration of production stages  
- reduced inter-regional transport of intermediate goods  

Co-location is pursued where:

> corridor viability scores indicate sufficient logistics capacity, supplier density potential, and connectivity

Distributed production without functional necessity is avoided.

---

## Continuous Coordination

During deployment:

- WCA adjusts workforce pipelines  
- supplier systems adapt to demand shifts  
- logistics systems rebalance throughput  
- DoDA monitors system performance  

The system operates as a **continuous coordination loop**, not a static plan.

---

## System Monitoring (DoDA)

DoDA evaluates:

- readiness alignment across systems  
- labor supply vs demand  
- supplier capacity growth  
- logistics throughput  
- housing absorption  

This informs:

- system adjustments  
- investment prioritization  
- future corridor design  

DoDA does not intervene in real-time operations.

---

## Guardrails

1. **No Partial Deployment**  
   All core systems must be activated together.

2. **No Sequential Bottlenecks**  
   Systems must scale in parallel.

3. **No Politically Fragmented Siting**  
   Deployment must align with viability criteria.

4. **No Unabsorbed Growth**  
   Workforce inflows must match housing capacity.

---

## Plain-English Explanation

This compact is the rulebook for building industrial regions the right way.

Instead of approving factories one at a time and hoping everything else works out, the system activates everything together:

- workers are trained  
- suppliers are built  
- housing is expanded  
- infrastructure is ready  

All at the same time.

It also makes sure everything is built in the right places—where the system can actually support it.

The goal is simple:

> when a corridor turns on, the entire system is ready to operate—not just the factory

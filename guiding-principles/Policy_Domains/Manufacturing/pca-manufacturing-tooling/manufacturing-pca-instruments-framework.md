## Purpose

This framework defines how the Public Capital Authority deploys capital into
manufacturing sectors: which instruments it uses, how they are structured,
when and how they exit, and how returns are recycled into the next deployment
cycle.

It establishes the financial logic that makes industrial policy catalytic rather
than permanent. Government as risk absorber and coordination catalyst, not as
operator, majority owner, or indefinite subsidizer.

This document is a proof of concept for a different theory of public capital.
The prevailing model — permanent subsidy regardless of performance, no exit
discipline, no return requirement, no crowding-in mechanism — produces exactly
what its critics claim: fiscal drag, market distortion, political entrenchment,
and industrial results that depend entirely on whether the subsidy keeps flowing.
When the subsidy stops, so does the industry.

The PCA manufacturing framework replaces that model with something structurally
different: time-bound instruments with defined exit conditions, private
co-investment requirements that make public capital a minority participant rather
than the primary funder, performance thresholds that gate continued deployment,
and capital recycling mechanisms that convert successful investments into
resources for the next frontier rather than permanent state ownership.

The state acts as catalyst. Ownership is temporary. Upside is capped. Exit is
automatic. What remains when the public capital exits is a functioning cluster —
not a dependency.

## I. The Crowding-In Thesis

The central claim of this framework is that public capital deployed correctly
multiplies private investment rather than displacing it. This is not assumed;
it is designed for and measured against.

### Why Private Capital Underinvests in Manufacturing

Private capital markets systematically underinvest in certain manufacturing
categories for structural reasons that are not corrected by price signals alone.

**Coordination failures.** A semiconductor fab requires a supplier ecosystem,
a trained workforce, a logistics spine, and reliable power — none of which exist
until others commit. Each private actor waits for others to move first. The
equilibrium is no investment despite positive expected returns if coordination
were achieved. Public capital that resolves the coordination failure — by
anchoring the first node, publishing corridor readiness data, and pre-committing
to infrastructure — changes the game for every subsequent private actor.

**First-mover risk.** The first domestic producer in a category absorbs the full
cost of building supplier relationships, training the workforce, and navigating
regulatory uncertainty. Subsequent entrants free-ride on that infrastructure.
Private capital rationally underweights first-mover positions relative to their
social value. Public capital that absorbs first-mover costs — and exits once the
cluster is self-sustaining — corrects this asymmetry without permanently owning
the result.

**Horizon mismatch.** Manufacturing investments have 15–30 year capital cycles.
Private capital markets optimize over 3–7 year return horizons. The mismatch is
structural, not a failure of will. Instruments like long-duration structured
debt, revenue participation, and patient first-loss tranches bridge the horizon
gap without requiring private capital to violate its own return logic.

**Information asymmetry.** Corridor readiness, regulatory timeline risk, and
supply chain development trajectories are difficult for private actors to assess
independently. DoDA dashboard transparency — publishing what individual investors
cannot cost-effectively discover — resolves information asymmetry at scale and
reduces the risk premium private capital charges for uncertainty it cannot
quantify.

**Strategic risk concentration.** Some manufacturing categories carry adversary-
coercion risk, supply chain disruption risk, and geopolitical event risk that
private capital cannot hedge through diversification. These risks are real but
not amenable to private pricing. Public capital can absorb the strategic premium
because the state's time horizon and risk tolerance are structurally different —
and because absorbing that premium is precisely what makes private capital viable
in the category at all.

### What Crowding-In Requires

Public capital crowds in private capital only when it is structured to do so.
The conditions are specific and must be designed in, not assumed.

**Minority position.** PCA capital must not crowd the investment opportunity. If
public capital takes the majority position, private capital has no compelling
reason to participate. PCA holds minority stakes — typically 10–20% — so that
private actors capture the majority of returns and have strong incentive to
deploy.

**First-loss absorption.** PCA takes the most junior position in the capital
stack. This makes the senior positions held by private capital significantly
safer, reducing their required return and unlocking investment that would not
otherwise clear private hurdle rates.

**Milestone-gated tranching.** PCA capital is disbursed in stages tied to
independently verified performance milestones, not released in lump sums. This
disciplines deployment, reduces moral hazard, and signals to private co-investors
that public capital is not a backstop for poor execution.

**Co-investment requirements.** PCA instruments require private co-investment as
a condition of deployment. The ratio varies by instrument and phase — higher
public share in earliest, highest-risk phases; declining toward zero as clusters
mature. No instrument deploys without private capital alongside it.

**Transparent exit conditions.** Private capital cannot plan around public
capital that might stay indefinitely or withdraw unpredictably. PCA exit
conditions are defined in advance, published, and contractually binding. Private
investors know exactly when and how public capital will exit and can price their
own positions accordingly.

**Performance linkage.** Continued PCA deployment is conditioned on measurable
outcomes certified by DoDA. This prevents the entrenchment dynamic where
underperforming investments persist because they have political constituencies.
It also signals to private capital that the public investor exercises genuine
discipline.

### Measuring Crowding-In

Private capital crowd-in ratio is the primary portfolio health metric for the
PCA manufacturing stack. The target ratio is 4:1 or higher for mature instruments
— meaning every dollar of PCA capital deployed generates at least four dollars of
private investment. Early-phase first-loss instruments may have lower ratios;
this is acceptable because they unlock subsequent deployment rounds with higher
ratios.

DoDA publishes crowd-in ratios quarterly by instrument type, sector, and
corridor. If a corridor or instrument consistently underperforms on crowd-in,
the instrument design is reviewed before the next deployment cycle.

## II. Instrument Stack

The PCA manufacturing stack comprises six core instruments. Each addresses a
distinct failure mode. Instrument selection is driven by constraint
identification, not by political preference or sectoral lobbying.

---

### Instrument 1: Minority Equity Participation

**Failure mode addressed:** Long-horizon underinvestment; strategic risk
concentration; signaling gap.

**When deployed:** Alliance-critical sectors (designated in the Alliance
Industrial Target Map) where private capital underinvests due to long payback
timelines, geopolitical risk premiums, or both. Primarily used in anchor
manufacturing facility development and strategic joint ventures.

**Structure:**
- PCA holds minority, non-voting equity — maximum 20% post-money
- No board representation; no operational control
- Stake is passive; PCA does not direct management decisions
- Returns accrue through dividend participation and exit appreciation

**Exit conditions (any trigger exits):**
- Private refinancing round above a defined threshold
- IPO or public listing
- Acquisition by qualified buyer
- Defined time limit reached (typically 7–10 years)
- Performance thresholds achieved and private capital available to replace

**Return cap:** PCA's financial return is capped at 2–3× invested capital.
Returns above this cap are released to private investors or reinvested at the
firm level. Capping upside prevents perception of state profit extraction and
preserves the catalytic rather than commercial nature of the instrument.

**Sample clause — equity cap and non-voting rights:**
> "The PCA's equity investment shall not exceed 20% of the firm's fully-diluted
> capitalization at the time of investment. Such shares shall carry no voting
> rights or board representation. PCA is a passive minority investor and shall
> exercise no operational control over the investee."

**Sample clause — return cap and sell-down:**
> "Upon a liquidity event, PCA shall sell sufficient shares to reduce its
> holding to no more than 5% of outstanding equity. Any remaining stake shall
> convert to a capped revenue participation instrument yielding [X]% of gross
> revenues until PCA achieves a [2.5×] return on invested capital, after which
> the instrument terminates automatically."

---

### Instrument 2: First-Loss Capital Tranches

**Failure mode addressed:** Capital market hesitation in high-uncertainty
categories; coordination failure in early-phase cluster formation.

**When deployed:** Categories where private capital fundamentals are sound but
perceived risk — regulatory uncertainty, first-mover exposure, supply chain
immaturity — creates a hesitation gap. First-loss tranches are the highest-
leverage instrument in the stack: absorbing a small amount of first-loss risk
unlocks large amounts of senior private capital.

**Structure:**
- PCA takes the most junior position in the capital stack
- Absorbs the first defined percentage of losses before any private capital
  is impaired — typically 10–20% of total project capital
- Private senior lenders and equity hold remaining positions
- PCA's loss exposure is capped at the defined first-loss tranche size

**Target leverage:** Each dollar of PCA first-loss capital should unlock at
least $5–8 of private senior capital. If leverage falls below $4:1 in a given
deployment, instrument design is reviewed.

**Milestone gating:** First-loss tranche is committed but disbursed in stages
tied to verified milestones. Commitment without full disbursement allows private
capital to underwrite against the PCA position while release discipline is
maintained.

**Drawdown transparency:** Every first-loss drawdown is publicly logged through
DoDA systems. Drawdown rates are a leading indicator of project health and are
reported quarterly to Congress.

**Sample clause — milestone disbursement:**
> "PCA's first-loss commitment of $[X] shall be disbursed in tranches only upon
> independent verification of predefined milestones. Failure to achieve any
> milestone within the agreed timeframe automatically suspends further
> disbursement. PCA's total loss exposure shall not exceed the committed tranche
> size regardless of project outcome."

---

### Instrument 3: Revenue Participation and Quasi-Equity

**Failure mode addressed:** Horizon mismatch; preference for non-dilutive
capital among established manufacturers scaling existing operations.

**When deployed:** Mid-stage manufacturing expansion where cash flow is expected
but timing is uncertain. Preferred by manufacturers with existing equity
structures they do not wish to dilute. Appropriate for scaling production lines,
adding manufacturing nodes, and expanding supplier facilities.

**Structure:**
- PCA provides capital in exchange for a defined percentage of gross or net
  revenues for a defined period, or until a return cap is reached
- No equity ownership; no board involvement
- Revenue participation rate and cap are set at deployment and cannot be
  renegotiated upward
- Instrument terminates automatically when the return cap is achieved

**Return cap:** PCA targets 1.5–2.5× invested capital through revenue
participation. Intentionally modest — the objective is capital recovery and
recycling, not profit maximization.

**Conversion option:** If revenues are insufficient to return capital within a
defined window, PCA may convert the outstanding balance to a long-duration
structured loan at a predetermined rate. This preserves recovery without
requiring equity dilution or default proceedings.

**Sample clause — revenue participation:**
> "In exchange for PCA's investment of $[X], the firm agrees to pay [Y]% of
> gross manufacturing revenues quarterly until PCA has received total payments
> equal to [Z×] the invested principal, at which point this obligation
> terminates automatically and irrevocably."

---

### Instrument 4: Tooling and Equipment Finance

**Failure mode addressed:** Equipment bottleneck; SME supplier scaling
constraints; automation adoption barriers.

**When deployed:** When the binding constraint is physical production capability
rather than project-level capital formation. Particularly important for Tier 1
and Tier 2 supplier firms that cannot access capital markets efficiently and for
whom equipment cost is the primary scaling barrier. Also deployed for robotics
and automation adoption across manufacturing clusters.

**Structure:**
- Structured as low-interest loans with deferred repayment tied to production
  milestones
- Collateralized against the equipment financed
- Loan terms aligned to equipment useful life — typically 7–15 years
- Repayment schedules calibrated to production ramp, not calendar dates

**Priority targeting:**
- SME suppliers in IDP-aligned production networks
- Firms adopting automation and robotics in target corridors
- Equipment enabling surge capacity consistent with IDP doctrine —
  particularly tooling replication packages

**IDP integration:** Tooling finance for IDP-standard system production
contributes directly to the N+2 node requirement. Where tooling replication
packages are developed and escrowed under alliance custody, PCA tooling finance
may fund the physical production of those packages.

**Sample clause — production-milestone repayment:**
> "Repayment of tooling finance principal shall commence upon achievement of
> [defined production threshold] and shall be structured as [X]% of monthly
> production revenues until principal is retired. If production thresholds are
> not achieved within [Y] months, the instrument converts to a fixed amortization
> schedule at [Z]% per annum."

---

### Instrument 5: Cluster Infrastructure Debt

**Failure mode addressed:** Shared infrastructure as the binding corridor
constraint; horizon mismatch for infrastructure capital; public goods problem
in infrastructure investment.

**When deployed:** When the binding constraint is not firm-level capital but
shared corridor infrastructure — grid interconnection, substations, freight rail
spurs, water and waste treatment systems, industrial land preparation, or port
equipment. These assets benefit multiple firms simultaneously but no single firm
can justify funding them unilaterally.

**Structure:**
- Long-duration structured debt — 15–30 year terms aligned to infrastructure
  useful life
- Interest rates below market to reflect public goods character and
  strategic value
- Repayment structured as usage-based tolls, corridor assessments, or blended
  public-private revenue streams
- PCA holds a senior secured position against the infrastructure asset

**Governance:** Infrastructure assets financed through cluster debt remain under
public or public-private governance — not transferred to a single firm. Shared
access terms are defined in advance to prevent a single manufacturer from
capturing infrastructure financed by public capital.

**IDP alignment:** Cluster infrastructure debt is the primary instrument for
building the physical prerequisites of N+2 production nodes. Power, logistics,
and water infrastructure funded here directly enables IDP compliance across
multiple firms simultaneously.

**Exit:** Infrastructure debt is repaid through usage revenues or corridor
assessments over the loan term. PCA retains no ongoing ownership interest after
debt retirement.

---

### Instrument 6: Alliance Offtake Compact Participation

**Failure mode addressed:** Demand uncertainty as the primary investment
barrier; single-buyer dependency; insufficient volume to justify production scale.

**When deployed:** Categories where manufacturers can produce competitively but
lack sufficient demand visibility to justify capital investment. Particularly
relevant for IDP-standard system production, defense-adjacent manufacturing, and
early-stage domestic production in import-dependent categories.

**Structure:**
- PCA coordinates multi-year conditional purchase commitments from federal
  agencies, allied governments, and large private offtakers
- Commitments are conditional on price-performance thresholds — not
  unconditional guarantees
- Volume commitments activate automatically under defined triggers
- Manufacturers underwrite capital investment against committed demand

**Relation to IDP Alliance Offtake Compact:** For IDP-standard systems, PCA
offtake coordination integrates directly with the Alliance Offtake Compact under
IDP industrial capacity doctrine. Multi-year minimum purchase volumes, learning-
curve price adjustments, and contingency lot expansion follow IDP compact terms.

**Anti-lock-in design:** Offtake commitments step down as domestic supply becomes
competitive. The objective is demand visibility sufficient to anchor investment,
not permanent market share guarantee. Price-performance conditions ensure
commitments phase out as the domestic producer reaches cost parity.

**Sample clause — conditional offtake:**
> "Subject to meeting the price-performance threshold defined in Schedule A,
> purchasing parties commit to purchase a minimum of [X] units annually for [Y]
> years. This commitment is contingent on quality standards and shall step down
> by [Z]% per year once domestic market pricing reaches competitive parity as
> determined by DoDA benchmarking."

## III. Instrument Selection Logic

PCA deployment is governed by constraint identification, not sector preference.
The selection sequence is:

**Step 1 — Identify the binding constraint.** DoDA signal ingestion identifies
the specific failure mode: coordination gap, capital market hesitation, equipment
bottleneck, infrastructure gap, demand uncertainty, or strategic risk premium.
The constraint determines the instrument.

**Step 2 — Assess sector and corridor classification.** Alliance Industrial
Target Map domain classification and corridor viability tier determine urgency
and scale. Domestic-Critical domains in Tier I corridors receive priority.

**Step 3 — Size the instrument to the constraint.** PCA deploys the minimum
intervention that shifts the investment equilibrium. Oversizing crowds out
private capital and creates dependency.

**Step 4 — Set co-investment requirements.** Every instrument requires private
co-investment. Minimum ratios:
- First-loss tranches: 4:1 private to public minimum
- Minority equity: 4:1 minimum; 8:1 target at scale
- Revenue participation: 2:1 minimum
- Tooling finance: 1:1 minimum for SMEs; higher for larger firms
- Cluster infrastructure: 1:1 public-private minimum
- Offtake compacts: demand coordination only; no capital co-investment required

**Step 5 — Define exit conditions before deploying.** Exit conditions are
contractually binding, DoDA-verifiable, and published before capital deploys.
Instruments without predefined exit conditions are not deployed.

### Constraint-to-Instrument Mapping

| Binding Constraint | Primary Instrument | Secondary Instrument |
|---|---|---|
| Coordination failure / first-mover | First-loss tranche | Minority equity |
| Long-horizon underinvestment | Minority equity | Revenue participation |
| Capital market hesitation | First-loss tranche | Cluster infrastructure debt |
| Equipment bottleneck (anchor firm) | Tooling finance | Minority equity |
| Equipment bottleneck (SME supplier) | Tooling finance | — |
| Shared infrastructure gap | Cluster infrastructure debt | — |
| Demand uncertainty | Offtake compact | Revenue participation |
| Strategic risk premium (IDP-critical) | First-loss tranche + offtake compact | Minority equity |

## IV. Exit Mechanics

Every PCA instrument exits. The form varies by instrument and trigger.

| Exit Trigger | PCA Action | Post-Exit Instrument | Return Profile | Distortion Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Private refinancing | Sell down equity to ≤5%; convert remainder | Capped revenue participation | Moderate yield + capped upside | Low |
| IPO or public listing | Staged sell-down to ≤10% | Revenue participation until cap | Steady moderate yield | Low |
| Acquisition or buyout | Full exit; royalty if ongoing production | Cash or fixed royalty | Immediate recovery + premium | Low |
| Revenue cap achieved | Instrument terminates automatically | None | Cap reached; closes | None |
| Milestone failure | Halt disbursement; restructure or write off | Restructured terms or loss | Transparent loss within band | Low |
| Time limit reached | Mandate sale or convert to structured debt | Debt at predetermined rate | Cost recovery | Moderate |
| IDP offtake target achieved | Step-down per schedule | Market pricing takes over | N/A | Low |

**Transparency:** All exit events are published through DoDA within 30 days.
Exit price, return multiple achieved, and comparison to projected return are
disclosed. Losses are reported symmetrically with gains.

## V. Capital Recycling Architecture

PCA capital is not consumed. It is cycled.

Returns from exits — equity appreciation, revenue participation receipts, loan
repayments, royalties — flow back into the manufacturing capital portfolio rather
than returning to general treasury as one-time revenue. This creates a revolving
deployment capacity that compounds over time.

**Cycle 1:** Public capital absorbs first-mover risk in an emerging category.
Private capital co-invests at 4:1. Cluster forms. PCA exits.

**Cycle 2:** Exit proceeds redeploy into the next constrained category. Private
capital co-invests at 6:1 because earlier deployment demonstrated credibility
and reduced category uncertainty.

**Cycle 3:** Portfolio generates sufficient internal returns to sustain
deployment without additional congressional appropriation at the margin.

This is the structural alternative to the permanent subsidy model: public
capital that builds the conditions for private capital sufficiency and then
funds the next frontier from its own returns.

### Recycling Governance

Capital recycling operates within the congressional-authorized portfolio
envelope. Redeployment decisions are made by the PCA capital board within
predefined sectoral allocation bands. Congress is notified of material
reallocations. Recycled capital cannot expand the total portfolio beyond
authorized limits without new appropriation.

DoDA publishes the portfolio recycling rate — total capital redeployed from
exits as a percentage of total portfolio — quarterly. A healthy recycling rate
is a primary indicator of portfolio discipline.

## VI. Integration with the Institutional Stack

PCA manufacturing instruments are one layer of a coordinated system. The
instruments function as designed only when the rest of the stack operates
correctly.

### PCA ↔ DoDA

DoDA signal ingestion identifies constraints and directs instrument selection.
DoDA milestone verification gates tranche disbursement. DoDA certifies exit
conditions and publishes portfolio performance.

The critical design feature: DoDA certifies reality independently. It does not
evaluate whether PCA's investment thesis was correct — that is PCA's
accountability. DoDA determines whether predefined performance conditions have
been met. This separation prevents the capital institution from grading its own
work.

### PCA ↔ RMC

Regulatory friction is a primary driver of manufacturing investment risk.
Unpredictable permitting timelines, sequential agency reviews, and indefinite
interconnection queues force PCA to deploy larger first-loss tranches and higher
minority equity positions to compensate for avoidable procedural dysfunction.
This is waste.

The RMC addresses this directly. Manufacturing RMS deployments compress
procedural timelines, parallelize agency reviews, and create version-controlled
permitting pathways. When RMC is functioning, PCA can deploy smaller, more
targeted instruments because regulatory risk has been structurally reduced rather
than subsidized.

PCA incorporates RMC procedural friction metrics into its risk modeling.
Corridors with active RMS deployments receive more favorable instrument terms.
This creates a direct incentive for corridors to participate in regulatory
modernization without mandating it.

### PCA ↔ Execution Corps

PCA capital officers forward-deploy into corridor Execution Corps teams. They
coordinate capital timing with execution sequencing — they do not direct the
Corps. Infrastructure debt disburses when infrastructure milestones are achieved.
Tooling finance releases when facilities are permitted and ready. Offtake
commitments activate when production capacity is certified.

Capital follows execution. PCA does not pre-position capital in anticipation of
execution. This prevents the common failure mode where public capital is deployed
before the surrounding system is ready and sits idle or is absorbed into overhead.

### PCA ↔ Workforce and Housing Systems

Manufacturing capital instruments are only effective if labor is available and
housing is affordable. PCA deployment signals activate workforce pipeline and
housing overlay systems simultaneously. The Industrial Signal Authority receives
PCA deployment notifications and triggers sector-specific training activation.
Housing density overlays are triggered by corridor activation, not by population
pressure appearing after the fact.

The sequencing principle: PCA deploys capital into production facilities; other
systems deploy capacity into the surrounding ecosystem simultaneously. No
instrument deploys in isolation from the stack.

### PCA ↔ Congressional Authorization

Congress sets the capital ceiling, eligible objectives, risk tolerance bands,
and time horizons for each sectoral portfolio. PCA allocates within those bounds
without requiring project-level congressional approval — which is the source of
micromanagement dysfunction in existing programs.

Congressional oversight focuses on portfolio-level performance: crowd-in ratios,
exit rates, recycling velocity, and DoDA-certified outcome achievement. Not on
individual investment decisions. This is the governance model that makes
disciplined portfolio management possible within a democratic system.

## VII. Guardrails and Failure Mode Prevention

### Permanent Subsidy Capture

**Risk:** Political constituencies form around PCA-supported industries.
Underperforming investments persist because exit would harm those constituencies.

**Prevention:** Exit conditions are contractually binding and DoDA-certified —
not subject to political discretion. Loss events are transparent and treated as
expected portfolio outcomes within approved bands, removing the political cost
of disciplined exit.

### Champion Selection and Rent-Seeking

**Risk:** Firms compete for PCA capital through political relationships rather
than investment merit.

**Prevention:** Instrument deployment is constraint-driven and DoDA-verified.
Corridor viability scores determine deployment priority. All investment terms,
valuations, and selection criteria are publicly disclosed. PCA officers are
subject to cooling-off periods and conflict-of-interest rules from the Execution
Corps specification.

### Market Crowding-Out

**Risk:** PCA capital displaces private investment rather than catalyzing it.

**Prevention:** Mandatory co-investment requirements mean PCA cannot deploy
without private capital alongside. Crowd-in ratio monitoring detects displacement
early. Instruments are sized to the constraint, not the total investment
opportunity. If private capital is available without PCA support, PCA does not
deploy.

### Scope and Mission Creep

**Risk:** PCA expands instrument menu, sector eligibility, or capital envelope
beyond authorization through incremental precedent.

**Prevention:** PCA may not invent new instruments or expand eligibility without
congressional reauthorization. All instruments are defined in this framework.
The Execution Corps specification's prohibition on in-place mandate expansion
applies in full.

### Administrative Capture

**Risk:** Sophisticated private actors exploit information asymmetries to extract
favorable terms from PCA officers.

**Prevention:** Instrument terms are standardized and published. PCA does not
negotiate bespoke deals — it deploys standardized instruments within predefined
parameter bands. DoDA independent verification prevents private actors from
controlling the information that gates their own disbursements.

## VIII. The Political Argument

This framework exists in a contested political environment. Critics on the right
argue that public capital distorts markets and picks winners. Critics on the left
argue that market-rate return requirements make public capital inaccessible to
the sectors that need it most. Both critiques contain real observations.

The PCA manufacturing framework is a direct response to both.

**To the market distortion critique:** Every instrument in this stack is designed
to make markets work, not to replace them. First-loss tranches exist because
private capital cannot price strategic risk — not because government should own
factories. Minority equity positions exist because coordination failures produce
underinvestment — not because government should direct manufacturing strategy.
Exit is automatic, return is capped, and ownership is temporary. When the
instrument works, private capital takes over. PCA's success is measured by how
quickly and completely it can exit, not by how much it owns.

**To the access critique:** The crowding-in structure explicitly targets the
categories where private capital fails most severely — first-mover positions,
high-uncertainty frontiers, shared infrastructure no single firm will build. The
SME supplier tooling finance instrument specifically targets firms least able to
access capital markets. Cluster infrastructure debt finances the public goods
that make entire regions viable, not just anchor firms that would locate there
anyway.

**To both:** The alternative is not a market without public capital. It is a
market with public capital deployed through tax expenditures that are invisible,
unaccountable, permanent, and measured by no one. The PCA framework makes public
capital visible, accountable, time-bound, and measured against published
performance standards. Whatever one's view of industrial policy, the transparent
version is better than the opaque one.

## IX. Success Metrics

**Portfolio-level:**
- Private capital crowd-in ratio (target: ≥4:1; published quarterly)
- Capital recycling rate (exits redeployed as share of total portfolio)
- Portfolio return rate (financial returns relative to invested capital)
- Loss rate (within approved band; published symmetrically with gains)

**Deployment-level:**
- Time from constraint identification to instrument deployment
- Milestone achievement rate across active instruments
- Exit rate (share of instruments that have exited versus extended)
- DoDA certification rate (predefined conditions independently verified)

**System-level:**
- Manufacturing capacity added in Alliance-critical domains (vs. N+2 targets)
- Time-to-deployment reduction in RMS corridors
- Corridor tier advancement rate
- IDP compliance improvement across Alliance-Critical Domains

---

## Closing

The PCA manufacturing instruments framework is a proof of concept for how
government capital should work: targeted at specific failure modes, structured
to attract private capital rather than replace it, disciplined by performance
requirements that gate continued deployment, and designed to exit once the
market can stand on its own.

The alternative — permanent subsidy, regardless of performance, with no exit
discipline and no crowding-in requirement — is not a neutral baseline. It is an
active choice that produces fiscal drag, market distortion, and industrial
results that disappear when the political will to fund them does.

This framework chooses differently.

Capital is catalytic, not permanent.
Ownership is temporary, not entrenched.
Exit is success, not failure.
Markets are the destination, not the obstacle.

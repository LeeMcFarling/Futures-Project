## Purpose

This document defines a pilot framework for testing whether land value
appreciation generated by coordinated industrial corridor deployment can
be partially captured and distributed to existing residents — before
displacement pressure materializes, and in forms that convert rising
land values from a cost into a benefit for the people who live there.

This is an experiment, not a proven model. The instruments described
here are hypotheses about what might work. They are structured as
quasi-experimental pilots with control regions, defined success and
failure criteria, and explicit sunset conditions. If they work at
corridor scale, they scale. If they don't, they stop and the lessons
inform a redesign. The framework does not assume its own success.

The underlying problem is real and well-documented. Industrial corridors
increase land values as a direct consequence of system design —
infrastructure investment, job creation, population inflow, and demand
concentration are all value-generating events. Without deliberate
intervention, that value accrues to landowners and external capital
while existing residents absorb cost increases without corresponding
gains. The result is the standard gentrification cycle: growth arrives,
costs rise, long-term residents are displaced, political opposition to
further development intensifies, and the industrial strategy that
created the growth becomes politically unsustainable.

The objective is to test whether that cycle can be interrupted — not
by suppressing growth, but by ensuring that existing residents are
participants in it rather than externalities of it.

---

## Problem Statement

Land value appreciation is a deterministic outcome of successful
corridor deployment. The causal chain is straightforward:

> infrastructure investment → industrial activity → job creation
> → population inflow → housing demand → land appreciation

This is not a side effect. It is the mechanism by which corridor
investment produces economic value. The question is not whether
appreciation will occur — it will — but who captures it.

Without deliberate structure, the answer is: whoever holds land
before the appreciation event. That is typically not long-term
residents, who are renters or modest homeowners with limited
capital to hold through appreciation cycles, and not local small
businesses, who face rent increases that compress or eliminate
their margins.

Traditional policy responses have poor track records:

**Rent control** suppresses supply formation, creates insider-outsider
dynamics between protected and unprotected tenants, and does not
address displacement of homeowners or small businesses.

**Affordable housing set-asides** create some protected units but at
the cost of raising market-rate development costs, and do not provide
residents with any participation in upside — just protection from
downside.

**Community benefit agreements** are negotiated project by project,
inconsistently enforced, and do not scale to corridor-level deployment.

None of these approaches converts existing residents from victims of
appreciation into participants in it. This pilot tests whether that
conversion is achievable in practice.

---

## Strategic Frame: Inverted Gentrification

The organizing concept is inverted gentrification: growth is not
constrained, but its benefits are broadened. Appreciation is not
prevented; it is shared.

Three principles govern pilot design:

**Participation over protection.** The goal is not to shield residents
from market forces but to give them a stake in the outcome. A resident
who benefits from rising land values has different incentives toward
continued development than one who is harmed by them. Political
legitimacy for industrial expansion is more durable when it is built
on genuine shared benefit than on managed harm reduction.

**Early entry, before the bonanza.** The entire mechanism depends on
PCA entering land positions — and residents receiving participation
rights — before significant appreciation occurs. Value that has already
been captured cannot be redistributed. The pilot tests whether early
entry is operationally achievable given the lead times of corridor
activation, and whether early participation rights are meaningful enough
to retain residents through the appreciation cycle.

**Non-distortion of supply.** Every instrument in this pilot is designed
to operate alongside aggressive housing supply expansion, not as a
substitute for it. Supply expansion is the primary affordability lever.
Participation mechanisms are the equity layer. If the pilot instruments
are found to suppress supply formation or increase construction costs
materially, they are redesigned or discontinued. Supply and
participation are complements, not tradeoffs.

---

## The Timing Problem

Before describing the instruments, one structural challenge requires
explicit acknowledgment: the primary equity instrument — the corridor
equity pool — pays out at liquidity events that may be 7–10 years away.
The displacement pressure it is designed to address arrives in years
2–4.

This creates a risk window where residents feel cost pressure before
they see any benefit, political support weakens before the system
proves itself, and the narrative around the pilot is set by early
cost increases rather than eventual equity outcomes.

This is not a fatal design flaw — equity credits that compound with
appreciation are structurally superior to small cash distributions
that are quickly consumed. But the timing gap is real and requires
a partial bridge.

The pilot therefore includes a thin interim distribution layer —
described under Instrument 1b — that provides a modest periodic
signal of participation without converting the equity pool into
a cash distribution program. The objective is to smooth the
experience curve in early phases, not to replace the liquidity-event
structure.

The 24-month operational review is also designed with this risk in
mind. Take-up rates, early distribution signals, and supply neutrality
data are surfaced at 24 months specifically to establish a factual
baseline before the narrative around the pilot solidifies. If those
early signals are positive, they become the political foundation for
the longer-horizon equity outcomes. If they are weak, the design
is adjusted before the pilot is locked in for the full evaluation
period.

## Pilot Instrument Set

The pilot tests six instrument clusters simultaneously across
designated corridor zones. Each addresses a different aspect of
the participation problem. They are designed to work together but
are evaluated independently so that instruments that work can be
retained while those that don't can be discontinued without
collapsing the whole.

---

### Instrument 1: PCA Early Land Entry and Corridor Equity Pool

**The hypothesis:** If PCA acquires strategic land positions in
designated corridor zones before industrial deployment begins —
and before major appreciation — a defined share of realized
appreciation can be allocated to a resident equity pool without
requiring value that has already accrued to be clawed back.

**Structure:**

PCA acquires or co-develops parcels in designated corridor zones
at pre-activation prices, consistent with its existing land
stabilization authority under the cluster infrastructure financing
framework. Acquisitions are tied to validated corridor activation
plans — this is not speculative land banking.

As corridor development proceeds and PCA positions are realized
through sale, long-term lease, or refinancing, a defined percentage
of the appreciation gain — the delta between acquisition cost and
realized value — is allocated to a corridor-level resident equity
pool rather than recycled entirely into the PCA capital portfolio.

The equity pool is denominated in corridor land value index units.
The index is based on a hybrid of two inputs: PCA's own realized
land gains from corridor positions (which are auditable and concrete)
and a DoDA-maintained assessed corridor land value index updated
quarterly from transaction records and appraisal data within the zone.
The hybrid approach prevents the pool from being entirely dependent
on PCA transaction timing while keeping it grounded in verified
realized value rather than modeled projections. Residents receive
participation units that track the pool's performance. Units convert
to cash at defined liquidity events: when the resident sells their
home, exits a lease, or at defined pool distribution windows.

**Why equity credits rather than cash distributions:** Cash
distributions are small relative to appreciation-driven cost
increases and are quickly consumed. Equity credits that convert
at liquidity events give residents a meaningful asset that grows
with the corridor — the objective is to put residents on the same
side of the appreciation trade as landowners, not to give them
a consolation payment.

**Instrument 1b: Interim Advance Layer**

To bridge the timing gap between early displacement pressure and
eventual liquidity-event payouts, the pool distributes a thin
periodic advance against accrued equity.

When the pool's performance band crosses a defined threshold —
meaning appreciable value has been confirmed in the corridor —
eligible residents receive a modest annual distribution, sized
at a defined fraction of their accrued unit value. This is not
a separate distribution program; it is a partial early realization
of equity already accrued, with the balance retained in the pool
for the liquidity-event conversion.

The advance is small by design. Its function is signal, not
substance: it demonstrates to residents in years 2–4 that the
system is producing real value, maintains political engagement
before the full equity outcome is realized, and reduces the
behavioral risk of residents making short-term decisions
(accepting buyouts, moving before equity vests) that forfeit
their long-term position.

Advance amounts are subject to a dual cap to prevent the
instrument from drifting into quasi-income support: advances
may not exceed a defined percentage of the resident's accrued
unit value, and they may not in aggregate exceed a defined
percentage of the pool's realized gains over the prior
measurement period. Both caps must be satisfied simultaneously.
This means the pool cannot distribute advances against
unrealized appreciation or future expected gains — only
against value that has already been confirmed in the index.
The pool cannot pay what it has not yet earned.

### Instrument 2: Right of First Refusal on New Housing Buildouts

**The hypothesis:** Mid-rise density development — the primary
housing form in corridor zones — increases the total number of
units in a given area. That expansion of supply is the primary
affordability mechanism. The ROFR instrument does not create
below-market units or restrict what developers can charge. It
designates a defined tranche of units in each new development
as available first to verified existing residents, at the same
market price, before those units are offered to the broader
market.

**Structure:**

New residential developments within designated corridor zones
that receive PCA infrastructure support or RMS regulatory
acceleration designate approximately one-fifth of units —
a ROFR tranche — as available to verified existing residents
during a defined pre-marketing window, typically 60–90 days
before public offering.

The ROFR tranche is priced at the developer's pre-marketing
valuation — fair value before external demand is layered on
through the public sales process. This is not a subsidy or
a below-market set-aside. It is first access at a price that
does not yet incorporate the demand premium generated by the
influx of buyers the corridor activation will attract.

The one-fifth tranche is the operational parameter being
tested. It is large enough to be meaningful — in a 200-unit
mid-rise, 40 units are ROFR-eligible — without being so large
that it creates developer pricing distortions or extends the
pre-marketing window in ways that affect project economics.

**This is not a low-income housing program.** Eligible
residents span all income levels within the corridor zone.
Within the ROFR window, lower-income residents are sequenced
earlier and receive first access within the tranche, but the
instrument is not means-tested. A moderate-income long-term
renter who wants to become a homeowner in the neighborhood
they already occupy is exactly the target participant.

Residency verification uses SSI-based identity and address
verification. Tenure weighting determines sequencing within
the window. ROFR units carry owner-occupancy requirements
and resale restrictions for a 3–5 year holding period to
prevent immediate flipping.

**What the pilot tests:** Take-up rates among eligible
residents; whether the one-fifth tranche parameter creates
developer friction or is absorbed within normal pre-marketing
variation; and whether ROFR participation measurably shifts
homeownership rates among long-term corridor residents
relative to control regions.

### Instrument 3: Rent-to-Downpayment Conversion

**The hypothesis:** Long-term renters in corridor zones have
absorbed cost increases as appreciation built around them.
A portion of their rent history should be convertible into
homeownership equity — not as charity but as recognition
that long-term tenancy in an appreciating corridor has
contributed to neighborhood stability in ways the market
does not price.

**Structure:**

Participating rental developments within corridor zones —
those receiving PCA financing, RMS overlay, or cluster
infrastructure support — offer a rent-to-downpayment
conversion option for qualifying long-term tenants.

A defined percentage of monthly rent paid over a minimum
qualifying period accrues as downpayment credits toward
a unit purchase. Credits accumulate in a resident savings
account administered through SSI/UPI rails and are portable
within the corridor zone — if the resident purchases a unit
from any participating developer, not just their current
landlord, the credits apply.

Credits are not transferable outside the housing purchase
context and cannot be cashed out. This keeps the instrument
focused on converting rent expenditure into ownership equity
rather than functioning as a general income supplement.

Participation is a condition of PCA support for rental
developments, not a voluntary program. Developers who
accept public support participate. Those who do not are
not penalized — they simply do not access the public
support that is the instrument's incentive lever.

---

### Instrument 4: External Demand Conversion Surcharge

**The hypothesis:** When a major external demand generator —
a stadium, entertainment district, tourism zone, or large
employer campus — is introduced into or adjacent to an
existing residential corridor, it generates demand-driven
appreciation that existing residents had no role in creating
and receive no benefit from. A modest transaction surcharge
on designated external-demand activities, with proceeds
distributed to verified residents, converts extraction
into participation.

**The Inglewood parallel:** SoFi Stadium illustrates the
failure mode: a major demand generator arrives, land values
spike, longtime residents are displaced, and the economic
activity flows to external investors rather than the
community that absorbed the disruption. A 1% surcharge on
stadium, venue, and high-demand commercial transactions
within a defined radius, distributed to verified residents
via digital rails, would have materially changed that outcome
without complex legal structures or suppression of the
development.

**Structure:**

In corridor zones where a major external demand generator
is introduced, approximately 1% applies to qualifying
transactions within a defined radius: event tickets,
venue admissions, hotel stays, and high-turnover food
and beverage within the demand zone.

Proceeds pool and distribute quarterly to verified primary
residents within the corridor zone via SSI/UPI rails, with
modest tenure weighting. The surcharge is disclosed at
point of transaction — visitors to a stadium in a
residential corridor should know that a small fraction
of their purchase flows to the people who live with the
disruption they create.

### Instrument 5: Small Business Participation Layer

**The gap:** Small businesses are more liquidity-constrained
than residents, more immediately exposed to rent shocks, and
more politically visible when displaced. The previous
instrument set stabilizes households but leaves neighborhood
commercial fabric exposed — which feeds back into political
perception of failure even when household outcomes are good.

**The hypothesis:** A commercial lease stabilization and
participation hybrid, parallel to the residential instruments,
can protect small business operators from displacement while
giving them a participation stake in corridor appreciation.

**Structure:**

Small businesses with verified long-term presence in the
corridor zone — defined as operating within the zone for
a minimum period before activation — receive two protections:

*Commercial ROFR on new ground-floor space:* New mixed-use
developments within the corridor zone designate a defined
tranche of ground-floor commercial space as available first
to verified existing small businesses at pre-marketing rates,
structured identically to the residential ROFR.

*Commercial equity participation:* Small businesses meeting
tenure thresholds receive a reduced-rate allocation of
corridor equity pool units, sized as a fraction of the
residential allocation but subject to the same liquidity-event
conversion structure. Eligibility attaches to the operating
business entity — specifically the entity holding the
commercial lease or occupancy agreement within the corridor
zone — not to the beneficial owner. This means equity
allocation does not follow an owner who sells the business,
restructures into a new legal entity, or relocates operations
outside the zone. Entity restructuring that does not change
physical presence and operational continuity in the corridor
does not affect eligibility; restructuring that terminates
the qualifying occupancy does.

*Interim cost stabilization:* For small businesses in leased
spaces that are not part of PCA-supported developments, a
corridor-level commercial stabilization fund — funded from
the reinvestment pool — provides transitional support for
lease increases above a defined annual threshold during the
ramp phase of corridor activation. This is a time-limited
bridge, not a permanent subsidy: it phases out as the
business either relocates into new corridor space, exercises
its commercial ROFR, or achieves sufficient revenue growth
to absorb market rates.

**What the pilot tests:** Whether commercial equity allocation
meaningfully affects small business retention rates; whether
commercial ROFR take-up is viable given the capital requirements
of small business lease commitments; and whether transitional
cost stabilization is administratively feasible without
becoming a permanent support program.

---

### Instrument 6: Land Value Capture for System Reinvestment

A defined percentage of PCA land development gains — separate
from the individual equity pool allocation — is directed into
a corridor reinvestment fund governed by a community board
with resident representation. The fund is allocated to:
housing supply expansion (priority use), transit and public
space improvements, neighborhood service capacity, and
small business stabilization support.

The reinvestment fund is tied explicitly to maintaining the
conditions that make the corridor viable for residents.
Allocations are published and audited by DoDA.

## Boundary Design and Spillover Management

Corridor zone boundaries are a design variable, not a fixed
parameter. Land markets do not respect administrative lines.
Appreciation outside the boundary — in adjacent areas that
receive indirect benefit from corridor investment without
participation mechanisms — produces displacement spillover
and political complaints from residents who are "adjacent
but excluded."

The pilot tests two boundary approaches across different
sites:

**Hard boundary with revision triggers:** A defined corridor
zone with explicit triggers for boundary expansion if DoDA
detects appreciation spillover in adjacent areas above a
defined threshold. Boundary revisions are administrative,
not legislative, and activate automatically when spillover
thresholds are crossed.

**Gradient model:** A core corridor zone with full
participation instrument coverage and an adjacent buffer
zone with a reduced participation layer — equity pool
participation at a lower allocation rate, no ROFR tranche,
but commercial stabilization and surcharge distribution
where applicable.

DoDA measures spillover appreciation in adjacent areas for
all pilot sites regardless of boundary model, so the
comparison between approaches can be evaluated. The pilot
treats the choice between hard-boundary and gradient models
as an empirical question — neither approach is assumed
superior in advance. The pilot is designed to produce
evidence on which performs better across the dimensions
of resident participation, spillover containment, and
administrative viability, with that evidence informing
the standard approach for any subsequent scaling.

## Developer Equilibrium and Dominant Path Design

The pilot instruments impose constraints on developers who
accept PCA support. The risk is market bifurcation: high-quality
developers opt out of the program, build unconstrained premium
supply, and the participation instruments apply only to a
"program-compliant" tier that is slower, smaller, and less
attractive to the residents it is designed to help.

This risk is real and must be managed through instrument
design rather than mandates.

The principle is dominant path formation: PCA support, RMS
overlay, infrastructure coordination, demand signal access,
and interconnection priority should collectively make
participation the easiest and most economically attractive
way to build in corridor zones — not the most regulated one.
If the public support package is sufficiently valuable,
developers participate not because they are required to but
because opting out means building without the stack that
makes corridor development viable.

This means:

- RMS procedural acceleration must genuinely compress
  timelines, not just promise to. Developers model timeline
  risk carefully; if RMS delivers, participation becomes
  a clear economic benefit.
- Infrastructure coordination must be real — power, water,
  and logistics capacity committed before construction, not
  promised for later.
- ROFR tranche and rent-to-downpayment requirements must
  be scoped so that they do not materially affect project
  economics relative to the value of the public support
  package.

DoDA tracks developer participation rates by corridor and
by project type. If high-margin project categories show
systematically lower participation rates than lower-margin
categories, the instrument terms for high-margin projects
are reviewed — not to reduce standards but to ensure the
public support package remains sufficiently attractive
relative to constraints. To be direct: if developer
participation rates in strong market segments remain low
after public support is offered, the public support package
is mispriced relative to its obligations and must be
recalibrated. The failure is in the package design, not
in developer behavior.

Market bifurcation is treated as a system design failure,
not developer misbehavior.

## Eligibility Framework

**Who qualifies:** Verified primary residents and long-term
small business operators within the designated corridor zone.
Workers who commute into the corridor but do not live there
are not eligible for residential instruments.

**Verification:** SSI-based identity and residency verification
using utility records, lease history, and tax filings.
Low-burden for genuine long-term residents; high-friction
for new entrants attempting to qualify retroactively.

**Tenure weighting:** All instruments apply tenure weighting.
Long-term residents receive larger participation shares.
The weighting recognizes that longer-term residents have
absorbed more transition cost and contributed more to
neighborhood stability — it is not punitive toward newer
residents but it is directionally honest.

**Income sequencing within ROFR:** Lower-income residents
are sequenced earlier within ROFR windows. This is not
means-testing — it is access sequencing within an
instrument that is available to all eligible residents.

## Pilot Governance and Measurement

**Pilot selection:** Two to four corridors representing
different typologies: a manufacturing-led corridor, a
mixed-use revitalization corridor, and if possible one
with an external demand generator to test Instrument 4.
Control corridors are established for each site. DoDA
establishes baselines before activation.

**The 24-month review as narrative defense:**

The 24-month review is not a formal outcome evaluation —
it is an early signal review designed specifically to
surface data before the narrative around the pilot is
set by external framing.

At 24 months, DoDA publishes:
- Take-up rates by instrument (are residents actually using this?)
- Early interim distribution amounts (is the pool producing real value?)
- Supply formation rate comparison to control corridors
  (is the pilot suppressing housing?)
- Developer participation rates by project type

These are not final outcome measures. They are process
indicators that establish a factual baseline. If they
are positive, they become the evidence base for the
longer-horizon equity outcomes. If they are weak,
the design is adjusted before the evaluation period
is locked.

The 24-month publication is unconditional — it occurs
regardless of whether signals are positive or negative.
The political defense of this pilot is its measurement
transparency, not its framing. Data that is withheld
or delayed becomes evidence of concealment. Data that
is published promptly, even when mixed, becomes evidence
of honest evaluation.

**Formal evaluation windows:** 48 months for initial
outcome assessment. 72 months for scaling or sunset
determination.

**Success criteria for scaling:**
- Long-term resident retention rates measurably higher
  than control corridors
- Net wealth change for long-term residents is positive
  net of increased housing cost exposure — meaning
  participation gains must exceed the cost-of-living
  increase attributable to corridor appreciation, not
  merely be positive in nominal terms. A small nominal
  gain against a large cost increase does not constitute
  success. The threshold is whether residents are
  meaningfully better off in real terms relative to
  what they would have experienced without the instruments.
- Housing supply formation rates not materially lower
  than control corridors
- Take-up rates indicate instruments are accessible and used

**Failure criteria for redesign or sunset:**
- Supply formation materially lower than controls
  (instrument is suppressing housing)
- Distribution amounts demonstrably too small to affect
  resident economic outcomes at any reasonable pool size
- Administrative costs consume a large share of value
  distributed
- Market bifurcation: high-quality developers systematically
  opt out of program participation

Individual instruments can fail without the whole pilot
failing. DoDA evaluates each instrument independently.

## Anti-Gaming Design

**Retroactive residency claims:** Tenure requirements
established before corridor activation announcement;
historical SSI records used for verification; residency
established after a defined cutoff does not qualify
for baseline allocation.

**ROFR flipping:** ROFR units carry owner-occupancy
requirements and resale restrictions for 3–5 years.
Units sold within the holding period return the ROFR
premium to the corridor equity pool.

**Participation unit concentration:** Hard caps on
unit holdings per SSI-verified identity; no transferability
outside defined liquidity windows.

**Developer non-participation:** ROFR and rent-to-downpayment
are conditions of PCA support access, not voluntary. Developers
who opt out of participation opt out of the public support
package.

---

## Interaction with Housing Supply

If participation mechanisms suppress housing supply formation
the instruments have failed regardless of any other outcome.

At 24 months, if supply suppression evidence is detected
in pilot corridors relative to controls, implicated
instruments are suspended pending redesign. This is
non-negotiable and is built into the pilot governance
structure, not left to discretionary judgment.

---

## Success Metrics

**Primary outcomes:**
- Long-term resident retention rate: pilot vs. control
- Net wealth change for residents with 3+ years pre-activation
  tenure: pilot vs. control
- Homeownership rate change among long-term residents:
  pilot vs. control
- Small business retention rate: pilot vs. control

**Instrument-level:**
- Equity pool value at liquidity windows relative to
  corridor appreciation
- ROFR take-up rate and tranche utilization
- Rent-to-downpayment accrual and conversion rates
- External demand surcharge distribution relative to
  rent increases
- Interim advance distribution amounts and timing
- Small business commercial ROFR take-up rate

**Supply and developer behavior:**
- Housing unit formation rate: pilot vs. control
- Developer participation rate by project type
- Market bifurcation indicator (premium vs. program supply ratio)

**System health:**
- Corridor political support indicators
- Anti-gaming violation rate
- Administrative cost as share of value distributed
- Boundary spillover appreciation in adjacent areas

## Closing

The standard gentrification cycle is not a law of nature.
It is the outcome of a system that captures appreciation
privately while distributing costs broadly. That structure
is a choice, and different choices produce different outcomes.

This pilot tests whether the choice can be made differently
at corridor scale — not through suppression of growth but
through deliberate design of who participates in it.

The honest answer is: we don't know if it works at this
scale. The instruments are grounded in real precedents —
community land trusts, ROFR programs, value capture
mechanisms in other contexts — but they have not been
tested in the specific context of rapid industrially-driven
corridor appreciation in the United States.

That is why this is a pilot. The measurement framework is
designed to produce real answers, not confirm assumed
conclusions. If the instruments work, they scale. If
specific instruments fail, the successful ones continue.
If the framework fails to produce meaningful resident
economic outcomes, it stops.

The 24-month data is the first real test. Not of outcomes
— those take longer — but of whether the system is
functioning as designed, whether residents are participating,
and whether supply is being formed. If that data is strong,
the pilot has earned its longer evaluation window. If it
is weak, the pilot adjusts rather than continuing on
theoretical grounds.

What is not optional is trying. The alternative — deploying
industrial corridors without any resident participation
mechanism — has a clear historical record. It produces
displacement, political backlash, and corridor instability
that undermines the industrial strategy itself.

This pilot is a bet that it can be done better. The results
will tell us if we're right.

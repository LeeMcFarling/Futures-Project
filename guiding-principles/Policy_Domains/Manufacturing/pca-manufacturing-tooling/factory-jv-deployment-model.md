## Purpose

Factory Joint Ventures (JVs) are the deployment vehicle for manufacturing
capacity in categories where the coordination failure is too complex for a
single capital instrument to resolve. Where the PCA instruments framework
defines the financial tools, this document defines how those tools are
assembled into a single project structure that aligns capital providers,
operators, suppliers, infrastructure systems, and allied partners around a
specific facility.

The Factory JV is not a general-purpose financing vehicle. It is a
constraint-resolution mechanism with a strict lifecycle: initiation,
stabilization, and transition to full private ownership. Public participation
is a temporary scaffold, not a permanent structural element.

---

## Problem Statement

Strategic manufacturing capacity frequently fails to materialize despite clear
long-term demand. The failure is rarely technological or even financial in the
simple sense. It is a coordination breakdown: capital providers wait for
operators to commit, operators wait for infrastructure to be ready,
infrastructure investors wait for demand to be demonstrated, suppliers wait
for anchor manufacturers to confirm volumes, and the whole system waits
indefinitely.

Each actor's hesitation is individually rational. The collective outcome is
persistent undercapacity in sectors the country demonstrably needs.

This failure mode is most acute in:

- first-of-a-kind domestic facilities in categories previously dependent on
  imports
- categories with high capital intensity and long payback periods relative to
  private capital horizons
- sectors where supply chain immaturity creates chicken-and-egg dynamics
  between anchor manufacturers and their supplier ecosystems
- IDP-aligned sectors where alliance resilience requirements create urgency
  that market timing alone will not satisfy

The Factory JV model resolves the coordination failure by providing a single
structure within which all parties can commit simultaneously, with defined
responsibilities, risk allocation, and exit conditions established in advance.

## Deployment Criteria

A Factory JV may only be authorized when all five of the following conditions
are verified by DoDA. These are hard triggers, not guidelines.

**1. Sector Qualification**
The target sector is classified as Alliance-Critical in the Alliance Industrial
Target Map and falls within a Domestic-Critical or Alliance-Distributed domain.
Market-rate or Globally Competitive categories do not qualify.

**2. Capacity Gap Threshold**
Either:
- Domestic or alliance production falls below the N+2 redundancy requirement
  for the domain, as certified by DoDA against Alliance Industrial Target Map
  standards; or
- DoDA identifies active single-node or concentrated dependency risk creating
  measurable cascade exposure across allied industrial systems.

**3. Time-to-Scale Constraint**
Private market deployment timelines, as assessed through DoDA corridor
readiness monitoring and capital market signaling, exceed acceptable thresholds
for system resilience under current threat posture. The urgency assessment is
updated at each quarterly review cycle.

**4. Corridor Readiness**
The target corridor meets minimum viability criteria across power availability,
logistics connectivity, workforce pipeline, housing elasticity, and regulatory
execution capacity. Corridors that do not meet minimum criteria receive
enablement investment first; Factory JV authorization does not substitute for
corridor readiness.

**5. Capital Formation Failure**
Verified inability to secure sufficient private capital under reasonable market
conditions within the required time horizon, demonstrated through market
sounding and DoDA capital availability assessment. If private capital is
available to deploy at adequate scale and speed, a Factory JV is not
authorized — the appropriate response is PCA instrument deployment alongside
private capital, not a JV structure.

If all five conditions are met, the PCA capital board may authorize JV
formation. Authorization is public, documented, and the triggering conditions
are published.

## Structure

Each Factory JV is a project-specific special purpose vehicle (SPV)
incorporating a defined set of participants, a capital stack assembled from
PCA instruments, and a governance structure that preserves private operational
control while maintaining PCA protective rights and DoDA monitoring access.

### Participant Roles

**Public Capital Authority**
Provides structured capital across the instrument stack appropriate to the
constraint profile. Holds minority, non-voting equity where equity is used.
Absorbs defined first-loss exposure where capital market hesitation is the
binding constraint. Does not hold operational authority, management control,
or majority governance rights. PCA's role is financial participation and
protective governance — not management.

**Operator (Anchor Firm)**
The private firm responsible for facility design, construction, commissioning,
and ongoing operations. Holds primary operational control and management
authority. The operator selects site, manages construction contracts, hires
workforce, and runs the facility. PCA does not direct these decisions.

Operator selection is competitive and transparent. PCA does not designate
operators through political relationship or sole-source arrangement. Selection
criteria — operational capability, financial standing, supply chain
integration plan, workforce development commitment — are published in advance.

**Strategic Co-Investors**
May include allied firms participating under Category B alliance coordination
protocols, strategic suppliers seeking production proximity, offtakers seeking
supply security through equity participation, and financial co-investors
providing senior capital above the PCA first-loss position.

Co-investor participation is welcomed and structured to reinforce rather than
complicate the governance and exit design. Allied firm participation is
governed by the Reciprocal Siting Agreements framework and IDP sovereignty
assurance provisions — co-investment does not entail IP transfer or loss of
domestic production authority.

**Supplier Formation Partners**
Key Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers are identified at JV formation and integrated
into the deployment plan from the outset, rather than treated as a downstream
problem to be solved after the anchor facility is built. Supplier credit
facilities from the PCA supplier stack are pre-committed for qualifying
suppliers simultaneously with JV authorization.

### Capital Stack Assembly

The capital stack is assembled from PCA instruments based on the specific
constraint profile of the JV. There is no standard template — the stack is
built to resolve the identified constraints, not to fill a predefined form.

**Typical stack for a first-of-a-kind domestic facility
in a high-uncertainty category:**

| Layer | Instrument | Typical Size | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Senior debt | Private lenders | 50–60% | Primary financing |
| Mezzanine / quasi-equity | Revenue participation | 10–15% | Bridge horizon mismatch |
| First-loss tranche | PCA first-loss | 10–15% | De-risk senior positions |
| Equity | Private equity + PCA minority | 15–20% | Alignment and upside |

**Typical stack for a corridor expansion
in a maturing category:**

| Layer | Instrument | Typical Size | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Senior debt | Private lenders | 65–75% | Primary financing |
| Equity | Private equity majority + PCA minority | 20–25% | Alignment |
| Offtake backstop | PCA offtake compact | Conditional | Demand visibility |

Stack design is approved by the PCA capital board with DoDA constraint
verification. Stacks that allocate excessive public capital relative to the
identified constraint are rejected and resubmitted. The discipline is: minimum
public capital sufficient to resolve the identified failure mode.

## Allied Firm Participation (Category B Systems)

For JVs in Alliance-Distributed domains, allied firm co-investment is actively
encouraged and may be a condition of JV authorization where IDP compliance
requires multi-theater production redundancy.

Allied firm participation operates under the following constraints, consistent
with IDP sovereignty assurance doctrine:

**What is shared:** Production capacity, facility cross-certification,
quality assurance equivalence, and supply chain integration. Allied co-
investors gain production exposure in the US domestic market.

**What is not shared:** Crown-jewel IP, proprietary process architecture,
software and control system source code, and classified technical specifications.
Co-investment is a financial and production relationship, not a technology
transfer.

**Governance:** Allied co-investors hold minority equity positions under the
same non-voting, non-operational constraint as PCA. No allied firm holds
operational control over a domestic facility. Host-nation production authority
is preserved.

**IDP integration:** Allied firm participation in domestic JVs creates
cross-certified production relationships consistent with IDP multi-node
doctrine. The domestic facility and the allied firm's home facility become
mutually recognized production nodes — each capable of serving as backup for
the other under defined contingency conditions, without IP transfer in either
direction.

This structure directly advances the N+2 production node requirement while
strengthening bilateral industrial ties within the alliance.

## Governance Structure

Operational authority resides with the private operator. PCA's governance
role is protective and performance-oriented, not managerial.

### PCA Protective Rights

PCA retains the following governance rights, which may be exercised only
under defined trigger conditions and not as routine management participation:

- Veto on major structural decisions that would impair PCA's capital position
  or violate JV formation conditions (facility sale, change of control,
  fundamental business model shift)
- Step-in rights in the event of operator default or sustained performance
  failure beyond defined thresholds
- Information rights: full access to financial statements, production data,
  cost variance reports, and supply chain integration metrics on a defined
  reporting schedule
- Audit rights: independent verification of reported metrics at defined
  intervals

PCA does not hold:
- Board representation (in voting capacity)
- Authority over hiring, procurement, pricing, or operational decisions
- Approval rights over routine capital expenditures below a defined threshold

### Required Governance Elements

Every Factory JV must include at formation:

- Construction milestone schedule with defined variance tolerance bands
- Production ramp targets with quarterly measurement cadence
- Cost variance reporting with escalation triggers
- Supplier ecosystem formation plan with milestone checkpoints
- DoDA data access agreement covering all performance metrics
- Exit condition definitions with objective verification criteria
- Allied participant protocols (for Category B systems)

## Execution Integration

Factory JVs deploy within the full-stack corridor activation model. JV
authorization does not substitute for the activation stack — it requires it.

### Execution Corps Role

The corridor Execution Corps team is the operational coordination layer
between the JV structure and the surrounding enabling systems. Execution Corps
responsibilities for an active Factory JV:

- Site activation and preparation: zoning confirmation, utility commitments,
  access infrastructure
- Infrastructure sequencing: ensuring power, water, logistics, and
  telecommunications capacity milestones are met ahead of facility construction
  and commissioning schedules
- Regulatory pathway compression: Manufacturing RMS deployment, shot clock
  activation, consolidated environmental review, interconnection acceleration
- Local stakeholder alignment: workforce pipeline activation, housing density
  overlay triggering, municipal coordination
- Supplier ecosystem facilitation: connecting JV supplier formation plan to
  PCA supplier credit facilities and Execution Corps support for Tier 2 and
  Tier 3 firms

The Execution Corps does not manage the facility. It manages the environment
in which the facility can be built and operate. The distinction matters: PCA
and Execution Corps failure to maintain that boundary is the mechanism by which
public industrial intervention drifts into operational control.

### DoDA Monitoring

DoDA maintains continuous real-time monitoring of Factory JV performance
against formation targets. Monitored metrics:

- Time-to-construction-start versus milestone schedule
- Time-to-initial-production versus target
- Cost variance against baseline
- Capacity utilization trajectory against ramp projection
- Supplier network density against formation plan milestones
- Workforce hiring and training achievement
- Housing cost stability in the corridor

DoDA does not manage the JV. It certifies whether performance conditions are
being met and publishes results publicly. The separation between performance
measurement and performance management is structural and maintained as a
design requirement.

## Supplier Ecosystem Integration

The supplier ecosystem is not an afterthought. It is a formation requirement.

A Factory JV that builds an anchor facility without building the surrounding
supplier ecosystem has not solved the industrial capacity problem — it has
created a single-node dependency at the facility level rather than the national
level. The anchor firm becomes dependent on imported inputs, and the cluster
fails to form.

### Supplier Formation Plan Requirements

Every Factory JV must include a Supplier Formation Plan at authorization,
covering:

**Tier mapping:** Identification of all Tier 1 and Tier 2 input categories,
current domestic availability, import dependency levels, and qualification
requirements for domestic sourcing.

**Gap identification:** Explicit identification of supplier gaps — categories
where domestic Tier 1 or Tier 2 supply does not exist or is insufficient —
and a plan for each gap: domestic development, allied sourcing under IDP
protocols, or accepted import dependency with risk acknowledgment.

**Development timeline:** Milestone-based timeline for domestic supplier
development aligned to facility construction and ramp schedule. Suppliers
must be qualified and producing before the anchor facility needs their inputs
at scale.

**PCA supplier credit integration:** Pre-commitment of PCA supplier credit
facilities for qualifying domestic suppliers identified in the gap analysis.
Supplier credit authorization happens concurrently with JV authorization, not
after the anchor facility is built.

**Multi-node sourcing:** Where feasible, Tier 1 and Tier 2 inputs are sourced
from multiple domestic suppliers rather than single-source relationships.
This applies the same no-single-node-dependency logic that IDP uses at the
alliance network level — applied here at the supplier input layer of an
individual JV. Note that this is an operational supply chain practice, not
a replication of the IDP N+2 standard itself, which operates across the
alliance as a whole rather than within any individual facility.

Failure to develop the supplier ecosystem to plan milestones is treated as a
JV performance failure and triggers the same escalation and intervention
protocols as facility construction or production ramp failures.

## Performance Monitoring and Intervention

### Intervention Triggers

DoDA triggers an intervention review when any of the following thresholds
are breached:

| Metric | Trigger Threshold |
|---|---|
| Construction timeline variance | Greater than defined band (set at formation) |
| Cost overrun | Exceeds contingency ceiling without reauthorization |
| Production ramp | Below quarterly trajectory by defined margin |
| Capacity utilization | Below minimum threshold at defined time post-commissioning |
| Supplier formation | Behind milestone by more than one quarter |
| Crowd-in ratio | Falls below 3:1 at any six-month measurement point |

### Intervention Response Ladder

**Level 1 — Performance Review**
DoDA publishes performance assessment. PCA and operator enter formal review
with 60-day remediation plan requirement. No capital restructuring yet.

**Level 2 — Capital Restructuring**
If Level 1 remediation plan is not achieved, PCA may restructure the capital
stack: adjusting tranche timing, tightening performance covenants, or
converting instruments. Operator retains operational control.

**Level 3 — Operator Remediation**
If Level 2 restructuring does not restore trajectory, PCA exercises step-in
rights to require operator remediation or management changes. This is a severe
measure reserved for sustained failure.

**Level 4 — JV Restructuring or Termination**
If no remediation path is viable, the JV is restructured — potentially with
a new operator — or wound down in an orderly fashion. Capital recovery is
maximized within loss tolerance bands. The facility asset is preserved where
possible; the operator relationship is terminated.

Level 4 outcomes are public, documented, and treated as operational data for
improving future JV design — not as political failures to be concealed.

## Exit Framework

Exit is not an endpoint. It is the success condition.

Every Factory JV is authorized with a target exit window — the period within
which PCA expects to substantially exit its capital position. The target exit
window is typically 7–12 years from JV formation, depending on sector maturity
and capital intensity.

### Exit Conditions

Exit proceeds when all of the following are verified by DoDA:

- Sustained operational profitability at or above baseline projections for at
  least four consecutive quarters
- Stable demand visibility through private offtake agreements or demonstrated
  market depth not dependent on PCA offtake compacts
- Supplier ecosystem maturity: Tier 1 and Tier 2 supply domestically available
  and multi-sourced at the levels defined in the Supplier Formation Plan
- Private refinancing available at reasonable market rates, demonstrated
  through market sounding rather than assumed

### Exit Mechanisms

**Operator buyback (preferred):** The operating firm purchases PCA's equity
stake at fair market value, with any premium above PCA's invested capital
subject to the return cap. Buyback terms are pre-negotiated at JV formation
rather than litigated at exit, reducing friction and uncertainty.

**Staged divestment:** Where operator buyback is not feasible, PCA sells its
equity stake in stages to qualified private buyers. Staged divestment prevents
market disruption from a large single-exit position and allows price discovery.

**Private refinancing:** Where PCA holds structured debt instruments, the
operator refinances into private capital markets when commercial rates are
available. PCA debt is retired from refinancing proceeds.

**Revenue participation completion:** Revenue participation instruments
terminate automatically upon reaching the defined return cap. No action
required; the instrument closes by its own terms.

**Time-limit-triggered exit:** If exit conditions are not achieved within the
authorized exit window, DoDA initiates a mandatory exit review. Options are:
operator-initiated buyback at fair value, third-party sale, or conversion of
remaining PCA equity to structured debt with defined amortization. The
objective is capital recovery, not perpetual ownership. This trigger exists
precisely to prevent the drift into permanent public shareholding that has
characterized previous industrial interventions.

### Return Recycling at Exit

Exit proceeds flow into the PCA manufacturing capital recycling pool,
consistent with the capital recycling architecture in the PCA instruments
framework. Returns are not returned to general treasury as one-time revenue
— they redeploy into the next constrained sector or corridor.

The compounding logic: successful JV exits fund the next generation of
first-mover risk absorption, reducing the congressional appropriation
required at the margin over time.

### Exit Transparency

All exits are documented and published:

- exit mechanism used
- exit price and return multiple achieved versus projection
- whether exit conditions were fully met or time-limit-triggered
- capital recycled and redeployment destination
- post-exit facility performance (tracked for two years to verify cluster
  sustainability)

Exit transparency is not optional. The political legitimacy of the entire
model depends on the public being able to see that public capital entered,
performed, and left — rather than quietly becoming permanent ownership.

## Failure Modes and Mitigation

### Sector Misclassification

**Risk:** JVs authorized in categories that did not genuinely require public
intervention, driven by political pressure rather than DoDA-verified criteria.

**Mitigation:** Hard deployment criteria verified by DoDA before authorization.
All five conditions must be met; no waivers on criteria without explicit
congressional authorization. Criteria verification is published. Authorizations
that do not meet criteria can be challenged through audit processes.

### Political Capture of Operator Selection

**Risk:** Anchor firm selection driven by political relationship rather than
operational capability, producing underperforming operators who persist because
replacement is politically costly.

**Mitigation:** Competitive, transparent operator selection with published
criteria. Operator performance is continuously monitored through DoDA with
published metrics. Intervention triggers are objective and DoDA-certified —
operator replacement is a performance management decision, not a political one.

### Supplier Ecosystem Failure

**Risk:** Anchor facility built without corresponding supplier ecosystem,
creating single-node facility dependency and import reliance for inputs.

**Mitigation:** Supplier Formation Plan is a hard requirement at authorization.
Supplier milestone failures trigger the same intervention protocols as facility
failures. PCA supplier credit facilities are authorized concurrently with the
JV, not as a future consideration.

### Exit Drift into Permanent Ownership

**Risk:** PCA equity positions persist beyond the exit window because exit
conditions are never quite met, creating de facto permanent state shareholding
in manufacturing firms.

**Mitigation:** Time-limit-triggered exit is contractually binding. DoDA
initiates the review automatically. Exit mechanisms are pre-negotiated at
formation, not litigated at exit. Congressional reporting on exit rates is
mandatory. Persistent non-exit rates trigger oversight review of whether exit
conditions were properly designed.

### Allied Participant Overreach

**Risk:** Allied firm co-investors use JV participation to acquire leverage
over domestic production decisions or to access IP beyond the co-investment
scope.

**Mitigation:** Allied co-investors hold non-voting, non-operational minority
positions under identical governance constraints to PCA. IP sharing is
contractually prohibited beyond defined cross-certification scope. IDP
sovereignty assurance clause applies. Counterintelligence protocols are
integrated into allied participant agreements consistent with IDP security
standards.

## Relationship to Other Instruments

The Factory JV is the most capital-intensive and operationally complex
intervention in the PCA manufacturing stack. It is not the default.

**Use Factory JVs when:** Coordination failure across multiple parties makes
a single instrument insufficient. The scale of the capacity gap and the
complexity of the supply chain require a structure that aligns all parties
simultaneously.

**Use PCA instruments directly when:** The binding constraint is addressable
by a single instrument — a first-loss tranche that unlocks private capital,
tooling finance for a supplier, or an offtake compact that provides demand
visibility. Most manufacturing interventions should not require JV structures.

**Use supplier credit facilities separately when:** Supplier ecosystem gaps
can be addressed independently of anchor facility development, particularly
in categories where anchor manufacturers exist but the supply chain beneath
them is weak.

The Factory JV is the heavy instrument. It is authorized when the constraint
is complex enough to require it, and not before.

## Success Metrics

**Deployment metrics:**
- Time-to-construction-start versus industry baseline
- Time-to-initial-production versus formation target
- Cost variance against baseline at commissioning
- Crowd-in ratio at formation and at full deployment

**Operational metrics:**
- Capacity utilization at 12, 24, and 48 months post-commissioning
- Supplier ecosystem density versus Supplier Formation Plan milestones
- Workforce hiring and training achievement versus pipeline targets

**System metrics:**
- N+2 redundancy achievement in target domain (DoDA-certified)
- Cascade risk reduction in dependent downstream domains
- Alliance compliance improvement for the relevant Alliance Industrial
  Target Map domain

**Exit metrics:**
- Exit achieved within authorized window (binary)
- Return multiple achieved versus projection
- Capital recycled and time-to-redeployment
- Post-exit facility performance at 12 and 24 months

## Closing

Factory Joint Ventures exist to resolve the coordination failures that prevent
manufacturing capacity from forming when it should. They are not a statement
that government should own factories. They are a mechanism for getting the
right parties into the same structure at the same time, with aligned
incentives and defined exit conditions, so that the coordination problem
dissolves and private capital can take over.

The lifecycle is the point.

Initiation: resolve the coordination failure.
Stabilization: demonstrate the economics.
Transition: exit and recycle.

What remains is a functioning facility, a developed supplier ecosystem, a
trained workforce, and a corridor that has demonstrated it can absorb
manufacturing investment. Public capital made that possible. Private capital
now sustains it.

That is the outcome. Not ownership. Not dependency. Capacity.

## Purpose

This document translates Integrated Defense Partnership (IDP) capability requirements into domestic corridor prioritization logic. It defines which industrial capabilities the alliance must collectively maintain, maps those requirements to the resilience standards established in IDP doctrine, and aligns domestic manufacturing corridor strategy accordingly.

This is the bridge between what the alliance needs and where and how the United States builds it.

It is not a standalone industrial policy framework. It is a targeting layer that directs PCA capital deployment, corridor viability scoring, and Execution Corps engagement toward alliance-critical production nodes.

---

## Problem Statement

IDP doctrine establishes binding resilience standards for defense-relevant industrial capacity: N+2 production nodes, surge capability without greenfield build, tooling replication over factory preservation, treaty-bound offtake, and strict chokepoint separation. These requirements are operationally precise.

Domestic manufacturing corridor strategy establishes where and how to build industrial capacity: corridor viability criteria, PCA instruments, Execution Corps deployment, RMS pilots, and Dutch Disease mitigation. This execution architecture is institutionally mature.

The gap is the translation layer between the two. Without it, IDP compliance requirements do not drive domestic corridor prioritization, and corridor investments are not validated against alliance resilience standards. Industrial policy and alliance industrial security operate in parallel rather than in coordination.

This document closes that gap.

---

## Translation Methodology

Each Alliance-Critical Domain is assessed across four dimensions:

**IDP Resilience Requirements**
The binding standards from IDP doctrine that apply to this domain: N+2 node requirements, surge thresholds, chokepoint separation standards, theater distribution rules, and offtake compact eligibility.

A note on N+2: this is an alliance network standard, not a facility-level specification. N+2 means the alliance as a whole maintains at least two additional capable production nodes beyond minimum operational requirement, distributed across independent theaters. It does not mean any individual facility or corridor must independently replicate its own capacity. US domestic corridors contribute nodes to the alliance network — they are not each required to be self-redundant. The distinction matters for how corridor investment is sized and justified: corridors are built to serve the alliance network, not to be standalone resilience islands.

**Designation Logic**
Whether the domain qualifies as Alliance-Critical under the Critical Materials & Energy framework (any two of six criteria, or any one at elevated threat posture). Designation logic is applied explicitly, not assumed.

**Domestic Corridor Assignment**
Which domestic corridors are primary candidates for production node development, based on the Industrial Corridor Viability Criteria. Assignments are tiered: Deployment Ready, Enablement Required, or Strategic Long-Term.

**PCA Instrument Alignment**
Which Public Capital Authority instruments are appropriate for this domain: minority equity, first-loss tranches, offtake floor contracts, tooling finance, or infrastructure co-investment.

## Alliance-Critical Industrial Domains

---

### Domain 1: Energy Systems & Grid Hardware

**Strategic Role**

Foundation layer. Every other industrial domain depends on stable, scalable electricity supply. Grid hardware — transformers, switchgear, transmission components, grid-scale storage, industrial power electronics — is the physical substrate of all manufacturing capacity.

**IDP Designation Logic**

Meets Alliance-Critical criteria on:
- Loss of supply degrades military and industrial output within 12 months (Criterion 1)
- Replacement time for major grid hardware exceeds 24 months under disrupted conditions (Criterion 3)
- Large transformer manufacturing is concentrated in fewer than three independent jurisdictions globally (Criterion 4)

Designation threshold: met. Elevated priority at current threat posture.

**IDP Resilience Requirements**

- N+2 production nodes required across at least two theaters
- Surge capacity: threefold expansion within six to nine months without greenfield build
- No single-node dependency permitted for large transformer or switchgear production
- Domestic-first baseline required; alliance redundancy supplements
- Tooling replication packages maintained under alliance custody

**Domestic Corridor Assignment**

| Corridor | Tier | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Texas (Houston–San Antonio axis) | I — Deployment Ready | Existing energy hardware industrial base, grid infrastructure, logistics depth |
| Great Lakes (Detroit–Cleveland) | I — Deployment Ready | Legacy heavy industrial real estate, freight rail, workforce |
| Southeast (Tennessee Valley) | II — Enablement Required | TVA grid infrastructure, available industrial land, requires workforce scaling |
| Arizona (Phoenix metro) | II — Enablement Required | Power availability, requires transformer-specific supplier ecosystem |

**PCA Instrument Alignment**

- Minority equity in domestic transformer and switchgear manufacturers
- First-loss tranches for manufacturing scale-up
- Tooling finance for replicable production line packages
- Infrastructure co-investment for grid hardware manufacturing clusters
- Offtake floor contracts coordinated with IDP Alliance Offtake Compact

**Corridor Viability Criteria Weighting**

Power availability and grid headroom: critical. Transportation and logistics: high. Historical manufacturing base: high. Materials proximity (steel, copper, rare earth components): high.

### Domain 2: Advanced Materials & Processing

**Strategic Role**

Upstream constraint layer. Rare earth refinement, battery materials, advanced alloys, semiconductor substrates, and industrial chemicals are the input layer for every downstream domain. Failure here propagates immediately into semiconductors, energy hardware, defense production, and transportation systems simultaneously.

**IDP Designation Logic**

Meets Alliance-Critical criteria on:
- Subject to coercive leverage and export restriction by adversary actors (Criterion 5)
- Processing concentrated in fewer than three independent jurisdictions (Criterion 4)
- Cascading failure across multiple civilian and military sectors (Criterion 2)

Designation threshold: met at standard posture. Highest single-domain coercion risk in the alliance.

**IDP Resilience Requirements**

- N+2 processing and refinement nodes required, explicitly including recycling loop integration
- Domestic-first baseline capacity required before alliance distribution
- Dual sourcing across independent jurisdictions (per Critical Materials & Energy framework Section 5)
- Dual routing across non-shared logistics pathways
- Stockpile sized to replacement timeline, not consumption rate (per Section 6)
- Triggers pre-negotiated: coercive export restriction activates offtake compact regardless of political conditions

**Domestic Corridor Assignment**

| Corridor | Tier | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Great Lakes (Michigan–Ohio) | I — Deployment Ready | Existing metals and chemical processing, freight access, brownfield sites |
| Mountain West (Wyoming–Nevada) | I — Deployment Ready | Domestic rare earth deposits, existing mining infrastructure, available land |
| Gulf Coast (Louisiana–Texas) | II — Enablement Required | Chemical processing infrastructure, port access, requires rare earth refining investment |
| Appalachian Corridor | III — Strategic Long-Term | Critical mineral deposits, long-term extraction and processing potential, governance investment required |

**PCA Instrument Alignment**

- First-loss tranches for rare earth refining facility development
- Minority equity in domestic processing and recycling firms
- Offtake floor contracts for domestically refined materials
- Infrastructure co-investment for processing cluster logistics spines
- Coordination with IDP alliance offtake compact for cross-border processing redundancy

**Corridor Viability Criteria Weighting**

Materials and supplier proximity: critical. Power availability: high (refining is energy-intensive). Transportation and logistics: high. Climate stability and water availability: medium-high (refining is water-intensive).

### Domain 3: Semiconductor & Compute Hardware

**Strategic Role**

Cognitive infrastructure layer. Leading-edge fabrication, mature-node chips, packaging and testing, industrial control systems, and AI/HPC infrastructure underpin military coordination, industrial automation, economic management, and civil infrastructure simultaneously. Degradation here is immediate and cross-domain.

**IDP Designation Logic**

Meets Alliance-Critical criteria on:
- Loss degrades military operations, industrial output, and strategic deterrence within 12 months (Criterion 1)
- Processing concentrated in fewer than three independent jurisdictions globally (Criterion 4)
- Subject to coercive leverage and export restriction (Criterion 5)

Designation threshold: met. Highest strategic priority for alliance-level investment coordination.

**IDP Resilience Requirements**

- Tiered production structure: leading-edge and mature-node treated separately
- N+2 nodes required at both tiers; mature-node redundancy is achievable domestically; leading-edge requires allied distribution
- Tooling replication for mature-node production; leading-edge cross-certification with allied fabs (Japan, South Korea, Netherlands-anchored ecosystem)
- Surge capacity: mature-node threefold expansion within six to nine months; leading-edge surge defined by alliance-level coordination
- No single-nation veto over production permitted at either tier

**Domestic Corridor Assignment**

| Corridor | Tier | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Arizona (Phoenix–Chandler) | I — Deployment Ready | TSMC, Intel presence; existing fab ecosystem; power infrastructure; workforce pipeline developing |
| Texas (Austin–San Antonio) | I — Deployment Ready | Samsung, NXP presence; energy infrastructure; university research pipeline |
| Ohio (Columbus metro) | II — Enablement Required | Intel investment anchor; requires water infrastructure, workforce scaling, supplier ecosystem |
| Pacific Northwest (Portland–Boise) | II — Enablement Required | Micron presence; mature-node potential; requires significant workforce and water infrastructure |

**PCA Instrument Alignment**

- First-loss tranches for mature-node fab development
- Minority equity in domestic fab operators and packaging/testing facilities
- Tooling finance for replicable mature-node production line packages, escrowed under alliance custody per IDP industrial continuity doctrine
- Supplier credit facilities for specialty gases, process chemicals, and semiconductor-grade materials
- Infrastructure co-investment for water systems and power capacity in fab corridors

**Corridor Viability Criteria Weighting**

Power availability and grid headroom: critical (fabs require stable, uninterruptible supply). Water availability and infrastructure: critical (fabs are water-intensive; a primary siting constraint). Workforce availability: high. Transportation and logistics: medium (inputs and outputs are high-value, low-volume). Climate stability: medium-high (long capital cycle makes 20–30 year climate risk relevant).

### Domain 4: Industrial Machinery & Robotics

**Strategic Role**

Production multiplier layer. CNC machines, robotic systems, automation platforms, factory tooling, and control systems determine the productivity ceiling of every other manufacturing domain. A nation that cannot produce its own advanced manufacturing equipment is dependent on others for the means of production itself — a strategic vulnerability that compounds across all other domains.

**IDP Designation Logic**

Meets Alliance-Critical criteria on:
- Loss of supply would impair defense industrial output within 12 months as production lines cannot be maintained or scaled (Criterion 1)
- Replacement time for specialized industrial machinery exceeds 24 months under disrupted conditions (Criterion 3)
- High-precision manufacturing equipment production is concentrated in fewer than three independent jurisdictions — Japan, Germany, and increasingly China (Criterion 4)

Designation threshold: met. Qualifies as Alliance-Critical with elevated urgency given concentration in adversary-adjacent supply chains.

**IDP Resilience Requirements**

- N+2 production nodes for critical machinery categories across at least two theaters
- Surge capacity emphasis: production lines themselves must be surge-capable, which requires the machinery that builds them to be available domestically
- Tooling replication packages for key machine tool categories maintained under alliance custody
- Overlap with civilian manufacturing permitted and encouraged where security allows — the IDP fungible modular production doctrine applies
- Allied cross-certification with Japan and Germany for high-precision categories where domestic capacity is not achievable within surge timelines

**Domestic Corridor Assignment**

| Corridor | Tier | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Great Lakes (Michigan–Ohio–Pittsburgh) | I — Deployment Ready | Precision manufacturing heritage, existing machine tool base, engineering workforce, freight infrastructure |
| New England (Connecticut–Massachusetts) | I — Deployment Ready | Aerospace precision manufacturing heritage, advanced tooling capability, university research pipeline |
| Southeast (Tennessee–Alabama) | II — Enablement Required | Growing advanced manufacturing base, lower cost structure, requires precision manufacturing workforce scaling |
| Texas (San Antonio–Dallas) | II — Enablement Required | Defense-adjacent manufacturing base, energy infrastructure, requires machine tool ecosystem development |

**PCA Instrument Alignment**

- Minority equity in domestic machine tool and robotics manufacturers
- Tooling finance for production line development
- Supplier credit facilities for precision component manufacturers (servo drives, harmonic gearboxes, control systems)
- Demand aggregation coordination to provide domestic manufacturers with visible forward demand — addresses the chicken-and-egg problem in low-volume, high-precision manufacturing
- First-loss tranches for scale-up of advanced robotics manufacturing facilities

**Corridor Viability Criteria Weighting**

Historical manufacturing base: critical (precision manufacturing is a skill-intensive industry where workforce heritage matters). Workforce availability: critical. Power availability: high. Transportation and logistics: medium-high. Materials proximity (specialty metals, electronic components): high.

### Domain 5: Logistics & Transportation Systems

**Strategic Role**

Movement layer. Production that cannot move does not exist strategically. Port infrastructure equipment, freight rail systems, heavy transport, intermodal logistics, and warehouse automation are the circulatory system of industrial capacity. Adversaries already target this layer in gray-zone operations — port congestion, infrastructure sabotage, insurance withdrawal — before conventional conflict begins.

**IDP Designation Logic**

Meets Alliance-Critical criteria on:
- Loss of logistics capacity cascades across all civilian sectors essential to wartime sustainment (Criterion 2)
- Specialized port and logistics equipment has replacement times exceeding 24 months (Criterion 3)
- Port crane manufacturing is concentrated in a single adversary jurisdiction — China's ZPMC dominates the global market (Criterion 4)
- Subject to coercive leverage through equipment dependencies, maintenance contracts, and embedded software (Criterion 5)

Designation threshold: met. Port crane dependency on Chinese manufacturing is an active, recognized vulnerability requiring immediate action.

**IDP Resilience Requirements**

- Domestic port equipment manufacturing capacity: N+1 minimum for port cranes and container handling equipment; N+2 for critical components
- Multi-route redundancy pre-planned for all major logistics corridors per IDP Logistics & Transport framework
- Degradation-tolerant bypass plans for each critical port and rail node
- Pre-clearance regimes for cross-border logistics under crisis conditions, pre-negotiated and trigger-bound
- Civil–military logistics integration contracts standing and exercised, not emergency-improvised
- No single port or rail corridor designated as irreplaceable — operational continuity doctrine requires distributed throughput capability

**Domestic Corridor Assignment**

| Corridor | Tier | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Gulf Coast (Houston–New Orleans) | I — Deployment Ready | Largest US cargo throughput, existing heavy industrial base, energy logistics infrastructure |
| Pacific Northwest (Tacoma–Portland) | I — Deployment Ready | Trans-Pacific gateway, existing port infrastructure, rail connectivity to inland |
| Great Lakes (Chicago–Detroit) | I — Deployment Ready | Inland waterway system, freight rail hub, intermodal capacity |
| Atlantic Seaboard (Norfolk–Savannah) | II — Enablement Required | Defense logistics significance (Norfolk), growing container throughput (Savannah), requires equipment manufacturing investment |

**PCA Instrument Alignment**

- Minority equity in domestic port equipment manufacturing — specifically crane and container handling equipment to reduce ZPMC dependency
- Infrastructure co-investment for port modernization and intermodal expansion
- Supplier credit facilities for logistics automation and warehouse robotics manufacturers
- Offtake floor contracts for domestically manufactured port equipment coordinated with port authority procurement
- Long-duration structured debt for freight rail infrastructure where private capital cycles are misaligned with infrastructure lifespans

**Corridor Viability Criteria Weighting**

Transportation and logistics: critical (by definition). Power availability: high (electrification of port and logistics equipment). Historical manufacturing base: medium (port equipment is a specialized category). Workforce availability: high. Climate stability: high (coastal infrastructure faces elevated long-term risk; inland logistics nodes are preferred for new capacity).

### Domain 6: Defense Industrial Base (IDP-Core)

**Strategic Role**

Direct IDP implementation layer. Munitions, sustainment systems, repair infrastructure, and logistics support equipment are the consumption layer of alliance military capability. This domain is the primary subject of IDP industrial capacity doctrine and is therefore governed by the full IDP framework rather than this target map's translation layer.

**IDP Designation Logic**

IDP-Core by definition. Governed directly by the Industrial Capacity & Defense Production framework. Not subject to the two-criteria designation threshold — designation is structural and unconditional.

**IDP Resilience Requirements**

All requirements from the Industrial Capacity & Defense Production framework apply in full:

- Arsenal bifurcation: Tier A (IDP-standard stockpile systems) and Tier B (sovereign crown-jewel systems) maintained as distinct categories
- N+2 executable production nodes across at least two theaters — hard requirement, no waiver
- Surge capacity: threefold expansion within six to nine months without greenfield build, validated through regular exercise
- Munitions scalability gate: systems requiring years to replenish months of expenditure are disqualified from IDP-standard designation
- Alliance Offtake Compact governs multi-year minimum purchase volumes with automatic contingency expansion triggers
- Anti-capture rules for primes: competitive re-bidding, cost transparency, caps on proprietary lock-in
- Sovereign Right-to-Repair: treaty-bound for all IDP-standard systems
- Tooling replication packages digitally escrowed under alliance custody

**Domestic Corridor Assignment**

Governed by existing defense industrial base mapping and IDP Production Board certification process. This target map defers to that framework for specific corridor assignments. General principle: geographic distribution across at least two non-adjacent theaters, with no single state or metropolitan area holding sole production authority for any IDP-standard system category.

**PCA Instrument Alignment**

Defense industrial investment operates through dedicated defense appropriations and the IDP Alliance Offtake Compact rather than standard PCA instruments. PCA may support dual-use manufacturing infrastructure where civilian and defense production overlap, consistent with IDP fungible modular production doctrine.

**Corridor Viability Criteria Weighting**

Governance and institutional capacity: critical (security requirements make institutional alignment non-negotiable). Transportation and logistics: critical. Historical manufacturing base: high. Power availability: high. Climate stability: high (long-term defense industrial investment requires multi-decade site viability).

### Domain 7: Construction & Built Environment Systems

**Strategic Role**

Absorption and scaling layer. Modular housing systems, construction materials, prefabricated manufacturing components, and infrastructure elements determine how fast the physical environment can scale to absorb population and industrial growth. Without elastic construction capacity, every other domain suffers from the infrastructure that cannot be built fast enough to support it.

**IDP Designation Logic**

Meets Alliance-Critical criteria on:
- Loss of construction capacity would cause cascading failure across sectors essential to wartime sustainment — military basing, worker housing, logistics infrastructure (Criterion 2)
- Specialty construction materials and prefab systems have replacement times exceeding 24 months for large-scale deployment (Criterion 3)

Designation threshold: met at standard posture. Elevated priority in climate absorption corridor context.

**IDP Resilience Requirements**

- Modular and prefabricated construction systems must be domestically producible at surge scale — wartime basing and infrastructure deployment cannot depend on import-dependent construction supply chains
- Distributed production: modular housing and construction component manufacturing should be regionally distributed to enable rapid deployment without long-haul logistics
- Material inputs (structural steel, cement, glass, insulation) must meet domestic sourcing minimums consistent with Critical Materials framework standards
- Surge deployment protocols pre-planned for military basing, disaster response, and large-scale population absorption scenarios

**Domestic Corridor Assignment**

Construction manufacturing is less geographically concentrated than other domains by design — distributed production is a resilience requirement, not a preference. Priority investments target regions with highest absorption demand: Great Lakes climate absorption corridors, Southeastern growth corridors, and Southwestern infrastructure expansion zones. Manufacturing nodes should be within 500-mile logistics reach of major population centers.

**PCA Instrument Alignment**

- Minority equity in modular and prefabricated construction manufacturers
- Tooling finance for replicable modular production systems
- Infrastructure co-investment for construction materials manufacturing near high-demand corridors
- Coordination with Housing Density Spine deployment to align construction manufacturing capacity with housing policy demand signals

**Corridor Viability Criteria Weighting**

Transportation and logistics: critical (construction materials are high-weight, low-value; logistics cost dominates). Materials proximity: critical (structural steel, cement, lumber sourcing). Housing expansion capacity: high (the output of this domain determines housing scalability). Power availability: medium.

### Domain 8: Water, Environmental & Resilience Systems

**Strategic Role**

Long-term habitability layer. Water treatment systems, desalination components, flood control infrastructure, and wildfire mitigation systems determine whether regions remain economically viable under accelerating climate stress. This domain is the infrastructure prerequisite for climate absorption corridor strategy — without it, population redistribution produces crisis rather than managed transition.

**IDP Designation Logic**

Meets Alliance-Critical criteria on:
- Loss of water treatment capacity would cause cascading civilian sector failure within 12 months in affected regions (Criterion 1 and Criterion 2)
- Specialized desalination and water treatment components have replacement times exceeding 24 months (Criterion 3)
- Critical water treatment chemicals and membrane materials are concentrated in fewer than three independent jurisdictions (Criterion 4)

Designation threshold: met. Elevated urgency as climate stress timelines accelerate.

**IDP Resilience Requirements**

- Domestic manufacturing capacity for water treatment components: membranes, pumps, chemical dosing systems, monitoring equipment
- Pre-positioned capacity in high-risk regions consistent with IDP stockpile doctrine — sized to replacement timeline, distributed rather than centralized
- Dual sourcing for critical water treatment chemicals across independent jurisdictions
- Surge deployment protocols for mobile water treatment systems in disaster and conflict scenarios
- Integration with climate absorption corridor strategy: water infrastructure investment sequenced before population inflows, not after

**Domestic Corridor Assignment**

| Corridor | Tier | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Southwest (Arizona–Nevada) | I — Deployment Ready | Immediate water stress, existing desalination technology base, strong demand signal |
| Gulf Coast | I — Deployment Ready | Flood control requirements, industrial water treatment demand, port resilience |
| Great Lakes | II — Enablement Required | Long-term water security advantage, treatment infrastructure modernization needed |
| Pacific Coast (California) | II — Enablement Required | Wildfire mitigation systems, coastal water management, high demand but high cost structure |

**PCA Instrument Alignment**

- Minority equity in domestic water treatment component manufacturers
- First-loss tranches for desalination technology scale-up
- Infrastructure co-investment for regional water treatment manufacturing clusters
- Coordination with climate absorption corridor capital deployment

**Corridor Viability Criteria Weighting**

Climate stability and water availability: critical (this domain exists to address that constraint). Power availability: high (desalination is energy-intensive; pairing with renewable energy is strategic). Transportation and logistics: medium (distributed deployment requires regional manufacturing nodes). Governance and institutional capacity: high (water infrastructure involves complex multi-jurisdictional authorities).

## Classification Matrix

Each domain is classified across three dimensions. This creates a capability-geography matrix that governs capital prioritization and alliance coordination requirements.

| Domain | Domestic-Critical | Alliance-Distributed | Globally Competitive |
|---|---|---|---|
| Energy Systems & Grid Hardware | Yes — baseline capacity required | Yes — N+2 across allied theaters | Partial — commodity components only |
| Advanced Materials & Processing | Yes — domestic-first baseline | Yes — redundancy and recycling loops | No — strategic inputs exempt |
| Semiconductor & Compute | Yes — mature-node | Yes — leading-edge requires allied fab network | No |
| Industrial Machinery & Robotics | Yes | Yes — allied cross-certification | Partial — non-critical categories |
| Logistics & Transportation | Yes — port equipment and rail | Yes — maritime routing and bypass planning | No — strategic nodes exempt |
| Defense Industrial Base | Yes — full IDP compliance | Yes — N+2 across theaters mandatory | No |
| Construction & Built Environment | Yes — surge deployment capacity | Partial — material inputs | Yes — commodity construction materials |
| Water & Resilience Systems | Yes | Partial — technology sharing | Partial — non-critical components |

**Domestic-Critical** means the capability must exist within US sovereign territory at baseline scale, independent of alliance supply.

**Alliance-Distributed** means the capability must be replicated across IDP partner territories to meet N+2 requirements and eliminate single-theater dependency.

**Globally Competitive** means market-driven production is acceptable for this category; strategic reserves or domestic baseline requirements do not apply.

## Capital and Execution Alignment

PCA deployment, RMC regulatory modernization, and Execution Corps engagement are sequenced against this target map as follows:

**Tier I corridors in Domestic-Critical domains** receive immediate priority. These are the nodes where alliance resilience requirements are unmet and execution capacity exists. Capital deployment begins within existing authorization envelopes.

**Tier II corridors in Alliance-Distributed domains** receive enablement investment: targeted capital to resolve binding constraints (power, water, workforce, logistics) before anchor manufacturing deployment. Execution Corps engagement is pre-deployment, not concurrent.

**Tier III corridors** receive strategic development investment: long-duration infrastructure, governance capacity building, and DoDa monitoring. Capital intensity is lower; timeline horizons are longer.

Alliance coordination — reciprocal siting agreements, allied surge manufacturing protocols, and supply chain interoperability standards — runs in parallel with domestic activation, not sequentially. IDP compliance cannot be achieved unilaterally.

## Dependency and Cascade Map

The eight domains are not independent. Failure in upstream domains cascades into downstream domains faster than the domain entries suggest. The critical cascade paths are:

**Energy → Everything.** Grid constraint is the fastest-propagating failure mode. Every other domain depends on stable power. Energy Systems & Grid Hardware is therefore the first-priority investment regardless of other domain readiness.

**Advanced Materials → Semiconductors, Energy Hardware, Defense.** Input scarcity in rare earths, battery materials, and industrial chemicals propagates simultaneously into three domains within months.

**Semiconductors → Logistics, Robotics, Defense, Water.** Industrial control systems run on chips. Loss of semiconductor supply degrades operational capacity across domains that appear unrelated to compute.

**Logistics → Defense, Construction, Water.** Production that cannot move cannot surge. Logistics domain resilience is a precondition for surge capacity in every other domain.

This cascade structure means that IDP resilience requirements cannot be met domain by domain in isolation. A domain where the US corridor meets its contribution to the alliance N+2 network, but depends on a non-compliant upstream domain, is not actually resilient at the system level. Alliance industrial strategy must track compliance at the network level across all domains, not at the individual node level within each domain.

## Governance and Compliance

A standing Alliance Industrial Coordination Board — operating in parallel with the IDP Production Board for defense-specific systems — monitors compliance across all eight domains. The Board:

- publishes annual domain compliance assessments against IDP resilience standards
- certifies corridor-level production node executability
- reallocates PCA capital based on performance and threat posture changes
- maintains the tooling replication and industrial continuity escrow packages
- coordinates with IDP Production Board on dual-use and defense-adjacent investments
- triggers offtake compact activation when compliance thresholds are breached

DoDa dashboards publish domain-level compliance indicators publicly and continuously. Corridor readiness scores are updated on a rolling basis and are accessible to manufacturers, allied governments, and state planners.

## Closing

Industrial strength in the modern era is not defined by national self-sufficiency, nor by alliance coordination alone. It is defined by the disciplined integration of both: sovereign baseline capacity across all Domestic-Critical domains, distributed redundancy across Alliance-Distributed domains, and continuous compliance with IDP resilience standards at the system level.

This target map is not a static document. It is a living targeting layer that directs capital, execution capacity, and alliance coordination toward the gaps that matter most. As threat postures change, as corridor readiness improves, and as IDP doctrine evolves, the map updates.

The cascade dependencies are real. The designation logic is binding. The corridor assignments are evidence-based and continuously monitored.

Industry survives contact with reality. So must industrial strategy.

## Purpose

Industrial corridor strategy requires clear identification of the real-world conditions that enable durable manufacturing cluster formation. This brief defines the structural characteristics that make a region capable of supporting large-scale industrial deployment.

The goal is to prioritize locations where industrial growth can occur rapidly, sustainably, and with minimal systemic disruption.

---

## Core Principle

Successful manufacturing corridors are not created solely through incentives or land designation.

They emerge where **industrial systems already partially exist** and can be scaled through coordinated investment and policy sequencing. Regions that align on most or all criteria are immidiately targeted for industrial expansion whereas regions with less matching criteria are targeted for long term growth, strategic development, and expansion.

---

# Methodology: 

Upon inception, the Department of Data and Accountability (DoDa) will evaluate cities and geographic regions across the United States according to the following criteria. These criteria will be continueally monitrored and dashboards will be made public so that city planners and Governers can evaluate their regions suitability for industrial revitilization. Additionally, manufacturers wishing to move production to the United States will be able to see in advance which geographic regions contain the suitability criteria for their sector's needs. 

These dashboards will be updated on a regular basis and: 

- regions can move tiers 
- readiness can be engineered
- federal intervention can accelerate eligibility

To note: Manufacturing dashboard availability criteria dashboards should be stratified and evaluated on a sector specific basis. As such, corridor viability for any specific sector is evaluated using sector-specific weighting of these criteria depending on the industrial cluster targeted.

## Power Availability and Grid Headroom

Manufacturing requires stable, affordable, and expandable electricity supply. As long-term manufacturing competitiveness increasingly correlates with marginal energy input cost stability, viable corridors typically exhibit:

- Existing high-voltage transmission access  
- Grid reserve margin sufficient for industrial load growth  
- Substation and interconnection capacity  
- Expandable generation pipeline  
- Predictable long-term power pricing  

Regions with constrained interconnection queues or volatile marginal power costs face elevated industrial deployment risk (and can catalyze elevated political backlash / risk for surrounding populations). Therefore, abundance power supply is targeted as a criteria for manufacturing overlays. Regions lacking this infrastructure are targeted for electrical grid expansion and stabilization. 

---

## Adaptable Industrial Real Estate

Manufacturing expansion is accelerated where suitable sites already exist or can be rapidly prepared.

Key characteristics include:

- Brownfield or legacy industrial sites suitable for redevelopment  
- Large parcel availability  
- Existing heavy industrial zoning or industrial overlays  
- Utility servicing infrastructure  
- Environmental remediation feasibility  

Corridors meeting either sufficient amoutns of pre-zoned demand and / or adaptable real estate significantly reduce time-to-deployment, and are therefore targeted in the short term. 

---

## Historical Manufacturing Base

Regions with prior industrial activity often retain latent capabilities. These advantages include:

- Skilled workforce familiarity with industrial operations  
- Existing supplier networks  
- Union and management institutional knowledge  
- Embedded logistics infrastructure  
- Community familiarity with industrial land use  

Again while this is not stricly necessary, these factors lower coordination costs during cluster formation and are therefore targeted in the short term. 

---

## Transportation and Logistics Connectivity

Manufacturing competitiveness depends on efficient movement of inputs and outputs.

Viable corridors typically include:

- Freight rail access  
- Interstate highway connectivity  
- Proximity to inland logistics hubs or ports  
- Intermodal transfer capacity  
- Last-mile industrial road infrastructure  

Logistics friction directly increases production cost and cluster fragility.

---

## Workforce Availability and Mobility

Industrial corridors require both immediate labor access and scalable workforce pipelines. 

Indicators include:

- Regional labor force participation  
- Industrial training institutions  
- Occupational mobility potential (through trades or community colleges)
- Housing access for incoming workers  
- Local cultural acceptance of industrial employment

Labor shortages can destabilize cost structures if not anticipated and ameliorated. Targeting areas with existing labor pools, and / or labor skills pathways is crucial in order to scale manufacturing effectively. 

---

## Housing Expansion Capacity

Industrial growth must be matched by residential development. Viable corridors should allow:

- Density increases near employment centers  
- Rapid permitting of new housing supply  
- Infrastructure servicing expansion  
- Mixed-income development integration  

Too much housing rigidity paired with industrical success risks throwing surrounding regions into an affordability crisis. Pairing regional zones with housing overlays beforehand is critical. And existing housing supply and flexibility is prefered. 

---

## Materials and Supplier Proximity

As is evidenced in [NORAM Critical Materials and Manufacturing] locations benefit from proximity to:

- Refining and processing facilities  
- Component manufacturing ecosystems  
- Recycling infrastructure  
- Strategic stockpile logistics  

Distance from these upstream inputs increases operational risk. 

---

## Climate Stability

Long-term manufacturing investment requires confidence in regional stability. Long term climate change increases long term climate risk for industrial investments including: 

- Water availability  
- Heat exposure trends  
- Wildfire exposure and risk
- Hurricane / Flooding and extreme weather risk
- Insurance market viability  

Climate risk increasingly influences industrial siting decisions as this is inherently a long term production choice, therefore regions exposed to climate related risks may face consequential downgraded elligibility. 

---

## Governance and Institutional Capacity

Industrial deployment requires coordinated public-sector execution. Viable regions demonstrate:

- Permitting throughput capability  
- Infrastructure delivery competence  
- Political alignment on industrial strategy  
- Public-private collaboration history  
- Certainty in legal timelines

Execution Corps deployments exist to decrease friction while keeping existing environmental and humanitarian legislation intact. Local opt-In is critical in manufactuing deployments as institutional failure can stall otherwise strong corridors.

---

## Closing

Industrial corridor viability depends on the presence of scalable industrial systems rather than incentive competition.

Regions that combine power availability, adaptable real estate, logistics connectivity, workforce depth, and governance execution capacity provide the strongest foundation for durable manufacturing expansion.

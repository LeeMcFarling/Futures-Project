# Department of Education Standards (DES) — Trigger Thresholds & Intervention Protocol

## Executive Summary

This framework defines how the Department of Education Standards (DES) identifies, evaluates, and responds to meaningful deviations in school and program performance within a decentralized, AI-enabled education ecosystem.

DES does not manage schools. It defines expected learning trajectories, monitors validated outcome signals, and intervenes only when pre-specified trigger conditions are met. The goal is to preserve experimentation while preventing low-quality or extractive models from scaling.

The system is built on five principles:

- Trajectory-based evaluation (not absolute scores)
- Composite triggers (not single metrics)
- Persistence requirements (to filter noise)
- Cohort-adjusted comparisons (to reduce bias)
- Tiered intervention (not binary enforcement)

---

## Strategic Principle

DES evaluates **performance derivatives**, not static performance levels.

The core question is:

> Are learning outcomes materializing at the expected rate given a student’s starting point?

Intervention is triggered by **material deviation from expected trajectory**, not by rankings or prestige.

---

## Core Measurement Model

Each student is evaluated against an **expected learning trajectory**, derived from:

- prior mastery state  
- matched cohort comparisons  
- historical program distributions  
- retention and transfer performance  

### Primary Outcome Signals

- **Δ Mastery** (learning velocity)
- **Retention** (does learning persist over time)
- **Transfer** (can knowledge be applied in new contexts)
- **Variance** (are outcomes evenly distributed or collapsing for subsets)

### Secondary Signals (Support Only)

- attendance  
- engagement  
- program demand  

These may inform diagnosis but do not independently trigger intervention.

---

## Trigger Classes

### 1. Performance Degradation (Outcome Failure)

Activated when:

- Δ mastery falls materially below expected trajectory  
- retention declines across multiple periods  
- transfer performance weakens  
- cohort variance increases (uneven benefit distribution)

This is the **primary negative trigger** and carries highest intervention authority.

---

### 2. Demand–Outcome Divergence (Market Distortion)

Activated when:

- **Demand ↑ + Outcomes ↓ or flat**  
  → indicates popularity without learning

- **Outcomes ↑ + Demand ↓**  
  → indicates under-recognized or poorly communicated value

The first is treated as a **system risk**.  
The second triggers **diagnosis and potential scaling**.

---

### 3. Anomalous Performance (Outliers)

Activated when:

- a program significantly outperforms peers  
- rapid improvement exceeds expected bounds  
- performance volatility is unexplained  

Positive outliers → **replication candidates**  
Negative outliers → **failure-mode investigation**

---

### 4. System-Level Drift

Activated when:

- average Δ mastery declines across regions  
- inter-campus inequality increases  
- demand concentrates in low-outcome programs  
- retention/transfer degrade system-wide  

Triggers **standards or incentive redesign**, not school-level punishment.

---

## Persistence Rules

Triggers require sustained deviation.

| State | Condition | Action |
|------|----------|--------|
| Flag | 1-period deviation | Internal monitoring |
| Soft Trigger | 2–3 periods | Diagnostic request |
| Full Trigger | Persistent deviation | Intervention |

Single-period noise does not trigger intervention.

---

## Cohort Adjustment Rules

All evaluations are **relative to comparable baselines**, including:

- prior mastery distribution  
- age band  
- specialization type  
- learning accommodations  
- language context  
- access constraints  

Adjustment isolates **program effect**, not excuses failure.

---

## Intervention Tiers

### Tier 1 — Passive Flag
- Internal monitoring only  
- No school-facing action  

---

### Tier 2 — Diagnostic Request
- School provides explanation  
- Identifies operational vs instructional issues  

---

### Tier 3 — Targeted Review
- Temporary DES field deployment  
- Hypothesis-driven investigation  
- Time-bounded  

---

### Tier 4 — Constraint / Probation
- Enrollment caps  
- Required redesign  
- Limited expansion rights  

---

### Tier 5 — Sunset / Decertification
- Program loses certification  
- Funding eligibility removed  
- Student transition required  

---

## Positive Intervention Pathway

High-performing programs trigger:

- Reference campus designation  
- Formal study and documentation  
- Replication or scaling support  
- Standards integration review  

DES learns from success, not just failure.

---

## Demand Interpretation Framework

Low demand is not automatically failure.

DES diagnoses:

- Access constraints  
- Information gaps  
- Design issues  
- True preference  

Only confirmed preference failure justifies contraction.

High demand does not justify scaling without outcomes.

---

## Protected Core vs Specialization Layer

### Educational Spine (Core Layer)

- ~2 hours/day mastery-based learning  
- Literacy, numeracy, science, reasoning  
- Governed strictly by outcomes  
- Protected from demand distortion  

---

### Specialization Layer

- Sports, esports, entrepreneurship, arts, etc.  
- Demand informs exploration  
- Outcomes determine survival and scale  

Constraint:

> Specialization may not degrade spine mastery trajectories.

---

## Field Deployment Protocol

DES personnel may deploy under Tier 3 or positive triggers.

Rules:

- Time-bounded  
- Hypothesis-driven  
- No operational authority  
- Must produce formal output  

Continuous on-site presence is prohibited.

---

## Governance & Transparency

All trigger logic must be:

- Versioned  
- Auditable  
- Public at framework level  
- Protected from ad hoc override  

Threshold changes require formal revision.

---

## Default Threshold Structure

- 1 deviation → monitor  
- 2–3 deviations → diagnostic  
- persistent deviation → intervention  
- strong outperformance → replication review  
- demand–outcome mismatch → constraint or investigation  

---

## Success Criteria

- Early detection of failure  
- Protection against low-learning equilibria  
- Preservation of experimentation  
- Identification of high-performing models  
- Reduced subjective intervention  

---

## Failure Conditions

- Over-intervention (innovation suppression)  
- Under-detection (low-quality persistence)  
- Evaluator gaming behavior  
- Metric manipulation  
- Inability to explain success cases  

---

## Strategic Impact

This framework enables:

- Decentralized innovation with credible accountability  
- Continuous system learning  
- Outcome-driven legitimacy  
- Reduced reliance on bureaucracy  

---

## Conclusion

A decentralized education system requires explicit rules for when to observe, intervene, constrain, and scale.

This protocol ensures DES operates as a **calibration engine**, not a management layer—allowing the system to evolve while maintaining trust and performance integrity.

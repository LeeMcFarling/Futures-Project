## Purpose

This document opens the Higher Education domain by establishing what
universities are for in the system being built — and what they are not for.

The answer is not obvious. As K-12 education transitions to a spine-based,
competency-validated model, as the competency transcript begins to replace
the degree as the primary signal of human capability, and as the four-year
residential degree model declines in centrality as the default path from
education to work, universities face a structural identity question that
cannot be resolved by incremental reform. The question is not how to
preserve the university as it currently exists. It is what role universities
should play in a society that is redesigning how knowledge is formed, how
capability is validated, and how discovery reaches application.

This framework answers that question by repositioning universities as
**discovery pre-seeding infrastructure** — institutions optimized to explore
uncertainty, validate foundational results, and surface discoveries worthy
of downstream scaling. Not credential factories. Not venture studios. Not
prestige signaling mechanisms. Option generators: the upstream layer of a
knowledge economy that requires genuine exploration to function, and that
currently has no institution better suited to provide it than the research
university — if that institution can be freed from the conflicting roles
that currently prevent it from doing so.

## The Problem with the Current University Research Model

The research university is simultaneously one of the most valuable
institutional innovations in human history and one of the most structurally
conflicted. It is asked to do things that are genuinely in tension with each
other, and the accumulated weight of those tensions has produced a system
that serves none of its stated purposes as well as it could.

The first tension is between mass credentialing and knowledge generation.
Universities award degrees to millions of students annually — a function
that requires enormous administrative infrastructure, faculty time, and
institutional attention directed toward instruction, student services, and
credential management. Research is expected to happen alongside this function,
often by the same people performing it. The result is that the institutional
pressure to enroll students, manage their experience, and award credentials
that they and their employers expect constantly competes with the attention
required for genuine scientific exploration. Neither function is performed
as well as it would be by an institution designed specifically for it.

The second tension is between prestige-driven signaling and discovery quality.
The metrics by which universities are evaluated — rankings, grant volume,
publication counts, citation rates — reward incremental work in established
paradigms more reliably than they reward genuinely exploratory research.
A researcher who identifies a promising direction that requires years of
foundational work before producing publishable results is operating at a
structural disadvantage relative to a researcher who produces a steady stream
of papers building on established methods in a well-funded field. The
incentives select for volume and safety rather than for the kind of risk-
tolerant exploration that produces genuine breakthroughs.

The third tension is between the university as a knowledge commons and the
university as an economic actor. Technology transfer offices, patent portfolios,
licensing revenues, and startup ecosystems have become significant parts of
university economic strategy. This is not inherently wrong — universities
that generate commercially valuable discoveries should capture some of that
value to fund continued exploration. But the economic incentives around
commercialization can distort research priorities, create pressure to oversell
preliminary findings, push discoveries toward premature productization, and
produce the kind of incumbency bias in research funding that privileges
established players with commercialization infrastructure over new entrants
with better ideas.

The fourth tension is between the grant funding system and the research that
actually needs to happen. Grant structures favor incremental work with
predictable outcomes, clear applicability to funder priorities, and
demonstrated track records from established researchers. The work that most
needs to happen — genuinely exploratory, high-uncertainty, potentially
paradigm-shifting research — is structurally disadvantaged in a competitive
grant environment where reviewers favor tractable problems with clear
methodologies and expected results that fit within the review cycle.

The cumulative effect of these tensions is a research ecosystem where
reputational advantage frequently outweighs discovery quality, where early-
stage ideas are oversold into premature startups that fail to deliver on
their promise, where negative results are systematically underreported because
they do not support publication or funding renewal, and where the genuine
scientific exploration that universities are uniquely positioned to perform
is under-resourced relative to the incremental work that fits neatly into
existing incentive structures.

## What Universities Are For

The answer that this framework proposes is narrow but consequential:
universities should generate discovery options that society then selects
and scales.

This is a specific claim about comparative advantage. Academic research
excels at exploration and validation under uncertainty — at asking questions
without knowing whether the answers will be useful, at accumulating the
foundational knowledge that makes later application possible, at maintaining
the long-horizon patient capital of intellectual inquiry that neither markets
nor governments can sustain reliably. Translation, capital deployment, and
operational scaling are better handled by downstream actors — firms, public
capital authorities, and execution bodies — with aligned incentives and
risk-bearing capacity appropriate to those functions.

The failure of the current system is not that universities do research. It
is that they are also expected to be the primary vehicle for taking that
research to market, for managing the commercial transition, and for capturing
the value of discoveries in ways that distort the research process itself.
When universities become venture studios — when the pressure to generate
commercially viable discoveries shapes what research gets done and how it
is reported — they stop being the kind of exploratory institutions that
generate the discoveries worth commercializing in the first place.

The reorientation this framework proposes is structural: universities generate
discovery options, and the selection and scaling of those options happens
downstream, through institutions with the mandate and the incentives to do
that work well. Universities become the frontier of uncertainty exploration
rather than the bottleneck of translation.

## Discovery Units: Formalizing Research Output

The operational expression of this reorientation is the **Discovery Unit**
— a formalized research output designed for selection rather than promotion.

A Discovery Unit is a discrete, reviewable research asset that contains the
validated experimental or theoretical results of a defined research effort,
the methods, protocols, and datasets that produced those results, replication
and reproducibility metadata that allows downstream actors to evaluate the
result's robustness, documented negative results where they are scientifically
informative, and clearly scoped IP claims or open-licensing status that
establish what downstream actors are acquiring rights to.

The Discovery Unit format is designed to solve a specific problem: the current
research output — the journal article — is optimized for academic communication,
not for downstream selection. A paper tells a story, makes a claim, and
positions itself within a literature. It does not provide the operational
information that a pharmaceutical company, a materials firm, or a public
research authority needs to evaluate whether this result is worth building
on. Discovery Units provide that information in a standardized, reviewable
format that makes selection possible without requiring extensive due diligence
that the current system imposes on every potential downstream actor.

Discovery Units are designed for selection, not promotion. A researcher who
produces a Discovery Unit is documenting what they found and what it enables,
not pitching an investment opportunity or claiming transformative significance.
The selection decision belongs to downstream actors with the domain expertise,
capital access, and risk tolerance to evaluate it appropriately. Universities
generate the options. Others choose which ones to pursue.

## The Funding Stack

The funding architecture for discovery pre-seeding operates in three layers,
each serving a different function in the knowledge generation chain.

**Baseline public funding** supports the exploration that markets cannot
price efficiently: high-uncertainty investigation with no clear commercial
pathway, replication work that validates or invalidates existing findings,
and foundational research that builds the conceptual infrastructure on which
later application depends. This layer exists because genuine exploration
requires patient capital that is not contingent on predictable returns.
Markets systematically underprovide it because the benefits are diffuse,
the timelines are long, and the connection between specific investment and
specific application is too uncertain to price. Public funding absorbs this
risk as a deliberate policy choice, accepting that most of what it funds
will not produce commercial value in exchange for the fraction that does —
which produces value disproportionate to the investment.

**Discovery Unit selection** generates revenue for universities and researchers
when downstream actors — pharmaceutical companies, materials firms, technology
developers, public research authorities — identify Discovery Units worth
building on. Downstream actors may license Discovery Units, purchase them
outright, or enter co-development agreements. These transactions generate
non-dilutive revenue for universities and direct compensation for researchers
through defined revenue-sharing arrangements. The revenue flows back into
funding continued exploration rather than into overhead structures that
persist regardless of research quality.

**Portfolio sustainability** is the long-run equilibrium that the Discovery
Unit model makes possible. Most Discovery Units will not be selected for
commercialization or downstream development — that is expected and appropriate.
A small fraction of high-value selections generate revenues that cross-
subsidize the broader exploration portfolio, reducing reliance on grant
cycles that reward incremental work and on prestige-based funding that
concentrates resources in established institutions. The portfolio logic
mirrors how venture capital functions, but applied to knowledge generation
rather than to firm formation: most bets lose, some break even, a few pay
for everything else, and the expected value of the portfolio justifies
the exploration.

## Researcher Incentives

The Discovery Unit model realigns researcher incentives in ways the current
grant and publication system cannot.

Under the current system, researcher reputation is tied primarily to
publication volume, citation counts, and grant success — metrics that
reward incremental work, established methodologies, and alignment with
funder priorities. Originality and rigor are rewarded only insofar as they
produce publishable results that fit within recognized paradigms. Replication
is systematically underincentivized because it does not produce novel
publications. Negative results are systematically underdisclosed because
they do not support continued funding narratives.

Under the Discovery Unit model, researcher reputation is tied to reuse and
validation of their work. A researcher whose Discovery Unit is selected by
multiple downstream actors, validated by independent replication, and built
into successful applications has demonstrated something more meaningful than
publication count: their work was worth building on. Revenue sharing from
Discovery Unit selection provides direct financial incentive for the kind
of rigorous, reproducible, carefully documented research that the current
system undervalues. And career mobility across academia, industry, and public
research becomes more tractable when researchers have a portable record of
validated Discovery Units rather than an academic CV that does not translate
cleanly into non-academic environments.

The shift in incentive structure also changes what kinds of research get
done. Researchers who benefit from their work being selected and used have
incentives to produce results that are genuine, reproducible, and well-
documented — not results that are publishable in high-impact journals but
fail to replicate or support downstream application. The Discovery Unit model
rewards quality of output, not quantity of publication.

## Guardrails Against Capture and Exploitation

The Discovery Unit model creates value by making research outputs available
for downstream selection. It also creates risks of exploitation that require
structural protection.

Minimum buyout pricing floors prevent researchers and universities from being
pressured into licensing or selling Discovery Units below their value —
a risk that is particularly acute when institutions with significant capital
and legal resources negotiate with individual researchers or smaller
universities. Mandatory revenue-sharing with originating institutions and
researchers ensures that the value generated by publicly funded research
flows back to the people who produced it rather than being captured entirely
by downstream commercial actors.

Right of publication protections ensure that commercial licensing agreements
cannot suppress the academic communication of results — a practice that
has historically been used to prevent competitors from building on publicly
funded research that has been licensed exclusively. Anti-exclusivity and
anti-hoarding rules prevent single actors from acquiring Discovery Units
and then sitting on them without development — a strategy that can be used
to block competitors rather than to advance application. Public-interest
licensing fallbacks ensure that Discovery Units in critical domains — medical
treatments, public health interventions, infrastructure technologies — cannot
be captured by commercial actors in ways that prevent public access to the
results of publicly funded research.

These guardrails are not restrictions on the market for Discovery Units. They
are the conditions under which that market can operate without producing the
kinds of exploitation that would undermine support for the model.

## Connection to Modular Education and the Competency Pipeline

As the degree model modularizes and the competency transcript replaces the
degree as the primary capability signal, universities increasingly differentiate
through their research function rather than through their credentialing
function. The institutions that attract the best students and the best
researchers in this environment are those that offer the most genuine
opportunities for discovery participation — not the ones that award the
most prestigious degrees.

This creates a natural alignment between the Discovery Unit model and the
evolving student relationship to universities. Research apprenticeships —
where students contribute to real Discovery Unit production rather than to
simulated research exercises — generate contribution-based credentials that
carry weight in the competency transcript alongside the NCF domain competencies
developed through the spine. A student who has contributed meaningfully to
a Discovery Unit that was subsequently selected by a downstream actor has
demonstrated something that no coursework credential can replicate: the
ability to produce knowledge that is worth building on.

The connection to lifelong learning and reskilling is equally direct. A
professional who returns to university to engage with frontier research in
a domain relevant to their work — not to earn a degree, but to contribute
to Discovery Unit production and update their competency record — represents
a sustainable revenue model for universities that does not depend on the
declining four-year residential degree pipeline. Universities that develop
the infrastructure for this kind of engagement are positioning themselves
for a future in which the credential function is distributed across many
institutions and the discovery function remains their distinctive contribution.

## Relationship to the Broader Execution Architecture

Discovery Units feed the de-risked R&D pipelines that the Public Capital
Authority is designed to support. A Discovery Unit that has been validated,
documented, and made available for selection provides exactly the kind of
de-risked early-stage asset that the PCA's capital instruments are designed
to take to the next stage — reducing the risk that prevents private capital
from engaging with genuinely early-stage work, and making the transition
from discovery to application faster and more honest than the current system
allows.

The Department of Data and Accountability's role in validating research
outcomes — consistent with its broader mandate to certify whether public
investments are producing the outcomes they were designed to produce — extends
naturally to Discovery Unit quality assurance. DoDA does not evaluate the
scientific merit of individual research programs. It validates whether the
Discovery Unit production process is meeting the documentation, reproducibility,
and disclosure standards that make selection trustworthy. That validation
is what gives downstream actors confidence that the Discovery Units they
are selecting represent genuine results rather than oversold preliminary
findings.

## Failure Modes

**Incumbency capture** of the Discovery Unit selection process is the most
serious risk. If large commercial actors develop the ability to systematically
identify and acquire the most valuable Discovery Units before smaller actors
or public authorities can evaluate them, the model reproduces the existing
concentration of research application value rather than distributing it. The
minimum pricing floors, anti-exclusivity rules, and public-interest fallbacks
are the structural defenses, but they require active enforcement and periodic
recalibration as actors develop strategies to work within the letter of the
rules while violating their intent.

**Premature commercialization pressure** can distort Discovery Unit production
if researchers or universities experience pressure to frame exploratory results
as more commercially applicable than they are in order to attract selection
and the revenue it generates. The fundamental separation between generation
and selection — universities produce options, others choose — is the design
defense, but it requires institutional culture as well as structural rules.
A university that evaluates its researchers primarily on Discovery Unit
selection rates has recreated the commercial pressure it was designed to
escape.

**Replication infrastructure underinvestment** remains the most persistent
failure mode in research systems generally. If baseline public funding
prioritizes novel exploration over replication and validation — which it
tends to do because novel exploration is more exciting and more publishable
— the Discovery Unit corpus accumulates results of uncertain robustness that
downstream actors cannot confidently build on. Dedicated funding for
replication work, treated as a first-class research output rather than as
a lesser form of scholarship, is the primary defense.

## Conclusion

Universities have been the primary institutions of knowledge generation in
the modern world because they have had the combination of resources, talent,
and institutional autonomy to pursue questions whose answers are not yet
known. That combination is still valuable. What has changed is everything
around it: the funding structures that shape what research gets done, the
credentialing functions that compete with the research mission for institutional
attention, and the commercialization pressures that distort research priorities
toward applications that can be captured rather than knowledge that can be
shared.

Discovery pre-seeding does not propose to fix universities by making them
more like their current ideal. It proposes to clarify what they are
distinctively good at — generating options under uncertainty — and to build
the institutional and funding structures that allow them to do that well.
Universities that do this well become indispensable to a knowledge economy
that needs genuine exploration. Universities that continue to try to do
everything — credential factory, research lab, startup studio, prestige
signal, and community anchor simultaneously — will find that they do none
of those things as well as institutions designed specifically for each.

The discovery function is the one that cannot be replicated elsewhere. This
framework is built to protect and strengthen it.

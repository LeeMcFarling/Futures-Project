# Capability Apprenticeship Compact

## Purpose

This brief defines the Capability Apprenticeship Compact — a structured,
demand-driven pathway that braids paid work with credentialed instruction
from high school senior year through an Associate degree and into stable
employment, with a clear upward mobility lane for those who want it.

The compact is not a government training program. It is a coordination
architecture: the WCA certifies competency frameworks and demand signals,
community colleges provide credentialed instruction, employers provide paid
work and real equipment, and high schools provide early entry. Each institution
does what it does well. None of them replaces the others.

The goal is simple to state: workers graduate with the competencies and
credentials they need — without debt — with wages already being earned and
a job pathway already established. Employers get workers with the specific
capabilities the role requires, not graduates who learned adjacent things and
need expensive remediation. Communities get a pipeline of capable workers in
the sectors that need them most.

---

## The Structural Problem This Solves

The United States is facing a compounding workforce crisis in skilled trades
and technical roles that has been building for decades and is now acute.

**The knowledge transfer cliff.** A generation of skilled tradespeople —
machinists, tool-and-die makers, electricians, pipefitters, industrial
maintenance technicians, precision welders — is approaching retirement age.
They carry knowledge that is not in any textbook: the tacit understanding of
how a machine behaves at the edge of its tolerance, how to read a part for
failure before it fails, how to improvise a repair under production pressure.
That knowledge transfers through apprenticeship, mentorship, and years of
working alongside someone who has it. When the retiring generation leaves,
the knowledge leaves with them unless deliberate structures exist to transfer
it. Those structures have largely not been built. The cliff is not approaching
— it is already here in many sectors.

**The reshoring demand gap.** The Futures Project's trade and manufacturing
architecture — regulatory modernization, reciprocal trade doctrine, industrial
corridor development, IDP production integration — is actively reshoring
industries and job classes that were offshored over decades. That reshoring
creates demand for workers in roles that, in many cases, have not existed
at meaningful scale in the United States for a generation. The firms reopening
facilities can find the capital, the real estate, and sometimes the equipment.
They frequently cannot find the workers — not because Americans don't want
these roles, but because the pipeline that would produce those workers was
allowed to atrophy at the same time the jobs were leaving. The demand has
returned before the supply infrastructure has.

**The multi-decade learning debt.** Offshoring did not just eliminate jobs.
It eliminated the institutional knowledge of how to train people for those
jobs. Community colleges shuttered programs. Apprenticeship pipelines lost
employer partners. High school vocational programs were cut in favor of
college-prep tracks based on the assumption that manufacturing was a sunset
sector. The curricula, the equipment, the instructor relationships with
industry, the employer connections that made programs real rather than
theoretical — all of it degraded over a generation. Rebuilding it takes
time that the reshoring timeline does not automatically allow.

**The supply-side program failure.** The traditional response to workforce
shortages — create a training program and assume people will come — is
insufficient for this problem. Community colleges that build advanced
manufacturing programs without employer anchor partnerships produce graduates
who cannot get hired because the credential is not what employers recognize
or trust. Employers who want workers cannot find qualified applicants because
the training pathway does not exist or does not produce the specific
capabilities they need. Students who might thrive in technical roles never
discover those roles exist, or cannot see a clear pathway from where they
are to where the job is. The information asymmetry, the credential
misalignment, and the absence of a real hiring commitment at the end of
the pathway combine to make supply-side program creation ineffective at scale.

**What is actually needed.** The problem requires a continuous, real-time
signal architecture — not a program design and launch cycle. Employers must
be able to communicate what they need in a form that training institutions
can act on. Training institutions must be able to translate that signal into
curriculum that produces the capabilities employers actually require.
Students must be able to discover these pathways before they have committed
to a different direction. And all of this must happen at scale, continuously,
as the demand signal evolves with technology, with reshoring progress, and
with the retirement of the knowledge-holding generation.

That is what the WCA's demand signal infrastructure, the DoDA labor market
telemetry, and the Workforce Access Points are built to provide. The
Capability Apprenticeship Compact is how that signal gets converted into
an actual pathway — with a real employer commitment at the end, a real
credential from a real institution, and real wages from day one.

## The Prototype: TRAC at Stadler Rail

The Talent Ready Apprenticeship Connection (TRAC) program, operated by Stadler
Rail out of their manufacturing headquarters in Salt Lake City, Utah, is the
proof-of-concept that this architecture already works in miniature.

The structure is three phases:

**Phase One — High school senior year.** Students apply during junior year
and begin the program as seniors. They operate on a split block schedule:
attending regular high school classes on some days and working a paid,
hands-on schedule at Stadler on the others. They earn wages from day one.

**Phase Two — Associate degree pathway.** After high school graduation,
students transition into a structured split between working at Stadler and
attending Salt Lake Community College. By the third year, they graduate
debt-free with an Associate of Applied Science in Advanced Manufacturing.
The credential is real, recognized, and earned through a combination of
classroom instruction and demonstrated workplace capability.

**Phase Three — Full employment and optional upward mobility.** Upon AAS
completion, students receive full-time employment at Stadler. Those who
want to continue can pursue a four-year bachelor's degree in engineering
or a related field through Stadler's tuition reimbursement program. The
university pathway is available, not required. It is upward mobility, not
the gate.

The program has proven successful enough that other regional manufacturers —
including Doppelmayr, the Swiss cable car company — have joined the TRAC
consortium. The braid works. It produces workers who are better prepared,
less indebted, and more deeply embedded in the regional industrial ecosystem
than four-year degree graduates who enter the same firms through conventional
recruitment.

The Capability Apprenticeship Compact takes this prototype and builds the
coordination architecture that allows it to scale across sectors, regions,
and capability domains.

## What the Compact Is

A Capability Apprenticeship Compact is a formal, WCA-registered agreement
among three parties:

**An employer anchor** — a firm, consortium of firms, or sector partnership
that commits to providing paid work placements, real equipment access, and
hiring commitments for students who complete the pathway. The employer anchor
defines the capability requirements, aligns with the WCA competency framework
for the relevant domain, and participates in curriculum validation.

**A credentialing institution** — typically a community college, but
potentially a regional university, technical school, or accredited provider
that offers the Associate degree or other stackable credential relevant to
the domain. The credentialing institution delivers the formal instruction
component, issues the credential, and maintains articulation agreements that
make credits transferable into four-year degree programs for students who
choose that path.

**A K-12 feeder pathway** — a high school or network of high schools that
provides early entry through a split-schedule senior year program, aligned
with the NCF competency transcript so that the capability record students
carry into the compact is legible, verified, and portable.

The WCA coordinates the compact: certifying that the employer's demand signal
is real and sustained, validating the competency framework against the NCF
where applicable, publishing the pathway through Workforce Access Points so
students and families can find it, and tracking outcomes against pre-registered
performance metrics.

## The Institutional Braid

The architecture works because each institution contributes what it is
distinctively good at, and none of them is asked to do what the others
do better.

**Employers provide** paid work, real equipment, operational context, and
hiring commitments. They define the capability requirements that the training
must meet. They do not design curriculum or issue credentials. The employer
anchor is the demand signal that makes the whole pathway real rather than
aspirational — without a genuine hiring commitment at the end, the compact
is a training program, not an apprenticeship.

**Community colleges provide** credentialed instruction, academic rigor,
and transferable credits. They translate the employer's capability requirements
into a structured curriculum that meets accreditation standards and produces
a credential that is portable beyond any single employer. They maintain the
articulation agreements that preserve the upward mobility lane. Community
colleges are the institutional backbone of the compact because they sit
at the intersection of workforce preparation and academic credentialing —
genuinely committed to both in a way that four-year universities often are
not.

**High schools provide** early entry and the competency transcript foundation.
The split-schedule senior year is possible because the NCF's mastery-based
progression means students who have cleared the core competency requirements
can redirect time toward applied work without academic penalty. The competency
transcript they carry into the compact is the verified capability record that
the employer and the community college can read without translation.

**The WCA provides** coordination, demand signal certification, competency
framework validation, and outcome tracking. It does not run the program.
It ensures that the three parties are aligned, that the demand signal is
real before the pathway activates, and that outcomes are tracked against
pre-registered metrics so the system can learn what works.

**Workforce Access Points** are where the pathway shows up in real life for
students and families. A student who walks into a WAP can see which compacts
are active in their region, what the work involves, what the credential is,
what the wages are during training, what the hiring commitment looks like,
and what the upward mobility lane offers. The information is clear, complete,
and honest. The pathway is presented as what it is: a specific job, a
specific credential, a specific wage, a specific employer. Not a general
preparation for a vague future.

## Sector Scope

The Capability Apprenticeship Compact is not limited to manufacturing,
though manufacturing is the natural starting point given TRAC's success
and the concentrated demand signals from the IDP industrial architecture,
advanced manufacturing corridors, and the semiconductor and defense supply
chain expansion.

The compact architecture applies wherever three conditions hold: a real
employer demand signal that is sustained and verifiable, a credentialing
institution capable of delivering the relevant instruction, and a K-12
pipeline with students ready to enter a split-schedule senior year.

Priority sectors for early compact development include:

**Advanced manufacturing and industrial production.** Precision machining,
tool-and-die, CNC operation, quality systems, additive manufacturing,
semiconductor process technician roles. These are the roles where the
N+2 distributed production doctrine and the IDP supply chain architecture
create concentrated demand that is predictable enough to anchor a multi-year
compact. The TRAC model maps directly.

**Grid infrastructure and energy systems.** Electrical lineworkers, substation
technicians, distributed energy system installers and operators, battery
storage system technicians, geothermal drilling and operations crews. The
clean energy buildout requires a workforce that does not yet exist at the
required scale. Community colleges in energy-producing regions are the
natural credentialing partners.

**Healthcare technical roles.** Surgical technologists, radiologic
technologists, medical laboratory technicians, respiratory therapy, dental
hygiene. These roles require hands-on clinical training that cannot be
delivered through conventional classroom instruction alone. The compact
braid — classroom instruction at the community college, supervised clinical
work at the employer anchor — is the natural architecture.

**Construction and building systems.** Masonry, structural steel, HVAC,
plumbing, electrical, building automation systems. The housing stack's
construction cost reduction objectives require a workforce expansion in
these trades that the current training pipeline cannot deliver at the
required speed or scale.

**Defense and aerospace technical roles.** Avionics technicians, composites
fabricators, electronic systems technicians, UAV maintenance and operations.
These roles have concentrated demand from the defense industrial base and
require security clearance eligibility — a constraint that the compact can
accommodate through background screening integrated into the employer
onboarding process.

**Data infrastructure and digital operations.** Network operations, data
center operations, cybersecurity operations, cloud infrastructure support.
Not software engineering — that has existing pathways — but the operational
and technical support roles that scale with digital infrastructure buildout
and that community colleges can credentialize through recognized certifications
and Associate programs.

## Compact Design Requirements

Every registered Capability Apprenticeship Compact must satisfy the following
before WCA registration is granted and before the pathway is published through
Workforce Access Points.

**Verified demand signal.** The employer anchor must demonstrate that the
hiring commitment is real and sustained — not a projection or a hope.
WCA certifies demand using DoDA's labor market signal methodology. A compact
whose demand signal is uncertain or short-term does not get registered.
Students do not enter a pathway built on an unverified promise.

Where demand exists in an emerging sector — geothermal energy, advanced
nuclear, frontier manufacturing processes — that is credible but not yet
fully mature, compacts may be registered as limited pilots: smaller initial
cohorts, enhanced disclosure to students about the sector's developmental
stage, and no long-term pathway claims until demand verification is sustained
across at least two consecutive annual cycles. The carveout exists because
some of the most important workforce needs are in sectors where the industrial
buildout is ahead of the labor market signal. Excluding them entirely would
reproduce the supply-side lag the compact architecture is designed to prevent.
Registering them at full scale before demand is verified would expose students
to pathway risk. The pilot tier is the resolution.

**Pre-registered competency framework.** The capabilities the compact develops
must be specified before the pathway opens, mapped to the NCF where applicable,
and validated jointly by the employer anchor and the credentialing institution.
The framework is published through Workforce Access Points so students know
exactly what they are learning, why it matters, and how it is assessed.

**Paid work from entry.** Students receive wages from the first day they
enter the paid work component. The compact is not an internship, a practicum,
or an unpaid learning experience. It is employment combined with instruction.
Minimum wage floors and standard labor protections apply throughout.

**Debt-free credential pathway.** The compact is designed so that a student
who completes it does so without tuition debt for the credentialed instruction
component. Employer contributions, WCA-facilitated subsidies, and Pell Grant
eligibility for the community college component combine to make the credential
financially accessible. The debt-free design is a requirement, not an
aspiration — a compact that leaves students with significant tuition debt
has failed its core purpose.

**Preserved upward mobility.** Credits earned through the compact's community
college component must be transferable into four-year degree programs through
active articulation agreements. The Associate degree is a complete, recognized
credential that produces employment. It is also a foundation for further
education for students who choose that path. Neither use forecloses the other.

**Pre-registered outcome metrics.** Before the compact opens, the WCA and
the participating institutions pre-register the metrics against which the
compact will be evaluated: completion rates, placement rates, wage outcomes
at six months and two years, credential transfer rates for students who
choose the university pathway. DoDA tracks these outcomes. Compacts that
persistently underperform against pre-registered metrics face review and
redesign. Compacts that perform well become the template for replication
in other regions.

## The Upward Mobility Lane

One of the most important design features of the TRAC model — and one that
must be preserved at scale — is that the Associate degree pathway is not
a ceiling. Stadler's tuition reimbursement program allows graduates who
want a four-year degree to pursue it while employed, with the employer
covering the cost.

The Capability Apprenticeship Compact formalizes this as a required design
element. Every compact must maintain an active upward mobility lane: a
documented pathway from the Associate credential into a four-year program
through articulation agreements with one or more regional universities,
with employer-supported tuition assistance available for graduates who
choose to continue.

The lane must be real and documented, not aspirational. An articulation
agreement means the credits actually transfer, the receiving institution
has confirmed acceptance of those credits, and a student can map their
path from AAS completion to bachelor's degree completion without repeating
coursework. WCA verifies articulation agreements as part of compact
registration.

The upward mobility lane matters for two reasons beyond the obvious benefit
to individual students.

First, it makes the compact politically and socially durable. Parents and
students who might otherwise resist a non-university pathway are more likely
to enter it if they can see clearly that the door to university is not
closing — it is just being opened through a different entry point. The
compact is not tracked. It is a chosen pathway with preserved optionality.

Second, it is the mechanism through which the compact connects to the
university crowding-in model. A graduate of a defense-sector compact who
develops an interest in materials engineering during their years working
with advanced composites has a documented pathway into an engineering
program. A healthcare technical graduate who discovers an aptitude for
research during clinical rotations can transfer into a biology or chemistry
program. The compact produces workers. It also produces students who arrive
at university with real-world technical context, verified competencies, and
no debt — which is a far more capable student than the median 18-year-old
arriving from a conventional high school pathway.

## Relationship to the WCA and Demand Signals

The WCA does not run Capability Apprenticeship Compacts. It coordinates them.

The WCA's role is to ensure that the demand signal driving a compact is real
before students commit to a pathway built on it, to certify that the competency
framework the compact develops is aligned with the national architecture, to
publish pathways through Workforce Access Points so students can find them,
and to track outcomes so the system learns which compact designs work and
which need redesign.

When the WCA identifies a verified labor shortage in a specific sector and
region — through DoDA's labor market signal methodology — it does not
automatically create a compact. It activates the coordination process: reaching
out to potential employer anchors, identifying community college partners with
relevant program capacity, and working with the K-12 feeder schools in the
region to establish the early entry pipeline. The compact forms when all three
parties commit. The WCA facilitates; it does not mandate.

Where demand exists but no employer anchor has yet formed, the WCA can
publish the demand signal openly through Workforce Access Points and through
sector-specific industry associations, inviting employers to step into the
anchor role. In some cases — particularly in sectors where the demand is
distributed across many small employers rather than concentrated in a few
large ones — employer consortia can serve as the anchor collectively, with
the consortium making the hiring commitment that any individual employer
could not sustain alone.

## Relationship to the National Competency Framework

The NCF is the shared language that makes the compact's credentialing
function legible across institutions and employers.

A student entering a compact carries a competency transcript that documents
what they have already demonstrated. The community college and the employer
can read that transcript without translation — it tells them what foundation
the student has, what they need to develop, and what assessment has already
been completed. The compact does not start from scratch with every entering
student. It starts from a verified baseline.

The competency framework the compact develops is also mapped to the NCF
where applicable. Technical and trade competencies that are domain-specific
— precision machining tolerances, electrical code compliance, clinical
sterile technique — are not NCF competencies in the narrow sense. But the
broader cognitive and operational competencies that the NCF certifies —
quantitative reasoning, communication, systems thinking, problem-solving
under constraint — are developed and demonstrated through the compact's
work, and that development is legible in the competency transcript the
student carries forward.

This means a compact graduate who applies to a university program arrives
with a competency transcript that the university can evaluate. The admissions
alignment framework establishes that university programs should be reading
competency transcripts as meaningful signals of capability. A compact graduate
who has spent three years doing precision work, troubleshooting real systems,
and developing domain expertise has a richer competency record than most
traditional applicants — even if the specific courses look different.

## Relationship to the K-12 Campus Colocation Model

The student-based funding and campus colocation framework concentrates K-12
resources on shared campuses that serve as community anchors and innovation
environments. The Capability Apprenticeship Compact is the natural extension
of that logic into post-secondary pathways.

A high school campus that hosts a manufacturing program, an engineering track,
and a healthcare sciences track is already building the pipeline that feeds
compacts in those sectors. The split-schedule senior year does not require
students to leave their campus — in many cases, the employer partner's facility
can be co-located with or adjacent to the campus, or the WAP embedded in the
campus serves as the interface between the school day and the employer component.

The campus colocation model concentrates resources at the K-12 level. The
compact is how students transition from that concentrated environment into
a pathway that continues to develop their capabilities in a real-world context.
The continuity matters: a student who moves from a well-resourced campus
environment directly into a paid compact pathway does not experience the cliff
that the traditional graduation-to-job-search transition represents. The
pathway is visible, the wages start immediately, and the institutional support
continues through the community college component.

## Relationship to Corporate Reciprocity

Employers that train workers through registered Capability Apprenticeship
Compacts are not merely consuming public workforce infrastructure. They are
helping build it. The compact requires genuine investment: paid wages during
training, equipment access, mentorship from experienced workers, curriculum
validation, and a binding hiring commitment at the end. These are real costs
that produce real national capability.

The corporate reciprocity framework asks which firms are contributing to the
national productive capacity that makes their operations possible, and which
are extracting from it. Participation in registered compacts is a direct and
measurable form of contribution to American capability formation — the kind
of investment that reciprocity scoring should recognize.

Compact participation therefore counts positively in evaluations for public
procurement preferences, PCA partnership eligibility, industrial corridor
incentive access, and workforce-related tax or financing programs. The weight
given to compact participation is proportionate to the scale and quality of
the commitment: number of active compact participants, completion and placement
rates of prior cohorts, wage levels and upward mobility lane utilization, and
consistency of hiring commitment over time.

The compact remains entirely voluntary. Firms may choose not to participate.
Firms that do participate — that invest in workers, in training infrastructure,
and in the regional capability ecosystem — receive recognition when public
programs evaluate which firms are building national capacity rather than
extracting from it. That recognition is the reciprocity incentive, not a
mandate. No firm is required to run an apprenticeship program. Firms that do
are recognized for it.

## Governance and Anti-Gaming

Capability Apprenticeship Compacts are registered with the WCA and subject
to ongoing monitoring through DoDA's outcome tracking infrastructure.

**Anti-displacement safeguard.** Employer anchors may not use compact
apprentices to displace existing workers or suppress wages for the roles
the compact is developing. WCA monitors workforce composition and wage
levels at participating employers. A compact that functions as a wage
suppression mechanism for an employer loses WCA registration.

**Credential integrity.** The Associate degree issued through the compact
must meet the same academic standards as any other degree from the
credentialing institution. The community college cannot issue a compact-
specific credential that is weaker than its standard program — the credential
must be fully portable and recognized by transfer institutions. WCA verifies
accreditation status and articulation agreement validity as part of compact
registration.

**Employer commitment enforcement.** A compact requires a genuine hiring
commitment, not a preference or a hope. If an employer anchor reduces its
hiring commitment during an active compact, WCA must be notified and the
compact enters review. Students currently in the pathway are protected:
the employer commitment to currently enrolled cohorts must be honored.
Future cohorts may be paused pending resolution.

**Outcome transparency.** All compact outcome metrics — completion rates,
placement rates, wage outcomes, transfer rates — are published through
Workforce Access Points and through DoDA's public dashboard. Students
and families evaluating a compact pathway can see how previous cohorts
fared before they commit. No compact operates with opaque outcomes.

**Portable exit rights.** If an employer anchor reduces or withdraws its
hiring commitment, or if a compact is suspended or terminated for any reason,
students currently in the pathway retain everything they have earned:
wage records, earned credits toward the Associate credential, competency
transcript entries, and documented pathway completion to date. The WCA
maintains priority transfer rights for affected students into equivalent
compacts in the same sector where available. No student loses earned
competencies, credits, or wage history because an employer's circumstances
changed. The pathway belongs to the student, not to the employer.

## Pilot Strategy

Initial compact registrations should concentrate in regions and sectors
where three conditions already exist: a verified labor shortage documented
by DoDA, at least one employer anchor willing to make a genuine hiring
commitment, and a community college with existing program capacity in the
relevant domain.

TRAC's consortium model — where Stadler is the anchor but Doppelmayr and
others join — points to the right early structure. A single large employer
with genuine demand and institutional willingness to invest in the pathway
is the easiest starting point. Consortium formation among smaller employers
in the same sector is the harder but ultimately more scalable model for
sectors where demand is distributed.

Year-one pilots should span at least three distinct sectors to demonstrate
generalizability: one manufacturing-oriented compact, one infrastructure or
energy compact, and one healthcare technical compact. Outcome data from
year-one pilots feeds into the WCA's compact design templates, making
subsequent compact formation faster and less dependent on bespoke negotiation.

Success in the pilot phase is defined by three metrics: completion rate
relative to pre-registered target, placement rate at compact completion,
and wage outcome at twelve months relative to the regional median for
comparable roles. Compacts that clear all three in the pilot phase become
eligible for replication support — WCA assistance in forming equivalent
compacts in other regions with similar demand profiles.

## The Governing Doctrine

Learn early. Work while learning. Graduate with the competencies and
credentials you need, wages already earned, and a job pathway already built.

That is what the Capability Apprenticeship Compact delivers. It is not
a new idea — TRAC has been demonstrating it in Salt Lake City. It is not
a government program — the employer provides the work, the community college
provides the credential, the high school provides the entry point. It is
a coordination architecture that makes the institutional braid work at scale,
in more sectors, in more regions, for more students.

The compact answers the credential skepticism problem not by defending the
four-year degree but by offering something better in many contexts: a
pathway that develops real capability, produces recognized credentials,
pays wages throughout, and keeps the door to further education open for
those who want it.

The institution is not the gate. The capability is.

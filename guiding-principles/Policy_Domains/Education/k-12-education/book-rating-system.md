## Purpose

This policy establishes a standardized, transparent book rating system for
use in K–12 education. Its objective is to replace the current pattern of
ad-hoc bans, political pressure, and opaque content decisions with a clear
classification framework that supports age-appropriate learning, parental
trust, and teacher discretion. The system is designed to inform choice, not
restrict access — and to serve as the foundational content layer for
personalized reading recommendation and supervised social learning systems.

---

## The Problem It Solves

Book challenges and removals have become one of the most visible proxy
battlefields in American education. The underlying conflict is not really
about books. It is about who gets to define what children are exposed to,
whose values govern public institutions, and whether schools can be trusted
to make those decisions without explicit parental oversight of every choice.

The current system handles this badly for everyone. Schools are forced into
reactive, high-stakes decisions about individual titles — decisions that are
inherently political because they are binary. A book is either in the
classroom or it is not. There is no middle ground that preserves teacher
discretion, provides parental visibility, and reflects the reality that a
book appropriate for a mature tenth-grader might genuinely not be appropriate
for a seventh-grader, and that both of those things can be true simultaneously
without either representing censorship or negligence.

Teachers and librarians are left without consistent guidance, making decisions
that are then contested, reversed, or made into local political events.
Parents who want meaningful visibility into what their children are reading
have no reliable mechanism for obtaining it. And students whose reading
abilities and emotional maturity vary enormously within the same classroom
are served by a system that can only make one decision per title regardless
of who is reading it.

The result is that the most contested titles get the most attention, the
loudest voices determine outcomes, and the system optimizes for conflict
avoidance rather than for student development.

---

## The Core Design Principle

The system classifies. It does not censor.

No book is removed from availability by default. Instead, every book in the
corpus receives a structured rating across standardized dimensions — reading
difficulty, developmental appropriateness, and content tags — so that
educators, parents, and recommendation systems can make informed, context-
sensitive decisions. Classification does not, by itself, restrict access.
Any removal or restriction requires a separate, explicitly justified decision
outside the rating system — one that runs through a distinct review standard
with documented rationale, independent of the classification process.
The classification record cannot be used as evidence for restriction without
undergoing that separate review process. The rating describes. It does not
decide.

This is exactly how movies, games, and streaming platforms handle content
classification — successfully, at enormous scale, without removing content
from existence. A film rated R is not banned. It is described. Adults make
decisions based on that description. The same logic applies here, with the
additional dimension that educational context matters: a book about historical
trauma is different in a history class than in a recreational reading list,
and the rating system provides the metadata that allows that distinction to
be made.

The system standardizes how books are described, not what is allowed. That
distinction is the entire point.

---

## The Rating Dimensions

Each book in the corpus receives a structured rating across three primary
axes, each designed to answer a different question for the different people
who need to make decisions.

**Reading difficulty** answers the question of whether a student is ready
for the text. It covers lexical and syntactic complexity, conceptual density,
and the background knowledge a reader needs to engage with the material
meaningfully. A single book may legitimately have multiple difficulty ratings
— a challenging novel that rewards a strong reader and frustrates a developing
one is not one thing or the other, and the rating reflects that range.
Difficulty is assessed against developmental reading trajectories, not against
any ideological standard. The system avoids collapsing everything into a
single "grade level" proxy, which is a poor measure of the different
dimensions of reading readiness.

**Age and maturity band** answers the question of whether the content is
developmentally appropriate. It covers five broad ranges — early elementary,
late elementary, middle school, early high school, late high school — defined
by cognitive and emotional maturity rather than by ideology or cultural
preference. A book that deals with mature themes in ways that require
significant emotional processing capacity to engage with constructively is
rated for the developmental stage that capacity typically appears, regardless
of whether the content itself is politically controversial. The maturity band
is separate from difficulty because a book can be simple to read and
emotionally complex, or technically demanding and emotionally straightforward.

**Content tags** answer the question of what is actually in the book, without
making any judgment about whether that content is good or bad. Tags cover
violence — with distinctions between contextual, graphic, and historical —
sexual content, strong language, substance use, mental health themes, and
historical oppression or trauma. Tags are descriptive and non-evaluative.
They indicate presence and intensity, not moral stance. Each tag category
includes standardized threshold definitions with illustrative examples to
ensure consistent application across reviewers — what counts as "graphic"
versus "contextual" violence is defined precisely, not left to individual
interpretation. The system prioritizes signal clarity over exhaustive labeling,
ensuring that tags meaningfully differentiate content rather than overwhelming
users with low-value information.

The three axes work together. A book might have low reading difficulty, a
middle school maturity band, and content tags for mild violence and mental
health themes. That profile tells a teacher, a parent, and a recommendation
system something meaningfully different from a book with high reading
difficulty, a late high school maturity band, and content tags for graphic
violence and sexual content — even if both are considered great literature.

## Governance and Process

Ratings are produced through a hybrid process that combines automated
consistency with human judgment.

Initial tagging is assisted by automated text analysis that provides a
consistent starting point — identifying lexical complexity, flagging content
categories based on textual signals, and producing a preliminary profile
that human reviewers then refine. Automation ensures that the process is
scalable and that obvious cases do not require extensive manual work. Human
judgment is reserved for the decisions that require it: edge cases, context-
dependent interpretations, and the distinctions that automated analysis
cannot reliably make.

Human review panels are composed of educators, librarians, and child
development experts — people with professional expertise in the relevant
domains, selected to represent a range of perspectives without being
constituted as a political body. Inter-rater reliability is continuously
measured across review panels, with discrepancies triggering calibration
reviews and updated tagging guidelines. Calibration reviews occur on a
defined cadence and are triggered dynamically when inter-rater divergence
exceeds predefined thresholds — this is active governance, not passive
monitoring. Comparable content across books is expected to receive comparable
tagging regardless of perspective, author, or historical context; systematic
asymmetry in how reviewers apply the same standards to comparable material
is itself a calibration trigger. This is not a soft commitment —
it is a measurement requirement, because a rating system whose outputs
vary significantly across reviewers applying the same standards has failed
at its core function.

Disputes trigger structured re-review with documented rationale rather than
political escalation. The review and appeal process is designed to be
accessible and time-bounded, ensuring that concerns are addressed through
structured channels rather than escalating into public conflict. Every rating
includes a short justification explaining the basis for the assigned dimensions
and tags, and a revision history that makes the evolution of any rating visible
and auditable.

The governance structure is designed to resist ideological capture at two
levels. Narrow, descriptive tags prevent the rating from becoming a vehicle
for editorial judgment — a tag says what is in the book, not whether that
content is acceptable. Mixed review panels prevent any single perspective
from dominating the process. And the explicit non-ban framing — every book
starts as included, and classification is about describing not deciding —
removes the binary pressure that makes content decisions political.

## Parent Visibility and Teacher Communication

The rating system makes reading assignments legible to families without
requiring constant oversight or intervention. This is the last-mile trust
mechanism: it shifts the question from "do I trust the system?" to "I can
see exactly what's happening."

Any parent can look up any book in the system and see its full rating profile
— reading difficulty, maturity band, content tags, and the short justification
explaining how those ratings were assigned. This is direct, standardized
information that replaces guesswork and secondhand descriptions. A parent
who is curious about what their child is reading for school does not need to
request a meeting or ask the teacher to describe the book. They look it up.
The profile is there.

Teachers use the same system to communicate proactively. When assigning a
book with a higher maturity band or more sensitive content tags, teachers
can send a straightforward notification that includes the book's rating
profile and a brief explanation of how it is being used in context. A history
text tagged for historical trauma might be accompanied by a note explaining
how that content will be discussed and supported in class. A novel tagged
for strong language might include a sentence about its literary context and
educational purpose. Notifications are triggered based on maturity band and content intensity
thresholds, ensuring that communication is consistent and not dependent on
individual teacher discretion — the same book gets the same notification
requirement regardless of which teacher is assigning it. Teachers may also
send notifications voluntarily below threshold when context warrants it,
preserving professional judgment alongside systematic consistency.

For cases where families prefer to opt their child out of a specific material,
the system provides a clear pathway to do so without disrupting the classroom
as a whole. An alternative text can be assigned that aligns with the same
competency goals and reading level. This allows parents to guide their own
child's experience without exercising a veto over what other students read.
The distinction between individual opt-out and curriculum control is
operationalized rather than just asserted: the mechanism exists, the pathway
is clear, and neither requires the other.

The result is a shift from reactive conflict to informed choice. Parents are
not asked to trust opaque decisions — they are given visibility into structured
information. Teachers are not forced into defensive justification — they are
equipped with a shared framework for explaining their choices. And students
are served by a system that can adapt to individual needs without collapsing
into one-size-fits-all decisions driven by whoever objects most loudly.

## How Schools Use the System

The rating system changes the nature of the decisions schools make about
reading materials. Instead of deciding whether a book is allowed, educators
decide which books serve which students in which contexts — a much more
tractable and educationally meaningful question.

Teachers use ratings to select age-appropriate classroom materials with
confidence, differentiate reading assignments within a classroom based on
individual students' reading levels and maturity, and communicate clearly
with parents about what students are reading and why. Librarians use ratings
to build collections that serve the full range of the student population,
make recommendations to students exploring beyond their assigned lists, and
respond to parent concerns with specific, documented information rather than
defensive judgment calls.

Parents retain the ability to guide their own child's reading exposure without
having the authority to dictate curriculum for other people's children. The
rating system makes that distinction navigable in practice. A parent who
objects to their child reading a specific title has a mechanism for acting
on that concern. A parent who wants to remove a title from the classroom
entirely has a review process to engage, but not a unilateral veto. The
boundary is clear, the mechanisms are real, and the system does not require
trust in the abstract — it provides information that makes trust verifiable.

## Integration with the Recommendation and Learning Systems

The rating system is foundational infrastructure for the personalized book
recommendation and supervised social learning pilot. Without standardized,
machine-readable metadata describing each book's difficulty, maturity, and
content profile, a recommendation system cannot make meaningful matches
between books and readers. With it, the system can match books to reading
level and emotional maturity simultaneously, build reading progressions that
advance difficulty and complexity in developmentally appropriate ways, and
filter content for supervised discussion environments based on explicit,
documented criteria rather than opaque algorithmic decisions.

This integration is why the rating system is designed to be machine-readable
and structured rather than narrative and descriptive. A human reviewer writing
"this book deals with mature themes" provides useful information for a reader.
A structured tag indicating "sexual content: implied; mental health themes:
present; reading difficulty: grade 8-10" provides the inputs a recommendation
system can act on. The rating system is designed to serve both audiences
simultaneously.

Over time, as the recommendation system generates data on which materials
contribute to reading progression and engagement at which developmental
stages, that data can inform how the rating system describes books and how
recommendations are sequenced — without prescribing content or overriding
educator judgment. The connection between the content layer and the outcome
layer closes gradually, through evidence rather than mandate.

---

## Privacy Architecture

The system tracks materials, not people. Ratings are public and fully
transparent — every book's rating, its justification, and its revision
history are accessible to anyone who wants to see them. Individual student
reading choices and behaviors are private, governed by the same SSI-linked
pseudonymous architecture that protects student data across the education
stack. A recommendation system knows that a student at a certain reading
level with certain content preferences exists — it does not know which student
that is, and that information does not flow to the rating system or to any
public record.

## Pilot Scope

The pilot focuses on core reading lists in opt-in districts, a limited but
diverse book corpus that includes titles across difficulty levels, genre,
and content profiles, and clear documentation and parent-facing interfaces
that make the rating system legible to families without requiring technical
knowledge. The pilot corpus is intentionally diverse — not just in content
but in the distribution of ratings. A pilot that only rates uncontroversial
books does not test the system's capacity to de-escalate the conflicts it
is designed to address. The pilot must include titles that have been challenged
or banned in other districts to demonstrate that the rating system provides
a genuine alternative to the binary allow-or-ban decision.

Initial success metrics are parental trust, reduction in the frequency and
intensity of book challenges, teacher confidence in making and defending
content decisions, and student engagement with assigned and recommended
reading. If the pilot data shows that tagged books are being used less than
comparable untagged books, that is a signal that the system is producing
chilling effects rather than informed choice — and the pilot revises or
terminates accordingly.

## Failure Modes

**Ideological capture** is the most serious failure mode. If the review panels
are constituted in ways that produce systematically slanted ratings —
consistently tagging books from certain perspectives more harshly than
comparable books from other perspectives — the system becomes a tool for
the political goal it was designed to replace. The narrow, descriptive tag
structure is the primary defense: a tag for "historical oppression or trauma"
applies the same way to a book about the Holocaust and a book about indigenous
land seizure. Inter-rater reliability measurement is the detection mechanism:
systematic divergence across panels applying the same standards surfaces
bias before it compounds.

**Over-labeling** occurs when reviewers apply tags more broadly than the
standardized thresholds warrant — treating a mild reference to alcohol as
equivalent to a book where substance use is a central theme, for example.
This degrades the signal value of the tags and produces the same kind of
undifferentiated caution that the system is designed to replace. Standardized
threshold definitions with illustrative examples, combined with inter-rater
reliability tracking, are the primary defenses.

**Chilling effects on authors and publishers** emerge if the rating system
becomes associated with content restriction rather than content description.
If authors or publishers perceive that certain content tags effectively prevent
their books from being used in schools, the system creates incentives to
self-censor before publication. The explicit non-ban framing, the default
inclusion principle, and the pilot metrics that track whether book challenges
decrease rather than book use decreases are the defenses. The hard
institutional boundary — classification does not restrict access, restriction
requires a separate explicit decision — must be maintained in practice, not
just in policy.

**Metadata laundering through euphemism** is a subtler capture risk specific
to descriptive taxonomies. Reviewers technically comply with the tag structure
but systematically soften or blur tags in one ideological direction —
describing graphic content as contextual, or applying trauma tags inconsistently
across books covering similar historical events depending on whose history is
being described. This is exactly how institutional capture tends to operate:
not through visible policy change, but through accumulated small judgment calls
that drift in a consistent direction. Inter-rater reliability measurement is
the primary detection mechanism, because systematic asymmetry in how reviewers
apply the same standards to comparable content surfaces in the data before
it compounds into a pattern that has lost the confidence of the communities
the system is supposed to serve.

---

## Conclusion

The book rating system does not resolve the underlying cultural conflict over
what children should read. That conflict is real and it is not going away.
What the system does is give that conflict a productive channel — one where
the question is "how do we describe this book accurately and use it
appropriately" rather than "is this book allowed to exist in schools."

Trust is not built by removing decisions from the system. It is built by
making decisions legible — by giving parents direct access to structured
information, by equipping teachers with a shared framework for explaining
their choices, and by creating opt-out pathways that respect individual
family values without vesting any family with veto power over the curriculum
for everyone else's children.

Classification instead of censorship. Description instead of decision.
Visibility without veto power. Information instead of politics.

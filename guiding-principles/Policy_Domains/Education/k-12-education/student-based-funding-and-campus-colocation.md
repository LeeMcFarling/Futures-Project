## Purpose

This framework replaces geographically allocated school funding with a
student-based model in which resources follow students through an SSI-linked
system, and pairs that funding reform with co-location campus infrastructure
that prevents the fragmentation and stratification that student-choice models
typically produce.

The funding reform and the campus model are designed together. Neither works
without the other. Student-based funding without shared infrastructure produces
geographic clustering and unequal access. Shared infrastructure without
reformed funding reproduces the property-tax inequality it was built to
address. Together, they create a system that is simultaneously competitive
and equitable — one where schools innovate and differentiate freely, students
and families choose among real options, and the structural conditions for
inequality are removed rather than managed around.

This is not a voucher system operating across fragmented schools. It is a
structured, shared-campus system where choice exists within a common
environment, competition operates under validated outcome standards, and
integration is built into the physical infrastructure rather than aspirationally
appended to it.

## The Problem with How Schools Are Currently Funded

School funding in the United States is tied primarily to local property taxes.
The consequence is structural and self-reinforcing: wealthy areas generate
high property tax revenue, which funds well-resourced schools, which attract
families who can afford to live near them, which sustains property values,
which sustains the funding advantage. Poor areas generate low property tax
revenue, which produces underfunded schools, which reduces the neighborhood's
attractiveness to families with options, which sustains low property values,
which sustains the funding deficit.

This cycle is not an accident of implementation. It is a designed output of
a system that makes educational access a function of where a family can afford
to live. A student born into a low-income neighborhood does not merely face
economic disadvantage — they face a school system that is structurally funded
to serve them less well than their peers across town, regardless of their
capability or potential. The geographic lock-in compounds it further: because
school assignment is tied to residence, a family cannot escape a failing school
without moving — which requires the economic resources that the cycle withholds.

The United States is the wealthiest country in the world. The persistence of
this cycle is not a resource problem. It is a design problem. And design
problems can be fixed.

## Scaling Excellence, Not Centralizing Control

Before describing the mechanics of the reform, it is worth naming the broader
principle it applies — because that principle clarifies what this model is
and what it is not.

In the United States, high-quality physical and institutional infrastructure
is already concentrated — but almost entirely at the university level. Research
universities and flagship institutions benefit from dense capital investment:
advanced labs, athletic and training facilities, performance spaces, libraries,
and specialized staff. These environments produce strong outcomes not because
of curriculum alone, but because resources are pooled, concentrated, and shared
across large populations of students who all benefit from the shared
infrastructure around them.

K–12 education, by contrast, distributes limited resources across thousands
of geographically fragmented schools, each attempting to replicate a full
educational environment at small scale. The result is not equity but dilution:
no single site has the scale to sustain the depth of infrastructure that already
exists elsewhere in the education system. A rural school serving four hundred
students cannot maintain a proper athletic training facility, a maker lab, a
performance space, and a specialized STEM environment simultaneously. So it
maintains none of them well.

The co-location campus model applies the same principle of resource
concentration that already works at the university level to K–12 — but pairs
it with student-based funding and universal access. Funding follows students
into shared regional campuses, allowing those campuses to sustain facilities
and staff at a level that individual schools cannot. The difference from the
university model is that access to these environments is not restricted by
admissions, income, or geography. Every student in the region participates
in a high-resource environment by default.

This is not a new idea imported from outside the system. It is the extension
of an existing pattern — concentrated educational infrastructure producing
stronger outcomes — to the level where it has the greatest public impact and
where it has historically been most absent.

The framing matters: this is about scaling excellence downward through the
system, not centralizing control upward. The campus concentrates resources.
It does not concentrate authority. Programs compete within it. Students choose
among them. Teachers specialize and build expertise within a richer environment
than any single small school could sustain. The outcome is more diversity of
educational experience, not less — enabled by the shared foundation beneath it.

## Campuses as Community Anchors

One concern this model must address directly: regional campuses can read as
consolidation — as school closures, loss of local identity, and the replacement
of neighborhood institutions with centralized facilities that feel distant from
the communities they serve.

That is not what this model proposes, and the design actively resists that
outcome.

Co-location campuses are community infrastructure, not replacement institutions.
They are designed to serve the surrounding community beyond school hours —
athletic facilities available to local leagues and adult programs, performance
spaces available for community events, common areas that function as genuine
public gathering spaces. The campus is a community anchor precisely because
it concentrates resources that the community can use, not a closed institution
that extracts students from local contexts and deposits them somewhere else.

The program diversity requirement reinforces this. A campus hosting a sports
academy, an arts program, a STEM track, and a classical humanities program is
not a single school with a large footprint. It is a community of distinct
educational cultures sharing infrastructure. Students in different programs
develop different identities, different communities of practice, and different
senses of belonging — within a shared physical environment that connects rather
than homogenizes them.

The community anchor framing also changes the political coalition for the
model. A co-location campus is not just an education policy — it is a community
development investment. Local governments, parks and recreation departments,
civic organizations, and community development advocates all have stakes in
high-quality shared physical infrastructure. The campus becomes a site of
convergence for interests that rarely align in education debates, which is
exactly the kind of broad coalition that durable reform requires.

## The Funding Model

The core reform is straightforward: funding follows the student, not the
address. Each student receives a baseline per-student allocation through
SSI-linked infrastructure, portable across schools and programs, allocated
in real time based on enrollment, and fully visible and auditable. A student
who enrolls in a program carries their funding with them. A program that
attracts more students receives more funding. A program that loses students
loses funding.

This is not a pure voucher system. Two constraints distinguish it from
unstructured school choice.

The first is weighted funding. Not all students carry the same allocation.
Students with higher needs — defined by socioeconomic status, disability or
special needs, language learning requirements, and regional cost differences
— carry larger per-student allocations. The design intent is explicit: higher-
need students carry more funding, not less. This inverts the current system's
logic, which effectively concentrates resources on students whose families
have the most options. Under weighted funding, programs are financially
incentivized to serve higher-need students because doing so increases their
revenue. Combined with cohort-adjusted outcome evaluation, programs cannot
game this incentive by enrolling high-need students and then underserving them
— the funding advantage only holds if the outcomes hold.

The second constraint is DES certification. Programs cannot receive funding
without maintaining validated learning outcomes. Funding eligibility is
contingent on continued DES certification, which is contingent on student
trajectories meeting expected standards. A program that is growing in
enrollment but declining in outcomes does not get to keep growing. The
demand-outcome divergence trigger activates, and constraint logic applies
before the program can scale its failure.

## The Co-Location Campus Model

A co-location campus is a shared physical infrastructure platform that hosts
multiple schools and programs with different specializations, serving students
of a common developmental stage, on a single site. Each campus includes proper
athletic and training facilities, academic classrooms and learning spaces,
technical labs and maker infrastructure, performance and arts venues, and
common social and community areas. These are core campus infrastructure —
shared across all programs, available to every student on the campus regardless
of which program they attend.

Campuses are stratified by developmental stage rather than by geography.
Elementary campuses serve early learners across programs. Middle school
campuses serve the transition years. High school campuses host the full range
of specializations available at that developmental level. This stratification
reflects the reality that what students need from their physical and social
environment changes significantly across the K-12 span, and that the programs
best suited to serve each stage should be co-located with programs serving
the same stage rather than scattered across a district.

Each campus must maintain a minimum diversity of program offerings across
core specialization domains, ensuring meaningful choice within the campus
environment rather than nominal choice between two near-identical programs.
A campus with only one or two programs is not a co-location campus — it is
a school with a large footprint. The minimum composition requirement ensures
real competition and real optionality within the shared environment.

Programs below minimum viable enrollment thresholds are subject to
consolidation or sunset, ensuring that campus resources are not diluted across
unsustainable offerings. The campus is a platform for viable programs, not a
permanent home for failing ones.

## The Spine as the Integration Layer

The shared spine is what makes programmatic diversity on a co-location campus
educationally coherent rather than administratively convenient. Every student
on the campus — regardless of which program they attend — develops the same
core NCF competencies through the same standardized learning infrastructure
during the core competency hours. Their programs diverge in the hours that
remain.

This shared foundation means that students across programs are genuinely
comparable in core academic development, and that switching between programs
does not require academic reconstruction. Because programs are co-located,
switching between them does not require relocation, transportation changes,
or academic reset — reducing switching cost to near zero. A student who
discovers that a sports academy is not the right fit can move to an arts
program on the same campus without disrupting their family's logistics, losing
their competency progress, or starting over socially.

Because students move between programs within a shared campus while maintaining
a consistent competency measurement framework, the system naturally generates
comparable learning trajectories across different educational models. DES can
isolate the causal contribution of program design to student outcomes by
comparing trajectories across programs within the shared measurement framework
— identifying which designs produce stronger competency development, which
specialization approaches enhance or degrade spine outcomes, and what the
causal contribution of different campus structures is to student trajectories.
These observed differences directly feed into DES trigger thresholds, informing
program expansion, constraint, or replication decisions. The campus is not just
a market for program competition. It is a learning market that continuously
produces knowledge about what works.

## Why Co-Location Prevents the Failure Modes of School Choice

School choice systems without shared infrastructure produce predictable failure
modes. Programs cluster geographically, reproducing the access inequalities
of the system they replaced. Students from different socioeconomic backgrounds
attend different schools in different neighborhoods and never encounter each
other. High-performing programs concentrate in areas with engaged families who
know how to navigate choice systems, while struggling communities are left with
whatever remains after sorting has occurred.

Co-location prevents each of these failures structurally.

Geographic clustering is prevented because programs are hosted on shared
campuses rather than operating as freestanding schools. A family chooses a
program on a campus that serves their developmental stage within a reasonable
regional travel distance — with transportation guaranteed as part of the
funding model. The campus is the geographic anchor; programs are portable
within it.

Income-based sorting is disrupted because the campus hosts multiple programs
simultaneously. Students from different economic backgrounds, attending
different programs, share the physical environment — common areas, athletic
facilities, social spaces, and campus-wide activities. The sorting that happens
when families with more information and more options select into better programs
at different locations does not eliminate mixing when those programs are
co-located on the same site.

When a program is oversubscribed, enrollment is allocated through transparent,
non-selective mechanisms — lottery within eligibility bands — with expansion
eligibility determined by sustained demand-outcome alignment under DES
validation. This prevents hidden admissions processes, implicit selection
criteria, and the early stratification that prestige capture produces when
popular programs are allowed to select their own students. Program expansion
and recognition are contingent on demonstrated ability to deliver outcomes
across diverse student populations, ensuring that prestige attaches to
demonstrated impact rather than to selective enrollment.

## Special Education and the Funding Pooling Advantage

The co-location model resolves a specific failure in how special education
is currently funded and delivered. Schools across the country are legally
required to meet IEP obligations but frequently lack the funding to do so,
because special education resources are distributed by district rather than
by student need. Districts with limited resources cannot afford the specialist
staff that effective special education requires. Students in lower-income
districts receive legally mandated services at lower quality than students in
wealthier districts — a reproduction of the general funding inequality at the
level of the students who can least absorb it.

Co-location campuses with weighted per-student funding change this dynamic
in two ways. First, because high-need students carry larger per-student
allocations, a campus that serves a mix of students — including those with
IEPs and significant needs — generates pooled funding sufficient to support
the specialist staff and infrastructure those students require. The specialist
is present on the campus, serving students across all programs, rather than
being distributed thinly across a district or absent from underfunded schools.

Second, the integration that co-location produces benefits all students, not
only as an equity measure for students with special needs. Students who
interact with peers with disabilities in shared campus environments develop
social understanding and relational capacity that geographically segregated
education systems do not produce. The campus breadth requirements — structured
activities that bring students from different programs together — ensure that
this integration is substantive rather than incidental. Proximity is not
enough; the campus design must create genuine interaction.

## The Bipartisan Structure of the Argument

The model is designed to be politically legible to constituencies with
different priorities, because it genuinely serves both sets of concerns rather
than simply claiming to.

For those who prioritize parental choice, competition, and reduced centralized
control: funding follows the student, families choose among real program
options on shared campuses, and schools compete for enrollment by producing
outcomes that families value. No central authority dictates which program a
student attends or which programs survive. The campus concentrates resources
— it does not concentrate authority.

For those who prioritize equity, integration, and public accountability:
weighted funding advantages higher-need students, shared campuses prevent
geographic and income-based segregation, and DES outcome validation ensures
that competition produces genuine learning rather than marketing. The equity
mechanisms are structural, not aspirational.

The design is not a compromise between these positions. It is an architecture
that makes both sets of values achievable simultaneously, because the failure
modes of each approach — pure centralization produces geographic inequality
and no innovation, pure choice produces fragmentation and stratification —
are addressed by the combination of the funding model and the campus model
working together.

## Failure Modes

**Market fragmentation** is the failure mode most associated with choice
systems. If programs proliferate without shared infrastructure, families face
an inequitably accessible menu of options, transportation costs become
prohibitive for lower-income families, and the social integration benefits of
diverse educational environments disappear. The co-location campus model is
the primary defense — programs compete within a shared physical environment
rather than as geographically dispersed institutions.

**Cream skimming** occurs when programs select for easier-to-serve students
to improve their measured outcomes. The weighted funding model addresses this
directly: programs that enroll higher-need students receive more funding, and
their outcomes are evaluated against cohort-adjusted expectations. Enrolling
a high-need student and then underserving them does not improve a program's
DES evaluation. The oversubscription lottery rule prevents programs from
selecting their own students implicitly through admissions processes.

**Low-depth program proliferation** is the risk that campus programs optimize
for engagement and popularity rather than genuine educational depth. The DES
demand-outcome divergence trigger is the primary defense: programs that attract
students without producing outcomes face enrollment caps and required redesign.
High-engagement, low-depth programs are subject to improvement requirements
or sunset — the system rewards substance, not performance.

**Infrastructure inequality** reproduces itself if campus facilities vary
significantly in quality across regions. Standardized facility baselines and
public capital investment in shared campus infrastructure are the structural
defenses. The opportunity here is real: the campus model can finally deliver
to K-12 students the kind of resource-dense environment that has long existed
for university students — but only if the investment standard is held uniformly
across income levels and regions.

**Community displacement** is the perception risk that regional campuses
replace rather than anchor communities. The community infrastructure design —
shared facilities open beyond school hours, campus spaces available for local
use, program diversity that creates distinct educational cultures rather than
a single institutional identity — is the primary defense. Campuses must be
designed and operated as community assets from the beginning, not retrofitted
as community-facing after the fact.

---

## Success Criteria

The model succeeds if the diversity of educational programs available to
students increases across the income distribution — not just in wealthy areas
where program diversity already exists. It succeeds if student outcomes improve
system-wide with particular strength in communities where the current funding
model is most harmful. It succeeds if geographic inequality in educational
access decreases measurably — in funding levels, in facility quality, and in
the range of program options accessible to students regardless of family income.
It succeeds if co-location produces genuine social integration — not just shared
addresses, but visible cross-program interaction and community formation. And
it succeeds if campus infrastructure serves the broader community beyond school
hours, functioning as a genuine regional anchor rather than a closed
institutional campus.

## Conclusion

The current school funding model is not a neutral system that produces
inequality as a side effect. It is a system designed around property values
that produces inequality as a structural output. Fixing it requires replacing
the mechanism, not adjusting it at the margins.

Student-based funding replaces the mechanism. Co-location infrastructure
prevents the fragmentation that funding reform alone produces. Weighted
allocations ensure that the students who most need better options carry the
funding that makes serving them financially viable. DES outcome validation
ensures that competition produces learning rather than marketing. And the
campus infrastructure investment extends to K-12 students the same principle
of resource concentration that already produces stronger outcomes at the
university level — this time paired with universal access rather than selective
admission.

The result is a system where schools compete on what they actually produce,
students choose among genuine options regardless of their family's address,
communities gain shared infrastructure that serves everyone rather than sorting
them apart, and the wealthiest country in the world finally builds educational
infrastructure worthy of that distinction — not for the few, but for all of
them.

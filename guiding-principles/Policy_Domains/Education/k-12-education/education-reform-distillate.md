# What We're Building and Why
## A Condensed Overview of Education Reform

*The Futures Project — April 2026*

---

## The Short Version

The American education system was designed for a different century, a different
economy, and a different country. It produces wildly unequal outcomes based
on where children happen to be born. It wastes enormous amounts of teacher
time on tasks that don't require teachers. It measures whether students were
present rather than whether they learned anything. And it has been patched
repeatedly without ever being redesigned.

This is the redesign.

The core idea is simple: stop standardizing what students are taught, and
start guaranteeing what they can do. Build shared infrastructure that delivers
that guarantee to every student regardless of where they live or which school
they attend. Give schools genuine freedom to specialize and differentiate on
top of that foundation. Measure outcomes honestly and hold programs accountable
for them. And make the whole thing transparent enough that parents, teachers,
and communities can actually see what is happening and trust it.

This document explains what that means in practice.

---

## Why the Current System Fails

The way American schools are funded is the first problem, and it is the
most straightforward. Schools are funded primarily through local property
taxes. Wealthy neighborhoods have high property values, which generate high
tax revenue, which fund well-resourced schools, which attract more families
who can afford to live there, which sustains the property values. Poor
neighborhoods have low property values, which generate low tax revenue, which
produce underfunded schools. This is not an accident or a side effect. It
is the designed output of a system that makes educational quality a function
of your family's address.

A child born in a well-funded suburb and a child born in an underfunded
rural district or urban neighborhood are not getting the same education.
They are not going to get the same education under the current system
regardless of how hard their teachers work, because the resources simply
are not there. And because school assignment is tied to where you live,
a family that cannot afford to move cannot escape a failing school.

The second problem is how time works. Schools advance students based on how
long they have been sitting in class, not on whether they have actually
learned anything. A student who masters a concept in a week has to wait for
the class to catch up. A student who needs three weeks moves on anyway when
the schedule says to. The result is that students accumulate gaps that never
get filled, and those gaps compound over years into the large achievement
differences we observe between students who are nominally in the same grade.

The third problem is what teachers are being asked to do. A single teacher
serving thirty students simultaneously, all at different levels, learning
at different speeds, with different backgrounds — and doing this while also
grading everything, managing administrative paperwork, and conducting parent
communications — is being asked to do something that is not humanly possible
at the quality level we claim to expect. The system produces teacher burnout
and high turnover not because teachers are uncommitted but because the job
as currently designed is unsustainable.

The fourth problem is measurement. We do not reliably know whether students
are learning. Grades vary so much in what they represent across schools and
teachers that a B in one school means something completely different from
a B in another. Standardized tests capture a snapshot of one kind of
performance on one kind of day, and they are heavily influenced by access
to test preparation resources — which families can afford to purchase. Neither
measure tells us whether a student can actually use what they have learned
in the real world.

---

## The Core Idea: Standardize Outcomes, Not Content

The fundamental reorientation of this reform is captured in one sentence:
standardize what students must be able to do, not what schools must teach.

This distinction matters enormously. When you standardize content — mandating
the same reading lists, the same curriculum sequences, the same instructional
methods — you immediately create political conflict over whose content gets
mandated, you constrain teachers and schools in ways that undermine creativity
and local relevance, and you still don't guarantee that students actually
learned anything. Exposure to content is not the same as capability.

When you standardize outcomes instead — defining what a student must be able
to demonstrate before advancing, and building the infrastructure to measure
whether they can actually do it — you get accountability for what matters
without controlling how schools get there. Two schools teaching entirely
different material can both produce students who can reason with evidence,
construct an argument, apply mathematical thinking to unfamiliar problems,
and understand how their government works. The destination is the same. The
path is theirs.

---

## The Learning Spine

The practical expression of this idea is what this reform calls the learning
spine: a standardized, open-source, AI-assisted infrastructure that every
student works through to develop core competencies. Think of it less like
a software product and more like a public utility — roads, not cars.

The spine does several things that individual teachers cannot do at scale.
It diagnoses where each student actually is, not where the calendar says
they should be. It adapts the pacing and approach to each learner. It
continuously checks whether understanding is genuine — not just whether a
student can repeat the right answer in a familiar format, but whether they
can apply what they know to a situation they haven't seen before. And when
a student is stuck, it keeps working with them until they are not stuck,
rather than moving on because the schedule demands it.

This is not a replacement for teachers. It is infrastructure that removes
the tasks that prevent teachers from doing their actual job. Grading —
eliminated. Repetitive instruction of the same concept to students at
different levels — handled by the system. Tracking individual progress
manually — automated. What remains is the work that only humans can do:
noticing that a student is discouraged and figuring out why, leading a
conversation in which students build genuine understanding through dialogue,
designing a project that makes an abstract concept real, being a person
a child can trust.

The spine handles the logistics of learning. Teachers handle the humanity
of it.

Evidence from schools that have piloted this approach suggests that core
academic instruction through the spine takes approximately two hours per
day. The rest of the school day — four to five hours — becomes available
for everything that the current system treats as an afterthought: physical
activity, arts, applied projects, social development, and life skills.
These are not less important than academic competencies. In many cases they
are more important. The current system cannot make time for them because
delivery of academic content consumes the entire day.

---

## What Students Must Learn

The reform defines four domains of competency that every student must
demonstrate before leaving school. These are not subjects in the traditional
sense. They are capabilities.

**Quantitative and scientific reasoning.** Can the student think with numbers
and evidence? Can they recognize when a mathematical approach is called for
and apply it? Can they evaluate a scientific claim, understand what evidence
supports it, and identify what would change their view? This is not about
memorizing formulas. It is about thinking.

**Language and communication.** Can the student read a complex text and
genuinely understand it? Can they construct a written argument that is clear,
structured, and supported by evidence? Can they explain their reasoning
verbally and engage with someone else's argument critically and charitably?

**Civics and historical reasoning.** Does the student understand how their
government works — not as an opinion about whether it is good, but as a
factual understanding of how the branches operate, what the separation of
powers means, how federal and state authority relate? Can they analyze a
primary historical source, identify who wrote it and why, and evaluate
competing accounts of what happened? Can they make an evidence-based argument
about a historical question?

**Physical and health competency.** Can the student demonstrate measurable
physical capability and improvement over time? Do they understand how training,
nutrition, and recovery affect the body? Can they design and adapt a plan
for their own physical development? This is about developing people who
understand and can take responsibility for their physical health — not about
sorting students by athletic talent.

These four domains are guaranteed through the spine. Every student, everywhere,
develops them through the same infrastructure. Schools can build whatever
they want on top of this foundation. The foundation itself is not optional.

---

## Schools Can Do Whatever They Want. On Top of That Foundation.

This is the design move that makes the whole system work politically and
practically. The spine guarantees the core competencies. Schools are genuinely
free to specialize in everything else.

A school that wants to center its extended program on competitive robotics
does that. A school focused on classical humanities does that. A sports
academy, an arts conservatory, an entrepreneurship program, an environmental
science track — all of these are legitimate and valuable, and the reform
does not constrain any of them. What it does is guarantee that every student
in all of those programs is also developing genuine mathematical reasoning,
reading comprehension, civic knowledge, and physical capability through the
shared infrastructure.

This is different from saying "schools can choose their curriculum." It
means something more specific: the guarantee is not in the school's choices,
it is in the infrastructure that delivers the outcomes regardless of those
choices. A parent does not have to trust that their child's school is making
good content decisions. They can look at their child's competency record and
see directly what their child can demonstrate.

## How Schools Are Organized and Funded

The reform replaces property-tax-based school funding with a system where
money follows students. Every student receives a per-student allocation that
they carry with them to whichever program they attend. Students with greater
needs — due to disability, economic disadvantage, language learning requirements
— carry larger allocations. This inverts the current system's logic: the
students who most need resources are the ones who currently receive the least,
because they tend to live in areas with lower property values.

These per-student allocations flow into co-location campuses — shared physical
infrastructure that hosts multiple schools and specialized programs on the
same site. Think of these less like a school and more like a community campus
that has several schools in it.

Why co-location? Because the standard failure mode of school choice systems
is that programs cluster geographically, wealthy families navigate the system
more effectively, and the diversity of options ends up reproducing the
inequality it was supposed to address. Co-location prevents this by making
the campus the geographic anchor rather than individual schools. A student
who wants a STEM program and a student who wants an arts program are both
on the same campus. They share a gym, a cafeteria, common areas, and
scheduled campus-wide activities. They interact across program boundaries
every day, by design.

Co-location also enables something that small individual schools cannot do:
resource concentration. The United States already knows how to build high-
quality educational environments — research universities have world-class
labs, athletic facilities, performance spaces, and specialized staff. We have
just never applied that ambition to K-12 education. A co-location campus with
proper infrastructure can sustain specialist staff, advanced facilities, and
diverse program offerings in ways that a small standalone school serving
four hundred students never could.

When a specialized program is oversubscribed, enrollment is allocated by
lottery rather than by admissions criteria. Programs that attract students
without producing good outcomes are capped, required to redesign, and
eventually shut down if they cannot improve. Programs that produce exceptional
outcomes are studied, documented, and their approaches are propagated across
the system. The market for school programs rewards actual learning, not
marketing.

## What Happens to Teachers

The teacher transition is where this reform is most easily misunderstood.
The spine does not replace teachers. It changes what teachers do.

Under the current system, teachers spend significant portions of their time
on tasks that do not require their expertise: grading repetitive work,
delivering the same explanation multiple times to students at different stages,
tracking individual progress manually, and managing administrative paperwork.
These tasks consume time without producing proportional value, and they crowd
out the work that actually requires a skilled human being.

When the spine handles those tasks, teachers gain time to do what only they
can do. They interpret signals from the system — when a student is stuck,
is it a conceptual misunderstanding, a motivation problem, or something
happening in their life? They lead Socratic discussions that build reasoning
rather than deliver conclusions. They design projects that make competencies
real in ways that engage students. They notice when a student has withdrawn
from the classroom community and figure out why. They build the relationships
that make learning feel worth the effort.

This is a more demanding and more meaningful job than the one it replaces.
It requires different skills — facilitation, intervention, mentorship —
that are developed through structured training in actual spine-enabled
classrooms, not through a weekend workshop.

The reform offers teachers a genuine deal: less grading, more meaningful
work, clearer career paths, and the ability to specialize in what they are
actually good at. A teacher with extraordinary skill at mathematical reasoning
facilitation can concentrate on that. A teacher with deep expertise in Socratic
discussion can build that as their professional identity. The current system
has no mechanism for this kind of specialization. The new system makes it
the basis of a career.

## Reading, Social Learning, and Digital Citizenship

One specific piece of the reform addresses a problem that has not been
adequately confronted: commercial social media platforms are competing for
student attention with extraordinary sophistication, and schools are mostly
pretending this isn't happening.

The book rating system and the reading recommendation pilot are built on the
recognition that you cannot restore reading as a habit by assigning more books.
You have to build something that competes for attention on different terms —
not by mimicking TikTok's engagement mechanics, but by offering something
TikTok cannot: reading as a social, communal, developmental experience.

The rating system classifies every book in a standardized corpus across three
dimensions: how hard it is to read, how mature the content is developmentally,
and what is actually in it — described factually rather than evaluated
morally. This classification is public. Any parent can look up any book and
see its full rating profile. Teachers send structured notifications when
assigning books above certain maturity thresholds. Families can opt their
child out of a specific title without acquiring the ability to remove it
from everyone else's child's education.

The recommendation system uses this classification to match students to books
based on where their competency trajectory is and what they have expressed
interest in. It does not maximize the time students spend on the platform.
It tries to match students to books they will actually read and benefit from.
Students who finish a book are matched into small discussion cohorts —
five to eight peers at similar reading levels and at similar points in the
book — where they discuss what they read in an asynchronous, supervised
environment that is structured around comprehension, interpretation, and
genuine disagreement rather than reaction counts and engagement metrics.

This is not school TikTok. It is a deliberate alternative to it — one that
treats reading as a social experience worth having, builds the discourse norms
that commercial platforms erode, and does so within a structure that parents
can trust and teachers can manage.

## Accountability Without Control

The reform is only as good as its ability to know whether it is working and
respond when it is not.

The Department of Educational Standards — a restructured version of the
current federal Department of Education — holds that accountability function.
It does not operate schools, prescribe curriculum, or manage local education
decisions. What it does is define what counts as learning, validate whether
programs are producing it, and trigger structured intervention when they are
not.

The key design choice is that DES reads outcome data directly from the spine,
independent of what schools report about themselves. A school cannot manage
its DES evaluation by managing its reporting. The learning signals are
generated by the spine and read by DES without school intermediation. This
is the feature that makes the accountability genuine rather than performative.

DES evaluates trajectories, not snapshots. The question is not whether a
school's students are scoring above a threshold on a given test. The question
is whether students are learning at the expected rate given where they started.
A school serving students who arrive with significant gaps may produce below-
average absolute outcomes while generating stronger trajectories than a school
serving advantaged students. The evaluation isolates what the program is
actually contributing rather than rewarding favorable demographics.

Intervention when programs are failing is structured, graduated, and
proportionate. A single bad period triggers monitoring. Sustained deviation
triggers a diagnostic request, then a targeted review, then enrollment
constraints, then potential shutdown if the program cannot recover. The same
framework rewards exceptional performance: programs that significantly
outperform comparable programs are studied, documented, and supported in
sharing what they have learned. The system catches failure early and propagates
success deliberately.

## Beyond K-12

The competency record a student builds through the spine does not stop being
useful when they graduate. It is the thread that connects K-12 to everything
that comes next.

Universities have historically evaluated applicants using three signals — GPA,
standardized test scores, and extracurricular activities — all of which are
poor measures of genuine capability and all of which are heavily influenced
by family wealth and access to preparation resources. The competency transcript
is a better signal: longitudinal, multi-modal, independently validated, and
impossible to inflate at the institutional level because DES reads it directly
rather than relying on school-reported grades.

The transition to competency-based university admissions is staged over a
decade. Universities are not required to adopt it. They are offered a
higher-quality predictive signal and allowed to validate it against their
own student success data before expanding their use of it. The argument for
adoption is not ideological. It is that competency profiles will predict
student success more accurately than GPA and test scores — which means fewer
students failing out, less remediation, better student-program matching —
and that prediction will be verifiable within each institution's own records.

Universities themselves are being repositioned in the broader system — not
as the primary gatekeepers of human capability, but as the places where
knowledge is generated at the frontier, where deep domain expertise is
developed, and where the long-horizon questions get asked. The full architecture
of what universities become is still being worked out, because it depends on
other domains — manufacturing, labor, research priorities — that are still
being specified. What is clear is the direction: away from credential factory
and toward genuine knowledge institution.

## What This Is Not

This reform is frequently mischaracterized, and it is worth naming what it
is not.

**It is not a voucher system.** Standard voucher programs provide funding
that families use to choose among schools in a fragmented market. This reform
provides per-student funding that flows through co-location campuses where
multiple programs share infrastructure, outcomes are externally validated,
and geographic access is guaranteed. The funding model is designed to expand
equity, not to produce the stratification that voucher programs tend to
generate.

**It is not surveillance.** Student learning data is de-identified by default.
The spine sees a pseudonymous identifier, not a student name. Personal
information is stored separately and never combined with learning data in
systems that outside parties can read. Parents can see what their child has
learned. They cannot see data that identifies other children. The system
tracks learning, not people.

**It is not the replacement of teachers with AI.** The spine handles delivery
and assessment. Teachers handle everything that requires human judgment,
relationship, and care. The reform is built on the explicit claim that the
things teachers are best at — mentorship, facilitation, intervention, building
community — have been crowded out by tasks that do not require them. Removing
those tasks is what elevates the teaching role, not what eliminates it.

**It is not a federal takeover of local education.** The National Competency
Framework defines what students must demonstrate. It does not control what
schools teach, which books they use, what pedagogical approaches they take,
or what their culture looks like. The diversity of educational approaches
is preserved and extended — schools specialize more, not less, under this
model. What is standardized is the outcome, not the path.

**It is not ideological.** The framework for civic and historical education
requires students to analyze primary sources, evaluate competing interpretations,
and construct evidence-based arguments. It requires that instruction be
consistent with established historical evidence. It does not specify which
events must be covered, which interpretations are correct, or which values
schools should teach. The epistemic guardrails are designed to catch the
failure mode where students cannot reason with evidence — not to prescribe
what conclusions they should reach.

## The Scale of the Opportunity

The United States spends more per student on K-12 education than most
developed countries and produces mediocre average outcomes with enormous
variance. The variance is the real problem: the difference between the best
and worst outcomes in American education is not a rounding error, it is a
fundamental failure of the system's basic promise.

The failure is not a resource problem. It is a design problem. The current
system is designed to reproduce geographic inequality, because it funds
schools through property taxes. It is designed to measure exposure rather
than learning, because it advances students by time rather than mastery.
It is designed to overwork teachers, because it asks them to personally
perform tasks that do not require their expertise. And it is designed to
produce political conflict, because it mandates content rather than
guaranteeing outcomes.

All of these are design choices. Design choices can be changed.

What this reform proposes is not a marginal improvement to the existing
system. It is a different architecture: one that guarantees capability rather
than mandating exposure, that concentrates resources where they are most
needed rather than where property values are highest, that measures what
actually matters rather than what is easy to count, and that makes education
transparent enough to deserve the trust it currently asks for without earning.

The implementation is gradual, pilot-tested, and evidence-driven. No component
scales nationally without demonstrated success at smaller scope. No mandate
is issued without proof. The system is designed to learn from what works and
stop what does not, rather than persisting by inertia.

The goal is an education system that actually works — for all the students
who depend on it, measured honestly, held accountable for real outcomes, and
designed to improve rather than persist.

## A Final Note

This document has tried to explain a complex system in plain terms. Some
nuance has been simplified in service of clarity. The full policy documents
describe the system with the precision that implementation requires — including
the failure modes, the edge cases, the governance structures, and the places
where the design is deliberately left open because the answers are not yet
known.

What this document has tried to preserve is the core argument: the current
system fails by design, the design can be changed, and the change requires
building new infrastructure rather than adding more pressure to the people
operating the current one.

The infrastructure is what this collection of briefs describes.

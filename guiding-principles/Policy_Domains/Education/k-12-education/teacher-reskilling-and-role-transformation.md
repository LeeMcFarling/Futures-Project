## Purpose

This framework defines how teachers transition from primary content deliverers
to learning facilitators, intervention specialists, and project mentors within
a spine-based, AI-enabled, competency-driven education system.

The transition is not optional to the reform. The AI learning spine changes
what the bottleneck in education is — from content delivery to human judgment
and intervention. A spine deployed into a workforce trained, evaluated, and
compensated for content delivery will underperform. Aligning the teacher role
to the system the spine creates is not a soft complement to the reform; it is
a hard dependency.

At the same time, this framework is not a mandate imposed on teachers. It is
a negotiated upgrade with a real offer on the table. The goal is selective
adoption and pull — teachers who understand the offer choose the transition,
and the transition proves itself through their students' outcomes. Coercive
nationwide retraining for a system still being validated would be premature
and counterproductive. This framework scales with the pilots, not ahead of them.

---

## Problem Statement

The current teaching model is structurally misaligned with what the spine
creates. Teachers are expected to simultaneously deliver content, manage
thirty-plus students at different levels, grade continuously, track individual
progress manually, and handle administrative overhead — all with coarse tools
and fixed schedules. The result is instruction toward the average, hidden
learning gaps, unsustainable workload, and declining retention among the
teachers the system most needs to keep.

The AI learning spine removes most of the delivery and assessment burden.
It continuously diagnoses where each student is, adapts pacing and content
accordingly, and maintains the longitudinal competency record that DES uses
for validation. What it cannot do — what only a skilled human can do — is
interpret why a student is stuck, decide which intervention to make, lead a
Socratic discussion that builds genuine reasoning, design applied projects
that make competencies meaningful, or read the social and emotional signals
that determine whether a classroom is functioning.

The spine shifts the bottleneck. The framework defines the job that fills it.

---

## The Offer to Teachers

This transition is framed as a professional upgrade, not a cost-cutting
measure, and not a displacement plan. When a school converts to the
spine-based model, teachers receive a materially different working life.

The grading burden is largely eliminated. The spine handles continuous
assessment; teachers no longer spend evenings and weekends on repetitive
evaluation tasks. Repetitive instruction — explaining the same concept
fifteen times to students at different stages — is absorbed by the AI system.
Administrative overhead decreases as the spine generates the progress records
that previously required manual documentation.

What teachers gain in exchange is time for the work most of them entered the
profession to do. Small-group Socratic dialogue. Mentorship relationships with
students navigating difficult material. Project design that connects
competencies to real-world contexts. Social and emotional presence in a
classroom that has bandwidth for it because the delivery burden is off the
teacher's plate.

Beyond daily working conditions, the conversion enables something the current
system structurally prevents: genuine specialization. A teacher who is
exceptional at mathematical reasoning facilitation, or at evidence-based
historical discussion, or at leading applied STEM projects, currently has
no career path that concentrates their work in that area. Under the
facilitator model on a co-location campus, specialization is not just
permitted — it defines the career ladder. Instructional Architect in
Mathematical Reasoning is a real, named, differentiated role with a
corresponding professional identity and compensation structure.

For principals and school leadership, conversion also delivers greater
operational autonomy. The principal budget autonomy pilot removes categorical
spending restrictions and enables rollover of unused funds — a material
improvement in how schools can respond to local needs. Teachers converting
to the spine model are converting into a school environment with more
discretion, not less.

This is the offer. It is real, and it is designed to be more attractive than
the status quo for the teachers who matter most to the transition's success.

## The Role in Full

The facilitator role is not a rebranding of the existing job. It is a
different job that draws on different skills and requires different judgment.

**Intervention triage** is the core responsibility that the current model
cannot accommodate at all. When the spine signals that a student is stuck
on a competency, the teacher's job is to determine why. The diagnostic
possibilities are meaningfully different from each other: a conceptual
misunderstanding requires a different intervention than a motivation failure,
which requires a different intervention than an external circumstance affecting
engagement. Getting the diagnosis wrong wastes time and can deepen the problem.
Getting it right is high-skill work that no AI system can reliably perform —
it requires reading a human being in context.

**Small-group Socratic instruction** becomes the primary delivery mode for
conceptual work. When multiple students are working through the same
misunderstanding, the teacher convenes them, leads dialogue that surfaces
their reasoning, and uses their own thinking to correct it. This is harder
than lecturing and more effective. It requires command of the subject matter,
the ability to ask questions that reveal rather than lead, and the patience
to let students arrive at understanding rather than providing it.

**Project design and mentorship** creates the applied dimension of competency
development. Competencies demonstrated in abstract contexts need to be
exercised in real ones. Teachers design projects that require students to
deploy multiple competencies together, then mentor them through the work.
This is where the specialization pathways matter most — a teacher with deep
expertise in a domain designs better projects in that domain.

**Social and emotional monitoring** is not a soft addition to the role; it is
a first-order function. Disengagement is an early signal of learning failure.
A student who is not engaging with the spine is not learning from it, and the
spine's trajectory signals will eventually reflect that — but a teacher who
notices disengagement before it appears in the data can intervene earlier.
Classroom culture, peer dynamics, and individual well-being determine whether
the spine's capabilities translate into actual learning.

**Structured discussion moderation** runs through the book recommendation
pilot and through the civics and history competencies in the NCF. Teaching
students to reason with evidence, engage conflicting perspectives, and
disagree productively is itself a core educational function that requires
skilled human facilitation. The teacher's role here is to model the norms, set
the conditions, and intervene when discussion deteriorates in ways that
reinforce rather than punish.

## Workforce Segmentation

The existing teaching workforce is not uniform, and a single retraining
approach will not work across it. Three segments require meaningfully different
strategies.

**Early-career teachers** are the most adaptable cohort. Many entered the
profession with expectations formed by a changing landscape and are not
invested in delivery-centered pedagogy as an identity. For this segment,
the transition is primarily about training in the specific skills of the
facilitator role. They are the easiest cohort to move and will become the
natural lead facilitators for future cohorts.

**Mid-career teachers** are the critical segment. They have the experience
that makes a skilled facilitator genuinely valuable — they know students,
they know failure modes, they know how to read a classroom. But they have
also built professional identities around roles and skills that the
transition redefines. Resistance from this group is the most predictable
threat to adoption, and it is not irrational — the transition asks them
to accept that much of what they have practiced is being restructured.
The offer has to be genuine and clearly communicated for this group. The
career ladder, the specialization pathways, and the reduced administrative
burden are specifically targeted here. If mid-career teachers in the first
cohorts experience the transition as a real improvement in professional life,
the subsequent adoption wave is largely self-propagating. If they experience
it as a demotion disguised as an upgrade, resistance spreads.

**Late-career teachers** are not the primary target for technical retraining
in AI system interpretation. The ROI on that investment is limited given
tenure horizon. The appropriate deployment for this segment is in the roles
that most benefit from accumulated experience — mentorship, project leadership,
cohort guidance, and in some cases contributing to the curriculum and
facilitation standards that DES and the training pipeline develop. These
teachers carry knowledge that the system needs and should be structured to
absorb.

## Training Delivery Model

Reskilling is not delivered through the institutions that produced the current
competency mismatch. University education schools train teachers for a
delivery-centered model. Running facilitator training through those institutions
would replicate the problem at one remove.

Instead, the training pipeline is anchored in existing AI-enabled pilot schools —
Alpha School-type models and comparable programs that have already demonstrated
the spine-based learning compression in real classrooms. These schools are the
proof of concept. More importantly, they are environments where teachers can
observe the facilitator role being performed before they are asked to perform it.
The difference between learning about a job and seeing that job done with real
students in a real classroom is the difference between understanding a model
and trusting it enough to stake your professional identity on it.

DES partners with these pilot schools as formal training environments, evaluated
on both student outcomes and facilitator training effectiveness. A pilot school's
contribution to the training pipeline is part of its certified role in the system,
not an informal side function.

## The Co-Teaching Rotation

The core mechanism is an embedded co-teaching rotation modeled on medical
residency and traditional student teaching — not because the analogy is
rhetorically convenient, but because the skill transfer challenge is genuinely
similar. Facilitation requires judgment, timing, and situational awareness that
cannot be abstracted into a workshop. These skills are acquired by doing them
alongside someone who already does them, in a real environment, with real
consequences.

A rotation runs for one academic semester, the minimum viable period for a
resident to move through observation, co-facilitation, and primary
responsibility with support. Shorter rotations do not produce transferable skill.

The **Lead Facilitator** is a certified teacher in a functioning spine-enabled
classroom. They manage the full learning environment, model intervention triage
and facilitation practice, and hold primary evaluation authority over the
resident. They are compensated for the training function through a per-resident
allocation from the Teacher Reskilling Fund — the training role is recognized
and paid, not assumed as volunteered overhead.

The **Resident Teacher** arrives at the pilot school having opted into the
program and been selected and nominated by their home principal. They begin
as observers, move into co-facilitation of small-group sessions, and
progressively assume primary responsibility for specific functions as their
competency develops. They practice intervention diagnosis against real AI
signals, lead facilitated discussions, design and support small projects, and
receive ongoing feedback from the Lead Facilitator on the quality of their
judgment.

Evaluation at the end of the rotation is conducted jointly by the host school
against practical performance criteria and by DES against a standardized
facilitation rubric. This dual evaluation ensures that the credentialing
outcome is independent of any single school's standards and that DES can
track training quality across the system.

Successful completion issues a **Facilitator Certification** — portable across
all spine-enabled schools, recognized in compensation frameworks, and required
for Lead Facilitator roles. The credential is stackable toward the advanced
roles in the career ladder.

---

## Resident Selection and Compensation

Participation is opt-in. Teachers enter a regional volunteer pool and are
prioritized by three criteria: their home school's position in the conversion
schedule (schools converting sooner have higher priority), geographic proximity
to available training sites, and principal nomination and approval.

Where demand exceeds available slots — which should be expected in the early
phases — selection is coordinated at the regional level with priority given to
Phase 1 and Phase 2 conversion schools. Waitlisted teachers remain in the pool
for subsequent cohorts.

The geographic design principle is deliberate. Rotations are placed in pilot
schools within the teacher's metro or regional area wherever possible. A
mid-career teacher with family obligations cannot do a semester-long out-of-state
placement; designing the system to require that eliminates the cohort the
transition most needs. As new co-location campuses come online and more schools
convert, the radius of available training sites expands and the geographic
constraint loosens naturally.

Compensation during rotation is maintained by the home district at full salary
and benefits. The Teacher Reskilling Fund covers travel, temporary housing if
required by placement distance, and incidental costs. No teacher incurs
financial loss by participating. This is the core rule and it is non-negotiable.
Adoption is voluntary; financial barriers to voluntary participation contradict
the model's own logic.

---

## Certification Outcomes and Failure Pathway

After a rotation, residents receive either a Facilitator Certification or a
Provisional status indicating they have not yet met the standard.

Teachers who do not certify may repeat the rotation once. They receive specific
feedback targeted at the gap areas — intervention decision quality, facilitation
technique, system interpretation — and are matched with a placement that
addresses those gaps. A second rotation is not a consolation; it is a designed
recovery pathway with a different structure than the first attempt.

If certification is not achieved after the second rotation, the teacher returns
to a traditional instructional role or to a non-spine school environment. There
is no loss of employment, compensation, or standing. Failure to certify is
treated as a slower transition path, not a penalty. The teacher remains in the
volunteer pool and may attempt a future rotation if circumstances change.

This non-punitive design is not sentimentality — it is a precondition for
voluntary adoption at the scale the transition requires. If teachers believe
that opting into the rotation program carries professional risk, the
opt-in pool collapses to the risk-tolerant minority and the self-propagating
model fails at launch.

---

## The Self-Propagating System

The knowledge transfer design is the most important structural property of
this framework. The reskilling pipeline does not require a standing central
institution. It requires the first cohort to be done well.

The sequence is: spine developed and validated → DES partners with pilot
schools → first cohort completes rotations → those teachers return to their
home schools as Lead Facilitators when their schools convert → converted schools
become training sites → the next cohort rotates through them → and so on.

Each converted school becomes a training node. The system's training capacity
grows in direct proportion to its deployment footprint. The Teacher Reskilling
Fund supports the marginal cost of each rotation cohort, but it does not fund
a bureaucracy that exists independently of the rollout.

This has a critical implication for system design: the quality of the first
cohort determines the quality of the second. Lead Facilitators in Phase 1
schools are the trainers in Phase 2 schools. If Phase 1 training is weak, the
degradation compounds. Investment in the pilot school partnership and the Lead
Facilitator certification standard at the beginning of the system is investment
in every cohort that follows.

---

## Pilot-Coupled Deployment

Teacher reskilling cohorts are sized to match pilot school enrollment. The
framework does not call for nationwide retraining ahead of a system that is
still being validated. It calls for reskilling the teachers who will work in
the schools that are converting, on the timeline those schools are converting,
funded proportionally to pilot enrollment.

This means the reskilling program is evaluated by the same DES telemetry that
evaluates student outcomes. The question is not whether teachers completed a
training program. The question is whether teachers who completed facilitation
certification are producing better student mastery trajectories in spine-enabled
classrooms than uncertified teachers in equivalent environments. If the answer
is no, the training content is revised. If the answer is yes and the effect
is strong, it is the evidence base for expanding the training program alongside
the pilot expansion.

The program scales only as pilot evidence supports expansion. No national
mandate without proof.

## Career Ladder and Specialization

The facilitator model enables a career structure that the current system
cannot support.

The baseline credential is Facilitator Certification, required to operate
independently in a spine-enabled classroom. Above that, Senior Facilitator
recognizes demonstrated effectiveness over multiple cohorts of students with
strong trajectory outcomes. Mentor and Project Lead roles formalize the function
late-career teachers are already best positioned to fill — guiding residents
through rotations and designing complex applied learning experiences. Campus
Instructional Leader is the school-level role responsible for facilitation
quality, training coordination, and DES reporting integration.

The specialization pathways branch from Senior Facilitator. An Instructional
Architect in Mathematical Reasoning concentrates on the STEM competency domain
— designing the highest-difficulty facilitated sessions, developing novel
validation prompts, mentoring teachers in quantitative reasoning facilitation.
The parallel roles in Language and Argumentation, Socratic Civics, and
Project-Based STEM or Arts are equivalent structures in their domains.

These are not honorific titles. They are differentiated professional roles with
differentiated compensation, defined responsibilities, and defined relationships
to the DES standards framework. A teacher who reaches Instructional Architect
in their domain has a career identity built on demonstrated expertise in a
specific high-skill function — a professional standing that the current system
has no structural way to create.

---

## Alignment with DES

DES provides the evaluation infrastructure that makes teacher effectiveness
visible without making it punitive. Student mastery trajectories in the spine
are cohort-adjusted — DES controls for prior mastery, demographics, and program
type. A teacher's contribution to those trajectories is partially observable
in the aggregate, at the classroom level, over time.

Teacher evaluation in the new framework is anchored to three primary signals:
student delta mastery trajectories adjusted for cohort, retention and transfer
outcomes in the teacher's intervention domains, and reduction in within-cohort
learning variance. These replace lecture quality scoring, content delivery
volume, and test score snapshots without trajectory context — metrics that
measured the job the spine is replacing, not the job the facilitator performs.

The shift in evaluation criteria must happen in parallel with the shift in role.
Retraining teachers as facilitators while continuing to evaluate them as
lecturers does not produce a transition — it produces confusion and resistance.
DES validation of the new evaluation framework, using the same outcome-based
logic it applies everywhere else in the system, is the mechanism that makes
the role change credible to teachers, unions, and school boards simultaneously.

## Union Strategy

The teacher unions represent approximately 4.5 million members. Their posture
toward the transition will be one of the most significant determinants of
whether pilot adoption is smooth or contested.

The offer to unions is genuine. Reduced administrative burden is not a
reframing — it is a measurable change in how teachers spend their time. Elevated
professional role is not rhetoric — it is a career ladder with real
differentiation. Clearer evaluation criteria are not a trap — they are a
replacement of opaque, often arbitrary assessment with outcome-linked standards
that teachers can understand and contest. No abrupt displacement is a
commitment — the failure pathway is non-punitive, the transition is
pilot-coupled, and no teacher is forced out of employment.

The engagement model is co-design, not consultation. Pilot programs are
developed with union involvement from the beginning, not presented for
approval after design. Evaluation criteria are negotiated, not mandated.
Feedback loops during rollout are structured and consequential — if
facilitators in the first cohort report that aspects of the rotation model
are not working, that feedback changes the model.

The transition asks unions to accept that some of what they have defended —
the primacy of the delivery role, the evaluation systems built around it —
is being restructured. That is a real ask. The offer has to be real enough
to justify it. If it is, early-adopting union locals become advocates for
the model. If it is not, the resistance is rational and the transition stalls.

---

## Failure Modes

**The offer fails to land.** If mid-career teachers experience the transition
as a deskilling exercise — if the facilitator role reads as less prestigious,
less skilled, or less compensated than the role it replaces — voluntary adoption
will be concentrated among early-career teachers and enthusiasts. The critical
mid-career cohort stays out, converted schools are staffed by inexperienced
facilitators, and student outcomes in early pilots underperform. Mitigation
requires genuine compensation alignment, visible specialization pathways, and
enough early adopters with strong outcomes to make the offer credible through
demonstration rather than assertion.

**The self-propagating model degrades over rotations.** If Phase 1 Lead
Facilitators are not selected and trained rigorously, the training they deliver
in Phase 2 reflects their own gaps. The system has no natural correction
mechanism for this if it compounds silently. Mitigation requires DES to track
facilitation quality longitudinally — not just whether students in converted
schools perform well, but whether the facilitation practices of later-cohort
residents are as strong as earlier-cohort residents. Degradation that appears
in that data triggers a review of the Lead Facilitator selection and certification
standards, not just the resident training content.

**Evaluation criteria shift without compensation structures shifting.** Schools
that adopt the new evaluation framework but maintain legacy compensation
structures produce a situation where teachers are assessed on new criteria but
rewarded on old ones. This is incoherent and will be perceived as such.
Compensation restructuring is a hard dependency on the evaluation shift, not
a subsequent phase.

**Unions block entry to pilot schools.** In districts where local union
contracts restrict the use of co-teaching arrangements or limit experimental
programs, the rotation model may face legal or contractual obstacles before
the question of teacher buy-in is even reached. Mitigation requires early
engagement with union leadership at the district level before pilot school
selection, not after. The co-design framing is the preventive mechanism here,
not a fallback.

## Success Criteria

The framework succeeds if, over the pilot period:

- Students in classrooms with certified facilitators show stronger mastery
  trajectories than matched students in equivalent schools without them,
  as validated by DES
- Teacher retention rates in converted schools exceed rates in comparable
  non-converted schools
- Facilitator burnout and workload indicators improve relative to pre-conversion
  baselines
- Rotation completion and certification rates are high enough that converted
  schools produce their own training capacity for subsequent cohorts
- Early union engagement in pilot districts results in formal co-development
  agreements rather than contested implementation

If these outcomes do not materialize, the framework is revised — training
content, rotation structure, evaluation criteria, or the offer itself. The
same logic that governs every other element of this system governs this one:
evidence drives continuation, and failure triggers redesign, not persistence.

---

## Closing

The AI learning spine changes what education requires from the adults in the
room. That change does not happen automatically, and it does not happen by
calling the same job by a different name.

What it requires is a teacher who can read a diagnostic signal from an AI
system and translate it into a human intervention that the AI cannot make.
A teacher who can lead a discussion in which students build reasoning rather
than receive conclusions. A teacher who can design a project that makes
a competency real. A teacher who can notice, before the data does, that
something is wrong with a student's engagement and do something about it.

That is a skilled, high-status, professionally meaningful job. It is not the
job most current teachers were hired to do or trained to perform. Closing that
gap is the purpose of this framework.

The transition is not coerced and not rushed. It is piloted, measured, and
scaled where it works. The offer to teachers is real, the pathway is designed
to be non-punitive, and the career structure that results is more differentiated
and more professionally meaningful than the one it replaces.

If this works, the teachers in converted schools will say so through their
retention, their outcomes, and their willingness to train the next cohort.
That is the validation the system is designed to produce.

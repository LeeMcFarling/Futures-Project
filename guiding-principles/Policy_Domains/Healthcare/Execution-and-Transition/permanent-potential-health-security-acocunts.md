# Permanent Health Security Accounts — Future-State Option and Guardrails

## Purpose and Scope

This brief defines a possible future-state pathway for Health Transition Accounts / Transitional Health Accounts if the account layer proves valuable during healthcare transition pilots.

The initial role of HTA/THA accounts is transitional. They stabilize routine-care affordability while the healthcare system shifts away from employer-sponsored insurance, opaque claims processing, fragmented benefit eligibility, and network-based coverage toward transparent market-layer care, Severe Event Coverage, income-graduated routine-care subsidies, and portable employer contributions.

This brief does not assume those accounts must become permanent. It defines the conditions under which Congress could authorize a permanent account layer and the guardrails required if it does.

If continuation is justified by evidence, the temporary HTA/THA structure would evolve into a permanent Health Security Account.

A permanent Health Security Account would remain individual-owned, portable across employment forms, and usable for defined market-layer healthcare services. It would not replace Severe Event Coverage, would not become private insurance, and would not recreate employer-sponsored healthcare under a new name.

---

## Core Design Principle

The account is a liquidity and settlement instrument, not an insurance product or wealth-building account.

Its function is to help households pay for routine, shoppable, market-layer healthcare services without relying on employer plan design, annual benefit cliffs, provider networks, or reimbursement paperwork.

The account should improve access to care that is predictable, schedulable, and price-comparable. Severe, acute, non-elective, high-cost events remain covered through SEC. The account does not insure catastrophic risk.

The policy question is practical: does a permanent account layer improve routine-care liquidity, reduce deferred care, simplify settlement, preserve consumer choice, and lower administrative cost without becoming a tax shelter, a surveillance tool, or a subsidy-capture mechanism?

---

## Why Permanence Might Be Considered

The transition account may prove useful beyond transition if it solves durable routine-care problems.

A permanent account layer may be justified if pilot evidence shows that accounts:

- increase timely use of routine and preventive services
- reduce deferred care
- lower administrative overhead
- simplify settlement for patients and providers
- improve price legibility
- reduce reliance on employer-sponsored plan design
- support labor mobility across jobs, states, and employment forms
- improve affordability for part-time, seasonal, gig, and dependent-contractor workers
- complement income-graduated subsidies without creating benefit cliffs
- allow routine-care savings to accumulate across low-utilization periods
- reduce avoidable severe-event escalation over time

The case for permanence should be earned by evidence, not assumed at authorization.

---

## Authorized Uses

Permanent Health Security Accounts may be used only for defined market-layer healthcare services authorized by statute and maintained through HETA service taxonomy rules.

Eligible uses may include:

- primary care
- urgent care
- routine diagnostics
- routine imaging where market-layer classification applies
- outpatient mental health services
- routine dental care
- routine vision care
- preventive services
- chronic-care support services that remain outside SEC episode boundaries
- rehabilitation and physical therapy where scheduled and market-layer eligible
- approved medical devices and supplies tied to routine care
- other shoppable services designated by HETA under statutory limits

The account may not be used for services that are SEC-covered at the episode level, except for any lawful patient cost-sharing or supplemental amount explicitly authorized under SEC rules.

The account may not be used for non-health consumption, speculative investments, unapproved wellness products, or services outside the statutory healthcare taxonomy.

---

## Relationship to SEC

Permanent Health Security Accounts do not alter SEC.

SEC remains the universal severe-event floor for emergency, trauma, major surgery, serious illness, and other defined high-cost clinical episodes. SEC is event-based, not deductible-based. It protects against medical financial shock.

Health Security Accounts operate below that level. They finance routine, predictable, market-layer services and support household liquidity for ordinary care.

The boundary must remain clear. If routine-care accounts are allowed to substitute for SEC, the system recreates high-deductible underinsurance. If SEC absorbs routine care, the system suppresses price discovery and recreates claims-adjudicated medicine. The permanent account layer only works if each layer stays in its lane.

---

## Funding Sources

Permanent accounts may receive funds from multiple authorized sources.

Potential sources include:

- monthly employer healthcare contribution floor deposits
- individual contributions
- income-graduated routine-care subsidies
- preventive utilization credits
- public child health reserve contributions
- authorized family transfers
- payroll or refundable tax credits
- limited interest or low-risk returns
- approved public or philanthropic access credits

The unified rail should combine sources at settlement without exposing the payer mix to providers.

Employer contributions are labor-market obligations. Public subsidies are affordability instruments. Individual contributions are household funds. Interest preserves purchasing power. Each source has a different policy justification and should be tracked separately for oversight.

---

## Account Ownership and Portability

Permanent Health Security Accounts are individual-owned.

Balances follow the individual across:

- employers
- unemployment
- part-time work
- seasonal work
- gig work
- dependent-contractor work
- state lines
- school enrollment
- caregiving interruptions
- retirement transition, subject to statutory rules

The account should attach to the individual's SSI health wallet and settle through UPI-compatible payment rails.

Employers may contribute to the account but do not own the balance. Employers may not reclaim individual balances. Employers may not see account utilization, health status, service history, provider choice, subsidy eligibility, actuarial classification, or account balance.

---

## Reserve, Stabilization, and Excess-Balance Structure

Permanent accounts require a tiered balance structure.

### Tier 1 — Reserve Floor

The reserve floor protects households from ordinary routine-care volatility.

Balances within the reserve floor are protected, roll forward, and are not subject to clawback. The reserve floor should be calibrated by household structure, regional price levels, age, disability status, and expected routine-care exposure.

### Tier 2 — Stabilization Band

The stabilization band allows households to smooth uneven routine-care needs across time.

Balances in this band roll forward year to year and may earn limited low-risk interest. The band is designed for predictable but lumpy care needs: diagnostics, therapy episodes, dental work, vision needs, urgent care, primary care subscriptions, chronic-care supports, rehabilitation, and family routine-care costs.

### Tier 3 — Excess-Balance Layer

Balances above the statutory sufficiency threshold enter the excess-balance layer.

Excess balances are not confiscated. Existing individual-owned balances remain protected. However, future employer contributions, public top-ups, or routine-care credits may taper prospectively while the account remains above the sufficiency threshold.

This prevents overfunding and avoids turning the account into an open-ended subsidized savings vehicle.

---

## Interest-Earning Authority

Permanent accounts may earn interest or low-risk returns on balances held within authorized bands.

The purpose of interest is preservation of routine-care purchasing power, not wealth accumulation.

Permitted options should be conservative and standardized, such as:

- Treasury-linked accounts
- insured cash-like instruments
- federally certified low-risk default funds
- inflation-protected public account structures where authorized

The baseline account should not permit speculative investment, self-directed brokerage, crypto assets, leveraged products, private equity exposure, or complex financial products.

If higher-yield options are ever authorized, they should be opt-in, tightly capped, and unavailable for public subsidy balances unless Congress separately authorizes them.

---

## Tapered Contribution Rule

If an individual's account balance exceeds the reserve and stabilization thresholds, required employer contributions may taper according to a statutory schedule.

The taper is prospective only. Existing balances are not clawed back.

Future contributions may be reduced until the account returns to the target band through ordinary healthcare spending, authorized family transfer, or passage of time.

This preserves employer responsibility while preventing permanent overfunding. It gives employers predictable obligations and prevents the account from accumulating large balances disconnected from routine-care need.

The taper should not activate where routine-care affordability remains independently at risk because of low income, disability, high regional healthcare prices, household caregiving burden, or medically complex dependents.

---

## Public Subsidy Ordering

Where employer contributions, public subsidies, and individual funds all apply, subsidy ordering matters.

Public routine-care subsidies should generally taper before employer contributions once an account exceeds the sufficiency threshold, unless the individual remains in a protected affordability category.

This protects fiscal sustainability while preserving the labor-market principle that firms should not externalize routine-care costs onto public systems.

The system should avoid converting employer obligations into public subsidies by allowing firms to undercontribute while the state fills the gap. It should also avoid forcing low-income households to spend down protected reserves before receiving necessary routine-care support.

The ordering rules should therefore distinguish between ordinary excess balances and protected reserves.

---

## Family and Child Portability

Permanent accounts may allow limited transfer of excess balances to dependent children or other covered dependents.

A child-linked Health Security Account would attach to the child's SSI health wallet and be controlled by a parent or guardian under fiduciary rules until the child reaches the statutory age of control. At adulthood, custody transfers to the individual.

Eligible uses may include authorized pediatric and dependent market-layer care, including primary care, urgent care, dental care, vision care, therapy, disability-related routine support, diagnostics, and other routine healthcare needs.

Transfers should be capped. Without caps, affluent households could use dependent accounts as unlimited tax-preferred savings vehicles.

A permanent child account system should include a baseline public child health reserve or income-weighted seed contribution. Parental transfers may supplement that floor, but should not be the only path by which children accumulate routine-care reserves.

The child account should include clear aging-out rules:

- guardians control spending while the child is a minor
- the child gains visibility rights at a defined age
- spending authority transfers at adulthood
- disability or guardianship exceptions are handled through existing legal frameworks
- historical sensitive medical data remains protected according to age-of-majority privacy rules

---

## Anti-Stratification Guardrails

Permanent accounts must not reproduce the failures of HSAs.

The account structure should include:

- caps on tax-preferred balances
- limited or no high-risk investment options
- income-weighted public top-ups for low-income households
- prospective contribution tapering above sufficiency thresholds
- transfer caps for dependent accounts
- provider-side blindness to account balances
- employer-side privacy firewalls
- DoDA monitoring of account inequality by income, region, age, disability status, household structure, race where legally permitted and privacy-protected, and employment type
- prohibition on using account balances to determine provider pricing
- prohibition on account-based underwriting or exclusion
- restrictions on marketing accounts as wealth-building products

The account should make routine care easier to afford. It should not create a two-tier system where high-income households accumulate large tax-advantaged balances while low-income households spend their accounts down immediately.

---

## Provider-Side Blindness

Providers should not see the patient's account balance, funding sources, employer contribution status, subsidy eligibility, or actuarial classification.

At settlement, the provider receives:

- confirmation of covered payment
- standardized service reference code
- amount paid
- settlement status

The provider does not receive the payer mix. This prevents providers from raising prices based on visible public subsidies, employer contributions, or large account balances.

Patient-side transparency remains full. The individual should see the posted price, available subsidies, account drawdown, employer contribution use, public credit use, and remaining balance.

---

## Employer Privacy Firewall

Employers may contribute to Health Security Accounts but may not monitor them.

Employers may not access:

- account balance
- care utilization
- provider selection
- diagnosis
- service category
- preventive utilization status
- subsidy eligibility
- family transfer activity
- actuarial classification

Employer compliance is limited to contribution obligations, payroll reporting, and any required labor-stack attestations.

This firewall is essential. Without it, the account becomes a workplace surveillance channel.

---

## Anti-Gaming and Enforcement

Permanent accounts create predictable gaming risks.

Potential abuse patterns include:

- employers underreporting hours to reduce contributions
- firms misclassifying workers to avoid account obligations
- providers inflating prices in response to visible account-funded demand
- vendors marketing low-value services as eligible care
- households overfunding accounts for tax preference rather than care need
- financial firms lobbying to expand investment options
- platforms fragmenting work relationships to evade contribution thresholds
- account administrators charging excessive fees

Controls should include:

- automated contribution reconciliation
- worker-classification parity enforcement
- provider price telemetry
- fee caps for account administration
- product eligibility taxonomy
- public reporting of aggregate account balances and utilization
- DoDA monitoring of subsidy capture
- PCA access suspension for firms evading contribution obligations
- prohibition on balance-based provider pricing
- annual audit of account administrator fees and practices

Human enforcement should focus on ambiguous cases. Routine violations should be detected through contribution and settlement data already flowing through the rails.

---

## Continuation Gate

Permanent authorization should require an explicit continuation gate.

At the end of the transition period, DoDA should certify whether HTA/THA accounts have achieved the following outcomes:

- improved routine-care access
- reduced deferred care
- lowered administrative overhead
- stabilized household healthcare liquidity
- improved price legibility
- avoided material provider subsidy capture
- avoided excessive account inequality
- preserved labor mobility
- reduced benefit cliffs
- complemented SEC without weakening SEC's severe-event role

If the evidence supports continuation, Congress may convert the transition account into a permanent Health Security Account with the guardrails defined in this brief.

If the evidence does not support continuation, the accounts should sunset on a prospective schedule. Remaining individual-owned balances should remain available for eligible healthcare use until spent down.

Continuation should be evidence-gated, not automatic.

---

## Sunset Rule

If the account layer fails to meet continuation standards, new contributions should phase down according to a published schedule.

Existing balances remain individual-owned and may be spent on eligible healthcare services. Public subsidies and employer contribution obligations may terminate prospectively. Account administration should remain available long enough for balances to be used without forcing households into rushed or wasteful spending.

Sunset should preserve trust. People must know that balances already earned or contributed will not disappear because Congress chooses not to continue the account structure.

---

## What This Is Not

This is not an HSA expansion.

This is not a high-deductible insurance model.

This is not a replacement for SEC.

This is not employer-sponsored insurance under a new name.

This is not a private investment account.

This is not a generalized savings account.

This is not a provider network.

This is not a surveillance tool.

This is not a mechanism for employers to monitor worker health.

This is a possible permanent account layer for routine, market-layer healthcare if the transition account proves empirically useful and if strong guardrails prevent stratification, subsidy capture, and financialization.

## Draft Statutory Language Sketch

### Section 1. Permanent Account Authorization

Upon certification by DoDA that Health Transition Accounts have met the statutory continuation criteria, Congress may authorize conversion of Health Transition Accounts into Permanent Health Security Accounts.

### Section 2. Account Ownership

Permanent Health Security Accounts shall be individually owned, portable across employment forms and jurisdictions, and used only for eligible market-layer healthcare services as defined by statute and maintained through the HETA service taxonomy.

### Section 3. Preservation of SEC

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to replace, reduce, or condition Severe Event Coverage. SEC shall remain the universal severe-event insurance floor.

### Section 4. Interest and Investment Limits

Permanent Health Security Accounts may earn interest or low-risk returns only through approved instruments. Speculative investment, self-directed brokerage, leveraged products, and non-certified financial products are prohibited in the baseline account.

### Section 5. Contribution Tapering

Employer contributions, public top-ups, and routine-care credits may taper prospectively when an account exceeds statutory reserve and stabilization thresholds. Existing individual-owned balances shall not be clawed back.

### Section 6. Child and Dependent Portability

Account holders may transfer limited excess balances to dependent Health Security Accounts subject to annual caps, fiduciary spending rules, and age-of-control transfer requirements.

### Section 7. Provider-Side Blindness

Providers shall not receive information regarding account balance, funding source, employer contribution status, public subsidy eligibility, or payer mix. Providers shall receive only confirmed payment and standardized settlement information.

### Section 8. Employer Privacy Firewall

Employers shall have no access to individual account balances, utilization data, provider selection, preventive utilization status, subsidy eligibility, family transfer activity, or health information.

### Section 9. Anti-Stratification Monitoring

DoDA shall monitor account distribution, utilization, balance accumulation, subsidy capture, and access outcomes by income, region, age, disability status, household structure, and employment type. Findings shall be reported publicly on a defined cadence.

### Section 10. Sunset

If continuation criteria are not met, new account contributions shall phase down prospectively. Existing balances shall remain available for eligible healthcare use until spent down.

---

## Summary

Permanent Health Security Accounts are a future-state option, not a required endpoint.

The transition account exists to stabilize routine-care affordability during healthcare restructuring. If it proves useful, it may become a permanent routine-care liquidity and settlement layer. If it does not, it should sunset.

The account can only become permanent safely if it remains bounded: individual-owned, portable, provider-blind, employer-blind, capped, low-risk, anti-stratified, and subordinate to SEC.

The goal is not to create a new tax shelter. The goal is to give households a durable, portable way to pay for routine market-layer care while preserving universal protection for severe events.

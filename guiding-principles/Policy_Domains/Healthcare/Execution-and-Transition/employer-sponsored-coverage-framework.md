# Employer-Sponsored Coverage Transition Framework

## Purpose

This brief defines the transition pathway for employer-sponsored health coverage as the healthcare system moves toward a unified market with a universal **Severe Event Coverage (SEC)** floor. The goal is to unwind fragmented, bespoke employer plans without disruption, preserve labor mobility, and deliver a healthcare system that is simple, predictable, and reliable for users while remaining institutionally plural.

The transition prioritizes continuity, cost legibility, and anti-capture safeguards.

---

## Core Design Principles

1. **Single Market Rule**  
   Employers must purchase healthcare coverage from the same market available to individuals. Scale is permitted; siloing is not.

2. **Universal Floor**  
   SEC provides non-fragmenting, zero point-of-care coverage for severe, high-cost, medically necessary events.

3. **Cash-Equivalent Compensation**  
   Employer healthcare contributions remain portable, transparent, and tied to the worker—not the firm.

4. **Transitional Instruments, Not Permanent Ones**  
   Transitional Health Accounts (THAs) exist to smooth system change, not to financialize healthcare long-term.

5. **Low-Friction User Experience**  
   From the user perspective, care should function through a single interface with minimal administrative or financial complexity.

---

## Transitional Health Accounts (THAs)

During pilot phases and early rollout, employer and employee healthcare contributions are routed into standardized **Transitional Health Accounts (THAs)**.

THAs are:
- Individual-owned and fully portable  
- Cash-equivalent and compensation-linked  
- Integrated with unified payment and data rails  
- Explicitly temporary  

They replace bespoke employer-sponsored insurance during transition and serve as a buffering and reconciliation mechanism.

---

## Account Structure

Each THA operates with three layers.

### 1. Mandatory Reserve Floor

A minimum balance is retained at all times to cover expected out-of-pocket exposure above SEC and short-term utilization variance.

- Carries forward across years  
- Not subject to clawback  
- Prevents calendar-based shock risk  

### 2. Rolling Stabilization Band

Balances above the reserve floor but below the cap:

- Roll forward year to year  
- Can be used for:
  - Primary care
  - Elective procedures
  - Cost-sharing for standardized supplemental plans  
- Remain fully portable with the worker  

### 3. Hard Cap and Reconciliation Layer

Balances above a defined cap (initially ~**$10,000**, adjustable by statute) are deemed excess contributions.

- Evaluated on a trailing basis (e.g. 12–24 months)  
- Automatically reconciled  
- Returned proportionally to:
  - Employers (via reduced future contributions or rebates)
  - Employees (via payroll offsets or cash-equivalent returns)

There is **no end-of-year zeroing**.

---

## Employer Role During Transition

Employers do **not** offer bespoke insurance plans.

Instead, employers:
- Contribute to THAs as part of total compensation  
- May negotiate **standardized supplemental packages** en masse, such as:
  - Primary care bundles
  - Preventive care add-ons
  - Occupational health services  

Constraints:
- All packages must be publicly available in the individual market  
- No closed networks, regulatory arbitrage, or hidden risk segmentation  
- Differentiation below the SEC floor is prohibited  

Employers retain leverage through scale, not fragmentation.

---

## Interaction With Severe Event Coverage (SEC)

SEC sits underneath all plans and accounts.

- Severe, high-cost, medically necessary care is covered at zero point of use  
- THAs are not drawn down for SEC-covered events  
- Individuals with high medical need are protected from catastrophic exposure regardless of employment status  

This ensures transitional mechanics never expose individuals to uninsurable risk.

---

## End-State Target

The intended end state includes:

- Universal SEC as a permanent, non-fragmenting floor  
- A unified insurance market serving individuals and employers alike  
- Standardized plans with constrained differentiation  
- A single payment and data interface  
- Minimal need for individual financial navigation  

In this state, employer-sponsored insurance converges into employer participation in the same market as everyone else.

---

## Sunset of Transitional Health Accounts

THAs are explicitly transitional.

A statutory sunset is triggered by sustained system stability metrics, including:
- Coverage continuity  
- Predictable out-of-pocket exposure  
- Provider participation thresholds  
- Controlled cost growth  

Once triggered:
- New THA contributions cease  
- Existing balances may:
  - Be drawn down for approved healthcare uses, or
  - Convert into standardized healthcare credits  

No confiscation or abrupt cutoff occurs. Sunset reflects system success, not retrenchment.

---

## Safeguards and Anti-Gaming Measures

- Uniform contribution limits tied to compensation bands or actuarial need  
- Employer contributions treated as taxable compensation for reporting  
- Hard caps and rolling buffers to prevent hoarding  
- No point-of-care incentives tied to account balances  
- Automatic, transparent reconciliation rules  

Gaming is constrained structurally rather than through discretionary enforcement.

---

## Summary

This framework dismantles employer-sponsored insurance without chaos, preserves labor mobility, and prevents re-fragmentation. It delivers a unified, low-friction healthcare experience through a single floor, single market, and standardized interfaces, while maintaining institutional pluralism.

Transitional Health Accounts are a buffer, not a destination. Their planned sunset is a core feature of the design.

---

## Acceptable Policy Flexibilities (If Required)

If full zero point-of-care pricing for all SEC-adjacent services cannot clear legislatively, the following adjustments are acceptable without breaking system integrity:

- Limited, capped cost-sharing for non-hospital or non-acute services  
- Regulated provider price dispersion within statutory bounds  
- Annual household out-of-pocket caps replacing strict zero pricing at the margins  
- Temporary coexistence of supplemental private coverage for access or convenience  

These concessions preserve universal access, bounded risk, and system legibility while allowing legislative compromise. They do not alter the core architecture: a universal floor, a unified market, and constrained differentiation.

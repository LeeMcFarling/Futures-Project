# HETA Revised Charter and Execution Authority Framework

## Healthcare Execution and Transition Authority (HETA)
### Statutory Language Sketch — Integrated Deployment Model

---

### Section 1. Establishment and Purpose

There is established a Healthcare Execution and Transition Authority (HETA) as a
federally chartered execution instrument for the limited purpose of implementing,
stabilizing, and evaluating healthcare market transition pilots authorized by Congress,
and for the permanent purpose of maintaining the integrity of the coverage boundary
between market-layer care and Severe Event Coverage.

HETA operates as a **Mission-Bound Execution Corps** under the Execution Corps
Instrument Specification, with the addition of a standing Provider Transition
Corps function that persists for the duration of the authorized transition period.

HETA's mandate is execution, coordination, evidence generation, provider
transition support, and permanent SEC boundary stewardship. HETA shall not operate
care delivery, administer insurance, or develop national digital identity or payment
infrastructure.

HETA executes through five integrated functions:

1. **Transition Management** — sequencing, pilots, phase gating, and rollback authority
2. **Market and Utility Enforcement** — price transparency, boundary rules, anti-gaming
3. **Infrastructure-Enabled Oversight** — compliance with authentication, payment, and
   data exchange standards
4. **Provider Transition Corps** — regional field deployment, provider onboarding,
   capital instrument delivery, and hands-on transition support
5. **SEC Boundary Authority** — permanent stewardship of the coverage boundary between
   market-layer care and Severe Event Coverage, including anti-gaming enforcement,
   boundary classification, emergency utility rate-band oversight, and standing
   authority to certify whether proposed changes to account architecture, benefit
   design, or coverage rules would compromise SEC's actuarial integrity

Functions 1–3 are enforcement-oriented. Function 4 is support-oriented. Function 5
is permanent. These functions are institutionally separated within HETA. Personnel,
reporting chains, and authority matrices do not overlap between enforcement and support
functions except at the HETA executive level.

**Functions 1–4 are transition authorities. Function 5 is HETA's permanent mandate.
Transition authorities sunset per Section 7. The SEC Boundary Authority does not
sunset.**

### Section 2. Integrated Transition Deployment Teams

**(a) Joint Deployment Requirement**

For each approved pilot state or cohort, HETA shall deploy **Integrated Transition
Teams** composed of:

- HETA execution personnel
- Public Capital Authority (PCA) personnel, where capital deployment or financing
  instruments are implicated
- Designated state regulatory representatives appointed by the participating state
- HETA SEC Boundary Authority personnel, where emergency, catastrophic, or bundled
  episode coverage boundaries are implicated
- Provider Transition Corps personnel, deployed through regional offices defined
  in Section 2A

No federal transition activity shall occur in isolation from state participation.

**(b) Purpose of Co-Deployment**

Integrated Transition Teams exist to:

- Resolve operational and regulatory conflicts in real time
- Align federal execution, state regulation, and capital deployment
- Prevent jurisdictional delay, blame-shifting, or regulatory deadlock
- Ensure that transition decisions reflect on-the-ground conditions
- Deliver direct provider support through regional offices without requiring
  providers to navigate multiple federal agencies independently
- Maintain SEC boundary integrity throughout transition, ensuring that pilot
  operations do not erode the coverage line between market-layer and SEC-covered
  care

**(c) Non-Preemption Clause**

Co-deployment does not constitute federal preemption of state authority except where
explicitly authorized under Market Transition Waivers. State representatives retain
their full statutory authority.

### Section 2A. Provider Transition Corps — Regional Office Deployment

**(a) Purpose**

The Provider Transition Corps (PTC) is the provider-facing function of HETA. Its
mandate is to ensure that clinicians, practices, and facilities in pilot districts
can successfully navigate the transition to the restructured healthcare market without
bearing the administrative cost of that navigation alone.

The PTC does not enforce. It supports. Enforcement authority is held by HETA's
Market and Utility Enforcement function, which operates through a separate personnel
chain under the firewall provisions of Section 9.

**(b) Regional Office Model**

The PTC operates through **Regional Transition Offices** established in each pilot
district. Each office is:

- Physically accessible to providers in the district
- Staffed with a defined multi-specialist team
- Open for walk-in, scheduled, and virtual engagement
- Co-located with or operationally connected to the district's Integrated
  Transition Team

Regional offices are the single point of contact for all provider transition needs
in their district. Providers do not navigate multiple agencies. They contact or
visit their regional office, and the office coordinates the response.

**(c) Transition Team Composition**

Each Regional Transition Office deploys a **Provider Transition Team** with the
following specialist roles:

- **Transition Case Managers** — own the provider relationship, triage needs,
  coordinate internal routing, and resolve issues within their defined authority
  envelope. Each case manager is assigned a defined provider cohort. Providers
  have a named case manager as their stable point of contact.

- **Transition Assistants** — handle day-to-day implementation support including
  price posting setup, payment rail connection, provider dashboard orientation,
  portal and app support, and onboarding workflow guidance.

- **PCA Capital Officers** — forward-deployed PCA personnel responsible for
  debt restructuring consultations, practice formation instrument applications,
  revenue guarantee assessments, and capital instrument routing within PCA
  authorized envelopes.

- **Regulatory Navigators** — handle waiver routing, compliance timeline
  questions, market designation inquiries, and regulatory interpretation. Where
  a provider situation requires a formal Market Transition Waiver, the navigator
  prepares and routes the application through HETA waiver authority per Section 3.

- **Technology Support Specialists** — dedicated support for the unified payment
  rail, SSI health wallet onboarding, provider dashboard configuration, patient
  price display setup, and bug escalation to the infrastructure layer.

Staffing ratios and specialty mix are calibrated per district based on provider
population, market composition, and transition phase. A district with a high density
of independent dental practices will carry a different mix than a district dominated
by hospital-employed physicians.

**(d) Case Manager Authority**

Transition Case Managers hold real, bounded resolution authority. They are not
referral-only agents. Within pre-authorized parameters, a case manager may:

- Approve standard-form PCA instrument applications
- Issue minor administrative waivers within pre-defined scope
- Authorize transition timeline extensions within phase parameters
- Escalate technology issues directly to the infrastructure support queue
  with full provider context attached
- Flag systemic issues upward through the corps reporting chain

Issues outside the case manager's authority envelope are escalated to the
appropriate specialist with the case manager retaining coordination ownership.
The provider continues to interact through their case manager, not directly
with the escalation target, unless the provider requests otherwise.

**(e) Provider Cohort Assignment**

Case managers are assigned a defined cohort of providers within their district.
Cohort size is determined by transition complexity and phase. A single case manager
does not handle unlimited providers — cohort caps are set by HETA central based on
observed workload data from pilot districts and adjusted as transition progresses.

Where provider demand exceeds cohort capacity, additional case managers are deployed
within the authorized staffing envelope before cohort sizes are expanded.

**(f) Regional Office Services**

Regional offices provide:

- Walk-in support during defined office hours
- Scheduled individual consultations (in-person and virtual)
- Group onboarding sessions for provider cohorts, practice managers, and
  billing staff
- Technical workshops for payment rail and portal setup
- Office-hours style drop-in sessions for quick questions
- Printed and digital transition guides in plain language

Onboarding sessions are offered on a recurring schedule, not one-time events,
so that providers who are not ready at Phase 0 can participate at Phase 1 or
Phase 2 without penalty.

**(g) Provider Transition Instruments Available Through the PTC**

The following instruments are accessible through Regional Transition Offices:

- PCA-backed low-interest practice loans
- Revenue guarantees for early-phase direct care adopters
- Practice formation credits for new independent practices
- Income stabilization credits (12–24 month windows)
- Panel-based credits for Medicare and Medicaid patients under standardized billing
- Debt restructuring consultations with PCA capital officers
- Equipment-backed instruments for diagnostic capacity in under-served areas

Instrument availability is phase-gated. Not all instruments are available in all
phases or all districts. Case managers inform providers of available instruments
based on their practice profile and transition phase status.

**(h) Non-Enforcement Mandate and Provider Trust**

The Provider Transition Corps operates under an explicit non-enforcement mandate.

PTC personnel:

- Do not conduct compliance audits
- Do not report provider pricing to HETA enforcement functions
- Do not use provider dashboard data for enforcement purposes
- Do not share provider financial or operational information with the Market
  and Utility Enforcement function except as required by anti-fraud statute

Providers must be able to trust that asking their case manager a question about
pricing does not create an enforcement record. This trust is structural, not
discretionary. The firewall provisions of Section 9 make it legally enforceable.

The provider dashboard data shared with case managers is used exclusively for
transition support purposes. Its informational, non-punitive character in early
phases is explicitly preserved per the Healthcare Transition Phase Strategy.

### Section 3. Market Transition Waiver Authority

**(a) Authorization**

HETA is granted authority to issue **Market Transition Waivers** solely to enable
approved healthcare transition pilots to function during the transition from the
legacy regulatory framework to the restructured healthcare market.

**(b) Joint Issuance and Review**

Where a waiver implicates state regulatory authority, the waiver shall be:

- Issued jointly with the designated state regulatory representative, or
- Issued by HETA following documented consultation if joint issuance is not feasible

All waivers shall be explicitly labeled and published as *Market Transition Waivers*.

Waivers requested through Regional Transition Offices are prepared by Regulatory
Navigators and routed through HETA waiver authority. The requesting provider is
notified of status through their case manager.

**(c) Limitations**

Market Transition Waivers:

- Must be tied to a named statute or regulation
- Must specify the operational barrier addressed
- Must be pilot-scoped, purpose-bound, and time-limited
- Shall automatically expire unless affirmatively renewed

This authority shall not extend to digital identity systems, payment rails, or
financial infrastructure regulation.

---

### Section 4. Waiver Revocation and Enforcement

HETA retains authority to suspend or revoke any Market Transition Waiver upon
evidence of abuse, gaming, fraud, anti-competitive conduct, or threats to access
or safety.

Revocation decisions shall be made in coordination with state regulatory
representatives where applicable and reported to Congress.

Revocation proceedings are handled exclusively by the Market and Utility
Enforcement function. The Provider Transition Corps is not involved in revocation
or enforcement proceedings.

### Section 5. Performance-Gated Expansion

**(a) Unified Performance Gates**

Expansion of pilot participation, transition phase escalation, or geographic scope
shall be contingent upon performance gates jointly evaluated by:

- HETA
- Participating state regulators
- PCA (where capital instruments are involved)
- HETA SEC Boundary Authority (where coverage boundary integrity is implicated)

**(b) Gate Criteria** include:

- Clinician participation and retention
- Patient access continuity
- Transparency and compliance integrity
- Administrative burden reduction
- Absence of material gaming or market foreclosure
- Provider Transition Corps capacity readiness in receiving districts
- SEC boundary integrity in existing pilot districts, as certified by HETA's
  SEC Boundary Authority

Failure to clear gates triggers pause, rule adjustment, or rollback rather than
expansion. PTC capacity in a prospective expansion district is a gating condition —
regional offices must be established and staffed before enforcement obligations
activate in that district.

HETA's SEC Boundary Authority may independently block expansion where it determines
that existing pilot operations have produced material erosion of the coverage boundary
between market-layer care and SEC. This determination is subject to public reporting
and Congressional review but does not require prior Congressional approval to take
effect.

### Section 6. Congressional Oversight, Learning, and Field Intelligence

**(a) Annual Transition Findings Report**

HETA shall submit an annual **Transition Findings Report** to Congress describing:

- What worked and what failed operationally
- Which regulations blocked transition and which proved unnecessary
- How federal, state, and capital authorities interacted in practice
- Provider participation rates, cohort outcomes, and instrument utilization
- PTC regional office performance metrics including caseload, resolution rates,
  and onboarding completion
- Patterns of provider confusion or resistance that indicate policy design gaps
- SEC boundary integrity findings from the SEC Boundary Authority, including
  classification disputes, gaming patterns, and boundary-adjacent legislative
  or regulatory proposals reviewed during the reporting period
- Recommendations for the steady-state regulatory framework

**(b) Field Intelligence Loop**

Regional Transition Offices are a primary source of field intelligence for the
Transition Findings Report. Case managers and transition assistants log:

- Common provider questions and confusion points
- Instrument requests that fall outside available options
- Technology pain points and bug patterns
- Systemic issues that appear across multiple providers in a cohort

This log is aggregated, de-identified at the provider level, and routed to HETA
central on a defined reporting cadence. It feeds directly into the annual
Congressional report and into mid-cycle policy adjustments within HETA's
authorized operational scope.

Field intelligence is not used for enforcement. Logs are held under the same
firewall provisions as case manager interactions.

**(c) Congressional Oversight Scope**

Congress retains authority to modify, expand, or sunset HETA but shall not be
required to approve individual execution actions taken within the authorized charter.

Congress may review PTC performance metrics, instrument utilization rates, and
regional office caseloads as part of annual oversight. Congress does not direct
individual case manager assignments or provider interactions.

---

### Section 7. Sunset

All extraordinary authorities granted under this Act, including Market Transition
Waiver authority and Provider Transition Corps deployment authority, shall sunset
unless affirmatively reauthorized by Congress following review of the Transition
Findings Report.

PTC sunset is phased. As markets stabilize and administrative burden drops, regional
office functions reduce in scope before closure. Providers who have completed
transition do not retain active case manager assignments. Offices in fully transitioned
districts are wound down on a published schedule with advance notice to remaining
active cohorts.

Knowledge artifacts — onboarding guides, instrument templates, transition playbooks —
are retained and published for use in subsequent districts and future administrations.

The SEC Boundary Authority established under Function 5 of Section 1 does not sunset
under this provision. Its continuation is governed by Section 7A.

### Section 7A. HETA Steady-State Form — SEC Boundary Authority

**(a) Conversion Upon Transition Completion**

Upon expiration or non-reauthorization of HETA's extraordinary transition authorities
under Section 7, HETA does not dissolve. It converts to its steady-state form as a
standing SEC Boundary Authority.

The conversion is automatic and does not require separate Congressional authorization.
It occurs when transition authorities lapse and the Provider Transition Corps has
completed its wind-down under the published district schedule.

The final Transition Findings Report submitted to Congress under Section 6 shall
include a proposed steady-state operating plan, including staffing, budget, mandate
scope, and reporting cadence for HETA in its boundary authority form.

**(b) Steady-State Mandate**

In steady state, HETA's mandate is limited to:

- Maintaining and enforcing the coverage boundary between market-layer care and
  Severe Event Coverage
- Classifying services and clinical episodes as market-layer or SEC-covered where
  disputes or novel presentations arise
- Enforcing emergency utility rate-band discipline and cost transparency requirements
- Detecting and acting on anti-gaming conduct that exploits the market-to-SEC
  boundary, including retroactive reclassification, upcoding, and episode boundary
  manipulation
- Maintaining the infrastructure compliance standards required for SEC participation
- Reporting to Congress on boundary integrity, gaming patterns, and classification
  disputes on a defined cadence

HETA in steady state does not manage pilots, issue Market Transition Waivers, deploy
regional offices, or administer transition instruments. Those authorities have lapsed.

**(c) Relationship to DoDA**

DoDA certifies whether the SEC boundary is holding actuarially — measuring claim
frequency, episode severity, boundary dispute rates, and gaming-adjusted cost trends.

HETA enforces that it holds operationally — classifying episodes, adjudicating
disputes, detecting manipulation, and imposing consequences for boundary violations.

These are parallel authorities. DoDA does not direct HETA's enforcement decisions.
HETA does not determine DoDA's actuarial methodology. Where DoDA's certification
indicates systemic boundary erosion, HETA is required to report to Congress within
a defined window and propose corrective action.

Where HETA's enforcement findings indicate patterns that affect actuarial assumptions,
HETA shall notify DoDA so that reserve and contribution sufficiency models can be
updated accordingly. The two bodies coordinate on methodology without subordinating
either authority to the other.

**(d) Standing Authority to Flag Legislative Risk**

HETA holds standing authority to submit a formal **SEC Boundary Impact Assessment**
to Congress prior to implementation of any proposed statutory or regulatory change
that would:

- Alter the definition of a covered SEC event or episode
- Expand or contract the market-layer service taxonomy
- Modify account architecture in ways that affect the boundary between routine-care
  accounts and SEC coverage
- Change insurance boundary rules in ways that affect SEC actuarial integrity
- Alter the coverage trigger conditions for any SEC-adjacent care category

A submitted Assessment does not constitute a veto. Congress retains full authority
to proceed. The Assessment creates a public record that the boundary implications
were evaluated before the change took effect.

HETA shall submit an Assessment within a defined statutory window from the date a
relevant proposal is introduced or published for public comment. Failure to submit
within that window does not constitute approval of the proposal.

**(e) Steady-State Footprint**

HETA's institutional footprint in steady state is materially smaller than during
transition.

Steady-state HETA retains:

- A boundary classification and adjudication function
- An anti-gaming and enforcement function
- An infrastructure compliance function
- A DoDA coordination function
- An Inspector General function
- A Congressional reporting function

Steady-state HETA does not retain:

- Regional transition offices
- Provider Transition Corps personnel
- PCA capital instrument delivery capacity
- Market Transition Waiver authority
- Performance-gated expansion authority

Staffing and budget are recalibrated at conversion. Congress receives the proposed
steady-state operating plan as part of the final Transition Findings Report and
may modify it within a defined review window before conversion takes effect.

**(f) Anti-Capture in Steady State**

The anti-capture provisions that apply during transition continue in steady state,
including cooling-off periods for senior staff, public disclosure of meetings with
regulated entities, audit team rotation, and Inspector General oversight.

A permanent boundary regulator with a narrow mandate is particularly vulnerable to
capture by the entities whose pricing power it constrains. The institutional
discipline required during transition does not relax because the transition is over.
Anti-capture rules in steady state shall be no weaker than those in force during
the transition period and shall be reviewed by the Inspector General on a defined
cadence with findings reported to Congress.

### Section 8. Execution Corps Structure and Reporting Chain

HETA's Provider Transition Corps operates within the Execution Corps framework
defined in the Execution Corps Instrument Specification.

The reporting chain is:

**Congressional authorization** (objectives, spend envelope, sunset conditions)
↓
**HETA Executive** (mandate oversight, enforcement/support firewall)
↓
**Regional Execution Corps Deployment** (district-level Integrated Transition Team)
↓
**Provider Transition Team** (case managers, specialists, office staff)
↓
**Named Case Manager** (provider relationship and cohort coordination)

Authority flows downward within pre-authorized envelopes. Field intelligence flows
upward through the same chain into HETA central and the annual Congressional report.

No individual in the chain holds unilateral authority. The internal counter-power
structure required by the Execution Corps Instrument Specification applies:

- Execution Lead (transition delivery and sequencing)
- Envelope / Compliance Lead (scope, spend, legality)
- Escalation Arbiter (tie-break only, publicly logged)

Rotation schedules for specialist roles within regional offices are misaligned by
design to prevent capture of any provider cohort by a single operator.

In steady state, the reporting chain contracts to HETA Executive oversight of the
boundary classification, enforcement, and Inspector General functions. The Execution
Corps deployment structure does not persist beyond transition.

### Section 9. Enforcement Firewall

The separation between HETA's enforcement functions and the Provider Transition
Corps is structural and legally enforceable.

**(a) Personnel Separation**

Enforcement personnel and PTC personnel operate in separate reporting chains that
converge only at the HETA executive level. No individual may hold simultaneous
roles in both functions.

**(b) Data Separation**

Provider data accessed or generated through PTC interactions — including case manager
notes, instrument applications, pricing questions, dashboard orientations, and field
intelligence logs — is not accessible to the Market and Utility Enforcement function
except under the following conditions:

- Evidence of active fraud or patient harm, requiring written authorization from
  the HETA Inspector General
- Statutory anti-fraud reporting obligations, applied narrowly and logged publicly

Routine pricing information shared with case managers in the context of transition
support is explicitly excluded from enforcement use.

**(c) Provider Notice**

Providers are informed of the firewall at first contact with their regional office.
The scope and limits of the firewall are explained in plain language. Case managers
are trained to redirect enforcement-adjacent questions to the appropriate channel
and to confirm to providers that their support interactions are protected.

**(d) Audit and Oversight**

The firewall is subject to annual audit by the HETA Inspector General. Audit findings
are reported to Congress as part of the Transition Findings Report. Firewall breaches
are treated as serious compliance failures requiring corrective action and public
disclosure.

### Section 10. Construction

Nothing in this Act shall be construed to:

- Establish a national healthcare payer
- Nationalize healthcare delivery
- Deregulate healthcare markets broadly
- Supersede state authority except as explicitly provided
- Grant HETA enforcement authority over provider clinical decisions
- Authorize PTC personnel to make compliance determinations
- Authorize HETA's SEC Boundary Impact Assessment to operate as a legislative veto

This Act establishes a **co-deployed execution, support, and learning instrument**
during the transition period, and a **permanent SEC boundary enforcement body**
in steady state.

The Provider Transition Corps exists to make transition survivable for the providers
whose participation is essential to the reform's success. It is not a subsidy program,
a compliance function, or a permanent government presence in private practice. It is
a time-bounded deployment designed to reduce friction, deliver instruments, and exit
when the transition is complete.

The SEC Boundary Authority exists because the line between market-layer care and
severe-event coverage is the structural load-bearing element of the entire healthcare
reform. Without a permanent, dedicated body to maintain and enforce that boundary,
incumbent pressure will erode it — gradually, through classification disputes,
upcoding, episode boundary manipulation, and legislative carve-outs — until the
reform's actuarial integrity is lost. HETA in steady state is the institutional
answer to that erosion risk.

# SEC Contribution Sufficiency and Wind-Down Framework

## Purpose and Scope

This brief defines the fiscal rule governing any public contribution, payroll charge, premium-equivalent assessment, or dedicated tax used to finance Severe Event Coverage, healthcare transition stabilization, or related risk-pool capitalization.

The core principle is simple: healthcare contributions should finance certified need, not become permanent revenue machines.

The framework establishes a statutory anti-ratchet rule. If the healthcare system reduces long-run actuarial risk through better primary care utilization, earlier intervention, lower emergency incidence, lower severe-event frequency, lower regulated episode cost, improved price transparency, or reduced administrative overhead, required contribution rates must decline automatically.

This brief applies to SEC-related public financing. It does not define the full benefit design of SEC, routine-care subsidies, HTA/THA accounts, or employer contribution obligations. It defines how public healthcare contributions are calibrated, segregated, invested, audited, and wound down when risk declines.

---

## Problem Statement

The current healthcare system already imposes large public and quasi-public costs. Those costs are not always visible as taxes.

They appear as:

- Medicaid and uncompensated-care spending
- emergency care for deferred routine conditions
- higher private premiums caused by cost shifting
- employer-sponsored insurance costs that suppress wages
- hospital billing and claims bureaucracy
- federal borrowing that becomes future taxation
- state budget pressure from healthcare growth
- household debt from unaffordable care
- delayed care that later becomes more expensive care

The existing system therefore operates as a hidden healthcare tax. The tax is collected through premiums, prices, debt, wage suppression, administrative overhead, and future public obligations rather than through a transparent, bounded contribution structure.

A reformed healthcare system should not pretend these costs disappear. It should make them visible, assign them to the correct layer, and manage them according to statutory rules.

The danger is that a new contribution system could recreate the same problem in public form: once a dedicated healthcare charge is created, the state may continue collecting it even after the underlying risk declines. That would convert a transition and risk-pool capitalization instrument into a permanent revenue ratchet.

This brief prevents that outcome.

---

## Core Design Principle

SEC contributions are risk-pool capitalization contributions, not permanent consumption taxes.

The public may be required to contribute to SEC only to the extent necessary to:

- pay current covered obligations
- maintain actuarially certified reserves
- satisfy stress-tested solvency requirements
- stabilize transition volatility
- finance authorized risk-pool start-up costs
- support temporary backstop obligations during rollout

Once those needs are met, excess collections must not disappear into the general fund. Surplus must be invested, returned, credited, or used to reduce future contribution rates according to statute.

If the system works, people should pay less over time.

---

## Market Realism and Fiscal Honesty

This framework is not a write-off scheme.

It does not assume healthcare costs vanish. It recognizes that healthcare costs already exist and are already being paid through opaque channels. The reform converts those hidden costs into visible, measurable, accountable obligations.

The fiscal case is market realism:

- risk should be pooled where risk is severe and non-shoppable
- routine care should be transparent and market-layer where feasible
- prevention and primary care should reduce downstream severe-event exposure
- lower actuarial exposure should reduce required contributions
- public funds should be segregated and governed by explicit reserve rules
- surplus should compound for public benefit or reduce future charges

The government does not get to keep money merely because it collected it.

---

## Covered Contributions

This framework applies to any dedicated public financing stream authorized for SEC or healthcare transition risk-pool purposes, including:

- SEC payroll contributions
- SEC premium-equivalent assessments
- income-based SEC contributions
- employer or employee SEC assessments where authorized
- transition stabilization assessments
- temporary federal backstop charges
- dedicated healthcare solvency surcharges
- other statutory healthcare risk-pool contributions

This framework does not automatically apply to individual HTA/THA balances, employer routine-care account contributions, or ordinary general-fund appropriations unless Congress explicitly incorporates them.

---

## Segregated Fund Rule

SEC-related contributions shall be segregated from the general fund.

Funds collected under this title may be used only for authorized purposes, including:

- paying SEC-covered obligations
- maintaining reserves
- satisfying solvency requirements
- financing transition volatility
- repaying authorized backstop advances
- making statutory rate-reduction transfers
- issuing authorized payroll offsets or account credits
- investing surplus under the Public Capital Stewardship Standard

Segregation is essential for legitimacy. If contributions flow into the general fund, taxpayers cannot distinguish risk-pool finance from ordinary taxation. The contribution then becomes politically indistinguishable from a permanent tax.

---

## Certified Reserve Target

DoDA shall certify the required SEC reserve target on a defined cadence.

The reserve target should account for:

- expected severe-event claims
- claim severity distribution
- demographic mix
- regional cost variation
- emergency and trauma utilization
- major surgery and complex episode frequency
- regulated rate-band assumptions
- transition volatility
- insurer participation and private supplemental coverage interaction
- stress scenarios, including pandemics, disasters, regional hospital failures, and economic downturns
- administrative settlement costs
- public backstop exposure

The reserve target should be published publicly, with methods, assumptions, confidence intervals, and stress-test results sufficient for democratic accountability.

DoDA certifies the methodology. HETA or the designated fiscal steward operates the payment and reserve infrastructure. Treasury may serve as custodian where appropriate. Institutional roles should remain separated to avoid self-grading.

---

## Contribution Sufficiency Rule

Contribution rates shall be set only at the level necessary to finance current obligations, maintain certified reserves, satisfy stress-tested solvency standards, and meet authorized transition costs.

Rates may not be set to generate unrestricted surplus.

Where collections exceed current obligations and required reserves, excess funds shall be governed by the surplus allocation rule.

Where reserves fall below certified thresholds, contribution rates may rise according to the statutory adjustment schedule, subject to public reporting and legislative limits.

The goal is not artificially low contributions. The goal is sufficient contributions with no revenue ratchet.

---

## Actuarial Risk Reduction Rule

If certified actuarial exposure declines, required contribution rates must decline.

DoDA shall evaluate whether actuarial exposure has fallen due to factors such as:

- increased primary care utilization
- increased preventive utilization
- lower emergency department use for avoidable conditions
- reduced severe-event frequency
- lower regulated episode costs
- lower administrative overhead
- improved chronic disease management
- reduced avoidable hospitalization
- improved regional health center access
- market-layer price compression
- earlier diagnosis and intervention
- reduced uncompensated care pressure

Where DoDA certifies that reduced exposure is durable and not merely a short-term utilization anomaly, the contribution rate shall automatically step down according to the statutory glide path.

The public should benefit from reduced risk through reduced contribution burden.

---

## Surplus Allocation Rule

When annual collections exceed current obligations and certified reserve requirements, surplus funds must be allocated according to a statutory hierarchy.

### First — Reserve Completion

Surplus first fills any gap between current reserves and the certified reserve target.

### Second — Backstop Repayment

Surplus then repays any authorized transition backstop advances, emergency appropriations, or temporary solvency support used to stabilize SEC rollout.

### Third — Public Capital Stewardship

Once reserve and repayment requirements are met, remaining surplus may be invested under the Public Capital Stewardship Standard.

Permitted investments should prioritize low-risk public instruments and healthcare cost-reduction capacity, including:

- Treasury securities
- federally certified reserve instruments
- PCA instruments that reduce long-run healthcare cost exposure
- regional health center infrastructure where certified to reduce severe-event risk
- provider transition infrastructure that lowers administrative cost
- health IT settlement infrastructure that reduces claims overhead
- workforce capacity investments tied to access adequacy and measurable cost reduction

Investment should be conservative, transparent, and tied to public benefit. The fund should not speculate.

### Fourth — Rate Reduction

When capitalization thresholds are reached, contribution rates automatically decline according to the statutory step-down schedule.

### Fifth — Direct Offsets or Credits

If surplus persists after rate reductions, excess may be returned through payroll offsets, SEC contribution rebates, HTA/HSA account credits for undercapitalized households, or other statutory return mechanisms.

---

## Public Capital Stewardship Standard

Surplus SEC funds may be invested only under a public capital stewardship standard.

Investments must satisfy the following conditions:

- capital preservation is prioritized over yield maximization
- investments must be publicly disclosed
- expected public benefit must be specified
- investments must not create conflicts with HETA enforcement duties
- DoDA must certify measurement methodology
- PCA instruments must include repayment, revenue-share, or public-benefit terms where appropriate
- private capture safeguards must apply
- no investment may be made for general industrial policy purposes unless directly tied to healthcare cost reduction or SEC solvency

This turns surplus into a compounding public asset rather than a slush fund.

---

## Contribution Step-Down Schedule

The statute shall define a contribution step-down schedule that activates automatically when capitalization thresholds are met.

A simplified structure may include:

- Threshold 1: reserve target met for four consecutive quarters; scheduled contribution increase pauses
- Threshold 2: reserve target exceeded by a defined margin for eight consecutive quarters; contribution rate falls by a defined percentage
- Threshold 3: stress-tested reserve plus backstop repayment complete; contribution rate falls to maintenance level
- Threshold 4: durable actuarial risk reduction certified for a defined period; additional rate reduction activates
- Threshold 5: persistent overcapitalization after maintenance level; payroll offsets or account credits activate

The schedule should be formula-based and published in advance.

Political discretion should not be required for taxpayers to receive the benefit of lower system risk.

---

## Maintenance Rate

A fully mature SEC system may still require a maintenance contribution.

The maintenance rate covers:

- expected annual severe-event obligations
- ordinary reserve replenishment
- settlement infrastructure
- fraud and anomaly detection
- stress-test buffer maintenance
- statutory readiness obligations where applicable

The maintenance rate should be materially lower than the transition capitalization rate if the reform succeeds.

The law should distinguish between the transition rate, capitalization rate, and maintenance rate. Without that distinction, temporary financing needs can become permanent charges.

---

## No General-Fund Absorption

Surplus SEC contributions shall not be absorbed into the general fund.

If excess collections occur, the statute must direct them toward reserve completion, backstop repayment, public capital stewardship, rate reduction, payroll offsets, or account credits.

This is a trust-building rule. It prevents the public from reasonably concluding that healthcare contributions are simply another revenue stream for unrelated spending.

---

## Relationship to HTA/THA and Health Security Accounts

HTA/THA accounts and any future Health Security Accounts operate in the routine-care layer.

SEC contributions operate in the severe-event risk-pool layer.

The two layers interact because better routine-care access may reduce severe-event risk. Increased primary care utilization, preventive care, chronic disease management, dental prevention, mental health access, and early diagnostics may lower future SEC exposure.

When that happens, the SEC contribution sufficiency rule requires public charges to decline. Routine-care investment should buy down severe-event risk, and reduced risk should return to the public as lower required contributions.

This connection is actuarial, not administrative. HTA balances do not replace SEC reserves. SEC reserves do not pay for ordinary routine care. The bridge between them is measured risk reduction.

---

## Relationship to Employer Contributions

Employer routine-care contributions and SEC public contributions serve different functions.

Employer HTA contributions help workers pay for routine market-layer care and prevent firms from externalizing routine-care costs onto workers and public systems.

SEC contributions finance severe-event risk pooling and transition capitalization.

If employer routine-care contributions and income-based subsidies increase routine utilization enough to reduce severe-event exposure, SEC contribution rates should fall accordingly.

This gives employers and workers a clear fiscal upside: supporting routine care can reduce long-run severe-event financing needs.

---

## Relationship to Benefits Gradient

The Benefits Gradient protects households from cliff effects as income rises.

SEC contribution schedules should be coordinated with the benefits gradient to avoid recreating effective marginal tax rate spikes. Contribution obligations should phase in and out smoothly, with DoDA modeling household-level net effects before rate changes take effect.

If SEC rates step down because actuarial exposure declines, those reductions should be visible in household resources rather than silently offset by other benefit withdrawals.

---

## DoDA Role

DoDA is the independent certifier of actuarial exposure, reserve sufficiency, contribution adequacy, and wind-down triggers.

DoDA shall publish:

- reserve methodology
- actuarial assumptions
- severe-event incidence trends
- contribution sufficiency analysis
- stress-test results
- surplus status
- wind-down trigger status
- administrative overhead trends
- routine-care utilization effects on SEC exposure
- rate-reduction recommendations or automatic trigger certifications

DoDA does not operate the fund. It certifies the measurement and publishes the evidence.

---

## HETA Role

HETA administers SEC operational mechanics and maintains the payment-routing infrastructure.

For this framework, HETA may:

- provide claims and settlement data required for reserve modeling
- maintain episode boundary definitions
- track severe-event utilization
- detect fraud and anomalies
- implement contribution-related settlement logic where authorized
- coordinate with Treasury, PCA, and DoDA
- publish operational performance data

HETA should not be the sole judge of whether contribution rates are adequate or should decline. That role belongs to DoDA and the statutory formula.

---

## Treasury and PCA Role

Treasury may serve as custodian for segregated funds.

PCA may manage or structure approved public capital instruments where surplus is invested for healthcare cost-reduction purposes.

PCA-managed investments must comply with the Public Capital Stewardship Standard and remain subordinate to reserve sufficiency and rate-reduction requirements.

Surplus investment authority should not become a workaround for avoiding contribution step-downs. If the statutory capitalization threshold is met, rate reductions must occur.

---

## Transparency Requirements

The public dashboard should display:

- contribution rate by category
- total collections
- total SEC obligations paid
- administrative cost
- reserve balance
- certified reserve target
- surplus or shortfall
- investment holdings where applicable
- investment returns
- rate step-down trigger status
- projected maintenance rate
- stress-test results
- actuarial risk trend
- expected date of next certification

Transparency should be plain-language as well as machine-readable. The point is not merely compliance; it is public trust.

---

## Anti-Ratchet Guardrails

The framework shall include explicit anti-ratchet safeguards:

- no general-fund absorption
- statutory reserve target
- automatic rate step-downs
- public surplus dashboard
- DoDA certification independent from fund operation
- legislative limits on raising rates outside certified need
- mandatory explanation for any rate increase
- prohibition on using SEC surplus for unrelated programs
- automatic review if reserves remain above target for a defined period
- taxpayer offset or account-credit trigger for persistent overcapitalization

A system that lowers risk but keeps charging the same amount has failed its fiscal compact.

---

## Risk of Underfunding

Anti-ratchet discipline must not become underfunding.

SEC protects households against severe medical financial shock. If contributions are set too low, reserves will fail, providers may face payment instability, and political pressure may force emergency general-fund bailouts.

The framework therefore uses sufficiency as the anchor. Rates decline when certified risk and reserve conditions permit. They do not decline merely because lower rates are politically attractive.

The rule is symmetrical: no overcollection without step-down, no undercollection without correction.

---

## Metrics and Evaluation

DoDA should evaluate the framework using metrics including:

- SEC contribution rate trend
- severe-event incidence rate
- severe-event claim severity
- emergency utilization rates
- avoidable hospitalization rates
- primary care utilization
- preventive utilization
- chronic disease control proxies where authorized
- regulated episode price trends
- administrative cost share
- reserve sufficiency
- surplus persistence
- rate step-down frequency
- household contribution burden by income quintile
- employer burden by firm size and sector where applicable
- interaction with benefits-gradient EMTR ceilings
- public trust and compliance measures

Evaluation should distinguish true risk reduction from temporary claim suppression, delayed care, coding shifts, or access barriers.

Lower claims are not automatically good. Lower claims are good only when access is preserved or improved and health outcomes do not deteriorate.

---

## Phase Strategy

### Phase 0 — Baseline and Authorization

Before SEC contributions begin, DoDA publishes baseline severe-event incidence, claim severity, administrative cost, reserve assumptions, and projected transition capitalization needs.

### Phase 1 — Segregated Fund Creation

Congress establishes the segregated SEC contribution fund, statutory reserve target methodology, surplus allocation hierarchy, and anti-ratchet rules.

### Phase 2 — Transition Capitalization

Contributions finance early SEC obligations, transition volatility, and reserve buildout. Public dashboards report collections, claims, reserves, and administrative costs.

### Phase 3 — Risk Reduction Measurement

DoDA evaluates whether routine-care reforms, price transparency, regional health centers, HTA/THA utilization, and market-layer competition are reducing severe-event exposure.

### Phase 4 — Step-Down Activation

When certified thresholds are met, contribution rates decline automatically according to the statutory glide path.

### Phase 5 — Maintenance State

Once the system reaches full reserve sufficiency and durable risk reduction, SEC contributions decline to the maintenance rate. Persistent surplus triggers payroll offsets, account credits, or additional rate reductions.

---

## What This Is Not

This is not a permanent healthcare tax machine.

This is not a general revenue stream.

This is not a blank check for healthcare spending.

This is not a promise that healthcare can be free.

This is not austerity or underfunding.

This is not a write-off scheme.

This is a statutory fiscal discipline framework that requires healthcare contributions to track certified need, invest or return surplus, and decline when the underlying actuarial risk declines.

## Draft Statutory Language Sketch

### Section 1. Purpose

The purpose of this title is to ensure that SEC-related contributions are collected only to the extent necessary to finance covered obligations, maintain certified reserves, satisfy stress-tested solvency requirements, and support authorized transition stabilization.

### Section 2. Segregated Fund

There is established a segregated SEC Contribution Fund. Amounts deposited into the Fund shall not be commingled with the general fund and may be used only for purposes authorized under this title.

### Section 3. Reserve Certification

DoDA shall certify the required reserve target for the SEC Contribution Fund on a defined cadence using publicly disclosed actuarial methodology, severe-event incidence data, claim severity data, regulated rate assumptions, administrative cost data, and stress-test scenarios.

### Section 4. Contribution Sufficiency

Contribution rates shall be calibrated to the amount necessary to satisfy current covered obligations, certified reserve requirements, stress-tested solvency standards, and authorized transition costs. Rates may not be set to generate unrestricted surplus.

### Section 5. Surplus Allocation

Collections exceeding current obligations and certified reserve requirements shall be allocated first to reserve completion, second to repayment of authorized backstop advances, third to public capital stewardship investments, fourth to statutory rate reductions, and fifth to payroll offsets, account credits, or other statutory return mechanisms.

### Section 6. Risk Reduction Step-Down

Where DoDA certifies durable reduction in SEC actuarial exposure, contribution rates shall automatically decline according to the statutory step-down schedule.

### Section 7. No General-Fund Absorption

No amount collected under this title may be transferred to the general fund or used for unrelated programs.

### Section 8. Public Dashboard

The Secretary, in coordination with HETA, Treasury, PCA, and DoDA, shall maintain a public dashboard reporting contribution rates, collections, obligations, reserves, reserve targets, surplus, investment holdings, administrative costs, actuarial risk trends, and step-down trigger status.

### Section 9. Maintenance Rate

Upon certification that the Fund has satisfied reserve, repayment, and stress-tested solvency requirements, contribution rates shall decline to the maintenance rate defined by statute, subject to automatic adjustment only where certified need changes.

### Section 10. Persistent Overcapitalization

If the Fund remains above the certified reserve target and maintenance threshold for a defined period, statutory payroll offsets, account credits, rebates, or additional rate reductions shall automatically activate.

---

## Summary

SEC contributions should be temporary where the need is temporary, sufficient where the risk is real, and lower when the system succeeds.

The current healthcare system already imposes hidden taxes through premiums, Medicaid spillover, uncompensated care, federal borrowing, wage suppression, and administrative waste. This framework converts those hidden costs into transparent, segregated, rules-bound contributions.

If better routine care, prevention, primary care access, price transparency, and market-layer competition reduce long-run actuarial risk, the law forces contributions down.

The promise is not that healthcare costs vanish. The promise is fiscal honesty: collect what is needed, publish the math, invest or return surplus, and stop charging people at transition rates once the system has bought down the risk.

# Healthcare Transition and State Pilot Framework

## Purpose

This brief defines a federally backed, opt-in state pilot framework for transitioning the
healthcare system to a unified market with a universal Severe Event Coverage (SEC) floor.
The objective is to move deliberately, measure rigorously, stabilize insurer risk modeling,
and preserve access while acknowledging that transition will involve short-term disruption.

State pilots are a sequencing mechanism, not an alternative end state. The destination is
nationwide participation.

---

## Federal Role and Authority

The federal government provides:

- Statutory authority for SEC coverage, pricing bands, and pooling rules  
- Full financial backstop for SEC risk during pilots  
- Narrow preemption of state rules that fragment catastrophic risk pools  
- A centralized Healthcare Transition and Execution Team  

States opt in voluntarily during the pilot phase. Opt-in reflects acceptance of SEC rules,
pooling mechanics, and transparency requirements.

---

## State Opt-In and National End State

- States vote to opt into the early pilot framework  
- Opt-in governs sequencing, not permanence  
- The long-term design assumes nationwide SEC coverage and pooling  

Pilots exist to de-risk execution, not to create permanent opt-out states.

---

## SEC as a National Risk Layer

SEC is a federally standardized catastrophic risk layer:

- Covers rare, severe, high-cost, medically necessary events  
- Federally backstopped during transition  
- Designed for national-scale pooling  

Risk pooling converges to full national scope as states enter.

---

## Cross-State Risk Pooling for Insurers

For SEC only, insurers participating in the pilot must pool risk across all opt-in states
within the active cohort.

Rules:
- No state-bounded SEC pools  
- All-in participation across the cohort (no cherry-picking)  
- Federally standardized pricing bands and capital treatment  
- Claims clearing through federal payment rails  

This restores actuarial stability and enables meaningful competition.

---

## Pilot State Selection

### Initial Cohort

Approximately five opt-in states are selected based on:

- Smaller population size to limit backstop exposure  
- Administrative and data readiness  
- Willingness to integrate federal systems  
- Geographic and demographic diversity  

---

## Pre-Pilot Econometric Baseline

Before final selection, a national baseline is constructed, including:

- Per-capita spending by category  
- Utilization rates across care settings  
- Provider density and facility mix  
- Insurance mix and premium levels  
- Price dispersion for standardized services  

Euclidean or Mahalanobis matching identifies control states.
Difference-in-differences and synthetic control methods are pre-specified.

---

## Transition Timeline and Early Signal Extraction

### Year 1: Federal Stand-Up and Announcement

- Stand up the Healthcare Transition Team in Year 1 of a presidency  
- Finalize SEC rules, pooling mechanics, and reporting standards  
- Open the state opt-in process  
- Announce initial pilot states publicly in May–June  

---

### Go-Live and Early Operational Signals (0–90 Days)

Within the first 90 days post-implementation, the following are monitored:

- Insurer participation and solvency behavior  
- Actuarial repricing accuracy (loss ratio drift vs projections)  
- Claims processing latency and failure rates  
- Provider participation and price submission compliance  
- Immediate access failures (PCP, urgent care, imaging, ED diversion)  

These signals determine whether the system is operationally stable.

---

### Early Outcome Signals (90–150 Days)

By 120–150 days, the evaluation expands to:

- Utilization shifts (ED to urgent care, inpatient to outpatient)  
- Price convergence for shoppable services  
- Facility closures versus replacement capacity  
- Early changes in out-of-pocket exposure relative to controls  

These indicators are sufficient to assess whether the pilot is functioning
without systemic harm.

---

## Mid–Year 2 Decision Gate (May–June)

A formal **Initial Stability Report** is published in January–February of Year 2.
Matched-control analysis is updated in March–April.

In May–June of Year 2, the federal government announces one of three actions:

1. **Expand** to the next cohort (approximately five additional states)  
2. **Pause and Adjust** specific rules or thresholds, then proceed  
3. **Freeze Expansion** temporarily to stabilize and correct  

This decision is evidence-based and independent of the election cycle.

---

## Provider Market Restructuring and Access Safeguards

During pilots:

- PCPs, urgent care, imaging, testing, and outpatient facilities are monitored continuously  
- Excess capacity contraction is expected as price inflation is removed  
- Closures may occur  

Loss of functional access is not permitted. Federal backstop mechanisms are deployed if
geographic or service gaps emerge.

---

## Embedded Federal–State Transition Teams

Federal teams are embedded locally to:

- Resolve operational issues in real time  
- Coordinate with regulators, insurers, and providers  
- Serve as escalation points for access, pricing, or pooling failures  

Public communication emphasizes transparency and live system learning.

---

## Annual Expansion Ratchet

Expansion is designed to be predictable and mechanical:

- Year 1: Initial pilot announcement (~5 states)  
- Mid-Year 2: Decision on +5 states  
- Year 3: Further expansion or conversion of pilots to default participation  
- Year 4: National rules locked based on accumulated evidence  

Expansion occurs annually unless evidence indicates pause is required.

---

## End-State Trajectory

As pilots scale:

- SEC pooling becomes fully national  
- Insurers compete on efficiency and service, not risk selection  
- States transition from pilot opt-in to full participation  

The pilot phase governs sequencing. The end state is nationwide coverage.

---

## Summary

This framework aligns healthcare reform with political reality by pairing rigorous
evaluation with early signal extraction and annual expansion decisions. The system is
designed to show visible progress within 12–18 months while preserving the ability to
pause, correct, and scale responsibly.

State pilots are the on-ramp. National rollout is the destination.

## Purpose and Scope

This brief establishes the **Healthcare Execution and Transition Authority (HETA)**, a permanent, mission-bound execution body responsible for implementing, stabilizing, and enforcing the healthcare reforms defined across this framework.

HETA is responsible for **execution, enforcement, and continuity**, not care delivery, benefit design, or payment policy. Its role is to ensure that healthcare markets, emergency utilities, and insurance boundaries function as designed and remain resistant to capture over time.

---

## Why HETA Is Required

Healthcare reform fails when:
- implementation authority is fragmented across agencies,
- enforcement is episodic rather than continuous,
- and incumbents can delay, game, or partially comply without consequence.

Because this framework restructures **care delivery markets, emergency utility regulation, and insurance boundaries simultaneously**, a single operational execution body is required to maintain coherence during transition and discipline in steady state.

HETA exists to own the *how*, not the *what*.

---

## HETA’s Operating Model

HETA executes reform through three integrated roles:

1. **Transition Management**  
2. **Market and Utility Enforcement**  
3. **Infrastructure-Enabled Oversight**

HETA does not operate through manual processes or bespoke negotiations. All execution is mediated through a standardized infrastructure layer for authentication, authorization, data exchange, and payment, defined separately and used across the system.

---

## Core Responsibilities

### 1. Transition Management

HETA is responsible for:
- Sequencing reforms across care markets and emergency utilities  
- Managing geographically bounded pilots  
- Deploying temporary stabilization mechanisms when access is threatened  
- Pausing, adjusting, or reversing rollout when predefined guardrails are breached  

Emergency access continuity is the non-negotiable constraint during transition.

---

### 2. Market and Utility Enforcement

HETA enforces:
- Market boundaries between primary care, urgent care, emergency care, and surgery  
- Price transparency and bundled pricing requirements  
- Emergency utility rate discipline and cost disclosure  
- Insurance boundary rules that preserve actuarial integrity  

Enforcement is rules-based, prospective, and uniform across public and private actors.

---

### 3. Infrastructure-Enabled Oversight

HETA relies on a shared **healthcare infrastructure layer** to execute oversight at scale.

This infrastructure layer provides:
- standardized authentication of patients, providers, facilities, and insurers,
- cryptographic authorization for data access and eligibility verification,
- uniform APIs for data exchange and audit,
- and real-time payment settlement rails.

HETA governs **compliance with the infrastructure**, not its day-to-day operation.

---

## Data, Audits, and Actuarial Benchmarking

Using infrastructure-generated data, HETA:
- Maintains independent actuarial reference models for catastrophic risk  
- Produces benchmark bands for emergency and catastrophic costs  
- Detects outliers in provider costs and insurer assumptions  
- Flags potential price fixing, upcoding, or misreporting  

HETA publishes benchmarks and methodologies, not proprietary pricing strategies.

---

## Anti-Gaming and Anti-Capture Mandate

HETA is explicitly charged with preventing:
- emergency upcoding and retroactive reclassification,
- facility-fee unbundling and shadow pricing,
- ownership-based referral steering,
- exclusive contracting that forecloses market entry.

To resist capture, HETA operates under:
- cooling-off periods for senior staff,
- public disclosure of meetings with regulated entities,
- audit team rotation,
- independent inspector-general oversight.

---

## Legal Authorities

Congress grants HETA:
- audit and data-collection authority across payers and providers,
- enforcement authority tied to participation in regulated markets,
- rate-review authority for emergency utility services,
- time-bounded regulatory waiver powers tied to named statutes.

All extraordinary authorities sunset unless affirmatively renewed.

---

## Enforcement Lever

Compliance with HETA rules and infrastructure standards is a condition of:
- emergency utility reimbursement,
- catastrophic insurance reimbursement,
- public subsidies,
- and participation in regulated care markets.

This ensures enforcement without centralizing care delivery or payment.

---

## What HETA Is Not

HETA is not:
- a national healthcare payer,
- a healthcare provider,
- a centralized health database,
- a single-vendor technology platform,
- or a substitute for state licensure or accreditation bodies.

It is an execution and discipline body.

---

## Bottom Line

Healthcare reform without execution fails. HETA provides the permanent institutional capacity to manage transition risk, enforce market rules, and sustain transparency—using standardized authentication, data exchange, and payment infrastructure—while preserving pluralistic care delivery and competitive insurance markets.

## Purpose and Scope

This brief defines the Regional Health Center Network — a system of federally
backstopped, contractor-operated integrated health facilities designed to ensure
baseline healthcare access across the full geographic range of the U.S. population.

The network exists to solve a specific structural failure. The transition to
transparent, competitive healthcare markets will compress prices and reduce
administrative overhead, but it may also contract excess capacity in ways that leave
rural and low-income populations without nearby access before replacement capacity
forms. The Regional Health Center Network closes that gap by design, before
enforcement phases activate.

This document defines the federal funding model and break-even backstop logic, the
integrated service model for regional centers, the population density coverage
standard that determines where centers are built, the network adequacy certification
that governs legacy facility subsidy eligibility, the transport and air access
obligations for frontier and remote populations, and the operator model and private
equity exclusion. It does not define clinical standards, operator procurement rules,
or state-level governance structures beyond the federal floor requirements.

---

## Core Design Principle

The federal question is not whether to close a hospital. The federal question is what
population density floor justifies federal infrastructure investment in a fixed
facility. Below that floor, the federal obligation shifts from facility construction
to transport and telehealth access. Above it, a Regional Health Center is built,
backstopped to break-even, and operated under a federal service obligation contract.

Legacy hospitals and facilities in regions where the network adequacy standard is met
lose their federal subsidy eligibility as a consequence of coverage being provided —
not as the result of an administrative closure order. They may continue to operate as
private or state-funded facilities. The federal government does not close them. It
stops subsidizing redundant infrastructure.

This distinction is not semantic. It means the political and legal weight of closing
a hospital never falls on the federal government. Network adequacy certification —
produced independently by DoDA — determines subsidy eligibility. HETA acts on that
certification. Neither institution orders closures.

## The Federal Backstop Model

Regional Health Centers are funded to break-even, not to profit. The backstop covers
capital buildout and facility costs through PCA instruments, operating costs to the
break-even floor defined as the cost of delivering the mandated service suite to the
covered population at regulated rates, and readiness costs including staffing,
equipment, and surge capacity maintained independently of utilization volume —
consistent with the SEC utility framework's separation of readiness funding from
per-episode reimbursement.

The backstop does not cover revenue shortfalls arising from operational inefficiency
above defined benchmarks, services beyond the mandated suite unless separately
authorized, expansion beyond the certified coverage area without a new coverage
determination, or private direct-to-consumer practices operating in surrounding
communities. Those practices compete in the market without federal backstop once the
regional center is operational.

The backstop is explicit and bounded. DoDA produces and maintains the
cost-per-covered-life models by density tier and region that define what break-even
means for each center. These models are publicly published, methodologically
transparent, and updated on a defined cadence. The federal government knows exactly
what it is funding.

## Operator Model

Regional Health Centers are federally backstopped but contractor-operated. The
federal government sets the service obligation standard, provides capital through PCA
instruments, backstops operating costs to the break-even floor, certifies network
adequacy through DoDA, and enforces service obligations through HETA. The operator
delivers the mandated service suite, employs or contracts clinical staff, manages
facility operations, meets defined access, quality, and reporting standards, and
accepts regulated payment rates through the unified payment rail.

Eligible operator structures include state-operated facilities, nonprofit health
systems and community health organizations, Federally Qualified Health Centers and
Look-Alike organizations, cooperative and community-owned health entities, tribal
health organizations under tribal governance agreements, and public hospital
districts.

Private equity-owned or PE-backed entities and for-profit corporations with external
shareholder distribution obligations are ineligible. The PE exclusion is enforced
through the service obligation contract. Operators must certify annual compliance
with a non-distribution reinvestment requirement — net revenues above defined
operating reserves must be reinvested in facility operations, workforce, or capital
maintenance. Structures that cannot satisfy this requirement without material
restructuring are ineligible.

This mirrors the Oregon Coordinated Care Organization model prior to private equity
consolidation — regionally accountable, structurally flexible on nonprofit versus
public versus cooperative ownership, but closed to extractive financial structures.
States retain authority to determine which eligible operator structures are permitted
within their jurisdiction above the federal floor.

## Integrated Service Model

Each Regional Health Center provides a full-suite integrated service model under one
roof or on one campus.

The mandatory service suite includes primary care offering continuous panel-based and
episodic access; urgent care with walk-in episodic service and extended hours; dental
care covering preventive, restorative, and oral surgery referral; vision care
including examinations, prescriptions, and basic dispensing; behavioral and mental
health offering outpatient therapy, psychiatric medication management, and crisis
triage; pharmacy dispensing, counseling, and medication management; basic diagnostics
including labs, imaging, and point-of-care testing; and care coordination providing
navigation support for SEC-covered referrals, specialist access, and hospital
transfer.

Each Regional Health Center includes a stabilization bay capable of managing acute
presentations pending transfer, a helicopter landing pad meeting FAA and air medical
transport standards, formal integration into the regional emergency transfer network
under the Emergency and Trauma Services Regulated Utility framework, and defined
transfer protocols to regional trauma centers and specialty hospitals. The center is
not a trauma center. It is the first stabilization and transfer node for its covered
population — its emergency role is to stabilize, assess, and route, not to provide
definitive trauma or complex surgical care.

Operators may offer services beyond the mandatory suite subject to state
authorization, DoDA utilization review confirming demand in the coverage area, and no
incremental federal backstop for optional services unless separately authorized by
Congress. Optional expansions may include specialist clinics on scheduled satellite
days, telehealth hub infrastructure, rehabilitation services, and maternal health
services in areas with documented gaps.

## Population Density Coverage Standard

The coverage standard determines where the federal government will fund a fixed
facility and where the obligation shifts to transport and telehealth. Coverage tiers
are defined by population density and drive-time modeling. DoDA maintains the
underlying models and produces binding coverage determinations for each geographic
area.

In Tier 1 — urban and suburban areas — private markets are assumed functional.
Regional Health Centers are not federally funded. The full market framework applies.
FQHCs and community health centers serving Medicaid and low-income populations in
Tier 1 areas continue under existing federal funding streams, rationalized into the
broader framework over time.

In Tier 2 — rural areas — federal backstop is available. A Regional Health Center
is funded where a defined minimum population floor is present within the catchment
area, no adequate private market coverage exists within defined drive-time standards,
and DoDA confirms a network gap. The policy target is 85% of the catchment population
within 60 minutes of a Regional Health Center, subject to DoDA modeling. Private
direct-to-consumer practices are welcome and encouraged in surrounding communities —
they operate in the market without federal backstop. Their presence does not
disqualify a Regional Health Center from federal funding. They are additive capacity,
not replacement capacity for the federal obligation.

In Tier 3 — frontier areas — fixed facility funding is available only where the
population density floor is met within a defined catchment radius. Where it is not
met, the federal obligation shifts to transport and telehealth access. Air medical
transport coverage is funded as infrastructure, not as an episodic benefit. Mobile
health units on defined schedules serve communities below the fixed facility
threshold. Telehealth infrastructure is funded at the community level rather than
the individual level. The helicopter landing pad requirement applies inversely here —
frontier communities below the fixed facility threshold are prioritized for air
transport infrastructure precisely because fixed facilities are not viable.

In Tier 4 — remote and isolated areas — the framework acknowledges explicitly that
60-minute drive-time access to a fixed facility is not achievable. The federal
obligation is air medical transport with defined response time standards, telehealth
access infrastructure, periodic mobile health unit visits on a defined schedule, and
community health worker programs for chronic disease management and care
coordination. Tier 4 is not a lesser tier. It is an honest acknowledgment that the
right infrastructure for genuinely remote populations is different from the right
infrastructure for rural populations, and that funding levels should reflect the
actual cost of delivering access through these mechanisms.

## Network Adequacy Certification

DoDA produces and maintains Network Adequacy Certifications for each geographic
coverage area. A certification confirms that the Regional Health Center serving the
area is operational and meeting service obligation standards, that transport access
obligations are being met for Tier 3 and 4 populations, and that the covered
population has functional access to the mandatory service suite within the applicable
drive-time or transport-time standard.

When DoDA issues a Network Adequacy Certification for a coverage area, legacy
hospitals and facilities in that area lose eligibility for federal operating subsidies
tied to access obligations. Legacy facilities may continue to operate under private,
nonprofit, or state funding. They remain eligible for SEC reimbursement at regulated
rates for services they continue to provide. HETA notifies affected facilities of
subsidy sunset timelines following certification, with a defined wind-down period.

The certification process includes public notice and a defined comment period, state
regulatory review and concurrence, a minimum 24-month subsidy wind-down period
following certification, and automatic suspension of certification if the Regional
Health Center fails to meet service obligations during the wind-down period. HETA
does not issue certifications. HETA acts on certifications issued by DoDA. This
separation ensures that the executing agency cannot certify its own network adequacy.

## Transport Obligations

Transport access is a prerequisite for coverage standard satisfaction, not an
afterthought. A Network Adequacy Certification cannot be issued for a coverage area
where transport access is not operational. The drive-time standard assumes patients
can actually get there.

Each Regional Health Center operates or contracts a Regional Health Transport
function covering its catchment area. This includes scheduled transport on defined
routes for non-emergency appointments, on-demand transport for urgent but
non-emergency needs within defined response windows, accessible vehicles meeting ADA
standards, and integration with the unified patient app for scheduling and tracking.
Transport is funded as part of the center's operating cost and is included in the
break-even backstop calculation. It is not a separate benefit that patients must
apply for.

For Tier 3 and Tier 4 populations, air medical transport coverage is funded as
regional infrastructure. Helicopter landing pads at Regional Health Centers are the
primary receiving points. Response time standards are defined by DoDA modeling based
on geography and population distribution. Air transport costs for SEC-covered events
are covered by SEC at regulated transport rates.

## PCA Capital Instruments

The Public Capital Authority provides the capital infrastructure for Regional Health
Center buildout through direct facility capital for construction, renovation, and
equipment; equipment instruments for imaging, diagnostic, dental, and pharmacy
equipment with utilization-linked repayment terms; technology buildout covering SSI
and UPI integration, telehealth infrastructure, and electronic health record systems
meeting framework interoperability standards; and helipad construction funded as
emergency infrastructure, not as a facility amenity.

Operators contribute matching capital where financially capable. In frontier and
remote areas, full federal capital provision is available where operator matching is
not feasible. PCA instruments for Regional Health Centers are not loans in the
conventional sense — they are infrastructure investments in public-purpose facilities.
Repayment terms reflect utilization and revenue, not market interest rates. Where a
center operates at break-even or below due to low-density population, the PCA
instrument converts to a grant upon DoDA certification of continued network necessity.

## Integration with the Broader Framework

Regional Health Centers participate in the market layer for shoppable services —
primary care, dental, vision, mental health — under the same transparency rules as
private providers. They post prices. Patients see their price through the unified app.
Subsidy credits apply automatically through the payment rail. The backstop does not
exempt centers from market transparency requirements. It funds the cost floor. It does
not fund opacity.

Regional Health Centers are integrated into the SEC-covered emergency and trauma
services framework as stabilization and transfer nodes. Their emergency interface
functions — stabilization bay, helipad, transfer protocols — are funded through
readiness payments under the SEC utility-rate framework, not through per-episode
reimbursement.

During transition phases, HETA Provider Transition Corps field teams support provider
recruitment and onboarding to centers, infrastructure connection to the payment rail
and provider dashboard, transition instrument delivery for providers relocating to or
joining regional centers, and community education about available services and
transport access.

DoDA maintains the population density and drive-time models underlying coverage tier
assignments, cost-per-covered-life benchmarks by density tier and region, Network
Adequacy Certifications and their renewal, utilization and outcomes dashboards for
all Regional Health Centers, and performance benchmarks against which operator
service obligations are assessed. HETA acts on DoDA certifications and benchmarks.
It does not produce them.

## What This Framework Is Not

This framework does not nationalize healthcare delivery — operators are independent
contractors under service obligation contracts. It does not close hospitals — it
removes subsidy eligibility from redundant infrastructure once adequate coverage
exists. It does not crowd out private markets — direct-to-consumer practices in
surrounding communities are welcome and unsubsidized. It does not create a federal
hospital system — the federal role is funding and standard-setting, not operations.
And it does not guarantee coverage everywhere regardless of population — the density
floor is explicit and honest about what is achievable with fixed facilities.

---

## Bottom Line

Rural and low-income access does not fail because of malice. It fails because the
market does not produce infrastructure where density is too low to sustain it, and
because the transition away from legacy systems creates gaps before replacement
capacity forms.

The Regional Health Center Network closes that gap by treating access infrastructure
the same way the country treats roads, post offices, and fire stations — as a public
obligation funded to a defined standard, operated by accountable contractors, and
measured independently by an institution that cannot cook the numbers.

The federal government funds access. States and operators deliver it. DoDA measures
it. HETA enforces it. Private markets fill in around it. That division of labor is
what makes the system coherent rather than collapsed into either a nationalized
delivery system or an access desert.

## Purpose and Scope

This brief defines emergency and trauma services as non-shoppable care covered
through Severe Event Coverage. The designation exists because of a single,
unavoidable fact: emergency patients cannot shop. They cannot delay care, compare
prices, or meaningfully consent to a complex financial transaction during a medical
crisis. That removes the preconditions for a functioning market and creates a monopoly
condition at the worst possible moment for the patient.

SEC is the payer of record for emergency and trauma care. Pricing discipline,
cost transparency, and access obligations are enforced through the utility-rate
governance that applies across the SEC layer — the same anti-monopoly and anti-gaming
controls that govern all SEC-covered non-shoppable care. Emergency care is not
governed by a separate regulatory tier. It is governed by the same framework that
governs every other service where patients cannot shop, because the same structural
problem — monopoly leverage at the point of need — is present across that category.

Private insurance may provide supplemental coverage above the SEC floor, but
emergency and trauma providers are reimbursed through the SEC utility-rate framework
rather than opaque negotiated charges. This brief governs emergency and trauma
services specifically. It does not regulate primary care, urgent care, scheduled
surgery, or insurance benefit design except to define how those layers interface with
emergency care.

---

## Why Emergency Care Cannot Function as a Market

When you are unconscious, in an ambulance, or in acute distress, you are not a
consumer. You cannot get a second opinion, walk to a competitor, or negotiate terms.
The provider knows this. In an unregulated environment, that asymmetry produces
extreme markups that have nothing to do with your doctor's decisions or with the
underlying cost of care.

Insurance coverage without price discipline masks this rather than fixing it. The
insurer absorbs the markup, passes it through premiums, and the patient never sees
the real number. The SEC utility-rate framework does what insurance cannot — it
constrains pricing behavior directly, at the source, where the monopoly condition
actually exists. This is the same logic that governs electricity, water, and fire
departments. Essential services that cannot be refused at the moment of need, and
where market competition cannot discipline pricing, are governed as public utilities.
Emergency and trauma care meets that definition.

---

## What SEC Utility-Rate Governance Means — and What It Doesn't

SEC utility-rate governance means mandatory cost and price transparency, regulated
payment frameworks, stabilization and access obligations, and oversight of margins
and markups. It does not mean free care at point of service, elimination of private
or public insurance payment, or nationalization of hospitals or emergency providers.

Emergency care remains covered through the SEC floor, with private insurance allowed
to supplement above it. The reform changes the information environment around
emergency care, not the ownership structure. Hospitals stay private. SEC remains the
baseline payer of record. What changes is that emergency pricing is no longer a black
box that neither the patient nor the payer can see into.

---

## Scope of SEC-Covered Emergency Services

SEC coverage and utility-rate governance apply to emergency departments, trauma
centers at all levels, and specialized emergency units covering stroke, cardiac, burn,
and neonatal presentations. Disaster response and surge capacity tied to emergency
readiness are included as well.

Designation is based on time-criticality and non-shoppability — not ownership
structure. A private hospital's emergency department is governed the same way a
public one is. What triggers the designation is the clinical condition of the patient,
not who owns the building.

---

## SEC as the Payer of Record

Severe Event Coverage is the baseline payer layer for emergency and trauma care.
Private insurance may provide supplemental coverage above the SEC floor, but emergency
providers are reimbursed through the SEC utility-rate framework rather than opaque
negotiated charges.

The structural benefit flows back to the insurance layer as well. Because routine care
is removed from insurance costs under this reform, emergency claim frequency drops.
Actuarial modeling becomes tractable again. Premiums come down. Insurance returns to
functioning the way it was designed to — people contribute into a shared pool, and
payouts are limited to the low-frequency, genuinely unpredictable events that
individual financial planning cannot absorb. Emergency care is exactly that kind of
event. It belongs in this layer.

---

## Cost Transparency Requirements

Emergency providers publish standardized, machine-readable cost disclosures covering
labor, facility and capital costs, acquisition costs for devices, drugs, and supplies,
overhead allocation methodology, and readiness and standby costs. Disclosures conform
to the national cost-accounting schema common to all SEC providers, so that costs are
comparable across facilities, regions, and provider types. A benchmark is enforceable
only when the underlying cost structure is visible.

---

## Regulated Rate Framework

Emergency services are reimbursed within the SEC utility-rate bands benchmarked
against comparable providers and constrained by efficiency standards so that cost
disclosure does not become a cost-plus entitlement. Rate bands reflect regional wage
indices and acuity mix, include explicit upper and lower bounds, and are adjusted
prospectively rather than retroactively.

SEC coverage does not entitle emergency providers to unlimited reimbursement. It
entitles them to transparent, audited, fair compensation for the services they
actually delivered at costs they have actually disclosed. The rate framework for
emergency and trauma services reflects the non-shoppable, time-critical nature of
the care — but the discipline mechanism is the same one that applies across the
SEC layer.

---

## Readiness and Surge Funding

Emergency readiness is funded explicitly and separately from per-episode
reimbursement. Readiness funding is tied to trauma level, staffing, equipment, and
surge obligations, and is reviewed independently of utilization volume.

This separation matters because the current system buries readiness costs inside
inflated per-episode charges, making it impossible to evaluate whether a high bill
reflects genuine care cost or amortized overhead. Separating the two makes both
numbers honest and allows readiness obligations to be evaluated on their own terms —
not as a justification for billing whatever the monopoly will bear.

---

## Access and Stabilization Obligations

Every SEC-participating emergency provider must stabilize all patients regardless of
insurance status, meet defined geographic access and response standards, and
participate in regional transfer and escalation networks. These are non-negotiable
conditions of SEC participation.

Failure to meet access obligations triggers corrective oversight. It does not trigger
a pricing adjustment. Access is not a variable that gets traded against revenue. The
obligation to stabilize every patient who arrives is unconditional.

---

## Interface with Market-Based Care

Emergency services are the escalation endpoint for urgent care, primary care, and
scheduled procedures when complications arise. When clinical thresholds are met,
SEC pricing and access rules supersede market rules. Market-based providers cannot
reclassify care retroactively as emergency care to capture higher reimbursement.
That boundary is enforced because it is the boundary that keeps both layers
financially coherent.

The market layer and the SEC layer depend on each other staying clean. If
market-based providers can reclassify upward without consequence, SEC coverage becomes
a billing channel rather than a clinical one. If the SEC layer bleeds into routine
care, it becomes unmanageable. Both break if the line between them is not maintained.

---

## Oversight and Enforcement

Oversight, audits, anti-gaming enforcement, and referral capture controls are governed
under the HETA framework and apply uniformly across emergency and non-emergency
services within the SEC layer. This brief defines what emergency and trauma services
are and how they are governed within that layer. How enforcement is executed across
SEC is defined in the SEC Administration brief.

In steady state, HETA maintains the boundary between the market layer and the SEC
layer, enforces utility-rate pricing discipline across all SEC-covered non-shoppable
care, and prevents reclassification games that would undermine the actuarial integrity
of the SEC risk pool. These functions apply to emergency care specifically because
they apply to the SEC layer as a whole.

---

## Transition and Implementation

SEC utility-rate governance for emergency and trauma services rolls out through
geographically bounded pilots with automatic access backstops. Emergency access
continuity is the non-negotiable constraint throughout the transition — no phase
proceeds if access is threatened. Temporary stabilization mechanisms are available
if needed. Rate frameworks are adjusted prospectively based on observed data, not
locked in at the start and defended regardless of what the evidence shows.

---

## Bottom Line

Emergency and trauma care are covered through SEC. They are governed by the same
utility-rate discipline, anti-monopoly pricing controls, and anti-gaming enforcement
that apply across the SEC layer — because the structural problem is the same: the
patient cannot shop, and an unregulated provider will price accordingly.

The analogy is electricity, water, and fire departments. Services that cannot be
refused and cannot be disciplined by market competition are governed as public
utilities. SEC is that framework for healthcare. Emergency and trauma care are its
clearest case.

The patient in the ambulance still gets covered. They just stop getting billed as if
the monopoly has no limit.

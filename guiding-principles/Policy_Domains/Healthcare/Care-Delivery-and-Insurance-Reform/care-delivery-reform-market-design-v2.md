## Purpose and Scope

The purpose of this brief is to define how non-emergency healthcare services in the
Futures Project are reorganized into transparent, competitive markets. It governs
primary care, urgent care, routine diagnostics, minor procedures, scheduled surgery,
and complex episodes. It also establishes the boundary between market-based shoppable
care and SEC-covered non-shoppable care.

The framework operates through two layers. Shoppable services — predictable,
schedulable, and comparable — operate in transparent, competitive markets.
Non-shoppable services — where patients cannot delay, compare, or meaningfully
consent in the moment — are covered through Severe Event Coverage and governed by
the SEC utility framework. Emergency and trauma care falls squarely in the
non-shoppable category. SEC is the payer of record for that care. The SEC utility
framework — transparent cost disclosure, regulated rate bands, anti-monopoly controls,
anti-gaming enforcement — applies across the full SEC layer, not only to emergency
presentations.

This brief does not define the full insurance system. It defines where insurance is
allowed to operate and where it is excluded. This is intentional to prevent insurance
in routine care, which consistently distorts price discovery, increases administrative
overhead, and weakens market competition. Those are not side-effects, but rather the
predictable outcome of routing transactions through insurance that were never suited
to it.

The objective of this reform is to remove these distortive effects by restoring price
discovery, consumer choice, and provider competition in the parts of healthcare where
shopping, scheduling, and informed consent are possible. Those conditions exist for
most of the care most people receive. The current system treats them as if they don't.

## Core Design Principle

The system separates two payment functions based on the clinical and economic nature
of the service.

If a service is predictable, schedulable, and comparable, it operates through a
transparent market, with Medicaid gradient support available by income and condition.
If an event is non-shoppable — whether because it is immediately life-threatening,
clinically urgent, or uncertain and high-cost enough that individual budgeting cannot
absorb it — it is covered through the Severe Event Coverage floor and governed by the
SEC utility framework. Emergency and trauma care is the clearest case in the
non-shoppable category: patients cannot shop, delay, or consent in the moment of
crisis. The same structural condition — monopoly leverage at the point of need —
is present across the SEC layer, which is why the same utility-rate discipline
applies throughout.

Markets are enforced, not assumed. Transparency, direct pricing, anti-gaming rules,
and payment-boundary enforcement are mandatory conditions of participation.

## Market Layer Definition

The market layer includes healthcare services where patients can reasonably compare
options before care is delivered. A service belongs in the market layer when it is
predictable enough to price in advance, schedulable or deferrable without serious
clinical harm, comparable across providers, suitable for posted and binding prices,
and capable of being delivered through direct payment, subscription, bundled pricing,
or other transparent payment models.

The market layer includes primary care, urgent care for stable non-emergency
conditions, routine diagnostics, minor procedures, minor or cosmetic scheduled
surgery, and other planned complex episodes where patients can compare providers in
advance. The market layer does not include emergency care, trauma care, or crisis
stabilization services. Those are non-shoppable by nature and governed accordingly
through the SEC layer.

## Primary Care

Primary care is the front door to the healthcare system. It operates as a
direct-to-consumer market.

Providers set their own prices and terms. Subscription-based models are allowed where
ongoing relationships add clinical value — a patient managing a complex chronic
condition benefits from a doctor who knows them over time. Per-visit pricing is
allowed where episodic care is appropriate. Both models are permitted because the
care relationship varies by patient need. What does not vary is the transparency
requirement. All primary care prices are binding, machine-readable, and posted on a
central medical platform.

Subscription offerings must disclose the scope of included services, what is
excluded, access standards, response-time standards, panel size limits, and renewal
and cancellation terms. Ancillary services — labs and imaging — must be priced
transparently when they are ordered, bundled, or referred. Patients cannot be
surprised by costs they had no way to anticipate before they agreed to care.

Insurance billing, prior authorization, and mandatory membership requirements for
episodic access are all prohibited in the primary care market layer. These mechanisms
are how insurance currently re-enters routine care, suppresses price signals, and
recreates administrative cost without corresponding clinical value. Removing them
is the point of this reform.

Anti-consolidation thresholds apply during the transition. Providers may not use the
reform period to build monopolistic market power before competitive norms are
established. Vertically integrated systems may continue to operate if they remain
below certified market-share thresholds within a geographic market and comply with
the same market-layer rules as independent providers. Network lock-in is prohibited.
Referral relationships must be clinically justified, transparent, and auditable.
Utility transparency applies by default, and providers cannot bundle primary care with
downstream facility use or steer patients toward co-owned facilities as a condition
of access.

## Urgent Care

Urgent care handles time-sensitive but stable conditions — the presentation that needs
attention today but is not an emergency. Its function in the system is to provide
fast, affordable episodic care and to prevent the inappropriate and expensive use of
emergency services.

Urgent care operates on pay-per-episode pricing only. Subscriptions are prohibited
because urgent care is episodic by definition. Standardized, all-in bundles are
required for common urgent care presentations. Bundles must include the ordinary
services needed to resolve or stabilize the presentation — basic evaluation, common
imaging, basic procedures, and discharge or follow-up instructions. The price is
visible before treatment whenever feasible. Post-hoc facility fees, unbundled
add-ons, and reclassification games are prohibited. The price the patient sees is the
price they pay, except where genuine clinical escalation changes the category of care.

The escalation boundary with SEC-covered care is explicit and enforced. When defined
clinical thresholds are met, escalation to emergency services is mandatory. Providers
who escalate appropriately receive legal safe harbor. Providers who bill urgent care
encounters as emergency events without meeting the clinical threshold face enforcement
consequences. That boundary protects patients and preserves the actuarial integrity
of the SEC risk pool. It is not optional.

## Routine Diagnostics and Minor Procedures

Routine diagnostics and minor procedures operate in the market layer when they are
predictable, schedulable, and comparable. This includes routine labs, common imaging,
screening services, and minor procedures that can be priced in advance.

Prices are posted, binding, and machine-readable. Providers must disclose whether
prices include interpretation, facility charges, professional fees, supplies, and
follow-up reporting. Unbundling is prohibited when the components are ordinarily
required to complete the service. Patients must be able to compare complete prices
across providers — not component-level prices that cannot be meaningfully compared
until the final bill arrives.

Insurance billing is excluded from this layer. Public supports, employer
contributions, health transition accounts, direct subsidies, and other affordability
mechanisms may support patients in the market layer. The key rule is that support
attaches to the patient or the transaction without recreating opaque provider-insurer
reimbursement.

---

## Scheduled Surgery and Complex Episodes

Scheduled surgery and complex episodes sit at the high end of the market layer.
These are services that can be priced in advance and where patients can compare
providers — but where cost and complexity are high enough that SEC coverage is
appropriate for the bundled episode.

All-in bundled episode prices are mandatory. The bundle includes facility fees,
professional services, anesthesia, standard post-operative care, and defined
complication windows. Patients compare complete prices across providers before
choosing. Surprise costs after a procedure are prohibited. The patient, their insurer,
or SEC — whichever covers the episode — pays the bundled price. Nothing else.

Bundled-episode pricing for scheduled procedures applies the same pricing discipline
logic as the SEC utility framework in the emergency context: the price is known,
transparent, and bounded before care is delivered. Competition among providers
operates within that structure.

---

## Insurance in the Market Layer

Insurance is excluded from primary care and urgent care. It is permitted for
scheduled complex procedures and other high-cost episodes in the upper market layer,
where SEC provides the baseline coverage floor.

Allowing insurance back into routine care recreates the distortion this reform is
designed to eliminate. Keeping it confined to high-cost, schedulable episodes — where
it complements the SEC floor rather than substituting for market pricing — is what
keeps both layers clean.

Provider prices remain transparent where care is schedulable. Insurance coverage does
not eliminate the requirement for posted, bundled, comparable prices. Transparency
is not an either-or with insurance — it is a requirement that applies everywhere care
is shoppable, regardless of who ultimately pays.

---

## Competition and Anti-Gaming Rules

Functioning markets require active protection. Left undefended, they develop the same
distortions the current system relies on.

Facility-fee unbundling is prohibited. Post-hoc add-on charges for ordinary bundled
services are prohibited. Ownership-based referral steering is prohibited. Routine or
urgent care cannot be reclassified as SEC-covered care without a genuine clinical
basis. Price discrimination based on payer type is prohibited within market layers.
Hidden insurer-provider contract pricing for market-layer services is prohibited.
Mandatory network restrictions for routine market-layer care are prohibited. Bundling
primary care access with downstream facility use is prohibited. Mandatory membership
requirements for episodic primary care access are prohibited.

Outcome reporting must be risk-adjusted and standardized. Quality comparisons must be
auditable and resistant to selection effects. Violations trigger escalating penalties,
including fines, restitution, loss of certification, exclusion from market-layer
participation, or exclusion from regulated insurance markets. The enforcement
mechanism is not advisory.

## Interface with SEC-Covered Non-Shoppable Care

When clinical thresholds are met, market-based care defers to SEC-covered services.
Emergency care cannot function as a consumer market. Patients cannot shop for
emergency care, delay care to compare prices, or meaningfully consent to a complex
financial transaction while injured, unconscious, septic, or in acute distress.

The market layer and the SEC layer depend on each other staying clean. If routine
care creeps back through gaming or reclassification, SEC becomes bloated again. If
SEC-covered care is forced to behave like a market, patients face impossible choices
at the moment they have the least agency. The boundary between them is explicit
because the economics of both break without it.

---

## Access and Affordability Interface

Market pricing does not mean every patient pays the full cost alone. Public supports,
employer contributions, health transition accounts, direct subsidies, and other
affordability mechanisms may support patients in the market layer. The key rule is
that support attaches to the patient or the transaction without recreating opaque
provider-insurer reimbursement.

A subsidy may help a patient pay for primary care. It may not allow primary care to
return to hidden insurance billing. An employer contribution may support routine care
access. It may not require a closed network that prevents price comparison. A public
benefit may reduce patient exposure. It may not eliminate posted, binding,
machine-readable provider prices.

The affordability layer must preserve price transparency. Otherwise it becomes a
backdoor for rebuilding the current system — and the current system did not fail
because of a lack of spending. It failed because of a lack of honesty about what
things cost and who was paying for them.

## Transition and Enforcement

HETA oversees market designation, certification, price transparency enforcement,
anti-gaming audits, pilot evaluation, and scaling. Rollout occurs through
geographically bounded pilots with automatic safeguards to protect access and
competition during the transition.

The transition is sequenced to build market capacity before enforcement tightens.
Providers are not required to operate in a new system before the infrastructure to
support it exists. Transition sequencing covers market-layer service classification,
provider price-posting infrastructure, machine-readable pricing standards, bundled
episode definitions, SEC escalation thresholds, Severe Event Coverage interfaces,
anti-gaming audit systems, access monitoring, pilot evaluation, and expansion,
revision, or termination based on measured performance.

HETA certifies local markets only when minimum conditions are met — sufficient
provider availability, transparent price infrastructure, access protections, and
enforcement capacity. Markets that are not ready do not get certified. Markets that
stop meeting the conditions get reviewed.

---

## Evaluation Metrics

Pilot evaluation measures whether the reform improves affordability, access,
competition, and system legibility. Core metrics include primary care price
dispersion, urgent care episode prices, routine diagnostic prices, scheduled surgery
bundled prices, and SEC diversion rates from appropriate primary and urgent care.
Patient out-of-pocket exposure, premium changes for Severe Event Coverage and
supplemental plans, administrative cost share, provider billing overhead, claim
denial frequency, prior authorization volume, and surprise billing incidence are all
tracked.

Provider market concentration, patient wait times, rural and underserved-area access,
complication rates, risk-adjusted outcomes, patient satisfaction, and plan competition
above the Severe Event Coverage floor round out the evaluation picture.

Evaluation must distinguish true cost reduction from cost shifting. A pilot is not
successful if premiums fall but patient exposure rises, access deteriorates, or
SEC-covered services absorb avoidable care. The metrics are designed to catch that
substitution before it gets reported as a win.

## Failure Modes and Safeguards

The reform monitors and responds to predictable failure modes. Providers may use
transparency rules while increasing market power. Insurers may attempt to re-enter
routine care through managed networks. Urgent care clinics may reclassify routine
encounters as SEC-covered events. Hospitals may unbundle scheduled procedures through
facility-fee games. Affordability supports may become hidden reimbursement channels.
Rural or underserved markets may fail to attract sufficient providers. Chronic-care
patients may face unstable direct-care costs. Patients may delay needed care because
the market layer is under-supported. Private plans may compete through risk selection
rather than service quality.

Safeguards include anti-consolidation thresholds, mandatory posted prices, standardized
bundles, SEC escalation rules, risk-adjusted outcome reporting, subsidy designs that
preserve price visibility, Severe Event Coverage floors, reinsurance and complication
pools, market certification gates, automatic review triggers, and pilot termination
or redesign authority.

The reform scales only where the market layer is functioning, access is stable, and
the boundary with the SEC layer is being maintained.

---

## Bottom Line

Care Delivery makes healthcare behave like a system designed on purpose. Routine care
becomes transparent, comparable, and market-priced. Urgent care becomes episodic,
bundled, and protected from upcoding games. Scheduled surgery becomes competitively
priced and SEC-eligible. Insurance stops functioning as a payment wrapper for every
ordinary healthcare transaction and returns to its proper role — pooling large,
uncertain risk through a utility-governed floor. Non-shoppable care is governed
through the SEC framework because crisis medicine cannot function as a consumer market.

HETA's role is to enforce the market boundary so that routine care does not collapse
back into opaque reimbursement, administrative bargaining, or discretionary price
control.

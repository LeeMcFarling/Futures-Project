# **Overview**

This reform reorganizes healthcare around a simple distinction: some parts of healthcare behave like normal markets, and some fundamentally do not. Where markets work, they are allowed to operate transparently and directly. Where markets fail—because patients cannot shop, delay, or meaningfully consent—those services are treated as utilities.

Healthcare is divided into four functional layers, each governed according to its economic reality:
	1. Primary Care — Direct-to-consumer market (subscription or per-visit where continuity adds value)
	2. Urgent Care — Episodic, pay-per-episode market with clear triage boundaries
	3. Scheduled Surgery & Complex Episodes — Covered by catastrophic insurance with regulated, bundled pricing
	4. Emergency & Trauma Care — Treated as essential public utility infrastructure

Markets are used where they work. Utilities are used where they do not. Insurance is confined to catastrophic risk rather than daily friction. Public spending shifts from subsidizing opacity to purchasing reliability.

This separation is visible from day one and implemented through phased pilots, geographic containment, and a standing execution authority responsible for protecting patient access while prices fall.

# Summary

This policy brief proposes a phased transition to treat healthcare as essential infrastructure by separating care delivery according to market reality. Routine and urgent care move to transparent, direct-to-consumer markets. Scheduled and emergency surgeries are covered by catastrophic insurance, with emergency care treated like as a public utility (like a fire department - everyone has access). Pharmaceutical pricing and innovation are restructured separately to preserve breakthroughs while eliminating current rent extraction practices. Competition shifts from billing tactics to measured, risk-adjusted health outcomes, with strict anti-gaming enforcement and explicit civilian-protection guarantees during transition.

# Problem

Our healthcare system behaves like a fragmented toll system: prices are opaque, intermediate actors extract spread via contracts and rebates, and the public sector pays a large share of the bill without receiving commensurate control, or accountability. The result is persistently high costs, uneven access, and incentives that prioritize revenue optimization over health outcomes.

# Policy Objective

Out policy objective is to make healthcare legible, governable, and outcomes-driven by aligning payment and regulation with economic reality. Reduce prices rapidly for routine care, protect universal access to emergency services, and restore insurance to its proper role as catastrophic risk pooling. Convert public spending from opaque subsidies into durable public value while preserving pluralism in care delivery and pharmaceutical innovation.

# Theory of Change

First, we visibly separate healthcare into market and non-market functions so incentives align with human behavior under stress. Second, force end-to-end transparency in unified websites, databases, and systems so prices and spreads cannot hide. Third, allow markets to compete where they work while imposing utility-style obligations where they do not. Fourth, simplify financing by confining insurance to catastrophic events and funding emergency readiness directly. Finally, execute the transition through pilots, real-time monitoring, and a standing authority empowered to stabilize access and correct failures quickly.

# Changes in Care Delivery

#### **Primary Care — Direct-to-Consumer Market**

Primary care becomes a direct-to-consumer service. Providers may offer subscriptions where longitudinal continuity adds value or per-visit pricing where it does not. Prices must be posted, binding, and comparable. Insurance billing and prior authorization are prohibited. Government and employers may subsidize patients directly via capped credits applied at enrollment.

#### **Urgent Care — Episodic Market**

Urgent care operates on standardized, pay-per-episode bundles with extended hours and walk-in access. Subscriptions are prohibited. Clear legal duty to triage applies; emergency conditions trigger mandatory escalation to EMS or ER under protected protocols.

#### **Scheduled Surgery & Complex Episodes — Catastrophic Coverage + Regulated Competition**

Planned surgeries and complex procedures are covered by catastrophic insurance. Delivery occurs through hospitals and regional surgery centers using all-in bundled pricing. Reference prices cap public contributions. Surgery centers compete openly with hospitals on price and outcomes.

#### **Emergency & Trauma Care — Public Utility**

Emergency care is treated as essential civic infrastructure. Availability and rediness are managed the same way we would manage access to water, power, or fire departments. Transparency into Hospital budgets will take   readiness, capacity, and outcomes into account to evaluate reasonable pricing and access for a community rather than transaction volume alone. 

Our centralized healthchare transition bodies will specifically evaluate and guard against public loss of emergency access due to financial failure, but these providers are not entitled to unlimited reimbursement by virtue of providing emergency care alone.

# **Execution, Transition, and Transparency**

A standing healthcare transition authority manages pilots, stabilization, and rollout. The authority operates with real-time dashboards, civilian-protection guarantees, and public-facing weekly operational briefs detailing progress, setbacks, and corrective actions. Emergency access is monitored and protected at all times; hospitals are stabilized or converted as needed to preserve service continuity.

--

# Bottom Line

Routine care becomes cheap, legible, and competitive while catastrophic care becomes insurable again. Emergency care access additionally gets treated as protected infrastructure. This reform prioritizes execution, transparency, and outcomes over ideology—designed to lower prices fast while protecting patients throughout the transition.

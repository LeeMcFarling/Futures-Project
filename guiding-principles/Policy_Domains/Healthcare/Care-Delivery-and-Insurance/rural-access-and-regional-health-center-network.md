## Purpose and Scope

This brief defines the **Regional Health Center Network** — a system of federally
backstopped, contractor-operated integrated health facilities designed to ensure
baseline healthcare access across the full geographic range of the U.S. population.

The network exists to solve a specific structural failure: the transition to
transparent, competitive healthcare markets will compress prices and reduce
administrative overhead, but it may also contract excess capacity in ways that
leave rural and low-income populations without nearby access before replacement
capacity forms. The Regional Health Center Network closes that gap by design,
before enforcement phases activate.

This document defines:
- The federal funding model and break-even backstop logic
- The integrated service model for regional centers
- The population density coverage standard that determines where centers are built
- The network adequacy certification that governs legacy facility subsidy eligibility
- The transport and air access obligations for frontier and remote populations
- The operator model and private equity exclusion

This document does not define clinical standards, operator procurement rules,
or state-level governance structures beyond federal floor requirements.

## Core Design Principle

**The federal question is not: should we close this hospital?**

**The federal question is: what population density floor justifies federal
infrastructure investment in a fixed facility?**

Below that floor, the federal obligation shifts from facility construction to
transport and telehealth access. Above it, a regional health center is built,
backstopped to break-even, and operated under a federal service obligation
contract.

Legacy hospitals and facilities in regions where the network adequacy standard
is met lose their federal subsidy eligibility as a consequence of coverage being
provided — not as the result of an administrative closure order. They may
continue to operate as private or state-funded facilities. The federal government
does not close them. It stops subsidizing redundant infrastructure.

This distinction is not semantic. It means the political and legal weight of
"closing a hospital" never falls on the federal government. Network adequacy
certification — produced independently by DoDA (forthcoming) — determines
subsidy eligibility. HETA acts on that certification. Neither institution
orders closures.

## The Federal Backstop Model

Regional Health Centers are funded to **break-even**, not to profit.

The backstop covers:
- Capital buildout and facility costs (via PCA instruments)
- Operating costs to the break-even floor, defined as the cost of delivering
  the mandated service suite to the covered population at regulated rates
- Readiness costs — staffing, equipment, and surge capacity maintained
  independently of utilization volume, analogous to the emergency utility
  readiness funding model

The backstop does **not** cover:
- Revenue shortfalls arising from operational inefficiency above defined benchmarks
- Services beyond the mandated suite unless separately authorized
- Expansion beyond the certified coverage area without a new coverage determination
- Private DTC practices operating in surrounding communities — those compete
  in the market without federal backstop once the regional center is operational

**The backstop is explicit and bounded.** DoDA (forthcoming) produces and
maintains the cost-per-covered-life models by density tier and region that
define what break-even means for each center. These models are publicly
published, methodologically transparent, and updated on a defined cadence.
The federal government knows exactly what it is funding.

## Operator Model

Regional Health Centers are **federally backstopped but contractor-operated**.

The federal government:
- Sets the service obligation standard
- Provides capital through PCA instruments
- Backstops operating costs to the break-even floor
- Certifies network adequacy through DoDA
- Enforces service obligations through HETA

The operator:
- Delivers the mandated service suite
- Employs or contracts clinical staff
- Manages facility operations
- Meets defined access, quality, and reporting standards
- Accepts regulated payment rates through the unified payment rail

**Eligible operator structures** include:
- State-operated facilities
- Nonprofit health systems and community health organizations
- Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) and Look-Alike organizations
- Cooperative and community-owned health entities
- Tribal health organizations under tribal governance agreements
- Public hospital districts

**Ineligible operator structures:**
- Private equity-owned or PE-backed entities
- For-profit corporations with external shareholder distribution obligations

The PE exclusion is enforced through the service obligation contract, not through
general corporate law. Operators must certify annual compliance with a
**non-distribution reinvestment requirement**: net revenues above defined
operating reserves must be reinvested in facility operations, workforce, or
capital maintenance. Structures that cannot satisfy this requirement without
material restructuring are ineligible.

This mirrors the Oregon Coordinated Care Organization model prior to private
equity consolidation — regionally accountable, structurally flexible on
nonprofit vs. public vs. cooperative ownership, but closed to extractive
financial structures. The state retains authority to determine which eligible
operator structures are permitted within its jurisdiction above the federal
floor.

## Integrated Service Model

Each Regional Health Center provides a **full-suite integrated service model**
under one roof or on one campus:

### Mandatory Service Suite

- **Primary care** — continuous panel-based and episodic access
- **Urgent care** — walk-in episodic care, extended hours
- **Dental care** — preventive, restorative, and oral surgery referral
- **Vision care** — examinations, prescriptions, basic dispensing
- **Behavioral and mental health** — outpatient therapy, psychiatric
  medication management, crisis triage
- **Pharmacy** — dispensing, counseling, medication management
- **Basic diagnostics** — labs, imaging (X-ray, basic ultrasound), point-of-care testing
- **Care coordination** — navigation support for SEC-covered referrals,
  specialist access, and hospital transfer

### Emergency Interface

Each Regional Health Center includes:
- A stabilization bay capable of managing acute presentations pending transfer
- A **helicopter landing pad** meeting FAA and air medical transport standards
- Formal integration into the regional emergency transfer network under the
  Emergency and Trauma Services Regulated Utility framework
- Defined transfer protocols to regional trauma centers and specialty hospitals

The center is not a trauma center. It is the first stabilization and transfer
node for its covered population. Its emergency role is to stabilize, assess,
and route — not to provide definitive trauma or complex surgical care.

### Optional Service Expansion

Operators may offer services beyond the mandatory suite subject to:
- State authorization
- DoDA utilization review confirming demand in the coverage area
- No incremental federal backstop for optional services unless separately
  authorized by Congress

Optional expansions may include specialist clinics on scheduled satellite days,
telehealth hub infrastructure, rehabilitation services, and maternal health
services in areas with documented gaps.

## Population Density Coverage Standard

The coverage standard determines **where the federal government will fund a
fixed facility** and where the obligation shifts to transport and telehealth.

Coverage tiers are defined by population density and drive-time modeling.
DoDA (forthcoming) maintains the underlying models and produces binding
coverage determinations for each geographic area. The tiers below represent
the policy framework; specific thresholds are set by DoDA modeling and
updated as population and infrastructure data changes.

### Tier 1 — Urban and Suburban

Private markets are assumed functional. Regional Health Centers are not
federally funded in Tier 1 areas. The full market framework — transparent
pricing, SEC coverage, discount wraps, patient-side price display — applies.

FQHCs and community health centers serving Medicaid and low-income populations
in Tier 1 areas continue under existing federal funding streams, which are
rationalized into the broader framework over time.

### Tier 2 — Rural

Federal backstop available. A Regional Health Center is funded where:
- A defined minimum population floor is present within the catchment area
- No adequate private market coverage exists within defined drive-time standards
- DoDA coverage determination confirms network gap

Drive-time standard (policy target, subject to DoDA modeling):
- 85% of the catchment population within 60 minutes of a Regional Health Center

Private DTC practices are welcome and encouraged in surrounding communities.
They operate in the market without federal backstop. Their presence does not
disqualify a Regional Health Center from federal funding — they are additive
capacity, not replacement capacity for the federal obligation.

### Tier 3 — Frontier

Fixed facility funding available only where the population density floor is
met within a defined catchment radius. Where it is not met:

- The federal obligation shifts to **transport and telehealth access**
- Air medical transport coverage is funded as infrastructure, not as an
  episodic benefit
- Mobile health units on defined schedules serve communities below the
  fixed facility threshold
- Telehealth infrastructure is funded at the community level (connectivity,
  devices, facilitated access points) rather than the individual level

The helicopter landing pad requirement applies inversely here: frontier
communities below the fixed facility threshold are prioritized for air
transport infrastructure investment precisely because fixed facilities
are not viable.

### Tier 4 — Remote and Isolated

For genuinely isolated populations — defined by a combination of population
density, road access, and geographic isolation scores maintained by DoDA —
the framework acknowledges explicitly that 60-minute drive-time access to
a fixed facility is not achievable.

The federal obligation in Tier 4 is:
- Air medical transport with defined response time standards
- Telehealth access infrastructure
- Periodic mobile health unit visits on a defined schedule
- Community health worker programs for chronic disease management and
  care coordination

Tier 4 is not a lesser tier — it is an honest acknowledgment that the
right infrastructure for remote populations is different from the right
infrastructure for rural populations. Funding levels reflect the actual
cost of delivering access through these mechanisms, modeled by DoDA.

## Network Adequacy Certification

DoDA (forthcoming) produces and maintains **Network Adequacy Certifications**
for each geographic coverage area.

A certification confirms:
- The Regional Health Center serving the area is operational and meeting
  service obligation standards
- Transport access obligations are being met for Tier 3 and 4 populations
- The covered population has functional access to the mandatory service suite
  within the applicable drive-time or transport-time standard

### Effect on Legacy Facility Subsidy Eligibility

When DoDA issues a Network Adequacy Certification for a coverage area:
- Legacy hospitals and facilities in that area lose eligibility for federal
  operating subsidies tied to access obligations
- Legacy facilities may continue to operate under private, nonprofit, or
  state funding
- Legacy facilities remain eligible for SEC reimbursement at regulated rates
  for services they continue to provide
- HETA notifies affected facilities of subsidy sunset timelines following
  certification, with a defined wind-down period

The certification process includes:
- Public notice and a defined comment period
- State regulatory review and concurrence
- A minimum 24-month subsidy wind-down period following certification
- Automatic suspension of certification if the Regional Health Center
  fails to meet service obligations during the wind-down period

HETA does not issue certifications. HETA acts on certifications issued by
DoDA. This separation ensures that the executing agency cannot certify its
own network adequacy.

## Transport Obligations

Transport access is a **prerequisite for coverage standard satisfaction**,
not an afterthought.

A Network Adequacy Certification cannot be issued for a coverage area where
transport access is not operational. The drive-time standard assumes patients
can actually get there.

### Regional Health Transport

Each Regional Health Center operates or contracts a **Regional Health
Transport** function covering its catchment area:

- Scheduled transport on defined routes for non-emergency appointments
- On-demand transport for urgent but non-emergency needs within defined
  response windows
- Accessible vehicles meeting ADA standards
- Integration with the unified patient app for scheduling and tracking

Transport is funded as part of the center's operating cost and is included
in the break-even backstop calculation. It is not a separate benefit that
patients must apply for.

### Air Medical Transport

For Tier 3 and Tier 4 populations:

- Air medical transport coverage is funded as regional infrastructure
- Helicopter landing pads at Regional Health Centers are the primary
  receiving points
- Response time standards are defined by DoDA modeling based on geography
  and population distribution
- Air transport costs for SEC-covered events are covered by SEC at
  regulated transport rates

## PCA Capital Instruments

The Public Capital Authority provides the capital infrastructure for
Regional Health Center buildout.

Available instruments include:

- **Direct facility capital** — construction, renovation, and equipment
  for new or converted facilities
- **Equipment instruments** — imaging, diagnostic, dental, and pharmacy
  equipment with utilization-linked repayment terms
- **Technology buildout** — SSI and UPI integration, telehealth
  infrastructure, electronic health record systems meeting framework
  interoperability standards
- **Helipad construction** — funded as emergency infrastructure, not
  as facility amenity

Operators contribute matching capital where financially capable. In
frontier and remote areas, full federal capital provision is available
where operator matching is not feasible.

PCA instruments for Regional Health Centers are not loans in the
conventional sense — they are infrastructure investments in public-purpose
facilities. Repayment terms reflect utilization and revenue, not
market interest rates. Where a center operates at break-even or below
due to low-density population, the PCA instrument converts to a grant
upon DoDA certification of continued network necessity.

## Integration with the Broader Framework

### Care Delivery Market Interface

Regional Health Centers participate in the market layer for shoppable
services — primary care, dental, vision, mental health — under the same
transparency rules as private providers. They post prices. Patients see
their "your price" through the unified app. Subsidy credits apply
automatically through the payment rail.

The backstop does not exempt centers from market transparency requirements.
It funds the cost floor. It does not fund opacity.

### Emergency Utility Interface

Regional Health Centers are integrated into the Emergency and Trauma
Services regulated utility framework as stabilization and transfer nodes.
Their emergency-interface functions — stabilization bay, helipad, transfer
protocols — are funded through readiness payments analogous to trauma center
readiness funding, not through per-episode reimbursement.

### HETA Provider Transition Corps

Regional Health Centers are a destination for the HETA Provider Transition
Corps deployment. During transition phases, PTC field teams support:

- Provider recruitment and onboarding to centers
- Infrastructure connection (payment rail, SSI, provider dashboard)
- Transition instrument delivery for providers relocating to or joining
  regional centers
- Community education about available services and transport access

### DoDA Accountability Interface

DoDA (forthcoming) maintains:
- Population density and drive-time models underlying coverage tier assignments
- Cost-per-covered-life benchmarks by density tier and region
- Network Adequacy Certifications and their renewal
- Utilization and outcomes dashboards for all Regional Health Centers
- Performance benchmarks against which operator service obligations are assessed

HETA acts on DoDA certifications and benchmarks. It does not produce them.

## What This Framework Is Not

This framework does not:
- Nationalize healthcare delivery — operators are independent contractors
- Close hospitals — it removes subsidy eligibility from redundant infrastructure
- Crowd out private markets — DTC practices in surrounding communities
  are welcome and unsubsidized
- Create a federal hospital system — the federal role is funding and
  standard-setting, not operations
- Guarantee coverage everywhere regardless of population — the density
  floor is explicit and honest about what is achievable with fixed facilities

## Bottom Line

Rural and low-income access does not fail because of malice. It fails because
the market does not produce infrastructure where density is too low to sustain
it, and because the transition away from legacy systems creates gaps before
replacement capacity forms.

The Regional Health Center Network closes that gap by treating access
infrastructure the same way the country treats roads, post offices, and fire
stations: as a public obligation funded to a defined standard, operated by
accountable contractors, and measured independently by an institution that
cannot cook the numbers.

The federal government funds access. States and operators deliver it.
DoDA measures it. HETA enforces it. Private markets fill in around it.

That division of labor is what makes the system coherent rather than
collapsed into either a nationalized delivery system or an access desert.

## Purpose and Scope

This brief defines emergency and trauma services as **regulated utilities** due to their non-shoppable, time-critical nature. Utility regulation applies to **pricing discipline, cost transparency, and access obligations**, while **catastrophic insurance remains the payer of record**.

This document governs emergency and trauma services only. It does not regulate primary care, urgent care, scheduled surgery, or insurance benefit design, except to define interfaces and boundaries.

---

## Why Emergency Care Cannot Function as a Market

Emergency patients cannot shop, delay care, compare prices, or meaningfully consent. Providers in emergency contexts operate as local monopolies, and unregulated pricing produces extreme markups disconnected from underlying costs.

Insurance coverage without price discipline masks this monopoly power rather than correcting it. Utility regulation constrains pricing behavior where markets cannot.

---

## Utility Designation: Meaning and Limits

Utility designation **does mean**:
- Mandatory cost and price transparency  
- Regulated payment frameworks  
- Stabilization and access obligations  
- Oversight of margins and markups  

Utility designation **does not mean**:
- Free care at point of service  
- Elimination of private or public insurance payment  
- Nationalization of hospitals or emergency providers  

Emergency care remains insured. We are just changing the market so that it is no longer opaque. 

---

## Scope of Utility-Regulated Emergency Services

Utility regulation applies to:
- Emergency departments  
- Trauma centers (all levels)  
- Stroke, cardiac, burn, and neonatal emergency units  
- Disaster response and surge capacity tied to emergency readiness  

Designation is based on **time-criticality and non-shoppability**, not ownership structure.

---

## Insurance as the Payer of Record

Emergency and trauma services are covered by catastrophic insurance, including public and private plans.

Insurance:
- Pools low-frequency, high-cost risk  
- Pays regulated emergency rates  
- Does not adjudicate prices beyond defined rate frameworks  

Because routine care is removed from insurance costs, emergency claim frequency drops, restoring actuarial tractability and lowering premiums. (In short, insurance returns to working the way insurance is intended. People pay into a system, and insurance pay-outs are limited to low-frequency risk-related events.)

---

## Cost Transparency Requirements

Emergency providers must publish standardized, machine-readable disclosures covering:
- Labor costs  
- Facility and capital costs  
- Devices, drugs, and supplies acquisition costs  
- Overhead allocation methodology  
- Readiness and standby costs  

Disclosures must conform to a national cost-accounting schema to ensure comparability across regions and providers.

---

## Regulated Rate Framework

Emergency services are reimbursed within **regulated rate bands** tied to audited cost inputs.

Rate bands:
- Reflect regional wage indices and acuity mix  
- Include explicit upper and lower bounds  
- Are adjusted prospectively, not retroactively  

Emergency designation does not entitle providers to unlimited reimbursement.

---

## Readiness and Surge Funding

Readiness obligations are funded **explicitly and separately** from per-episode reimbursement.

Readiness funding:
- Is tied to trauma level, staffing, equipment, and surge obligations  
- Prevents burial of readiness costs inside inflated per-episode charges  
- Is reviewed independently of utilization volume  

---

## Access and Stabilization Obligations

Utility-regulated emergency providers must:
- Stabilize all patients regardless of insurance status  
- Meet defined geographic access and response standards  
- Participate in regional transfer and escalation networks  

Failure to meet access obligations triggers corrective oversight, not pricing adjustments.

---

## Interface with Market-Based Care

Emergency services serve as the escalation endpoint for:
- Urgent care  
- Primary care  
- Scheduled procedures when complications arise  

Emergency pricing and access rules supersede market rules at the point of escalation. Market-based providers are not permitted to reclassify care retroactively as emergency care.

---

## Oversight and Enforcement (Interface Only)

Oversight, audits, anti-gaming enforcement, and referral capture controls are governed under the **Execution and Transition Authority** framework and apply uniformly across emergency and non-emergency services.

This document defines **what is regulated**, not **how enforcement is executed**.

---

## Transition and Implementation

Emergency utility regulation is rolled out through geographically bounded pilots with automatic access backstops.

During transition:
- Emergency access continuity is non-negotiable  
- Temporary stabilization mechanisms may be deployed  
- Rate frameworks are adjusted prospectively based on observed data  

---

## Bottom Line

Emergency and trauma care remain insured, but no longer operate as pricing black boxes. Utility regulation forces transparency, constrains monopoly behavior, and protects access without nationalizing care or issuing blank checks. Insurance pays disciplined prices. The public gains visibility into real costs for the first time.

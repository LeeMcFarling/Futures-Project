# Public Capital–Backed Drug Discovery and Translation Framework

## Purpose

This brief defines a framework for addressing structural failures in pharmaceutical research and development where market incentives alone are insufficient. The objective is to ensure continuous discovery, translation, and access for socially essential medicines—particularly antibiotics, rare disease therapies, and other low-volume, high-need domains—without relying on monopoly pricing or extended exclusivity.

The framework uses the Public Capital Authority (PCA) to absorb risk where markets fail, while partnering with private industry where it is operationally strongest.

---

## Problem Statement: Structural Market Failure

Certain categories of drugs consistently fail under conventional market incentives:

- Antibiotics, due to stewardship limits, resistance dynamics, and low sustained volumes  
- Rare disease therapies with small patient populations  
- Platform technologies with high uncertainty and diffuse downstream value  

In these domains, extending patents or raising prices does not reliably produce innovation and often produces perverse incentives. The failure occurs upstream (discovery and early translation), not just downstream (pricing).

---

## Core Design Principles

1. **Risk Should Sit Where Capacity Exists**  
   Public institutions can absorb early-stage and translational risk that private firms rationally avoid.

2. **Execution Should Sit Where Expertise Exists**  
   Private firms retain responsibility for trials, manufacturing, regulatory execution, and distribution.

3. **Returns Should Be Bounded and Predictable**  
   Publicly de-risked assets do not justify open-ended monopoly profits.

4. **Innovation Should Be Directed, Not Vague**  
   Incentives work best when challenges are tightly scoped and success is objectively measurable.

5. **The Public Should Not Pay Twice**  
   Public funding must translate into access, continuity, and pricing discipline.

---

## Role of the Public Capital Authority (PCA)

The PCA acts as a mission-oriented sponsor, not a drug company.

Its responsibilities include:
- Funding basic and pre-competitive research at universities and public labs  
- Supporting IND-enabling work and early clinical trials (Phase I, selective Phase IIa)  
- Coordinating parallel research efforts to reduce single-point failure  
- Contracting with private firms for downstream execution under public-interest terms  

The PCA works with Universities, and pharma companies to de-risk ventures that would traditionally be market failures. 

---

## University-Level Research and Tech Transfer

PCA funding prioritizes upstream discovery where scientific uncertainty is highest.

Key features:
- Milestone-based grants rather than perpetual funding  
- Multiple teams funded in parallel where appropriate  
- Clear separation between discovery funding and commercialization rights  
- No requirement for academic researchers to form startups  

Technology transfer pathways are standardized so that publicly funded discoveries move cleanly into PCA-managed translation rather than defaulting to incumbent capture.

---

## Translation and Industry Partnerships

Once assets reach defined readiness thresholds, the PCA partners with pharmaceutical companies, biotechs, or CROs to advance them through trials and manufacturing.

Partnership contracts specify:
- Cost-sharing or cost-recovery structures  
- Capped or fixed returns tied to recoupment, not volume maximization  
- Time-limited commercialization windows  
- Pricing constraints aligned with public investment  
- Step-in rights if development stalls or partners exit  

Private partners receive de-risked assets and execution certainty. In exchange, they accept bounded upside.

---

## Tightly Scoped Innovation Challenges

In domains with clearly definable success criteria, the PCA may issue narrowly scoped innovation challenges.

Examples include:
- Discovery of a novel antibiotic class effective against specified resistant strains  
- Demonstration of non-toxicity to humans under defined thresholds  
- Achievement of predefined preclinical or early clinical benchmarks  

Challenge design principles:
- Objective, verifiable success conditions  
- Fixed cash awards or milestone payments  
- Open eligibility (academic teams, startups, independent researchers)  
- No requirement to surrender future participation rights unless public funds advance translation  

These challenges are not substitutes for sustained research funding. They are precision tools for breakthroughs where goals can be clearly stated.

---

## Recognition and National Problem-Solving

This framework explicitly treats biomedical innovation as a public good and a civic contribution.

Successful teams may receive:
- Public recognition and citation in federal programs  
- Priority access to PCA translation pathways  
- Follow-on funding opportunities  
- Formal attribution in resulting therapies  

The intent is to encourage Americans to solve real national problems and to be visibly recognized for doing so—without converting recognition into rent-seeking.

---

## Interaction With Pricing and Access Frameworks

This framework complements recoupment-bound pricing and duration caps.

Because the PCA absorbs upstream and translational risk:
- Downstream pricing constraints can be tighter  
- Exclusivity periods can be shorter, especially for antibiotics  
- Supply continuity is easier to enforce  

This avoids the need to prop up broken incentives with extended monopolies.

---

## Summary

This framework addresses pharmaceutical market failure by moving risk upstream, execution downstream, and returns into bounded, predictable channels. It preserves private-sector strengths while aligning innovation with public need.

By combining public risk absorption, disciplined partnerships, and tightly scoped innovation challenges, the system delivers medicines that markets will not—without recreating monopoly dependency or institutional capture.

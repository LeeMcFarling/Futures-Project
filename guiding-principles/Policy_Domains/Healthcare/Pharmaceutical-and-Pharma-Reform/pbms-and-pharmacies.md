# Pharmacy and PBM Reform Framework

## Purpose

This brief defines reforms to the pharmacy and PBM layer to ensure that drug distribution,
pricing, and reimbursement align with public-interest objectives under a unified healthcare
system. The goal is to eliminate opaque pricing practices and rebate-driven distortions while
preserving logistical efficiency, patient access, and incentives for legitimate innovation.

Pharmacies and PBMs are not abolished. They are re-scoped, standardized, and regulated as
infrastructure.

---

## Pharmacies as Distribution Infrastructure

Under this model, pharmacies function primarily as **medication distribution hubs**, not
pricing or financial arbitrage centers.

Core pharmacy roles:
- Secure storage and distribution of medications  
- Dispensing and patient access points  
- Clinical services tied to medication use (e.g., counseling, adherence support)  
- Inventory and supply-chain coordination  

Pharmacies are compensated for execution and service, not for price spread or rebate capture.
This aligns pharmacy incentives with access, continuity, and safety.

---

## Full Formulary Transparency

All formularies are **mandated to be fully transparent**.

Requirements:
- Public disclosure of all covered drugs by tier  
- Explicit clinical and economic criteria for placement  
- Disclosure of all price concessions, rebates, or manufacturer payments  
- Clear substitution and step-therapy rules  

No formulary decisions may be based on undisclosed financial arrangements. Transparency is a
condition of participation.

---

## Pricing Structure: Floors, Ceilings, and Recoupment

### Cost-Plus Floor Pricing

For cost-plus or generics-based drugs:
- A statutory **floor price** is enforced  
- The floor covers manufacturing, distribution, quality control, and a modest margin  
- Below-cost pricing is prohibited to prevent supply erosion and market exit  

This ensures long-term availability and resilience.

### Recoupment-Bound Pricing Ceilings

For branded therapeutics and drugs with active R&D recoupment:

- Each drug has a **standardized price ceiling**  
- The ceiling is tied to documented R&D, manufacturing, and scaling costs  
- A publicly visible **recoupment gauge** shows:
  - Total eligible costs
  - Percentage recouped to date
  - Estimated time remaining before full recoupment  

Once recoupment is complete, prices automatically step down to a lower, post-recoupment band.

This applies regardless of payer or distribution channel.

---

## Role of PBMs and Insurers

PBMs and insurers may continue to negotiate with manufacturers, but only under **strict,
transparent rules**.

Permitted functions:
- Administrative claims processing  
- Price negotiation under pass-through or fixed-fee models  
- Rebates or payments from manufacturers that are:
  - Fully disclosed
  - Tied to volume certainty, logistics, or adherence support
  - Passed through to the system or patient  

Prohibited practices:
- Retained rebates tied to list price inflation  
- Spread pricing hidden from payers or patients  
- Formularies driven by undisclosed financial incentives  

PBMs are paid for services rendered, not for extracting margin from opacity.

---

## Integration With Public Capital Authority (PCA)

For drugs developed or de-risked with PCA involvement:

- Distribution terms are pre-specified  
- Recoupment ceilings and timelines are locked contractually  
- No rebates or spread pricing are permitted  
- Pharmacies and PBMs receive standardized service fees  

Public investment cannot be undermined downstream.

---

## Patient Pricing and Experience

From the patient perspective:
- Prices reflect true net cost and applicable caps  
- Any cost-sharing is predictable and bounded  
- For SEC-covered drugs tied to severe or life-threatening conditions, price at point of
  use is zero  

Patients never face surprise pricing driven by rebate mechanics.

---

## Oversight and Enforcement

Oversight includes:
- Mandatory disclosure of net prices, rebates, and margins  
- Standardized reporting through unified payment rails  
- Routine audits and public dashboards  
- Penalties for non-compliance, including exclusion from participation  

Compliance is structural, not discretionary.

---

## Acceptable Policy Flexibilities (If Required)

If full implementation cannot clear immediately:
- Temporary rebate pass-through requirements may precede elimination  
- Interim caps on spread pricing may be applied  
- Grandfathered arrangements must include sunset clauses  

These are transitional measures, not the end state.

---

## Summary

This framework redefines pharmacies as distribution infrastructure, mandates full formulary
transparency, and enforces cost-plus floors and recoupment-bound price ceilings. PBMs and
insurers may continue to exist, but only as transparent service providers operating within
clearly defined constraints.

The result is a drug distribution system that supports innovation, protects supply, and
serves patients—without relying on opacity or rent extraction.

## Purpose

This brief establishes a pricing framework that separates legitimate development cost recovery from indefinite rent extraction. Its objective is to ensure that pharmaceutical developers reliably recoup documented costs and reasonable returns while guaranteeing that prices collapse toward transparent cost-plus levels once recovery is achieved.

The framework replaces monopoly-duration pricing with **time-bounded, population-level recoupment followed by automatic transition to cost-plus pricing**.

## Problem Statement

Current pharmaceutical pricing embeds three structural failures:

- Development risk is borne privately but recovered through prolonged monopoly pricing.
- Fragmented payers free-ride on one another, inflating list prices.
- Intermediaries extract opaque rents that disconnect price from cost.

As a result, patients face high prices long after development costs have been recovered, while public-need areas remain underinvested.

## Core Principle

**Development costs should be recovered explicitly, predictably, and once.**

After recoupment, prices should reflect manufacturing, distribution, and modest operating margins—not sunk R&D risk.

## Recoupment-Bound Pricing Model

### Recoverable Cost Definition

Recoverable cost includes:
- Documented R&D expenditures
- Pro-rated costs of failed programs within the same development portfolio
- Manufacturing scale-up and validation
- A capped return multiple

Use of public de-risking infrastructure reduces the recoverable cost base proportionally.

### Population-Level Purchase Commitments

For qualifying public-need therapeutics, public payers may negotiate:

- Guaranteed volume or revenue floors
- Time-bounded recoupment windows
- Per-patient prices sufficient to meet recovery targets

Participation is optional but conditional on transparency and post-recoupment transition.

### Duration Caps

Recoupment is subject to explicit duration limits:

- 3–5 years: antibiotics, prevention, nutrition
- 5–7 years: common chronic conditions
- 7–10 years: rare diseases and biologics

If recovery targets are met early, transition occurs immediately.

## Automatic Cost-Plus Transition

Upon completion of recoupment:

- Prices automatically collapse toward transparent cost-plus benchmarks
- Exclusivity narrows to manufacturing and quality differentiation
- Generic or biosimilar entry accelerates

No further negotiation is required.

## Cost-Plus Benchmarking

Transparent manufacturers (e.g., cost-plus producers) establish credible acquisition benchmarks:

Benchmark price = verified production cost + standardized distribution allowance

These benchmarks anchor reimbursement and retail pricing.

## Pharmacy and Distribution Model

Pharmacies operate as competitive distribution and service nodes:

- Drugs reimbursed at benchmark + fixed dispensing fee
- No spread pricing or retroactive fees
- Direct-to-patient and ship-to-pharmacy fulfillment allowed

Pricing clears upstream; pharmacies compete on service quality.

## Role of Insurance (SEC)

Severe Event Coverage (SEC) applies where individual risk cannot be reasonably borne:

- Early-stage therapies under recoupment
- Rare diseases and high-cost biologics
- Safety-driven transitions or substitutions

For commodity and post-recoupment drugs, insurance becomes secondary as prices collapse.

## Interaction with Evidence Stages

Pricing aligns with evidence maturity:

- Stage 1–2: higher price bands, narrower populations, SEC coverage dominant
- Stage 3: broader access, declining prices
- Post-recoupment: cost-plus pricing

## Safeguards

- Full auditability of recoverable costs
- Public recoupment counters and timelines
- Ban on spread pricing and retroactive DIR fees
- Any-willing-pharmacy access at benchmark terms

## Expected Impact

- Rapid recovery of legitimate development costs
- Elimination of indefinite monopoly pricing
- Lower patient prices without suppressing innovation
- Restoration of antibiotics and prevention markets

## Relationship to Pharma R&D De-Risking Stack

Recoupment-bound pricing completes the Pharma R&D framework by aligning incentives across research, regulation, safety, and distribution. Together, these components replace opacity and rent-seeking with predictable recovery and durable affordability.

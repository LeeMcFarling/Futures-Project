## Purpose

This brief establishes a stage-based authorization and post-market safety framework for pharmaceutical, nutritional, and preventive interventions. Its objective is to replace the false binary of “approved vs. unsafe” with an explicit, evidence-aligned progression model that reflects scientific reality, preserves public trust, and enables responsible use while long-term risks continue to be evaluated.

The framework does not weaken safety standards or accelerate approvals. It formalizes uncertainty, specifies monitoring obligations in advance, and makes reversibility explicit.

## Problem Statement

Current regulatory communication implicitly treats approval as a declaration of comprehensive safety. When rare, delayed, or population-specific adverse effects later emerge, public trust is damaged and regulatory legitimacy is questioned. This dynamic discourages honest discussion of uncertainty and makes post-market intervention politically difficult.

At the same time, it is not feasible to test every intervention over decades before allowing use. The existing system obscures this reality instead of managing it.

## Core Principle

**Authorization is a stage transition, not a claim of final knowledge.**

Movement from controlled trials to broader use reflects that pre-specified evidentiary thresholds have been met—not that all risks are known.

## Stage-Based Authorization Model

Authorization proceeds through explicit stages, each with defined evidence requirements, scope of use, and safety obligations.

### Initial Authorization

Granted when evidentiary sufficiency criteria defined in the Certification Clarity framework are met.

Characteristics:
- Defined indication and population
- Explicit usage scope
- Required post-market safety monitoring
- Time-bounded review horizon

### Expanded Authorization

Granted after additional safety evidence accrues.

Characteristics:
- Broader populations or indications
- Reduced usage constraints
- Updated monitoring thresholds

### Full Authorization

Granted only when long-term safety and effectiveness are well characterized.

Characteristics:
- Routine market status
- Standard pharmacovigilance

Progression is not automatic. Each stage requires evidence review.

## Post-Market Safety Monitoring

Post-market safety is treated as a continuation of evidence generation, not an afterthought.

Monitoring is:
- Event-based, not continuous
- De-identified and aggregate by default
- Proportional to risk and exposure

SSI-based credentials may be used to attest to safety-relevant events (e.g., hospitalization, adverse reaction class) without revealing identity or raw medical records.

## Pre-Specified Safety Obligations

Safety obligations are defined at the time of authorization and include:
- Which signals are monitored
- Observation time horizons
- Statistical thresholds for action
- Required follow-up studies

No ad hoc reinterpretation.

## Automatic Triggers and Responses

Responses to safety signals are rule-based:

- Label narrowing
- Usage restrictions
- Mandatory additional studies
- Temporary suspension

Triggers and responses are published in advance.

## Reversibility and Sunset

All early-stage authorizations include:
- Mandatory review dates
- Automatic narrowing or lapse if obligations are unmet
- Explicit withdrawal criteria

This makes rollback procedurally normal rather than exceptional.

## Communication Standards

Public communication must:
- State what is known and unknown
- Describe why authorization is justified at this stage
- Explain ongoing monitoring and triggers

Approval language emphasizes progression, not finality.

## Therapeutic Evidence Label (TEL)

Each authorized therapeutic must be accompanied by a standardized **Therapeutic Evidence Label**, analogous to a nutrition label, provided at dispensing.

The label is factual, non-persuasive, and regulator-defined. It communicates evidence maturity, known risk boundaries, and monitoring status. 

### Required Fields

**Evidence Stage**
- Ordinal stage (1–4) for the specific indication and population
- NOTE: A single therapeutic may hold different stages for different uses and users.
- Plain-language description of what the stage means

**Approved Indication and Population**
- Explicit statement of studied indication(s)
- Clear identification of populations not yet fully evaluated

**Duration of Evidence**
- Longest studied duration of use
- Statement of unknown long-term effects where applicable

**Measured Benefits**
- Primary outcomes demonstrated in trials
- No marketing language

**Observed Risks**
- Common, rare, and ongoing-evaluation adverse effects

**Monitoring Status**
- Whether post-market monitoring is active
- Next scheduled safety review

**Stage Mismatch Notice**
- Flag when prescribed use differs from primary studied population or indication
- Display evidence stage applicable to that use. 
- This is for information only; it is not meant to interfere with clinical judgement or prescribing.

### Expanded Authorization (Stage 2)

### Full Authorization (Stage 3)

Granted only when long-term safety and effectiveness are well characterized.

Characteristics:
- Routine market status
- Standard pharmacovigilance

Progression is not automatic. Each stage requires evidence review.

## Post-Market Safety Monitoring (Stage 4)

Post-market safety is treated as a continuation of evidence generation. Stage 4 is not to be considered a higher authorization level. It represents ongoing population-level safety evaluation applicable to therapeutics at all authorization stages

Monitoring is:
- Event-based, not continuous
- De-identified and aggregate by default
- Proportional to risk and exposure

SSI-based credentials may be used to attest to safety-relevant events (e.g., hospitalization, adverse reaction class) without revealing identity or raw medical records.


**Contextual Note**
- Standardized statement clarifying that pharmacologic therapy does not substitute for preventive or non-pharmacologic care where relevant

### Governance

- Label language and format are standardized
- Historical versions are retained and publicly accessible
- Changes to stage or scope are auditable

The Therapeutic Evidence Label operationalizes stage-based authorization for clinicians and patients, making uncertainty visible without restricting clinical judgment.

## Safeguards

- No individual tracking
- No behavioral surveillance
- Independent safety signal review
- Public auditability of monitoring rules

## Relationship to Pharma R&D De-Risking Stack

Stage-based authorization complements certification clarity by ensuring that ex-ante success criteria and ex-post safety humility operate together. It completes the transition from implicit promises to explicit, evidence-bound governance.

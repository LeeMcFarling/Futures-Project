## Summary

Pilot vertically integrated farms and controlled-environment greenhouses in dense, high-food-demand nodes to stabilize access to fresh, perishable food, reduce logistics volatility, and complement—not replace—regional agriculture. These pilots function as infrastructure, not lifestyle or branding projects.

## Problem Statement

Fresh produce and other perishable foods face the highest spoilage risk, transport cost, and price volatility, particularly in dense urban areas. Long supply chains amplify shocks from weather, fuel costs, labor disruptions, and regional crop failures, disproportionately affecting low- and middle-income households.

At the same time, traditional agriculture is poorly suited to supply certain high-frequency, short-shelf-life foods into dense population centers at consistent prices. This creates gaps where demand is constant but supply is fragile.

Urban controlled-environment agriculture can address a narrow but important portion of this problem if treated as infrastructure rather than novelty.

## Policy Objective

Use vertical farms and greenhouses to:
- Stabilize supply of high-turnover perishable foods
- Reduce transport and spoilage costs in dense nodes
- Complement regional and rural producers, not compete with them
- Provide predictable baseline supply during shocks

The objective is resilience and price stability, not total self-sufficiency.

## Scope and Siting Strategy

Pilots are sited in:
- High foot-traffic, high food-demand 15-minute nodes
- New development corridors and transit-adjacent zones
- Pre-identified institutional clusters (schools, hospitals, public facilities)

Sites prioritize proximity to consumption over visibility or architectural novelty.

## Crop and Product Focus

Pilots focus on foods where controlled environments offer clear advantages:
- Leafy greens and herbs
- High-spoilage vegetables
- Seedlings and starter plants
- Limited specialty crops with short harvest cycles

Staple grains, commodity crops, and livestock are explicitly out of scope.

## Integration with Broader Food System

Vertical farm output is routed preferentially to:
- Public institutional procurement (schools, DOC facilities)
- Cooperative aggregation hubs
- Local retail and bulk purchasing channels

These facilities operate as supply stabilizers, not standalone businesses competing with farmers.

## Operating Model

- Facilities may be public, cooperative, or public–private
- Performance metrics focus on yield stability, cost per unit, energy efficiency, and spoilage reduction
- Designs favor modularity and replicability over bespoke builds

Subsidies, where used, are time-limited and performance-linked.

## Data and Transparency

All pilots report standardized data on:
- Output volume and consistency
- Cost curves and energy inputs
- Waste and spoilage rates
- Integration with institutional demand

Data feeds into the broader demand-signal infrastructure to inform future siting and scale decisions.

## What This Policy Is Not

- Not a replacement for rural farming
- Not a solution for all food categories
- Not a venture capital showcase
- Not a real estate branding exercise

Vertical farms are tools, not symbols.

## Pilot Strategy

Year-one pilots are limited in number and diversity of design. Expansion occurs only where pilots demonstrate:
- Meaningful reduction in supply volatility
- Competitive or improving unit economics
- Clear complementarity with regional producers

Failure is an acceptable outcome if it yields usable cost and performance data.

## Expected Year-One Outcomes

- Improved reliability of fresh food supply in dense nodes
- Reduced spoilage and transport costs for targeted categories
- Better shock tolerance during supply disruptions
- Actionable evidence on where controlled-environment agriculture makes sense

## Optional Extension: Community Ownership and Dividend Participation (Conditional)

If and only if a vertical farm or greenhouse pilot demonstrates sustained economic viability, select facilities may participate in a tokenized ownership and dividend pilot designed to align consumers, workers, and local stakeholders with long-term performance.

Key constraints:
- Participation is optional and facility-specific
- Tokens represent economic rights, not governance capture
- Dividends are paid only from verified operating surplus
- No guaranteed returns, subsidies, or price support
- No speculative trading during pilot phase

Eligible participants may include:
- Workers and operators
- Participating institutions or cooperatives
- Local residents or consumers who opt in
- Public entities where appropriate

The objective is alignment, not fundraising. Ownership mechanisms are introduced only where facilities function as stable infrastructure rather than experimental ventures.

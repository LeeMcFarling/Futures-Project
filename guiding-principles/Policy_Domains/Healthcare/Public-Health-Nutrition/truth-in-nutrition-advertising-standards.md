## Summary

Establish minimum, evidence-based thresholds for nutrition-related advertising claims and close serving-size loopholes that enable technically true but materially misleading food marketing. The policy governs claims, not product composition or consumer choice.

## Problem Statement

Food products are routinely marketed using nutrition claims that exploit small serving sizes, low-quality fillers, incomplete bioavailability, and legal definitions disconnected from metabolic impact. Existing rules allow claims that are formally compliant but practically misleading.

## Policy Objective

Restore meaning to nutrition claims by:
- Anchoring claims to nutritionally significant quantities
- Preventing serving-size manipulation
- Aligning claims with metabolic relevance
- Preserving legitimate product differentiation

## Scope of Covered Claims

This standard applies to all nutrition-related advertising claims, including but not limited to:
- “High protein”
- “Good source of protein”
- “Low sugar”
- “Low carb”
- “High fiber”
- “Whole grain”

Claims may appear on packaging, advertising, digital marketing, and point-of-sale materials.

## Protein Claim Standard

A product may be advertised as **“high protein”** only if it satisfies **both** of the following per standard serving:

### 1. Absolute Protein Floor

At least **30 grams of bioavailable protein**, adjusted for digestibility and amino acid completeness.

### 2. Protein Density Requirement

At least **X grams of protein per 100 calories** (or an equivalent percentage of total caloric content), with thresholds set through evidence-based rulemaking.

Both criteria must be satisfied.

## Low Sugar and Low Carb Claims

Claims of “low sugar” or “low carb” must reflect **metabolic impact**, not merely chemical classification. 

### Glycemic and Insulin Response Proxies

Claim eligibility is determined using:
- Glycemic load per serving as the primary proxy
- Insulin response indices or conservative category-based adjustments where applicable
- Default disqualification for known high–insulin-response ingredients unless demonstrated otherwise

Total sugars or total carbohydrates alone are insufficient for claim eligibility.

## Serving Size Integrity (Foundational Rule)

All nutrition claims are subject to **dual normalization**:

1. **Per standard serving**, using category-level reference serving sizes rather than manufacturer-selected portions  
2. **Per container**, where the full package is reasonably consumable in one sitting

If a claim fails under either evaluation, it may not be used.

### Additional Constraints

- Artificial micro-servings may not be used to qualify claims  
- Per-container values override per-serving values when applicable  
- Claim eligibility may not be averaged across servings

## Governance and Updates

Thresholds and proxies are established through transparent, evidence-based processes and reviewed periodically. Updates require published justification and advance notice.

Initial enforcement prioritizes high-abuse categories, with phased expansion based on measured outcomes.

## Enforcement

Strict liability applies to improper or misleading claims. Remedies focus on claim removal and corrective advertising, escalating to penalties for repeated or willful violations.

## Explicit Non-Goals

- No product bans  
- No changes to ingredient legality  
- No dietary prescriptions  
- No price or portion controls  

## Expected Year-One Outcomes

- Elimination of common claim-based marketing loopholes  
- Clearer differentiation for genuinely nutrient-dense foods  
- Improved consumer comprehension and trust  
- Complementary pressure toward substantive formulations

## Summary

This policy expands market access on both sides of the food system. For consumers, it lowers the cost of high-quality, perishable food by enabling bulk purchasing, shorter supply chains, and practical storage support. For farmers, it creates multiple, credible downstream markets—public institutional demand, cooperative processing, and direct-to-consumer bulk sales—reducing dependence on single buyers or crops.

A transparent data layer connects the two, giving farmers real-time signals about what people actually eat and where demand is stable or growing. This enables diversification across products and sales channels based on observed demand rather than speculation, improving resilience, reducing waste, and strengthening regional food systems without mandates or centralized planning.

## Problem Statement

Farmers face volatile and asymmetric markets not only because of weak information, but because downstream demand is fragmented, intermediated, and often inaccessible. Even where local demand exists, farmers frequently lack the processing capacity, aggregation, or contracting mechanisms required to reach it.

Quality, minimally processed food is typically more expensive and more perishable than shelf-stable, ultra-processed alternatives. This creates a structural mismatch: consumers face higher prices and spoilage risk when purchasing in small quantities, while farmers face narrow selling windows and elevated downstream risk for perishable goods.

At the same time, large public institutions purchase substantial volumes of food through opaque, national contracts that rarely support regional producers. Consumers who want to buy real food in cost-effective quantities often lack direct options, while farmers lack downstream outlets beyond concentrated processors.

The result is unnecessary volatility, wasted production, higher consumer prices, and missed opportunities for regional alignment.

## Policy Objective

Create stable baseline demand and credible downstream options by:
- Anchoring demand in public institutions
- Strengthening cooperatives and regional processing
- Enabling direct-to-consumer bulk purchasing
- Publishing non-coercive demand signals to inform production

This policy improves market function without mandating outcomes.

## Core Mechanism 1: Public Institutional Anchor Demand

Public schools and correctional facilities serve as initial demand anchors.

### Scope

- K–12 public schools  
- State and federal correctional facilities  
- Other eligible public institutions (phase two)

### Structure

- Institutions commit to multi-year, volume-based procurement targets for core food categories (produce, staples, proteins)
- Procurement prioritizes regional suppliers where feasible, without exclusivity
- Contracts are standardized, transparent, and predictable

This creates boring, reliable demand that farmers and cooperatives can plan around.

## Core Mechanism 2: Cooperative & Regional Processing Alignment

Anchor demand is routed preferentially through:
- Farmer cooperatives
- Regional aggregation hubs
- Public-interest processing and storage facilities supported under existing agriculture briefs

This ensures farmers can meet institutional demand without being forced through monopolized processors and allows more value to remain regional.

## Core Mechanism 3: Direct-to-Consumer Bulk Purchasing Pilot

In parallel, the system pilots voluntary bulk purchasing channels for households.

### Design

- Consumers may opt to buy meat, produce, and staple foods in bulk directly from farmers or cooperatives
- Offerings emphasize freezer packs, seasonal produce boxes, and staple bundles
- Pricing reflects reduced packaging, marketing, and intermediary costs

### Objectives

- Give consumers access to high-quality, perishable food at lower per-unit cost through bulk purchasing
- Reduce spoilage and timing risk for farmers producing real, minimally processed food
- Shift cost savings from intermediaries and packaging back to households and producers
- Provide farmers with downstream optionality beyond institutional buyers

### Adoption Support (Non-Binding)

This mechanism recognizes that real food spoils and costs more to move in small quantities; bulk, planned purchasing aligns household economics with agricultural reality. To support adoption, complementary pilots may temporarily lower the cost of household food storage (e.g., chest freezers) so consumers can practically participate in bulk purchasing without increasing waste.

Storage-support mechanisms are time-limited, capped, and pilot-based, and are not prerequisites for participation.

## Core Mechanism 4: Demand Signal Publication (Supporting Layer)

The Department of Data & Accountability aggregates anonymized data from:
- Institutional procurement volumes
- Cooperative and aggregation hub sales
- Bulk purchasing participation (aggregated only)

Published outputs include:
- Near-term demand forecasts by region
- Category-level consumption trends
- Seasonal demand stability indicators

These signals inform planting and production decisions but do not dictate them.

## Privacy and Governance Safeguards

- No individual consumer tracking
- No farm-level production mandates
- No use for enforcement, taxation, or eligibility screening
- Published methodologies and independent audits

Trust is treated as infrastructure.

## What This Policy Is Not

- Not price setting  
- Not guaranteed profit  
- Not forced localization  
- Not surveillance  
- Not central planning  

Farmers retain autonomy. Markets remain markets.

## Pilot Strategy

Year-one pilots focus on:
- Regions with existing cooperative or aggregation capacity
- School districts and correctional systems willing to standardize procurement
- Limited bulk-purchase SKUs to test logistics, uptake, and storage constraints

Scaling requires demonstrated reductions in volatility, waste, and per-unit costs.

## Expected Year-One Outcomes

- Stable baseline demand for regional producers
- Improved utilization of cooperative processing and storage
- Lower food costs for participating institutions and households
- Reduced spoilage for perishable foods
- Clearer downstream options and planning signals for farmers

# Supplement Integrity, Evidence, and Claims Reform

## Purpose

This brief establishes a lighter-touch but higher-trust regulatory framework for
dietary supplements.

The objective is not to regulate supplements as pharmaceuticals, require pre-market
efficacy approval for every product, or block rapid product innovation. The objective
is to ensure that supplement products contain what they claim, disclose material
safety and uncertainty information, and make only those claims supported by credible
evidence.

The doctrine is simple:

Fast product entry. Strict claim governance. High transparency. Severe penalties
for deliberate deception.

Supplements may move quickly into the market. Claims must earn credibility through
evidence.

---

## Problem Statement

The supplement industry occupies a structurally ambiguous space between food,
wellness, performance, and medicine. Many products are low risk and may provide
genuine consumer value. Others make physiological, metabolic, cognitive, hormonal,
athletic, fertility, immune, sleep, or disease-adjacent claims that consumers
interpret as functionally medical.

The current market has three recurring failures.

First, consumers often cannot verify whether a supplement contains the ingredients,
dosages, and purity levels listed on the label.

Second, marketing claims frequently exceed the quality of evidence supporting them.
Claims can be phrased in ways that sound scientific while resting on weak, selective,
underpowered, or sponsor-shaped research.

Third, firms face weak incentives to disclose negative findings, contamination issues,
dosage inconsistencies, or internal evidence contradicting public claims.

This creates a bad equilibrium. Legitimate manufacturers struggle to distinguish
themselves. Consumers cannot evaluate product quality. Low-integrity firms compete
through marketing arbitrage rather than evidence. And the research literature reflects
what sponsors chose to publish, not what studies actually found.

---

## Policy Objective

Create a supplement market in which:

- Product innovation remains fast
- Product integrity is independently verified
- Claims scale with evidence
- Sponsor-funded research is permitted but firewalled
- Consumers can distinguish between verified ingredients, plausible claims, strong
  evidence, and unsupported marketing
- Honest uncertainty is protected and visible
- Deliberate concealment and coordinated deception are punished

The policy governs integrity, labeling, evidence, and claims. It does not operate
as a supplement ban regime.

---

## Core Design Principle

The framework separates four questions that are routinely collapsed in the current
market:

1. **Product integrity:** Does the bottle contain what the label says?
2. **Safety screening:** Are there known contamination, interaction, or
   exposure concerns?
3. **Evidence quality:** What level of evidence supports the claim?
4. **Market integrity:** Has the firm honestly disclosed what it knows?

A product may be legally sold without strong efficacy evidence, provided it does
not make unsupported claims and satisfies basic integrity and safety standards.
These four questions have different answers, require different mechanisms, and should
not be collapsed into a single gatekeeping determination.

---

## Layer One: Product Integrity Verification

The first layer answers the most basic consumer-trust question: is the product
actually what it says it is?

Covered supplements must undergo independent verification for:

- Ingredient identity
- Dosage accuracy
- Purity
- Contaminant screening
- Manufacturing consistency
- Batch-level variance where applicable

Verification is performed by accredited independent laboratories operating under
published public standards. Verification does not certify efficacy. It does not mean
the supplement works. It confirms that the product matches its factual label
representations and clears applicable safety-screening thresholds. Verification
results are batch-linked and publicly accessible through a product QR code or
registry lookup, making the integrity record available to consumers at the point
of purchase rather than only to regulators after the fact.

---

## Layer Two: Supplement Safety Screening

Supplements that produce physiological effects require safety legibility.
Safety screening includes, where applicable:

- Heavy metals
- Adulterants
- Undeclared active pharmaceutical ingredients
- Microbial contamination
- Known allergen risks
- Interaction risks with common medications
- Dose-related exposure concerns
- Population-specific risk flags

### Risk-Proportionate Tiers

Screening intensity is calibrated to physiological risk profile. Products are
assigned to one of three tiers at registration. Tier assignment is based on
claimed mechanism and target population, not on manufacturer self-classification.

**Tier A — Basic nutritional supplementation.**
Vitamins, minerals, electrolytes, and basic macronutrient supplements with
well-characterized safety profiles and no claimed physiological mechanism beyond
nutritional sufficiency. Standard contaminant and identity screening applies.
Examples: vitamin D, magnesium, calcium, basic protein powders.

**Tier B — Performance, metabolic, and functional claims.**
Supplements claiming effects on athletic performance, metabolic function, cognitive
function, sleep, immune response, or general wellness beyond basic nutrition.
Enhanced screening applies, including interaction risk assessment and dose-exposure
review. Independent product verification required before claims may be authorized.
Examples: creatine, caffeine, adaptogens, omega-3s with specific claims, pre-workouts.

**Tier C — Hormonal, weight-loss, fertility, and vulnerable-population products.**
Supplements claiming hormonal effects, weight-loss mechanisms, fertility support,
or products directed at pediatric, pregnancy, elderly, or medically vulnerable
populations. Highest scrutiny applies, including mandatory undeclared API screening,
population-specific risk assessment, and pre-market safety notification. Evidence
stage requirements are more stringent before claims may be authorized.
Examples: testosterone support products, fat burners, prenatal supplements with
novel ingredients, pediatric formulations.

Tier assignment is published and auditable. A manufacturer may contest a tier
assignment through the certified review process. Tier determines screening
intensity and evidence requirements — not whether a product may be sold.

The policy is risk-proportionate. Creatine and a testosterone booster do not
face the same review intensity. Screening standards are published in advance
by tier so manufacturers know what is required before they enter the market.

---

## Layer Three: Evidence-Based Claim Classification

Supplement claims are evaluated separately from product entry. A product may enter
the market with verified identity and safety screening while carrying no authorized
efficacy claims.

Claims involving physiological, metabolic, cognitive, hormonal, athletic-performance,
immune, sleep, fertility, or disease-adjacent outcomes must be tied to evidence
classifications. Claim eligibility is determined through pre-specified evidentiary
templates. These templates define acceptable endpoints, study duration, population
relevance, statistical thresholds, safety observation windows, and replication
requirements before sponsors incur execution risk.

This approach draws directly from the evidentiary sufficiency and certification
clarity model used in the pharmaceutical reform stack. Success criteria are specified
before execution, not reinterpreted after results are known. A claim that could not
have been made on the pre-specified evidence template cannot be rescued by
post-hoc reinterpretation.

---

## Supplement Evidence Label

Covered supplements carry a standardized Supplement Evidence Label. The label is
factual, non-persuasive, and regulator-defined. It does not endorse the product.
It communicates integrity status, evidence quality, and uncertainty in a format
consumers can use before purchase.

### Pre-Claim Status

A product that has cleared ingredient verification and safety screening but carries
no authorized efficacy claims displays a Pre-Claim status marker. This is a floor
condition, not a rung on the evidence ladder. It establishes that the product is
what it claims to be. It does not imply any demonstrated physiological effect.

No efficacy claims are authorized at Pre-Claim status beyond factual composition,
traditional-use descriptions where clearly labeled as such, or general
non-specific wellness language that does not imply a demonstrated physiological
effect.

### Evidence Stage

Products with authorized efficacy claims carry one of three evidence stages:

**Stage 1 — Preliminary Evidence.** The product or ingredient has early human
evidence, limited clinical evidence, or credible mechanistic support. Permitted
claims must be narrow, qualified, and tied to the studied population and dose.

**Stage 2 — Replicated Evidence.** The claim is supported by multiple studies,
improved population coverage, independent replication, and consistent outcome
direction. Moderate claims may be permitted, provided they remain tied to
demonstrated effects and do not imply broader results than the evidence supports.

**Stage 3 — Mature Evidence.** The claim is supported by strong replicated evidence,
meaningful sample sizes, credible duration, safety characterization, and independent
validation. This is the highest supplement evidence designation. It does not convert
the product into a drug or imply universal effectiveness.

Evidence stage is determined through the certified methodology process described
below. It is not self-reported.

### Label Fields

The label displays:

**Product Integrity** — ingredient verification status, dosage verification status,
batch testing status, contaminant screening status, manufacturing audit status.

**Evidence Stage** — the assigned stage and plain-language description of what
the stage means.

**Claim Scope** — the specific claim evaluated, the studied dose, the studied
population, the studied duration, and the outcome measured.

**Replication Status** — whether the finding has been independently replicated,
is awaiting replication, or has failed to replicate in independent studies.

**Funding** — whether the supporting evidence base is primarily sponsor-funded,
publicly funded, or mixed.

**Risk and Uncertainty** — known risks, known interactions, populations not
adequately studied, and long-term uncertainty where relevant.

**Monitoring Status** — active monitoring status, ongoing studies, and next
evidence review date.

The Supplement Evidence Label adapts the logic of the Therapeutic Evidence Label
from the pharmaceutical reform stack to the supplement context. It makes uncertainty
visible without treating supplements as pharmaceuticals.

is standardized; content varies by product and evidence record.

under a minute and understand what the product contains, what the claim covers,
how strong the evidence is, who funded it, what is unknown, and where to find more.
That is the purpose of evidence communication doctrine applied to a specific
product category.

## Advertising and Claims Standards

Supplement advertising must align with the product's authorized evidence stage.

Manufacturers may not:

- Exceed demonstrated findings
- Convert preliminary evidence into definitive claims
- Use selective endpoints to imply broader effects than the evidence supports
- Manipulate dosage or serving-size presentation to qualify claims
- Hide null or negative findings
- Claim "clinically proven" without satisfying the relevant evidence template
- Use influencer, affiliate, or proxy channels to make claims the manufacturer
  could not make directly

The proxy-marketing prohibition carries organizational knowledge attribution where
the manufacturer funds, directs, approves, or materially benefits from a controlled
affiliate relationship. If the manufacturer funds the influencer, the affiliate, or
the content creator — or exercises approval rights, sets affiliate terms, or
repeatedly fails to correct known noncompliant claims made through those channels —
the manufacturer's knowledge and intent are attributed to the claims made through
that channel. The structural defense of "we didn't say it, our affiliate did" is not
available where a controlled relationship exists. This mirrors the organizational
knowledge aggregation principle applied in the broader market integrity framework
and closes the most common channel through which manufacturers launder noncompliant
claims. Random influencer speech by parties with no material relationship to the
manufacturer is not covered.

This section incorporates the same logic as the truth-in-nutrition advertising
reform: claims should reflect metabolically or physiologically meaningful evidence
rather than technically compliant but materially misleading phrasing.

## Research Integrity Governance

Research supporting supplement claims is governed by the Research Integrity and
Methodological Transparency Framework (RIF). Sponsor-funded studies used to support
supplement claims must satisfy the transparency, preregistration, disclosure,
replication, certification, and evidence communication requirements established
under that framework.

The supplement-specific application of that doctrine reflects the texture of
nutrition and biochemistry research. Studies in this domain tend to be shorter in
duration than pharmaceutical trials, frequently involve real-food matrices and
dietary patterns rather than isolated compounds, and face particular challenges
around blinding, dietary compliance, and population heterogeneity. These
characteristics do not relax the integrity requirements — they inform how those
requirements are applied. Pre-registration templates for supplement claims account
for crossover designs, washout periods, and bioavailability endpoints that are
common in this literature but absent from pharmaceutical trial templates.

Three RIF mechanisms are especially active in the supplement context.

**Preregistration and the public ledger.** Sponsor-funded studies must be
pre-registered before enrollment. Non-publication after enrollment closes is a
detectable gap in the public record, not a private decision. Endpoint switching
leaves a traceable discrepancy. Sponsors may pay for research. Sponsors may not
purchase conclusions.

**Independent replication.** The grant reform framework's replication and
validation funding bucket explicitly covers supplement and nutrition research.
Sponsors have no incentive to fund studies that might disprove their products'
claims. The public grant system is the institution positioned to fund that work.
Priority replication targets include high-sales categories where the evidence base
is primarily sponsor-funded, ingredients with plausible mechanisms but limited
independent replication, and populations not adequately represented in existing
trials.

**DoDA certification and monitoring.** Evidence stage assignment is not
self-reported. DoDA certifies the evidentiary templates used to evaluate supplement
claims and monitors for divergence between pre-registered protocols and published
results, selective publication patterns, and claim language that migrates beyond
the evidence stage of the underlying research. DoDA outputs are evidentiary inputs —
certification findings affect evidence-stage eligibility and audit priority but do
not impose penalties absent the ordinary enforcement process.

## Escalation Ladder

Market integrity enforcement in this framework follows a defined escalation sequence.
Most supplement integrity failures are resolved at the lower rungs. CDE is a ceiling
reserved for coordinated deception, not a first-response tool for weak evidence.

**Rung 1 — DoDA telemetry and monitoring.** Divergence between pre-registered
protocols and published results, selective publication patterns, claim language
exceeding evidence stage, and safety signal anomalies are flagged through ongoing
monitoring. Flags are used to prioritize review, not to trigger enforcement.

**Rung 2 — Certification review.** Where monitoring identifies concerns, the
dual-certification review process evaluates whether the underlying research
meets published evidentiary standards. Findings may result in evidence-stage
downgrade, claim removal requirement, enhanced disclosure obligations, or increased
audit frequency.

**Rung 3 — Standing disclosure track.** Firms that discover contamination issues,
dosage inconsistencies, adverse event signals, internal evidence contradictions,
or claim-support failures have a standing pathway to disclose. The CDE framework's
standing disclosure track is available to supplement firms under the same terms
as any other covered entity. The epistemic threshold schema — requiring two or more
trigger classes to be met before the disclosure obligation activates — applies.
The grace period decay function applies from the moment the threshold is crossed.

Early disclosure through the standing track is treated fundamentally differently
from concealment that is later uncovered through enforcement. A firm that discovers
its internal studies produced findings inconsistent with its public claims, and
discloses promptly through the DoDA portal, may receive standing-track protections
that are not available to firms that are caught through enforcement. The standing
track is the rational choice for firms operating in good faith. It should be
understood as the first available response pathway, not as a last resort.

**Rung 4 — Graduated enforcement.** Minor violations trigger corrective labeling,
public notices, or temporary claim suspension. Material product-integrity failures
trigger batch recalls, enhanced testing, or manufacturing audits. Unsupported claims
trigger removal, corrective advertising, and evidence-stage downgrade. Repeated or
intentional violations trigger civil penalties, market access restrictions, or
referral to broader fraud and market-integrity authorities.

**Rung 5 — CDE referral.** The Coordinated Deceptive Practices Enforcement framework
applies where conduct satisfies all five elements of the CDE definition: enterprise
coordination across multiple actors, internal knowledge contradiction, sustained
pattern of conduct, material impact, and active concealment mechanism. All five
elements must be present. No single element and no combination of fewer than five
triggers CDE liability.

The CDE referral pathway is not triggered by weak evidence, honest scientific
failure, or losing an evidence dispute. It is not a mechanism for punishing supplement
companies that funded studies that didn't work out. The escalation ladder handles
those cases at Rungs 1 through 4.

Methodology audits at any rung may examine undisclosed sponsor influence, endpoint
switching, selective publication, material protocol deviation, statistical
misrepresentation, data fabrication, suppression of adverse findings, or ghostwritten
publications. Audit findings affect evidence-stage classification, claim eligibility,
disclosure requirements, and audit frequency. They do not punish scientific
disagreement, unconventional hypotheses, or honest failure.

---

Conduct that satisfies the Coordinated Deceptive Practices framework's criteria
for coordinated concealment, fraud, or institutional deception — including
coordinated campaigns against the certification infrastructure itself — may be
referred under that framework. Supplement-specific examples are addressed in the
CDE source brief.

## Relationship to Food, Nutrition, and Metabolic Research

Supplement evidence generation connects directly to the food, nutrition, and
metabolic research track. That track applies clinical-grade execution standards —
pre-registered designs, standardized measurement, publication of positive, null,
and negative findings — to nutrition and metabolic questions.

Supplement claims involving metabolism, satiety, glycemic response, protein quality,
dietary substitution, or preventive health should use measurement standards
compatible with that track where applicable. Shared measurement infrastructure
allows supplement findings to be compared against nutrition research findings,
reducing the ability of any sector to maintain a siloed evidence standard that
would not survive comparison.

---

## Relationship to Pharmaceutical Reform Stack

This framework borrows proportionately from the pharmaceutical evidence and safety
architecture. Specifically:

The evidentiary sufficiency and certification clarity model provides the template
logic for pre-specified claim standards. Success criteria are fixed before execution.

Regional clinical trial infrastructure is available as optional neutral execution
capacity for supplement studies that require controlled trial settings, particularly
for claims in metabolic, cognitive, or physiological domains.

Stage-based authorization provides the evidence-progression logic underlying the
evidence label. Authorization is a stage transition reflecting that pre-specified
thresholds have been met — not a declaration of comprehensive effectiveness.

Post-market safety logic provides the risk-proportionate monitoring model. Monitoring
is event-based, aggregate by default, and proportional to the physiological risk
profile of the supplement category.

The framework does not import pharmaceutical approval wholesale. The supplement
context is faster-moving, lower-risk in most categories, and should not require
pharmaceutical-style pre-market gatekeeping for product entry. What it borrows is
the evidence, labeling, and integrity logic — calibrated to a consumer market, not
a prescription drug pipeline.

---

## What This Policy Is Not

This policy is not a supplement ban regime. Not a pre-market drug approval system.
Not a government endorsement program. Not a prohibition on sponsor-funded research.
Not a punishment mechanism for honest scientific failure. Not a substitute for
clinical care.

Consumers retain freedom of choice. Manufacturers retain freedom to innovate.
Claims must earn credibility through evidence. Deliberate deception faces
consequences proportionate to its coordination, its concealment, and its harm.

---

## Expected Year-One Outcomes

- Improved supplement label accuracy
- Reduced contamination and adulteration risk
- Clearer consumer understanding of claim strength
- Stronger differentiation for high-integrity manufacturers
- Reduced advantage for marketing-only supplement brands
- Better incentives for sponsor-funded research to follow published protocols
- Public evidence architecture for independent replication through the grant
  reform system
- Foundation for a more trustworthy supplement market

---

## Summary

Supplement reform should not choose between a permissive market full of unsupported
claims and a pharmaceutical-style gatekeeping regime that slows low-risk innovation.

The better model is fast product entry paired with strict claim governance,
independent product verification, evidence-stage labeling, DoDA-certified methodology
review, independent replication funded through the public research system, and a
calibrated escalation ladder that reserves the heaviest consequences for the most
deliberate conduct.

Product entry remains fast. Claims move at the speed of evidence.

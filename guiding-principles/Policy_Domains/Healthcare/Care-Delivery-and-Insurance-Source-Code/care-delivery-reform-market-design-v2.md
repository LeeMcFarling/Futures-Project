## Purpose and Scope

This brief defines how non-emergency healthcare services are reorganized into
transparent, competitive markets. It governs primary care, urgent care, routine
diagnostics, minor procedures, scheduled surgery, and complex episodes. It also
establishes the boundary between market-based care, insurance, and utility-regulated
emergency care.

This brief does not regulate emergency or trauma services directly. Those are governed
separately as regulated utilities because patients cannot shop, delay care, or
meaningfully consent to a complex financial transaction during a medical crisis.

This brief does not define the full insurance system. It defines where insurance is
allowed to operate and where it is excluded — because its presence in routine care
distorts price discovery, increases administrative overhead, and weakens market
competition. Those are not side-effects, but rather the predictable outcome of routing
transactions through insurance that were never suited to it. 

The objective of this reform is to remove these distortive effects by restoring price discovery, consumer choice, and provider competition in the parts of healthcare where shopping, scheduling, and informed consent are possible. Those conditions exist for most of the care most people receive. The current system treats them as if they don't.

---

## Core Design Principle

The system separates three payment functions based on the clinical and economic nature
of the service.

If a service is predictable, schedulable, and comparable, it operates through a
transparent market. If an event is uncertain, high-cost, and unsuitable for individual
budgeting, it is covered through the Severe Event Coverage floor. If an event is
immediate, life-threatening, or clinically urgent enough that patients cannot shop or
delay care, it is governed through the emergency utility layer and financed through
the Severe Event Coverage architecture.

Markets are enforced, not assumed. Transparency, direct pricing, anti-gaming rules,
and payment-boundary enforcement are mandatory conditions of participation. 

---

## Market Layer Definition

The market layer includes healthcare services where patients can reasonably compare
options before care is delivered. A service belongs in the market layer when it is
predictable enough to price in advance, schedulable or deferrable without serious
clinical harm, comparable across providers, suitable for posted and binding prices,
and capable of being delivered through direct payment, subscription, bundled pricing,
or other transparent payment models.

The market layer includes primary care, urgent care for stable non-emergency
conditions, routine diagnostics, minor procedures, minor or cosmetic scheduled surgery, 
and other planned complex episodes where patients can compare providers in advance. The 
market layer does not include emergency care, trauma care, or crisis stabilization services.
Those are structurally different and governed accordingly.

---

## Primary Care

Primary care is the front door to the healthcare system. It operates as a
direct-to-consumer market.

Providers set their own prices and terms. Subscription-based models are allowed where
ongoing relationships add clinical value — a patient managing a complex chronic
condition benefits from a doctor who knows them over time. Per-visit pricing is
allowed where episodic care is appropriate. Both models are permitted because the
care relationship varies by patient need. What does not vary is the transparency
requirement. All primary care prices are binding, machine-readable, and posted on a central medical platform.

Subscription offerings must disclose the scope of included services, what is excluded,
access standards, response-time standards, panel size limits, and renewal and
cancellation terms. Ancillary services — labs and imaging — must be priced
transparently when they are ordered, bundled, or referred. Patients cannot be
surprised by costs they had no way to anticipate before they agreed to care.

Insurance billing, prior authorization, and mandatory membership requirements for
episodic access are all prohibited in the primary care market layer. These mechanisms
are how insurance currently re-enters routine care, suppresses price signals, and
recreates administrative cost without corresponding clinical value. Removing them
is the point of this reform.

Anti-consolidation thresholds apply during the transition. Providers may not use the
reform period to build monopolistic market power before competitive norms are established. Vertically integrated systems may continue to operate if they remain below certified
market-share thresholds within a geographic market and comply with the same
market-layer rules as independent providers. Network lock-in is prohibited. Referral
relationships must be clinically justified, transparent, and auditable. Utility transparency still applies by default, and providers cannot bundle primary care with downstream facility use or steer patients toward co-owned facilities as a condition of access.

## Urgent Care

Urgent care handles time-sensitive but stable conditions — the presentation that needs
attention today but is not an emergency. Its function in the system is to provide
fast, affordable episodic care and to prevent the inappropriate and expensive use of
emergency departments.

Urgent care operates on pay-per-episode pricing only. Subscriptions are prohibited
because urgent care is episodic by definition. Standardized, all-in bundles are
required for common urgent care presentations. Bundles must include the ordinary
services needed to resolve or stabilize the presentation — basic evaluation, common
imaging, basic procedures, and discharge or follow-up instructions. The price is
visible before treatment whenever feasible. Post-hoc facility fees, unbundled add-ons,
and reclassification games are prohibited. The price the patient sees is the price
they pay, except where genuine clinical escalation changes the category of care.

The escalation boundary with emergency care is explicit and enforced. When defined
clinical thresholds are met, escalation to emergency services is mandatory. Providers
who escalate appropriately receive legal safe harbor. Providers who bill urgent care
encounters as emergency services without meeting the clinical threshold face
enforcement consequences. That boundary protects patients and preserves the actuarial
integrity of the emergency utility layer. It is not optional.

## Routine Diagnostics and Minor Procedures

Routine diagnostics and minor procedures operate in the market layer when they are
predictable, schedulable, and comparable. This includes routine labs, common imaging,
screening services, and minor procedures that can be priced in advance.

Prices are posted, binding, and machine-readable. Providers must disclose whether
prices include interpretation, facility charges, professional fees, supplies, and
follow-up reporting. Unbundling is prohibited when the components are ordinarily
required to complete the service. Patients must be able to compare complete prices
across providers — not component-level prices that cannot be meaningfully compared
until the final bill arrives.

Insurance billing is excluded from this layer. Public supports, employer contributions,
health transition accounts, or Severe Event Coverage interfaces may subsidize the
patient, but the provider-facing transaction stays transparent and market-priced.
Support attaches to the patient without recreating opaque provider-insurer
reimbursement on the back end.

## Scheduled Surgery and Complex Episodes

Planned surgeries and complex procedures operate under regulated competition and are
covered by Severe Event Coverage. This is the layer where markets and insurance meet.
Patients can research, compare, and choose providers in advance. The cost may be large
enough that individual financial exposure requires pooling.

All-in episode pricing is required. The posted episode price includes facility fees,
professional services, anesthesia, devices and implants, ordinary supplies, standard
post-operative care, defined follow-up windows, and ordinary episode-management costs.
No unbundling. No facility-fee gaming. Hospitals and ambulatory surgery centers
compete on price, outcomes, access, safety, and patient experience.

Eligibility criteria determine the appropriate site of care. Lower-risk patients may
be routed to lower-cost ambulatory settings. Higher-risk patients may require hospital
settings with greater emergency backup capacity. Mandatory complication insurance,
reinsurance pools, or Severe Event Coverage interfaces cover adverse events outside
the ordinary episode boundary. Transfer protocols are defined in advance, not
improvised after something goes wrong.

Bundled pricing is not a restriction on competition. It is the precondition for it.
Providers cannot compete on price when the real price is hidden behind separately
billed components. Bundling makes the price legible so competition can actually
function.

## Insurance Boundary Rules

Insurance is excluded from primary care, urgent care, routine diagnostics, and minor
procedures. Severe Event Coverage applies to major scheduled surgeries, complex episodes, 
severe events, catastrophic events, and emergency utility care. Private insurance may compete above the SEC floor, 
but it may not reabsorb primary care, urgent care, routine
diagnostics, or minor procedures into hidden reimbursement.

This boundary is not an attack on insurance, but rather a correction of what insurance is
for. Insurance is structurally suited to large, uncertain, pooled risk. It is poorly
suited to routine, predictable transactions that can be priced in advance.

The logic is familiar from every other insurance market. Car insurance covers
accidents. It does not cover gas, oil changes, tire rotations, or car washes. If
routine maintenance were routed through car insurance, the entire car-maintenance
market would reorganize around reimbursement. Mechanics, gas stations, and tire shops
would build administrative teams to negotiate covered rates, dispute eligibility, and
police utilization. The cost of that administrative layer would eventually be built
into premiums. The consumer would pay more for less transparency, and the market for
routine maintenance would lose the price discipline that keeps it honest.

Healthcare made that structural mistake. Routine care, urgent care,
diagnostics, scheduled procedures, catastrophic illness, and emergency care were all
placed inside the same insurance payment architecture. The result was predictable —
opaque prices, administrative overhead, network restriction, prior authorization,
billing disputes, surprise bills, and weak consumer price discipline.

This reform separates the payment functions. Routine care returns to transparent
market pricing. The SEC floor absorbs uncertain, high-cost risk. Private insurance competes above that floor instead of routing routine care back through opaque reimbursement. Emergency care is governed as a utility because patients cannot shop or meaningfully consent in a
crisis. Each part of the system performs the function it is capable of performing.

Above the Severe Event Coverage floor, private plans may compete on supplemental
protection, care navigation, complication coverage, speed, reliability, provider
access, customer experience, and service quality. The purpose of the boundary is to
prevent insurance from reabsorbing routine care and recreating the same administrative
structure this reform is designed to remove.

## Severe Event Coverage Interface

Severe Event Coverage provides the baseline insurance layer for high-cost healthcare
events that cannot reasonably be paid through routine direct payment. It applies where
the cost, uncertainty, or risk profile of care requires pooling.

Severe Event Coverage may interface with scheduled surgeries, complex episodes,
complication coverage, catastrophic illness, emergency utility care, and other
high-cost events defined by statute or certification. It does not convert routine care
back into insurance-covered care. It provides a floor beneath high-cost risk while
preserving the market layer for predictable services.

Provider prices remain transparent where care is schedulable. Insurance coverage does
not eliminate the requirement for posted, bundled, comparable prices. Transparency
is not an either-or with insurance — it is a requirement that applies everywhere care
is shoppable, regardless of who ultimately pays.

## Competition and Anti-Gaming Rules

Functioning markets require active protection. Left undefended, they develop the same
distortions the current system relies on.

Facility-fee unbundling is prohibited. Post-hoc add-on charges for ordinary bundled
services are prohibited. Ownership-based referral steering is prohibited. Routine or
urgent care cannot be reclassified as emergency care without a genuine clinical basis.
Price discrimination based on payer type is prohibited within market layers. Hidden
insurer-provider contract pricing for market-layer services is prohibited. Mandatory
network restrictions for routine market-layer care are prohibited. Bundling primary
care access with downstream facility use is prohibited. Mandatory membership
requirements for episodic primary care access are prohibited.

Outcome reporting must be risk-adjusted and standardized. Quality comparisons must be
auditable and resistant to selection effects. Violations trigger escalating penalties,
including fines, restitution, loss of certification, exclusion from market-layer
participation, or exclusion from regulated insurance markets. The enforcement
mechanism is not advisory.

## Interface with Emergency Utility Care

When clinical thresholds are met, market-based care defers to emergency utility
services. Emergency care cannot function as a consumer market. Patients cannot shop
for emergency care, delay care to compare prices, or meaningfully consent to a complex
financial transaction while injured, unconscious, septic, or in acute distress.
Emergency care therefore operates as a regulated utility layer, not a consumer market.

The market layer and the emergency utility layer depend on each other staying clean.
If routine care creeps back into insurance through gaming or reclassification, the
insurance layer becomes bloated again. If emergency care is forced to behave like a
market, patients face impossible choices at the moment they have the least agency.
The boundary is explicit because the economics of both layers break without it.

---

## Access and Affordability Interface

Market pricing does not mean every patient pays the full cost alone. Public supports,
employer contributions, health transition accounts, direct subsidies, and other
affordability mechanisms may support patients in the market layer. The key rule is
that support attaches to the patient or the transaction without recreating opaque
provider-insurer reimbursement.

A subsidy may help a patient pay for primary care. It may not allow primary care to
return to hidden insurance billing. An employer contribution may support routine care
access. It may not require a closed network that prevents price comparison. A public
benefit may reduce patient exposure. It may not eliminate posted, binding,
machine-readable provider prices.

The affordability layer must preserve price transparency. Otherwise it becomes a
backdoor for rebuilding the current system — and the current system did not fail
because of a lack of spending. It failed because of a lack of honesty about what
things cost and who was paying for them.

## Transition and Enforcement

HETA oversees market designation, certification, price transparency enforcement,
anti-gaming audits, pilot evaluation, and scaling. Rollout occurs through
geographically bounded pilots with automatic safeguards to protect access and
competition during the transition.

The transition is sequenced to build market capacity before enforcement tightens.
Providers are not required to operate in a new system before the infrastructure to
support it exists. Transition sequencing covers market-layer service classification,
provider price-posting infrastructure, machine-readable pricing standards, bundled
episode definitions, emergency escalation thresholds, Severe Event Coverage
interfaces, anti-gaming audit systems, access monitoring, pilot evaluation, and
expansion, revision, or termination based on measured performance.

HETA certifies local markets only when minimum conditions are met — sufficient
provider availability, transparent price infrastructure, access protections, and
enforcement capacity. Markets that are not ready do not get certified. Markets that
stop meeting the conditions get reviewed.

---

## Evaluation Metrics

Pilot evaluation measures whether the reform improves affordability, access,
competition, and system legibility. Core metrics include primary care price
dispersion, urgent care episode prices, routine diagnostic prices, scheduled surgery
bundled prices, and emergency department diversion rates. Patient out-of-pocket
exposure, premium changes for Severe Event Coverage and supplemental plans,
administrative cost share, provider billing overhead, claim denial frequency, prior
authorization volume, and surprise billing incidence are all tracked.

Provider market concentration, patient wait times, rural and underserved-area access,
complication rates, risk-adjusted outcomes, patient satisfaction, and plan competition
above the Severe Event Coverage floor round out the evaluation picture.

Evaluation must distinguish true cost reduction from cost shifting. A pilot is not
successful if premiums fall but patient exposure rises, access deteriorates, or
emergency departments absorb avoidable care. The metrics are designed to catch that
substitution before it gets reported as a win.

## Failure Modes and Safeguards

The reform monitors and responds to predictable failure modes. Providers may use
transparency rules while increasing market power. Insurers may attempt to re-enter
routine care through managed networks. Urgent care clinics may reclassify routine
encounters as emergency events. Hospitals may unbundle scheduled procedures through
facility-fee games. Affordability supports may become hidden reimbursement channels.
Rural or underserved markets may fail to attract sufficient providers. Chronic-care
patients may face unstable direct-care costs. Patients may delay needed care because
the market layer is under-supported. Private plans may compete through risk selection
rather than service quality.

Safeguards include anti-consolidation thresholds, mandatory posted prices, standardized
bundles, emergency escalation rules, risk-adjusted outcome reporting, subsidy designs
that preserve price visibility, Severe Event Coverage floors, reinsurance and
complication pools, market certification gates, automatic review triggers, and pilot
termination or redesign authority.

The reform scales only where the market layer is functioning, access is stable, and
insurance boundaries are being maintained.

---

## Bottom Line

Care Delivery makes healthcare behave like a system designed on purpose. Routine care
becomes transparent, comparable, and market-priced. Urgent care becomes episodic,
bundled, and protected from emergency-billing games. Scheduled surgery becomes
competitively priced and insurable. Insurance stops functioning as a payment wrapper
for every ordinary healthcare transaction and returns to its proper role — pooling
large, uncertain risk. Emergency care is governed separately from routine care because crisis medicine cannot function as a consumer market.

HETA’s role is to enforce the market boundary so that routine care does not collapse
back into opaque reimbursement, administrative bargaining, or discretionary price
control.

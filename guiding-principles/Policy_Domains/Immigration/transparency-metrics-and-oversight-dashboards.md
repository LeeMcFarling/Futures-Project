## Purpose

Immigration policy loses legitimacy when outcomes are opaque and enforcement appears
selective or anecdotal. This brief defines a transparency framework that makes the
system observable, auditable, and accountable to the public and to Congress.

The objective is to replace ideological debate with verifiable facts.

## Core principle

If a rule exists, its outcomes must be visible.

Transparency is not optional or discretionary. It is a structural requirement that
allows enforcement to be trusted and policies to be adjusted based on evidence.

## Public dashboard requirements

A standardized set of anonymized dashboards is published on a fixed cadence and made
publicly accessible. At minimum, dashboards include:

- Domestic labor-demand forecasts by sector and region
- Immigration caps and visas issued by category
- Geographic distribution of visa holders
- Compliance rates by program
- Overstays, removals, and voluntary departures
- Re-entry utilization rates for compliant participants
- Wage effects relative to local labor markets
- Employer participation and compliance rates

No personally identifiable information is published.

## Separation of metrics

Labor immigration and asylum are tracked and reported separately.

Labor migration dashboards focus on demand alignment, compliance, and wage effects.
Asylum dashboards focus on intake volumes, processing timelines, service capacity, and
case outcomes.

This prevents conflation and protects the integrity of both systems.

## Oversight and governance

Congressional oversight is conducted through dashboard review and outcome metrics,
not through micromanagement of individual cases.

Agencies are evaluated on speed, accuracy, compliance, and enforcement consistency.
Persistent underperformance triggers mandatory review and corrective action.

## Integration with SSI infrastructure

Dashboards draw directly from SSI-based systems to ensure real-time accuracy and reduce
manual reporting burdens.

This prevents data manipulation and lagged reporting.

## Feedback into policy

Forecast errors, compliance failures, or unexpected labor-market effects trigger
automatic review of caps, rules, or program design.

Policy iteration is evidence-driven rather than reactive.

## Plain-English explanation

People do not trust systems they cannot see.

Under this framework, immigration outcomes are published regularly in plain numbers:
how many people entered, for what jobs, where they went, and whether the rules were
followed.

When the data is public, enforcement becomes believable and policy debates become
grounded in facts instead of fear.

Transparency is how this system stays honest.

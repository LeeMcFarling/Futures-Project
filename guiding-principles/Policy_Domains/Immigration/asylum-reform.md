## Core principle

Asylum and refugee protection are humanitarian and legal obligations, not labor
mechanisms. The number of people admitted must be strictly constrained by the United
States’ verified ability to process, place, and integrate them humanely and securely.

Under conditions of instability, the system deliberately undershoots capacity and
scales upward only as processing and service capacity expand.

---

## Humanitarian Service Capacity (HSC)

The foundation of the system is a binding annual **Humanitarian Service Capacity (HSC)**
number.

The HSC represents the maximum number of asylum seekers and refugees the United States
can admit in a given year **without degrading processing speed, public safety, or
integration outcomes**.

### Capacity inputs

The HSC is calculated by a dedicated asylum and refugee coordination agency, interfacing
with DoDa, federal departments, and state and local partners. It is based on:

1. Verified intake and case-management slots at certified partner organizations  
2. Local integration capacity (housing, language training, credentialing, skills
   localization, community services)  
3. Adjudication capacity (asylum officers, immigration judges, interpreters, legal staff)

If any one of these inputs is constrained, intake is constrained.

The HSC is published annually and is binding, with limited emergency adjustment authority
subject to Congressional approval.

## Intake channels (steering away from the border)

Asylum and refugee claims may be initiated **only** through designated lawful intake
channels.

These include:
- U.S. embassies and consulates  
- Federally designated processing centers outside the United States  
- Congressionally authorized crisis-specific programs  

Arrival at the U.S. border is not an intake mechanism.

This design shifts processing upstream, allows pre-certification of placement
locations, and prevents uncontrolled border surges.

Large-scale emergency programs require explicit Congressional authorization and must
include parallel capacity measures (staffing, funding, logistics, and security support)
to prevent system overload.

## Pre-vetting and risk screening (step-based process)

All cases move through a standardized, sequential screening process:

1. **Identity capture**  
   Collection of biographic information and available documentation.

2. **Public safety screening**  
   Identification of disqualifying factors, including violent felony history,
   organized crime indicators, or credible security risks.

3. **Documentation assessment**  
   Evaluation of documentation gaps or uncertainty without automatic disqualification.

4. **Watchlist and biometric checks** (where feasible)  
   Cross-checking against available domestic and international systems.

Failure at any disqualifying step results in case termination. Documentation uncertainty
alone does not.

---

## Statutory adjudication timelines

Speed is enforced through statute, not discretion. Timelines are matched to staffing and
specialization.

Target architecture:

1. **Initial screening**  
   72 hours to 7 days  
   Identity capture, biometrics where feasible, intake questionnaire, preliminary
   disqualifier screening.

2. **Credible fear determination**  
   2–6 weeks  
   Written decision issued by specialized asylum officers.

3. **Full merits hearing**  
   3–6 months from credible fear approval  
   Conducted by dedicated asylum adjudicators using standardized evidentiary bundles.

4. **Appeals**  
   60–120 days  
   Narrow grounds, strict briefing schedules, no open-ended continuances.

Fast does not mean sloppy. It means specialization, standardized evidence, and hard
scheduling.

## Placement contracts and honest integration

Approval includes a binding placement plan.

Placement is determined by verified local capacity, not applicant preference alone.
States and localities must opt in and certify capacity to receive placements.

Each placement includes:
- Language onboarding  
- Credential and skills evaluation  
- Pathways to lawful self-sufficiency where feasible  
- Compliance and check-in requirements  

Federal funding follows the person for a defined ramp period and is tied to measurable
integration outcomes.

---

## Data, transparency, and limits

The system publishes separate, public dashboards covering:
- HSC capacity and utilization  
- Intake volumes  
- Processing timelines by stage  
- Approval and denial rates  
- Placement distribution  
- Cost per case  
- Integration outcomes  

Asylum metrics are fully separated from labor immigration metrics.

---

## Hard firewall from labor migration

Asylum is not a release valve for labor demand.

- No conversion from asylum intake into labor visas  
- No workarounds through parallel systems  
- Denied cases proceed to timely return or removal  

Labor migration operates entirely separately under its own criteria and caps.

---

## Unauthorized border arrival and system consequences

To preserve system integrity and prevent dangerous, irregular migration, unauthorized
arrival at the U.S. border outside designated intake channels triggers immediate and
permanent consequences.

Individuals who present themselves at the border without authorization will be detained
for processing, have biometrics collected for identity verification, and be returned
directly to their country’s capital through coordinated repatriation procedures, rather
than being released at or near the border.

Unauthorized border arrival renders an individual **ineligible for asylum or refugee
protection under this system**, regardless of future conditions. It also renders the
individual ineligible for participation in SSI-based immigration processing, labor
migration programs, student-to-industry pathways, or other lawful entry mechanisms.

These consequences are categorical and non-discretionary. They exist to ensure that
lawful pathways are respected, humanitarian capacity is not overwhelmed, and incentives
are aligned away from smuggling networks and dangerous journeys.

The United States provides legal, defined, and humane channels for humanitarian
protection. Entry outside those channels is not an application; it is a violation of
system rules and is treated as such.

## Why this approach reduces suffering

This system reduces suffering by organizing humanitarian protection around reality
rather than aspiration.

By committing only to the number of people the United States can fully process, place,
and support, the system ensures that those who are admitted move quickly through
adjudication, receive stable placement, and have a real chance to build successful
lives. It prioritizes depth of support over raw intake numbers.

Attempting to help everyone at once without sufficient capacity creates prolonged
limbo, overcrowded facilities, exhausted service providers, public backlash, and worse
outcomes for the people the system is meant to protect.

Unchecked refugee and irregular migration flows have been destabilizing across much of
the Western world over the past several decades. This framework makes a clear promise
instead: the United States will protect people, but only to the extent that it can do so
honestly, safely, and sustainably.

By matching humanitarian commitments to real capacity, this system replaces chaos with
care and gives those it admits a genuine opportunity to thrive.

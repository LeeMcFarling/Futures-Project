# Research Integrity and Methodological Transparency Framework

## Purpose

Modern societies increasingly rely on evidence to make decisions. Governments use research to design public programs. Universities generate new knowledge. Companies sponsor studies supporting commercial products. Nonprofits evaluate interventions. Regulators assess safety, efficacy, and public risk.

Despite these differences, most evidence systems suffer from the same structural weaknesses:

- Publication bias
- Replication failures
- Undisclosed sponsor influence
- Methodological opacity
- Selective reporting
- Weak incentives for negative-result publication
- Incentives that reward novelty more than verification

The objective of this framework is not to centralize science.

The objective is to establish a common infrastructure for research transparency, methodological accountability, replication, certification, and evidence communication across domains.

This framework governs process integrity.

It does not determine scientific truth.

---

## Core Principle

Scientific disagreement is normal.

Methodological deception is not.

The framework distinguishes between:

- Honest failure
- Scientific disagreement
- Weak evidence
- Methodological deficiency
- Research misconduct
- Coordinated deception

Each category requires a different institutional response.

The purpose of the framework is to ensure that evidence claims can be trusted because the process used to generate them is transparent, auditable, and reproducible.

---

## Institutional Philosophy

The framework rejects two extremes.

The first is complete decentralization, where every institution invents its own standards and consumers are expected to determine credibility themselves.

The second is a centralized technocratic authority that attempts to adjudicate every scientific claim in every domain.

Neither model scales.

Instead, the framework distributes responsibility across specialized institutions while creating common standards for transparency, certification, and accountability.

The principle is:

Domain experts evaluate science. Independent institutions verify methods. DoDA certifies process integrity. The IMC builds the systems that make all of this operational at scale.

---

## Research Integrity Is Infrastructure, Not Regulation

The framework is not a regulatory regime. It does not tell scientists what
conclusions to reach, restrict which hypotheses may be tested, or adjudicate
scientific disputes. It creates the conditions under which conclusions can be
trusted.

This distinction matters politically and institutionally.

Roads are infrastructure. Payment rails are infrastructure. Identity systems are
infrastructure. Observability layers are infrastructure. Research integrity
infrastructure is the same category. The public sector is not intervening in
science — it is building the substrate that makes scientific outputs usable for
public decisions.

When a policymaker, a regulator, a journalist, or a citizen encounters a study,
they face a structural information problem: they cannot independently verify
whether the study was conducted as claimed, whether the reported endpoints were
pre-specified, whether the funding was disclosed, or whether contradictory findings
were suppressed. That information asymmetry is what allows low-quality or
manipulated evidence to circulate alongside high-quality evidence without
distinction.

Research integrity infrastructure resolves that asymmetry. Preregistration creates
a public record. Disclosure requirements create a verifiable audit trail.
Replication infrastructure creates an independent check. Certification creates
a legible signal. The government is not grading science. It is building the
plumbing that makes the grading visible.

This framing also clarifies what the framework does not do. It does not protect
any particular scientific consensus. It does not restrict heterodox inquiry.
It does not require unconventional hypotheses to pass an approval gate before
research begins. A researcher pursuing an unusual hypothesis through a transparent,
pre-registered, properly disclosed study is exactly what the framework is designed
to support. The integrity infrastructure creates no obstacle to that researcher
and a meaningful obstacle to the researcher who switches endpoints after seeing
results.

The governing doctrine is:

The government does not determine what is true. It creates the conditions under
which truth is determinable.

# Layer One: Research Transparency

Research integrity begins with transparency.

Studies intended to support public decisions, regulatory claims, public labeling systems, grant outcomes, or government evaluations should disclose:

- Study protocol
- Primary endpoints
- Secondary endpoints
- Statistical analysis plan
- Funding sources
- Material conflicts of interest
- Significant protocol amendments
- Publication status

Where applicable, studies should be preregistered before enrollment or implementation begins.

Preregistration establishes a timestamped record against which later claims can be evaluated.

The purpose is not to constrain legitimate adaptation.

The purpose is to make adaptation visible.

---

# Layer Two: Research Execution Integrity

Transparency alone is insufficient.

The second layer evaluates whether researchers followed the process they claimed to follow.

Research execution reviews may examine:

- Protocol compliance
- Endpoint consistency
- Statistical reproducibility
- Disclosure completeness
- Data integrity
- Publication compliance
- Sponsor influence

The relevant question is:

Did researchers do what they said they were going to do?

The relevant question is not:

Did researchers reach the preferred conclusion?

Null findings, failed hypotheses, and unexpected results remain legitimate scientific outcomes.

# Layer Three: Distributed Certification Architecture

Research integrity requires independent verification, but not centralized scientific control.

The framework separates four institutional functions.

## Research Generation

Evidence is generated by:

- Universities
- Research institutes
- Hospitals
- Government agencies
- Contractors
- Independent laboratories
- Private firms

These institutions perform the work.

They do not certify themselves.

## Research Evaluation

Domain-specific experts evaluate substantive findings.

Examples include:

- Economists
- Molecular biologists
- Chemists
- Physicians
- Engineers
- Defense analysts
- Educational researchers

Scientific expertise remains domain-specific.

No central authority determines scientific truth across all disciplines.

## Methodological Certification

Independent institutions verify methodological integrity.

This function may be performed by:

- Universities
- Replication centers
- Independent review panels
- Research integrity boards
- Accredited evaluators

The purpose is to determine whether methods, disclosures, and analyses satisfy published standards.

## Framework Certification

The Department of Data and Accountability certifies the integrity of the evaluation framework itself.

DoDA may certify:

- Evaluation templates
- Transparency standards
- Certification procedures
- Measurement architectures
- Reporting requirements
- Audit protocols

DoDA does not certify substantive scientific truth.

DoDA certifies whether the approved process was followed.

# Layer Four: Relationship to Existing Domain Authorities

The Research Integrity Framework does not sit above NIST, NIH, FDA, or equivalent
domain authorities. It plugs into them.

This distinction is load-bearing. A framework that positioned itself above existing
domain authorities would recreate the single-point-of-failure problem it is trying
to solve, add a bureaucratic routing layer to every study that crosses domain lines,
and ignore decades of specialized methodological expertise that already exists in
those institutions. None of that is the goal.

The goal is a common data infrastructure, a shared transparency standard, and a
certified measurement layer that makes the outputs of domain authorities comparable,
auditable, and legible across the system — without duplicating the substantive
review those authorities already perform.

### The Load-Bearing Distinction

DoDA certifies frameworks, not studies.

When a clinical trial runs through FDA's evidentiary process, DoDA does not review
the trial again. DoDA certifies that FDA's methodology standards satisfy the
framework's transparency and preregistration requirements. One audit of the auditor,
not a parallel audit of every output the auditor produces.

When an NIH-funded study moves through peer review and grant accountability
processes, DoDA does not conduct a shadow review. DoDA certifies that NIH's
grant review and publication infrastructure includes the required preregistration,
disclosure, and replication components the framework mandates. The study goes
through one process. DoDA certifies the process, not the study.

### Gap-Filling Certification Role

The domains where DoDA operates as the active first-layer authority are the
domains where no credentialed authority with published standards already exists.
DoDA fills institutional voids — it does not claim general jurisdiction over
unclaimed research territory.

Supplement claims. Wage modernization pilots. Housing intervention evaluations.
Education program assessments. Procurement reform evidence. Public health campaigns.
Regulatory sandbox outputs.

These are not domains where FDA or NIST or NIH has established review processes and
published methodology standards. They are gaps in the existing institutional
landscape — the places where evidence-dependent policy decisions are made without
the infrastructure that makes those decisions trustworthy. DoDA certifies the
framework and routing rules for those gaps. Independent institutions perform
substantive review under that framework.

Where a credentialed domain authority with published standards exists:
- DoDA certifies that the authority's framework meets transparency, preregistration,
  and disclosure standards
- The domain authority continues to perform substantive methodological review
- DoDA publishes the certification status and any framework-level findings
- Studies flow through one process

Where no such authority exists:
- DoDA certifies the framework and routing rules directly
- Independent institutions or replication centers perform methodological review
  under that framework
- DoDA certifies process integrity and publishes outcomes

### Named Domain Relationships

**NIST.** NIST maintains authoritative measurement standards across physical,
chemical, and technological domains. The framework uses NIST standards where they
apply rather than developing parallel measurement specifications. DoDA certifies
whether research claiming NIST-standard measurement actually applied those standards
correctly — a process-integrity question, not a measurement-science question. NIST
retains authority over what the standards are.

**NIH.** NIH funds and oversees the majority of publicly supported biomedical
research. The framework's preregistration, disclosure, and replication requirements
apply to NIH-funded research, and NIH's existing peer review and grant accountability
infrastructure is the primary operational mechanism. DoDA certifies that NIH's
infrastructure includes the required transparency components, monitors aggregate
publication compliance across the NIH-funded literature, and publishes system-level
findings. NIH retains scientific and programmatic authority.

**FDA.** FDA's evidentiary standards for drug and device approval represent the most
developed existing framework for pre-specified evidence requirements in the policy
ecosystem. The pharmaceutical reform stack's evidentiary sufficiency model draws
explicitly from FDA's certification clarity logic. For FDA-reviewed therapeutics, the
framework's evidence-stage labeling and post-market safety logic operates downstream
of FDA authorization, not in parallel to it. DoDA certifies whether FDA's framework
meets the RIF's transparency and replication standards at the institutional level.
FDA retains regulatory authority over individual products.

**Other domain authorities.** The EPA, USDA, DOE, CDC, and equivalent bodies
maintain domain-specific evidence standards relevant to their regulatory functions.
The framework's relationship to each follows the same pattern: DoDA certifies the
institutional framework, the domain authority retains substantive review authority,
and studies flow through one process rather than two.

### What This Prevents

The explicit relationship structure prevents three failure modes.

The first is DoDA scope creep. Without a defined boundary between framework
certification and substantive review, DoDA faces continuous pressure to expand into
domain-specific authority. Named domain relationships create a legible scope
boundary that is enforceable and publicly auditable.

The second is bureaucratic duplication. Researchers and institutions should not face
parallel review processes — one from their domain authority and one from DoDA — for
the same work. Framework-level certification happens once, at the institutional
level, not on a per-study basis.

The third is the bottleneck problem. If DoDA were positioned as a mandatory routing
point for individual studies, the volume of federally funded research would overwhelm
any institutional capacity DoDA could realistically maintain. Framework-level
certification scales without creating per-study routing requirements. The automated
compliance infrastructure handles study-level monitoring at scale without human
routing.

### Relationship to Automated Compliance Infrastructure and IMC

The monitoring function that makes this architecture operational — detecting
divergence between pre-registered protocols and published results across the entire
funded literature, flagging endpoint drift, identifying selective publication patterns
— cannot be performed at scale through per-study human review. That is a digital
systems problem, not a staffing problem.

DoDA specifies and certifies the national evidence observability layer. The IMC
builds the implementing systems to those specifications. Flags route to the relevant
domain authority's queue. DoDA publishes the aggregate dashboard. Domain authorities
handle escalations within their jurisdictions.

The automated compliance brief defines that infrastructure in detail. It is a
dependency of this section. The monitoring function described here is aspirational
without it.

# Layer Five: Dual-Key Certification

Certain categories of evidence require both operational evaluation and independent
verification. This framework establishes a dual-key certification model for those
categories.

Under this model:

Key One certifies the evaluation — whether the program was implemented as designed,
whether the data was collected as specified, and whether the outcome measures reflect
what was authorized.

Key Two certifies the methodology — whether the study design is sound, whether the
causal inference approach is appropriate, whether the statistical methods match the
pre-specified plan, and whether the conclusions are proportionate to the evidence.

Neither key is sufficient on its own. Operational evaluation without independent
methodological review is self-grading. Methodological review without operational
evaluation misses whether the program was actually delivered as designed.

### The Self-Grading Problem

This structure exists because government programs evaluated by the same institutions
responsible for their success have a structural conflict of interest. The incentive
to demonstrate success is embedded in the evaluation apparatus. That incentive does
not require bad faith to produce biased results — it operates through selection of
outcome measures, framing of comparison groups, timing of evaluation windows, and
interpretation of ambiguous findings.

The dual-key model resolves this structurally rather than through appeals to
evaluator integrity. DoDA certifies the evaluation architecture — the pre-registered
success criteria, the baseline construction methodology, the comparison framework,
and the reporting standards. An independent institution — a university, a replication
center, an accredited research integrity board — certifies the methodology. Neither
can unilaterally clear the other's domain.

### Scope of Application

Dual-key certification applies wherever government is evaluating its own programs
or programs it has substantially funded. This covers a substantial share of the
policy experiments the platform envisions:

- Regional wage modernization pilots
- Housing intervention evaluations
- Education program assessments
- Procurement reform evidence
- Workforce coordination program outcomes
- Healthcare transition pilots
- Manufacturing corridor viability assessments
- Benefit modernization pilots
- Criminal justice intervention evaluations

In each case, DoDA certifies the evaluation architecture before the program
launches — not after results are in. The success criteria, baseline methodology,
comparison framework, and expansion triggers are public records before any data
is collected. Retrospective reinterpretation of those criteria is detectable as
a deviation from the pre-registered evaluation plan.

### Eligible Second-Key Institutions

The second key may be held by:

- Universities and research institutes with relevant domain expertise
- Independent replication and evaluation centers
- Accredited research integrity boards
- Multi-institution consortia convened for a specific evaluation
- International peer institutions where appropriate

The second-key institution is selected before the program launches and cannot be
changed after results are known. Selection criteria include domain expertise,
absence of financial relationship with the program, and demonstrated methodological
capacity. The second-key institution publishes its methodology certification
independently, regardless of whether it confirms or disputes the operational
evaluation.

### Relationship to Platform-Wide Evidence Doctrine

Government may run experiments. Government may not grade its own homework.

This principle appears across the platform wherever public programs are evaluated:
the healthcare transition pilot framework, the Execution Corps performance-gated
expansion model, the PCA capital instrument outcome tracking, and the grant approval
reform framework's adaptive allocation model. The dual-key certification layer is
the institutional mechanism that makes that principle enforceable rather than
aspirational.

As the volume of dual-key evaluations grows, the procedures, eligible institution
lists, and certification standards will likely warrant a dedicated brief. The
architecture described here is the foundational doctrine. Implementation details
belong in that companion document.

# Layer Six: Replication Infrastructure

Replication is a public good that the current research system systematically
underproduces. Incentive structures reward novelty, priority, and positive results.
They do not reward verification, validation, or the publication of null findings.
The result is a literature that accumulates claims faster than it can test them —
and a policy environment that must make decisions against that accumulated
uncertainty.

This framework treats replication, validation, benchmark testing, negative-result
publication, and shared methodological infrastructure as first-class research
outputs rather than support activities. They are not the cleanup crew for primary
research. They are the mechanism through which primary research becomes trustworthy
enough to act on.

### Replication as Infrastructure

The analogy to physical infrastructure is precise. Road networks do not produce
the economic activity that flows over them — they enable it. Replication
infrastructure does not produce the scientific findings that depend on it — it
makes those findings durable. A finding that has not been independently replicated
is a hypothesis. A finding that has survived independent replication under varied
conditions and populations is evidence. The distinction is not semantic. It is the
difference between a policy built on a single underpowered study and a policy built
on a robust evidentiary foundation.

Replication infrastructure includes:

- Independent study replication
- Benchmark validation against reference datasets
- Negative-result publication and archiving
- Methodological verification and stress testing
- Reanalysis of primary datasets under alternative assumptions
- Cross-population testing to establish generalizability
- Shared measurement platforms and standardized protocols that reduce
  replication cost across the field

### Relationship to Grant Reform

The Grant Approval Reform framework establishes a protected replication and
validation funding bucket as a mandatory portfolio allocation — not a discretionary
add-on that gets cut when budgets tighten. This framework is the demand side of
that funding: it defines what replication infrastructure is, why it is necessary,
and what activities qualify.

The protected bucket in grant reform exists because replication will not be funded
at sufficient scale by any other mechanism. Primary researchers do not fund
replication of their own work. Industry sponsors do not fund replication that might
undermine their products. Novelty-rewarding peer review does not route funding
toward validation. Public grant systems are the only institution positioned to fund
replication at the scale the evidence base requires. The RIF and grant reform
together close that gap: one defines the infrastructure, the other funds it.

Replication findings — including null results and failures to replicate — are
treated as positive scientific outputs eligible for the same publication and
recognition pathways as primary findings. A well-conducted replication that fails
to confirm an influential result is exactly as valuable as one that confirms it.
The value is in the honest answer, not in the direction of the result.

Replication is not volunteer work. Replication is infrastructure. It is funded
as such.

# Layer Seven: Machine-Readable Research Infrastructure

Research integrity should be operationalized through standardized digital systems rather than bespoke paperwork.

The framework establishes machine-readable research packets.

Research packets may contain:

- Protocol identifiers
- Study registration data
- Endpoints
- Statistical plans
- Funding disclosures
- Conflict disclosures
- Measurement standards
- Results
- Amendments
- Adverse-event reporting
- Claim requests where applicable

Research packets should be submitted through standardized APIs.

Automated systems should evaluate:

- Registration status
- Protocol consistency
- Disclosure completeness
- Measurement-standard compliance
- Evidence-template compliance
- Publication obligations

Human review is reserved for:

- Novel claim categories
- Methodological disputes
- Audit flags
- Safety concerns
- High-impact decisions

The governing principle is:

Standardize the rails. Automate the obvious. Escalate the unusual.

# Layer Eight: Measurement Standards

The framework distinguishes between measurement standards and evidence certification.

Specialized institutions remain responsible for domain-specific standards.

Examples may include:

- Measurement institutes
- Professional standards bodies
- Clinical standards organizations
- Scientific societies
- Technical certification organizations

Where authoritative standards already exist, the framework should use them rather than duplicate them.

Research integrity infrastructure should leverage existing expertise rather than centralize it.

# Layer Nine: Audit and Review

Research integrity audits may be triggered by:

- Undisclosed sponsor influence
- Endpoint switching
- Selective publication
- Statistical irregularities
- Data integrity concerns
- Material protocol deviations
- Disclosure failures

Audit findings may include:

- Methodology verified
- Disclosure deficiency
- Protocol deviation
- Statistical misrepresentation
- Certification deficiency
- Research misconduct referral

Audit findings primarily affect:

- Certification status
- Evidence eligibility
- Disclosure requirements
- Future audit frequency

They do not automatically trigger enforcement.

---

# Escalation Ladder

The framework establishes a graduated escalation model.

## Rung 1: Monitoring

Automated systems identify anomalies, inconsistencies, missing disclosures, publication gaps, and unusual patterns.

Monitoring creates review priorities.

Monitoring is not enforcement.

## Rung 2: Certification Review

Potential issues are reviewed against published standards.

Certification status may be updated.

Additional disclosures may be required.

## Rung 3: Standing Disclosure

Organizations discovering material issues may utilize standing disclosure pathways.

Early disclosure receives more favorable treatment than later concealment.

The framework is designed to encourage correction rather than concealment.

## Rung 4: Administrative Enforcement

Repeated violations, disclosure failures, certification abuse, or significant methodological misconduct may trigger administrative remedies.

## Rung 5: Coordinated Deceptive Practices Referral

Only conduct involving:

- Coordination
- Internal contradiction
- Sustained pattern
- Material impact
- Concealment

may be referred under the Coordinated Deceptive Practices framework.

Scientific disagreement alone never triggers CDE review.

# Layer Ten: Evidence Communication

Evidence generated under this framework should be translated into standardized,
publicly legible summaries whenever findings are used to support public decisions.

This is not a requirement about evidence generation or evaluation. Those functions
are governed by the layers above. This is a requirement about the final step in the
evidence chain — the translation from technical finding to public communication.

### The Communication Problem

The persistent failure in modern public evidence is not a shortage of research.
It is a translation failure. Findings are released through opaque PDFs, competing
press releases, and narratives shaped by whoever has the most media access. A
molecular biologist, an economist, and a journalist looking at the same study
walk away with different characterizations of what it showed, what its limitations
were, and how much confidence it warrants. The public — who has no specialized
training and no time to read primary literature — receives the downstream residue
of that conflict, which is usually the most marketable claim stripped of every
qualification that made it honest.

This is not a problem of public ignorance. It is a problem of institutional design.
The evidence system currently has no standardized output format that conveys
what a study actually claimed, how it was conducted, who funded it, whether it has
been replicated, and how much weight it should carry — in a form accessible to
someone who is not a domain specialist.

Standardized evidence communication is the layer that closes that gap.

### The Doctrine

Evidence generated under this framework, when used to support public decisions,
regulatory determinations, product claims, or policy recommendations, must be
accompanied by a standardized evidence summary that is:

**Factual, not persuasive.** The summary conveys what the evidence shows. It does
not advocate for a conclusion, a product, or a policy. The communication function
is translation, not marketing.

**Legible without domain expertise.** A non-specialist should be able to read the
summary and understand what was studied, how it was studied, what was found, how
confident the finding is, who funded the research, whether it has been independently
replicated, and what is still unknown. Technical detail belongs in the underlying
documents. The summary conveys the signal, not the methodology.

**Standardized in format.** Summaries follow a common structure so that a member
of the public can compare evidence across products, programs, and claims using the
same framework. A supplement evidence label and a pilot outcome dashboard should
convey analogous information in analogous formats, even though the underlying
research is different in kind.

**Uncertainty-honest.** The summary explicitly states what is not known, what the
evidence does not establish, and where the finding may not generalize. Overstated
confidence is a form of miscommunication. Stating that a finding is preliminary,
that replication is pending, or that long-term effects are unknown is not a
weakness in the communication — it is the communication doing its job.

**Auditable.** The summary is linked to the underlying evidence record — the
preregistration, the study report, the certification status, the replication
record. The public can verify the summary against the underlying record. The
summary is not a closed document.

### Minimum Summary Fields

Standardized evidence summaries include the following fields, adapted to context:

**What was studied** — the specific claim, intervention, product, or program
evaluated, and the population or context to which the finding applies.

**How it was studied** — the study type, whether pre-registered, sample size,
duration, and any material design limitations.

**What was found** — the primary outcome, the direction and magnitude of the
effect, and the statistical confidence level in plain language.

**Funding and independence** — who funded the research, whether the researchers
have disclosed conflicts of interest, and whether the study was independently
conducted or sponsor-funded.

**Replication status** — whether the finding has been independently replicated,
is awaiting replication, or has failed to replicate in independent studies.

**Evidence maturity** — the evidence stage, using the platform's standard
classification (preliminary, replicated, mature) or domain-equivalent.

**What is not known** — explicit statement of limitations, populations not
studied, long-term effects not yet evaluated, and open questions.

**Where to find more** — a link or reference to the full study, the preregistration
record, the certification status, and any available replication findings.

### Existing Instantiations

The evidence communication doctrine is already operating across the platform in
domain-specific forms. This layer establishes the unifying philosophy those
forms instantiate.

The **Supplement Evidence Label** conveys ingredient verification status, evidence
stage, claim scope, known risks, and monitoring status for supplement products.
A consumer can assess a supplement's evidentiary standing without reading the
underlying studies.

The **Therapeutic Evidence Label** conveys evidence stage, approved indication and
population, duration of evidence, measured benefits, observed risks, and monitoring
status for pharmaceutical products. A patient and clinician can assess where a
drug sits on the evidence maturity spectrum.

**Pilot outcome dashboards** published through DoDA translate program evaluation
findings into publicly readable performance summaries — what the pilot tested,
what the pre-registered success criteria were, what the observed outcomes were,
and whether expansion thresholds were met.

**Grant outcome reporting** under the grant reform framework publishes what publicly
funded research produced, in a form accessible to the public that funded it.

**DoDA certification outputs** are public records that convey whether an
evaluation, framework, reporting system, or evidence-communication artifact met
the framework's process integrity standards — not whether the science is correct,
but whether the process was sound.

Each of these is an evidence summary adapted to its domain. The standardized
fields above define what all of them share.

### What Evidence Communication Is Not

Evidence communication is not propaganda for the framework's conclusions. The
doctrine does not require that evidence summaries favor any particular policy
direction, product category, or research finding. A summary that conveys a null
result, a failed replication, or a finding that undermines a prior claim is
functioning exactly as intended.

Evidence communication is not a gatekeeping function. The requirement to produce
a standardized summary does not restrict what research may be conducted, what
products may be sold, or what policies may be adopted. It requires transparency
about the evidentiary basis for claims. Entities that cannot produce an honest
evidence summary for a claim they are making should not be making the claim.

Evidence communication is not a substitute for domain expertise. Summaries make
evidence legible to a general audience. They do not replace specialized review,
clinical judgment, or expert interpretation. They are the starting point for an
informed public, not the endpoint for domain specialists.

# What This Framework Is Not

This framework is not:

- A national science ministry
- A centralized truth authority
- A substitute for peer review
- A substitute for scientific debate
- A mechanism for determining correct conclusions
- A bureaucracy designed to slow research

The framework exists to make trustworthy research easier to identify and methodological deception harder to conceal.

# Summary

The Research Integrity and Methodological Transparency Framework establishes a common infrastructure for trustworthy evidence generation across government, academia, industry, and civil society.

Research remains decentralized.

Expertise remains domain-specific.

Verification becomes systematic.

Replication becomes funded.

Transparency becomes standardized.

Methodological integrity becomes auditable.

The framework's governing doctrine is:

Domain experts evaluate science. Independent institutions verify methods. DoDA certifies process integrity. The IMC builds the systems that make it operational.

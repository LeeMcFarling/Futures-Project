## Purpose

This brief explains how the Execution Corps creates a **stable, predictable, and time-bounded interface** between industry and government.

It is written for private-sector operators, investors, and firms that must decide whether engaging with the state is viable, legible, and survivable. It does not redefine the Execution Corps instrument and does not propose sector-specific policy.

The goal is not to promise favorable outcomes, but to make government interaction **rule-bound rather than discretionary**.

---

## The Problem Industry Faces Today

Industry interaction with government is often characterized by uncertainty and risk management rather than coherent regulation and stable governance. 

Common failure modes include:
- shifting or non-sensical regulatory requirements
- undefined discretionary approval timelines
- informal communication and escalation channels.
- discretionary scope expansion
- pilots that quietly become permanent
- vendor relationships that persist without clear justification

These conditions impose hidden costs, discourage entry, and reward political navigation over operational competence. They are execution failures, not market failures.

---

## What Changes Under the Execution Corps Model

The Execution Corps replaces ad hoc interaction with a **standardized execution instrument**.

The core shift is structural:
- from negotiation to pre-declared rules
- from discretion to spend envelopes
- from relationships to published artifacts
- from permanence to planned exit

Firms do not negotiate outcomes. They operate within known constraints.

---

## Predictable Entry Conditions

Under the Execution Corps model, firms know in advance:

- what problem is being addressed
- what activities are in scope
- what activities are out of scope
- how long the engagement lasts
- who has decision authority
- how disputes are resolved

Execution Corps deployments publish:
- a defined mandate
- a fixed duration
- a spend envelope with contingency
- a scope-change taxonomy
- decision clocks and escalation paths

This allows firms to price execution risk without pricing political uncertainty.

---

## No Subsidies, No Champions, No Soft Budget Constraints

The Execution Corps is explicitly **not** a subsidy mechanism.

It does not:
- provide open-ended financial support
- backstop losses
- select national or regional champions
- guarantee demand beyond defined pilots
- socialize failure through continued funding

State involvement is limited to:
- clearing execution bottlenecks
- building shared infrastructure
- defining standards and interfaces
- de-risking initial entry where markets fail to form

Competition, pricing, and survival remain private-sector functions.

---

## How Pilots Work — and How They End

Pilots under the Execution Corps are **comparative execution tests**, not marketing programs or procurement loopholes.

Each pilot includes:
- explicit success criteria
- explicit failure criteria
- fixed evaluation windows
- predefined termination conditions

Pilots are designed to conclude. Success may lead to scaling or handoff. Failure leads to termination and teardown.

There is no implicit path to permanence.

---

## Contracting and Vendor Interaction

Vendor interaction is standardized and constrained.

Key rules include:
- no repeated approvals by the same individuals
- no scope expansion through informal amendments
- no midstream rule changes
- standardized procurement artifacts
- published escalation decisions

These constraints reduce favoritism, reputational risk, and post-hoc reinterpretation of agreements.

---

## Contingency and Risk Allocation

Execution risk is handled through **pre-authorized contingency**, not renegotiation.

Contingency:
- is approved upfront
- is typed by scope category
- is exhausted before escalation
- cannot expand total spend authority

Firms are not expected to absorb uncertainty created by unclear governance.

---

## Exit and Handoff

Execution Corps deployments are designed to end.

When objectives are met or pilots conclude:
- authority sunsets
- teams disband
- artifacts persist (standards, code, templates)
- markets continue without state presence

State exit is an explicit success condition.

---

## What This Is Not

The Execution Corps is not:
- industrial policy by subsidy
- public–private capture
- permanent procurement reform
- a state-run enterprise
- a standing partner in private markets

It exists to solve execution failures and then leave.

---

## Why This Reduces Risk for Industry

The primary benefit to industry is **predictability**.

Firms gain:
- clearer timelines
- bounded exposure
- lower compliance overhead
- reduced political risk
- more reliable capital planning

Predictability matters more than generosity.

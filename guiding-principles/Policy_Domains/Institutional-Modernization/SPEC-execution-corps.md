## 1. Purpose and Scope

This document specifies the basic structure of the **Execution Corps**. A reusable institutional instrument for public-sector execution.

The Execution Corps is designed to translate democratically authorized objectives into operational reality under tightly bounded authority, cost, and time constraints. It exists to solve execution failure, not to originate policy, arbitrate values, or replace representative governance.

This specification is **domain-agnostic**, **branch-agnostic**, and **outcome-neutral**. Domain policies, sector strategies, and political priorities are defined elsewhere and may instantiate this instrument without modifying it.

---

## 2. Definition of the Instrument

The Execution Corps is a **time-bounded execution body** with delegated authority to deliver a specific outcome within a pre-authorized envelope.

It is:
- not a standing regulatory agency
- not a contractor or vendor
- not a task force or advisory committee
- not a policymaking body

It is an **execution instrument** with:
- a defined mandate
- a defined authority envelope
- a defined budget and contingency
- a defined lifecycle
- a default sunset

The Execution Corps owns *how* something is delivered, not *what* is decided.

---

## 3. Problems This Instrument Solves

The Execution Corps addresses structural execution failures common to modern governance:

- fragmentation of delivery authority across agencies
- diffusion of accountability for outcomes
- overreliance on permanent contractors
- inability to terminate failing programs
- excessive distance between policy design and operational reality
- politicization of execution decisions

These failures are sytemic in nature, and not due to the supossed 'moral failings' of any one party. 

---

## 4. Execution Paths

The Execution Corps has two formally distinct execution paths.

### 4.1 Standing Execution Corps

The first structure is a standing Execution Corps body which are used for **long-lived functions** requiring continuity, capital stewardship, or repeated execution.

Characteristics:
- permanent (or long term renewable charter)
- narrow, well-defined mandate
- fixed, non-renewable leadership terms
- slow rotation, high institutional memory
- explicit renewal requirements

Primary risk:
- long-term capture or mandate creep

Mitigation:
- leadership term limits
- cooling-off rules
- scope immutability
- renewal-by-default prohibition

### 4.2 Mission-Bound Execution Corps

Mission-Bound Execution Corps are used for **discrete, time-bounded problems**.

Characteristics:
- single mandate
- fixed duration
- explicit sunset
- teardown by default
- authority expires automatically

Primary risk:
- overreach or operational chaos

Mitigation:
- narrow scope
- typed contingency
- strict envelope enforcement
- mandatory dissolution

---

## 5. Authorization and Legitimacy

An Execution Corps may be authorized by:

### 5.1 Legislative Authorization
Congress may authorize an Execution Corps by statute, specifying:
- objectives
- legal constraints
- spend ceiling
- contingency ceiling
- duration
- renewal conditions

### 5.2 Executive Authorization
The Executive may authorize an Execution Corps by lawful executive instrument, limited to:
- delegated statutory authority
- operational coordination
- pilots and process reform
- non-rights-altering execution

The **instrument constraints are identical** regardless of authorizing branch. Legitimacy source differs; authority limits do not.

---

## 6. Spend Envelopes and Contingency

Every Execution Corps must operate within a **pre-authorized spend envelope**.

The envelope consists of:
1. Base authorized cost
2. Explicit contingency ceiling
3. Escalation trigger threshold

Contingency is treated as **delegated decision space**, not discretionary funding.

Rules:
- contingency must be exhausted before escalation
- unused contingency automatically sunsets
- contingency drawdowns are logged and published
- no midstream expansion of total spend authority

---

## 7. Scope Change Taxonomy

Scope changes are permitted **only** within a closed taxonomy.

Permitted scope types:
- Type A: latent conditions (e.g., unknown utilities)
- Type B: safety or statutory compliance
- Type C: equivalent substitutions
- Type D: force majeure

Each type has:
- predefined cost caps
- predefined schedule impact allowances
- predefined approval paths

Any change outside the taxonomy is out of scope and requires reauthorization.

---

## 8. Execution Mechanics

Execution Corps operate under the following defaults:

- pilot-first implementation
- parallel pilots for contested approaches
- artifact-based outputs (code, templates, standards)
- explicit success metrics
- kill authority treated as competence
- termination as an expected outcome

Programs do not persist by inertia.

---

## 9. Co-Deployment and Field Authority

Execution Corps may co-deploy personnel from relevant agencies.

Rules:
- co-located teams
- delegated signature authority
- parallel review by default
- fixed decision clocks
- escalation paths defined in advance

Execution authority is exercised **at the point of friction**, not remotely.

---

## 10. Internal Counter-Power Structure

No single role holds unilateral authority.

Each Execution Corps includes:
- an Execution Lead (delivery, sequencing)
- an Envelope / Compliance Lead (scope, legality)
- an Escalation Arbiter (tie-break only)

Controls:
- multi-signature thresholds
- misaligned rotation schedules
- public logging of escalations
- prohibition on repeated co-deployment

---

## 11. Transparency and Instrumentation

Execution Corps must publish standardized artifacts:
- spend execution
- schedule adherence
- contingency drawdowns
- pilot outcomes
- termination rationales
- postmortems

Reporting is descriptive, not narrative.

---

## 12. Sunset, Teardown, and Knowledge Capture

Sunset is the default.

Rules:
- automatic expiration unless renewed
- mandatory teardown
- artifact retention
- prohibition on mandate expansion in place

Knowledge persists; institutions do not.

---

## 13. Federalism and Opt-In Compatibility

- state and municipal participation is voluntary
- standards are uniform
- delegated execution agreements are supported

No jurisdiction is compelled to participate.

---

## 14. Explicit Non-Goals

The Execution Corps is not:
- a subsidy mechanism
- a vehicle for industrial champion selection
- a permanent outsourcing proxy
- a technocratic override of democratic authority
- a standing reform task force

---

## 15. Interfaces (Non-Normative)

This specification does not define:
- domain policy
- political priorities
- electoral reforms
- sector strategy

Those are defined elsewhere and may instantiate this instrument without modifying it.

---

## Guiding Maxim

Democratic institutions decide *what* is authorized.  
Execution Corps decide *how* it is delivered.  
The public decides whether it worked.

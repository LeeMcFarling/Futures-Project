# National Referendum Mechanism: Contingent Legislation and Public Ratification

## Purpose

This brief establishes the National Referendum Mechanism — a constitutionally grounded instrument through which Congress enacts law subject to a defined public ratification condition. It is not a replacement for representative governance. It is a structured pathway through which the public can exercise direct authority on questions where representative institutions have demonstrably failed to act on clear popular mandates, and through which Congress can credibly commit to reforms that benefit from democratic ratification before they take full operative effect.

The mechanism is designed as a **circuit breaker for institutional capture** — specifically the failure mode in which broadly popular reforms cannot pass through representative channels because the institutions responsible for enacting them are controlled by the interests that benefit from the status quo.

---

## Constitutional Basis

The National Referendum Mechanism rests on the contingent-legislation doctrine established in *Field v. Clark*, 143 U.S. 649 (1892). In *Field*, the Supreme Court held that Congress may enact law subject to a defined triggering condition — including a condition whose occurrence depends on future facts or actions — without violating the bicameralism and presentment requirements later elaborated in *INS v. Chadha*, 462 U.S. 919 (1983), and *Clinton v. City of New York*, 524 U.S. 417 (1998).

The load-bearing distinction is this: **the national ratification vote is not a legislative act. It is the triggering condition that Congress itself wrote into law.** Lawmaking remains entirely with Congress and the President. The public vote activates or sunsets law already enacted through full bicameral process and presented to and signed by the President. No post-enactment legislative act triggers or blocks operative effect — the triggering condition is the public vote, which Congress defined in advance and which operates by the terms of the statute itself.

*Chadha* and *Clinton* establish that Congress may not reserve a unilateral post-enactment veto over law it has already passed — a legislative veto exercised without presentment. The NRM does not create a legislative veto. It creates a public ratification condition that Congress wrote into its own statute before enactment. The President signed a law that includes that condition.

The NRM is further supported by Congress's broad authority under Article I to structure its own legislative processes, including conditional enactment, severable title architecture, and defined sunset provisions. Conditional enactment structures — including federal grant conditions, trigger-based regulatory activation, and contingent effective dates — are well-established features of federal statutory practice. The NRM applies the same logic to public democratic ratification as the triggering condition.

This is a plausible and carefully structured extension of contingent-legislation doctrine, not settled law. A national public ratification vote as a statutory triggering condition has not been tested at this scale, and hostile courts will scrutinize it. The mechanism is designed to fit within the doctrine as established — but that design should be understood as a serious constitutional argument, not a guarantee of judicial approval. The severability architecture and the fallback re-enactment pathway exist precisely because that challenge is foreseeable.

### The Sunset Mechanism

A title that does not receive the required affirmative ratification vote by its defined deadline **sunsets and expires**. It does not become dormant law available for future administrative activation. It ceases to exist as operative law. Re-enactment requires returning to Congress through ordinary bicameral process.

This sunset structure is constitutionally and politically load-bearing. A title with a hard ratification deadline and automatic expiration on failure is not a shelf of enacted legislation waiting for a future administration to trigger without public consent. It is law with a built-in democratic deadline.

If the mechanism is successfully challenged in court, Congress retains the demonstrated public mandate from the ratification vote to re-enact each approved title as standalone legislation through ordinary process. A successful legal challenge changes the enactment path — it does not erase the vote or the mandate it represents.

## The Problem This Solves

The United States has a well-documented class of broadly popular reforms that consistently fail to pass through representative channels. Campaign finance reform, democratic integrity measures, term limits, and stock trading prohibitions for elected officials have polled at sustained supermajorities across partisan lines for years. They do not pass because the legislators who would have to pass them benefit from the status quo they would change.

This is not a failure of democracy in principle. It is a predictable output of an institutional design that gives self-interested incumbents control over the rules governing their own accountability. The representative system has no internal correction mechanism for this specific failure mode.

The NRM creates that correction mechanism — within the constitutional framework rather than around it, preserving congressional primacy over lawmaking while giving the public a structured pathway to activate reforms that entrenched interests have blocked.

### Why Unconstrained Direct Democracy Fails

Pure direct democracy — unlimited citizen initiative with simple majority outcomes and no deliberation architecture — has a poor track record at scale. The failure modes are well-documented.

California's initiative process demonstrates capture at the initiation stage: well-funded interests control the signature-gathering infrastructure, write ballot language designed to confuse, and produce outcomes that frequently contradict the actual preferences of the voters who passed them.

Brexit demonstrates failure at the design stage: a binary question on a non-binary reality, no minimum participation threshold, no supermajority requirement for a constitutional-level change, no accountability for a deliberate misinformation campaign, and no defined implementation pathway — producing a generational political rupture from a 52-48 result on 37% turnout.

Switzerland and Australia demonstrate what works: high information requirements, genuine deliberation windows, independent question framing, tiered thresholds calibrated to the significance of the change, and clear implementation pathways defined before the vote. The NRM is designed around those features and against the failure modes that don't work.

## Part I — Enactment Structure

### How the Mechanism Works

Congress drafts and passes legislation containing one or more severable titles. Each title is complete — it contains findings, definitions, substantive provisions, enforcement mechanisms, and a ratification condition specifying the activation vote required and the ratification deadline. The President signs the legislation. The Act is enacted at that point through full constitutional process.

A severable title becomes **operative** only if it receives the required affirmative ratification vote on or before its defined deadline. A title that receives the required vote becomes operative on the effective date written into that title. A title that does not receive the required vote by its deadline sunsets and expires.

This structure allows Congress to pass comprehensive reform legislation while giving each severable element its own democratic ratification requirement. Voters evaluate each title on its own merits. Approval of one title does not carry others. Rejection of one title does not defeat others.

### Severable Title Architecture

Each title must be genuinely severable — the operative effect of every other title must be independent of whether any given title is activated or sunsets. Titles may not be drafted to create functional interdependencies that make rejection of one title defeat others by design. The FEIC certifies severability as part of the certification process.

### Congressional Trigger

Either house of Congress may initiate NRM legislation by introducing a bill containing the required severable title architecture. Passage follows ordinary bicameral process. The NRM does not create any expedited pathway or bypass of ordinary legislative procedure. It is a structural feature built into legislation that passes through normal channels.

The ratification deadline for each title must be set at least 180 days after enactment, providing the mandatory deliberation window before any public ratification vote.

### Citizen Petition Pathway — Phase 2

A citizen petition pathway is reserved for Phase 2 implementation, pending deployment of SSI cryptographic identity infrastructure under the Voter ID and Registration Reform framework.

Upon SSI deployment, a verified digital petition signed by a defined threshold of eligible voters — recommended at 5% of total eligible voters distributed across a minimum of 30 states with at least 2% representation in each — may require relevant congressional committees to hold public hearings on the petition subject within a defined window. The petition pathway requires Congress to consider; it does not require Congress to pass, and it does not place a title on the ratification ballot. Ballot placement requires Congress to subsequently enact NRM legislation through ordinary bicameral process. The citizen petition is an input to that process, not a bypass of it.

The citizen pathway is withheld until SSI infrastructure is operational to prevent the signature-gathering capture dynamic that has colonized state initiative systems.

## Part II — Ratification Vote Design

### Scheduling

Each title's ratification vote is held on a regularly scheduled federal Election Day — either unified Primary Day or general Election Day established under the Election Day Reform framework. Ratification votes may not be scheduled as standalone elections. No more than three severable titles from any single piece of NRM legislation may appear on the same Election Day ballot.

### Threshold Tiers

Ratification thresholds are calibrated to the significance and reversibility of the proposed change. The participation condition and the passage threshold are treated as distinct requirements, for reasons explained below.

**On participation floors.** Rather than requiring a percentage of *eligible* voters to participate in the ratification question specifically — a standard that can be defeated by voter fatigue or ballot length even on a high-turnout election — the participation condition attaches to the *election* on which the ratification vote appears. A ratification vote may only appear on an election that meets a defined minimum overall turnout floor for that tier. If the election itself meets the turnout floor, the ratification question's outcome is determined by votes cast on that question. This avoids the failure mode where a majority of participants support ratification but the title fails because not enough eligible voters participated in the ratification item specifically, while still ensuring the overall democratic context has sufficient legitimacy.

**On geographic distribution.** The "majority of states" standard creates a small-state veto dynamic: a reform supported by 65% of voters nationally can fail if that support is concentrated in large states. The more defensible standard is passage in states representing at least 50% of total eligible voting population — this tests genuine geographic breadth without allowing low-population states to structurally veto reforms supported by a clear national supermajority. This is a design choice that prioritizes anti-concentration over small-state protection, and should be understood as such.

---

**Tier 1 — Statutory Policy Changes.** Questions enacting, repealing, or modifying federal statute on matters of ordinary policy.
- Passage threshold: Simple majority of votes cast on the ratification question (50% + 1)
- Election participation floor: The election on which the title appears must achieve at least 50% overall eligible voter turnout
- Geographic distribution: None required
- *Design note: The 50% election turnout floor means Tier 1 questions are effectively limited to general Election Day ballots in most states. This is intentional — the NRM should be a high-legitimacy instrument used sparingly, not a mechanism for passing ordinary policy on low-turnout primary days.*

**Tier 2 — Structural Governance Reforms.** Questions altering the structure, composition, or operating rules of federal institutions — including electoral system changes, term limits, campaign finance structures, and congressional operating rules.
- Passage threshold: 55% of votes cast on the ratification question
- Election participation floor: The election on which the title appears must achieve at least 55% overall eligible voter turnout
- Geographic distribution: Passage must be achieved in states collectively representing at least 50% of the national eligible voting population
- *Design note: The population-weighted geographic standard tests genuine national breadth without creating a small-state veto. A reform supported by 60% of voters in large and mid-sized states will satisfy this requirement; a reform whose support is geographically narrow will not, regardless of national aggregate vote share.*

**Tier 3 — Constitutional Mandate Establishment.** Questions establishing a public mandate for constitutional amendment or fundamentally altering the relationship between branches. Tier 3 ratification results do not bypass Article V — they establish a documented public mandate that Congress may use to advance constitutional amendment through normal Article V process.
- Passage threshold: 60% of votes cast on the ratification question
- Election participation floor: The election on which the title appears must achieve at least 60% overall eligible voter turnout
- Geographic distribution: Passage must be achieved in states collectively representing at least 60% of the national eligible voting population

The applicable tier for each title is declared in the legislation and certified by the FEIC. Congress may not assign a Tier 1 classification to a provision whose actual operative effect is structural governance reform.

### Failed Participation Floor

If a ratification vote appears on an election that fails to meet the participation floor for its tier, the title fails regardless of the yes/no margin on the ratification question itself. The title may be resubmitted for the next eligible Election Day without restarting full certification, provided the underlying legislation has not been amended. Resubmission to a different election tier — for example, moving from a primary ballot to a general election ballot to access a higher-turnout context — is permitted and encouraged where the primary election failed the floor.

## Part III — Certification Process

All severable titles must pass independent certification before their ratification vote proceeds. Certification is not advisory. A title that fails certification may not appear on the ratification ballot in its current form.

### The Referendum Certification Panel

Each certification is conducted by an independently constituted **Referendum Certification Panel** of nine members:

- 3 members selected by judicial lottery from a standing pool of retired federal appellate judges who have not heard cases directly related to the subject matter in the prior five years
- 3 members selected by the National Academy of Sciences from relevant policy, legal, and subject-matter expertise pools
- 3 members selected by the Government Accountability Office from career civil service leadership with relevant domain expertise

Panel members serve for the duration of a single certification process only. No member may serve on more than two panels in any five-year period. No member with a financial, employment, organizational, or direct advocacy interest in the title's outcome may serve. All recusal determinations are published.

The Panel has no authority to block a title on policy grounds. Its authority is strictly limited to certification of process integrity.

### Certification Standards

**Scope and Severability.** Each title must address a single, severable policy question with a definable, specific meaning. Omnibus provisions bundling multiple distinct policy changes into a single ratification question are rejected and must be separated. The Panel certifies that the title has a clear implementation pathway and that its operative effect is independent of other titles.

**Plain Language.** The Panel produces three mandatory public documents: a neutral single-sentence ratification question at an eighth-grade reading level; a plain-language summary of what an affirmative vote activates and what a negative vote or sunset means in concrete terms, stated without advocacy; and a Voter Guide containing the full title text, the DoDA impact assessment, the CBO fiscal analysis, primary arguments for and against from certified proponent and opponent groups, and a factual claims registry. The sponsoring congressional chamber has no control over the final ratification question wording or plain-language summary.

**Implementation Pathway.** The title's implementation pathway must be reviewed by the CBO for fiscal impact, DoDA for outcome measurement feasibility, and relevant executive agencies for operational feasibility. The default implementation pathway — what happens if Congress fails to act within the mandatory response window after successful ratification — must be specific, legally grounded in existing statutory or regulatory authority, and operationally executable without further legislative action.

**Conflict of Law.** The ULRR sandbox environment tests each title against existing federal law, identifying conflicts, preemption issues, and constitutional questions before the vote. Identified conflicts are disclosed in the Voter Guide.

**Tier Classification.** The Panel certifies that the tier classification assigned by Congress accurately reflects the operative significance of the title. A provision classified as Tier 1 whose actual effect is structural governance reform is reclassified before certification proceeds.

## Part IV — Campaign Architecture and Anti-Capture

### Spending Architecture

Ratification campaigns are subject to a layered spending framework structured to survive constitutional scrutiny through severability. Each layer is independently defensible; the more contested layers are drafted as severable provisions so that a successful First Amendment challenge to one element does not defeat the others.

The most legally durable layer is the **absolute foreign-money ban**. Foreign spending on a direct domestic legislative process carries the government's strongest available interest and faces no serious First Amendment protection. Foreign spending of any kind is prohibited and subject to criminal penalty.

The second layer is **real-time disclosure**. All spending by all entities on ratification campaigns is disclosed through the Democracy Fund Portal — donor identity, amount, date, and purpose — within 24 hours of expenditure. No anonymous spending above $50 is permitted. Disclosure requirements are well-established under *Buckley v. Valeo* and subsequent doctrine and are the most legally secure element of this framework.

The third layer is **equal public matching** for certified proponent and opponent groups, selected through a transparent public process administered by the FEIC. Matching is equal regardless of which side has more private funding. This does not restrict speech — it amplifies the underfunded side.

The fourth layer is **contribution limits to certified campaign committees**. No single entity — individual, organization, corporation, union, or PAC — may contribute above a defined ceiling to any certified ratification campaign committee. Contribution limits are more constitutionally durable than direct expenditure caps under existing doctrine.

The fifth layer, drafted as an explicitly severable provision, is **direct expenditure caps** on independent spending on ratification campaigns. The constitutional argument for expenditure caps on direct legislative votes — that the government's interest in preventing capture of a direct democratic process is sufficiently compelling, and that ballot-measure spending is more analogous to lobbying than to candidate speech — is contested but not frivolous. Courts may or may not accept it. If this provision is struck, the foreign-money ban, disclosure requirements, public matching, and contribution limits remain operative by operation of the severability clause. The brief acknowledges this as the riskiest constitutional surface in the framework.

### Public Matching

Certified proponent and opponent groups — selected through a transparent public process administered by the FEIC — receive equal public matching funds from the National Democracy Fund for voter education and campaign materials, regardless of which side has more private funding.

### Factual Claims Accountability

A **Ratification Factual Claims Panel**, operating independently of the Certification Panel, reviews complaints about verifiable factual accuracy in ratification campaign materials within a 72-hour window. Its authority covers only verifiable factual claims — specific numbers, existing laws, documented effects — not opinions, predictions, or policy arguments. Materially false claims trigger mandatory correction notices. Repeated or egregious violations are referred to the Federal Democracy Commission.

## Part V — Deliberation Window and Voter Education

### Mandatory 180-Day Window

A minimum of 180 days must elapse between certification and the ratification vote. During the window: the Voter Guide is distributed to every registered voter by mail and digitally; public forums are held in every congressional district hosted by independent civic organizations with balanced representation of yes and no perspectives; the DoDA impact assessment is published and updated as new information emerges; all campaign spending is disclosed in real time; and the Factual Claims Panel operates continuously.

### Voter Guide Distribution

The Voter Guide is mailed to every registered voter no later than 60 days before the ratification vote; published in full on a dedicated public website; available in all languages spoken by more than 1% of the eligible voting population in any jurisdiction; distributed through public libraries, post offices, and government service centers; and produced in accessible formats for voters with disabilities. It is produced and distributed entirely by the independent Certification Panel.

## Part VI — Implementation Architecture

### Implementation Window

Ratification triggers a **statutory implementation window** of 180 days during which Congress may enact alternative implementing legislation consistent with the ratified mandate. Congress retains full authority over implementation detail — sequencing, appropriations, operational design, agency assignment. Absent such legislation within the window, the certified default implementation pathway activates by operation of the statute.

This framing is deliberate. Congress cannot be compelled by a prior Congress to legislate in a manner that raises bicameralism or presentment concerns — a "must pass" formulation would invite that challenge. The implementation window preserves congressional option while ensuring that inaction has defined consequences, rather than allowing the mandate to be buried through strategic non-response.

### Default Implementation Pathway

The default implementation pathway activates automatically by operation of the statute if Congress does not enact alternative implementing legislation within the 180-day window. It is defined in full before the ratification vote, published in the Voter Guide, and legally grounded in existing executive or regulatory authority. Voters know before they vote what happens if Congress does not act.

The default pathway operates under four explicit guardrails. First, it must be **ministerial or bounded in scope** — it executes what the ratified title specifies; it does not exercise discretionary policy judgment beyond that mandate. Second, it may **not create new policy** beyond what the ratified title directly authorizes — it cannot use implementation as a vehicle to expand the mandate's scope. Third, it may **not appropriate funds** beyond amounts already authorized by Congress in prior appropriations — it operates within existing fiscal authority. Fourth, it is **severable from the ratification result** — if a court finds the default pathway invalid or enjoins it, the ratification result and the 180-day congressional implementation window remain operative. A successful challenge to the default mechanism does not void the public mandate or prevent Congress from enacting alternative implementing legislation.

### Congressional Override

Congress may modify or supersede a ratification-mandated default implementation by passing alternative implementing legislation consistent with the ratified mandate, by a supermajority of both chambers proportional to the ratification tier. Legislation that functionally negates the ratified outcome does not satisfy this requirement.

## Part VII — Scope Limitations

The following categories are excluded from the NRM:

- Annual budget appropriations and specific spending levels
- Tax rate schedules and individual tax provisions
- Treaty ratification and foreign policy commitments
- Declaration of war or military authorization
- Individual rights adjudication — questions that would remove or restrict the constitutional rights of specific groups are subject to constitutional review before certification and will not be certified if they impose disparate burdens on protected classes
- Questions whose implementation requires ongoing administrative discretion that cannot be defined in advance
- Criminal law expansions creating new felony liability — such provisions require at minimum Tier 2 classification and are subject to heightened certification review

The criminal law exclusion warrants specific explanation. Direct popular votes on criminalization are a well-documented failure mode of unconstrained direct democracy — they tend to produce carceral populism, target politically disfavored minorities under neutral-sounding language, and create enforcement regimes that outlast the political conditions that produced them. The NRM is not a vehicle for crowd-sourced criminalization. Criminal law expansions that genuinely belong in the NRM framework — as structural governance questions with broad popular mandates — must clear the higher Tier 2 threshold and survive heightened certification scrutiny, including explicit constitutional review for disparate impact on protected classes.

## Part VIII — Relationship to Representative Democracy

The NRM is explicitly designed as a complement to representative democracy, not a replacement for it. Congress retains full legislative authority outside the NRM pathway, implementation authority within the mandate of ratified titles, authority to initiate or repeal ordinary legislation without NRM, and oversight authority over all executive implementation.

The mechanism cannot be used to strip individual rights, override constitutional protections, or bypass judicial review. It resolves the specific failure mode of institutional capture on popular questions — not majoritarian override of constitutional principles.

The NRM's relationship to the broader democratic integrity stack is direct. The Representation Fairness brief uses NRM-style contingent legislation to activate the state adoption incentive package for proportional representation methods. The Party Formation framework's anti-cartel certification mandate operates similarly. The NRM is both a standalone instrument and the activation architecture underlying other components of the democratic integrity stack.

## Red-Team Stress Tests

**Wealthy interest captures a ratification campaign.** The foreign-money ban removes the most dangerous funding vector entirely. Real-time disclosure through the Democracy Fund Portal makes domestic funding sources and amounts visible before the vote, allowing voters to evaluate who is funding which side. Equal public matching funds ensure the opposing side has a funded voice regardless of private funding asymmetry. Contribution limits to certified campaign committees constrain single-actor dominance without raising the most contested First Amendment questions. If the severable expenditure cap provision survives constitutional challenge, it adds a further ceiling on total independent spending. The plain-language ratification question and Voter Guide are written entirely by the independent Certification Panel — framing advantage is neutralized at the ballot level even if one side's campaign is better funded. Residual risk: the severable expenditure cap may not survive First Amendment challenge; technically true but misleading statements cannot be corrected by the Factual Claims Panel even if they are identified.

**Complexity manipulation.** The Scope and Implementation certification test requires "yes" to mean something specific and achievable. A title concealing incompatible implementation versions fails certification. The default implementation pathway must be defined and published before the vote. Residual risk: a sufficiently skilled drafting team might pass the scope test while concealing meaningful implementation ambiguity.

**Low-turnout capture.** Minimum participation thresholds calibrated to ratification tier mean the title fails if overall turnout is insufficient regardless of the yes/no margin. Ratification votes appear only on regular election ballots. Residual risk: participation in ratification questions specifically may be lower than overall turnout even on a regular election day.

**Congressional implementation evasion.** The default implementation pathway activates automatically if Congress fails to act within 180 days. If Congress acts but passes legislation contradicting the mandate, the default pathway remains available as a legal baseline and judicial review of consistency with the mandate is available. Residual risk: determining whether implementing legislation is "consistent with the mandate" involves genuine interpretive ambiguity — the implementation pathway definition must be specific enough to provide a clear legal baseline.

**Minority rights targeting.** Questions removing or restricting constitutional rights of specific groups are subject to constitutional review before certification. Questions imposing disparate burdens on protected classes fail certification. Residual risk: the boundary between legitimate policy questions and rights-restricting questions is not always facially clear — this is the highest-stakes failure mode and requires the most conservative certification design.

**Calendar manipulation.** The 180-day deliberation window prevents any title from being rushed to a ratification vote within a compressed political window. The mandatory congressional response requirement with default implementation pathway means a ratified title cannot be quietly shelved — it produces binding consequences regardless of subsequent political conditions. Residual risk: a chamber might call a ratification vote knowing it will fail, for political messaging purposes.

## Open Questions

Should the Certification Panel have authority to require redesign of a title, or only to certify or reject as submitted? What is the appropriate judicial review pathway for certification decisions — should there be an expedited federal court track with defined timelines? Should ratified titles be subject to a defined sunset period requiring reaffirmation, or treated as permanent until legislatively reversed? Should the threshold tiers be fixed in statute or adjustable by the Certification Panel based on assessed operative significance? How should the mechanism handle titles that interact with existing international treaty obligations? Should there be a cooling-off period preventing resubmission of a failed title — and if so, how long? What happens if the default implementation pathway is itself challenged in court and enjoined before activation?

# State Electoral System Standardization: Federal Integrity Floor, State Electoral Choice

## Purpose

This brief establishes the federal democratic integrity floor and the state electoral choice architecture. It defines what every legitimate election system must protect, what states may choose within that floor, how election systems are certified and enforced, and how the transition from the current fragmented landscape to a structured federal architecture is sequenced. It is the foundation on which party formation, regional caucus structure, presidential selection, and candidate accountability all operate.

---

## The Problem

American elections are fragmented across state systems in ways that reflect historical accident, partisan advantage, and administrative inertia rather than democratic design. States differ in primary type, ballot access requirements, redistricting rules, voter registration systems, ballot design, election timing, candidate disclosure standards, and certification procedures.

Some variation is legitimate and valuable. States should be able to experiment with different democratic structures, learn from each other, and adapt to local political cultures. That is federalism functioning as intended.

Variation becomes illegitimate when it allows incumbents or dominant parties to manipulate district lines, suppress competition, obscure candidate information, or design election rules that protect themselves from voters. That is not federalism. It is entrenchment wearing federalism's clothes.

The current system produces three structural failures that are not accidents of local preference but predictable outputs of the incentive structure governing those who design the rules.

The first is partisan self-entrenchment. When politicians draw their own district lines, they choose their voters rather than voters choosing their politicians. Safe districts breed ideological extremity, weaken accountability, and break the relationship between public preference and legislative power. Both parties have exploited this wherever they have held power. It is a structural problem requiring a structural solution.

The second is primary distortion. Low-turnout primaries controlled by activated partisan minorities systematically select candidates who do not represent the broader electorate. The gap between the primary electorate and the general electorate is not incidental — it is the mechanism through which extremity is selected and rewarded.

The third is ballot and candidate opacity. Voters are routinely asked to choose among candidates without standardized information about governing philosophy, institutional posture, funding sources, or performance records. The information asymmetry systematically advantages incumbents and well-funded challengers over candidates with better records and more representative platforms.

---

## Design Principle

**Standardize democratic guarantees. Decentralize electoral method.**

The federal government defines what every legitimate election system must protect. States retain authority to choose how they structure candidate selection and election mechanics within that floor.

States choose the model. Federal law prevents democratic failure modes.

---

## The Federal Democratic Integrity Floor

All federal elections, and state elections receiving federal election administration support, must comply with a baseline democratic integrity standard enforced by the Federal Electoral Integrity Commission established below.

### Universal Voter Access

Every eligible citizen must be able to register and vote without facing administrative burdens designed to suppress participation. The state bears the burden of ensuring every eligible citizen has a qualifying form of identification before any strict matching requirement activates. Automatic registration at point of government contact is the default enrollment mechanism.

This integrates directly with the Voter ID and Registration Reform framework, which establishes universal enrollment and SSI identity modernization as the access and security backbone for this system. The SSI credential pathway is the long-term identity standard. Existing government-issued ID is the bridge during the transition period.

### Nonpartisan Districting

No state may use partisan actors, legislative majorities, or incumbent-controlled processes to draw congressional or state legislative districts. Each state must establish an independent redistricting process satisfying federal conditions: commission independence with conflict-of-interest restrictions, public map data in machine-readable format, open-source or publicly auditable mapping tools, public hearings distributed across diverse regions, minority representation protection, compactness and community-preservation criteria, partisan fairness testing using certified metrics including efficiency gap and partisan symmetry, a judicial challenge pathway with defined timelines, and emergency fallback maps where states fail to produce compliant maps before an election.

### Transparent Ballot Design

Every ballot and voter guide must make clear: what office is being elected, what electoral system is being used, how votes are counted, how many candidates may be selected or ranked or scored, what party or parties nominated or endorsed each candidate, and where to access the candidate's standardized public profile under the Candidate Nutrition Label framework. Voters must not need a political science degree to understand the ballot they are completing.

### Auditability and Public Data

Every state must publish election data in a standardized machine-readable format maintained in the Unified Law and Regulation Repository. Required data includes precinct-level results, turnout by voting method, ballot rejection rates, wait times, provisional ballot rates, registration coverage, candidate ballot-access records, redistricting files, audit outcomes, recount outcomes, and public election complaints and resolution status.

All elections must produce auditable paper or equivalent verifiable records. Standardized recount and audit procedures must be available and judicially reviewable within defined timelines.

### Ballot Access Fairness

Ballot access requirements must be designed to ensure genuine democratic competition. Requirements must be reasonable, uniformly applied, and judicially reviewable. Signature requirements, filing fees, and party recognition thresholds must not be structured to make entry prohibitively difficult for new parties or independent candidates with genuine public support.

### Anti-Discrimination

No election rule may impose disparate burdens on voters based on race, ethnicity, language, disability, income, or any other protected characteristic. Voting materials must be available in all languages spoken by more than one percent of the eligible voting population in any jurisdiction.

## The State Electoral Choice Menu

Within the federal integrity floor, states may choose from a federally certified menu of election and primary systems. States may propose additional models through a certification process. The certifying body does not make political judgments about which system is preferable — it certifies whether the proposed system meets access, fairness, auditability, and voter-comprehension standards.

### Cardinal Voting Systems — Preferred Class

Cardinal voting systems allow voters to express preference intensity rather than just preference order. They produce winners with genuinely broad support rather than plurality winners or least-objectionable consensus candidates. Arrow's impossibility theorem applies to ordinal systems specifically — cardinal systems escape its most constraining implications. Cardinal systems are the preferred class of methods and anchor the certified menu.

**Score voting** allows voters to rate each candidate on a defined scale — typically zero to five — and the candidate with the highest average or total score wins. It captures preference intensity, does not require strategic bullet voting, and produces winners with demonstrated cross-electorate appeal.

**STAR voting — Score Then Automatic Runoff** adds a runoff between the top two score winners, using the ranked preference implied by each voter's scores to determine the finalist. This addresses the mediocre consensus candidate problem that pure score voting can occasionally produce and is the recommended default for federal elections where stakes justify the modest additional complexity.

**Approval voting** allows voters to check every candidate they find acceptable. The candidate approved by the most voters wins. It is the simplest cardinal method to explain and administer and is particularly well suited to primary elections with large candidate fields.

### Ranked Choice Voting — Available with Advisories

Ranked choice voting is available as a certified option for states that prefer an ordinal method. It is meaningfully better than first past the post — it eliminates spoilers and plurality winners and produces more representative outcomes in most conditions.

However it carries known limitations that states should understand before selecting it: it can filter out high-intensity minority preferences, performs poorly with very large candidate fields, and can produce counterintuitive results in certain competitive configurations. The California top-two jungle primary experience also demonstrates that ordinal primaries can accidentally disfavor reformist or non-establishment candidates relative to well-resourced incremental ones. States selecting ranked choice should document their awareness of these tradeoffs as part of the certification process.

### Other Certified Options

Nonpartisan top-four or top-five primaries followed by a general election using an approved scoring method are available and encouraged as a structure that maximizes general election participation and reduces primary distortion.

Party caucus conventions with defined public participation standards, transparency requirements, and auditability requirements are available as a primary selection method for states whose political cultures support them.

### First Past the Post — Transition to Sunset

First past the post enters sunset status for federal elections. States currently using first past the post may continue during a defined transition period while selecting and implementing a certified alternative. The transition period is set at two federal election cycles — sufficient time to design, pilot, and implement a new system. After the transition period, first past the post is no longer a certified option for federal elections.

There is no democratic integrity argument for retaining a system that routinely produces winners opposed by a majority of voters, structurally advantages two-party duopoly, and creates strong spoiler effects suppressing competitive entry. The transition period respects implementation realities without preserving a system whose failures are well documented.

## The Federal Electoral Integrity Commission

Election system certification, party recognition compliance, caucus formation review, and electoral administration standards are the mandate of the Federal Electoral Integrity Commission (FEIC) — a new independent institution established specifically for this purpose.

This function is explicitly separated from the Department of Data and Accountability. DoDA's mandate is policy outcome measurement and trigger integrity across the full governance stack. Asking DoDA to also certify electoral systems and compile candidate record data would make it simultaneously too powerful and too exposed to capture. The FEIC handles the electoral integrity domain. DoDA handles policy outcome measurement. The two institutions reference each other's outputs but neither controls the other.

### FEIC Composition and Appointment

The FEIC is composed of nine members serving staggered seven-year nonrenewable terms. Appointment is through a multi-body process designed so that no single administration or congressional majority can capture the institution through a single appointment cycle.

Three members are nominated by a judicial lottery from a pool of retired federal appellate judges who have not served on politically sensitive election-related cases in the prior five years. Three members are nominated by the National Academy of Sciences from the fields of electoral systems design, political science, and public administration. Three members are nominated by the Government Accountability Office from career civil service leadership with expertise in election administration.

All nominees are subject to Senate confirmation. No more than four members may have been registered with the same political party at any point in the prior ten years. Members are subject to strict financial disclosure, conflict-of-interest restrictions, and a five-year post-service cooling off period prohibiting work for any political party, campaign, or political advocacy organization.

### FEIC Mandate

The FEIC certifies state electoral systems against the federal integrity floor, investigates compliance complaints, issues binding compliance determinations, administers the party recognition framework, reviews caucus formation filings, compiles candidate public record data for the Nutrition Label framework, and publishes annual electoral system performance reports.

The FEIC has no authority over election outcomes, vote counts, or candidate eligibility determinations beyond what is specified in statute. It certifies processes and compiles public records. It does not determine who won.

### Enforcement Pathway

States that fail certification receive technical assistance first. Continued non-compliance triggers federal election administration funding consequences. Persistent non-compliance is referred to the Department of Justice for judicial enforcement. Emergency fallback rules activate only when an election would otherwise proceed under a noncompliant structure with no viable alternative.

## International Technical Advisory Framework

The United States is undertaking the most ambitious democratic modernization in its history. Several allied democracies and international institutions have decades of operational experience with the specific systems being built here — cardinal voting methods, multi-party coalition government, independent electoral commissions, and digital civic identity infrastructure. Engaging their expertise during the design and build-out phase is not a concession of democratic legitimacy. It is competent institutional design.

### Advisory Mandate

The international technical advisory framework operates as a stress-test and advisory body during the design, pilot, and early implementation phases of the electoral reform package and the SSI voting identity infrastructure. It is explicitly non-binding. All design decisions remain with American democratic institutions. Advisory findings are published in full and made available to Congress, the FEIC, and the public.

The advisory framework is particularly focused on three areas where international experience is most directly applicable: cardinal voting method implementation and transition management, independent electoral commission design and anti-capture architecture, and digital identity infrastructure for civic participation.

### Convening Structure

The UN Electoral Assistance Division serves as convener and coordination body. Primary technical input comes from the European Union's election observation and digital governance expertise, with particular emphasis on countries that have successfully implemented multi-party systems, coalition government, and independent electoral commissions at federal scale.

Germany, the Netherlands, New Zealand, and Australia contribute expertise on multi-party system design and coalition government mechanics. Estonia contributes expertise on digital national identity infrastructure directly applicable to the SSI framework build-out. Taiwan's digital democracy team — which has built the most sophisticated open-source participatory democracy infrastructure currently operating at national scale — contributes expertise on the Digital Democracy Platform's mandate methodology and participatory signal architecture.

The Commonwealth Electoral Network contributes comparative independent commission design experience. The Carter Center contributes election observation methodology applicable to the domestic audit and certification framework.

### Scope Limitations

The advisory framework has no authority over American electoral decisions, no veto over design choices, and no ongoing role after the defined advisory period concludes. It reviews draft framework designs, provides written technical assessments, identifies known failure modes from comparable international implementations, and recommends design modifications for consideration. American institutions decide.

The advisory period covers the design and initial pilot phases — anticipated at three to five years. After that period the framework sunsets unless Congress explicitly reauthorizes it for a defined additional purpose.

### Political Framing

This engagement should be framed precisely: the United States is inviting the world's leading electoral systems experts to stress-test designs before implementation — the same way any serious infrastructure project invites external technical review. This is particularly appropriate given the previous administration's demonstrated willingness to exploit ambiguities in democratic infrastructure. Building the new system under international technical scrutiny creates a legitimacy record that is very difficult for future bad actors to dismiss.

## Implementation Phasing

### Phase 1 — Statutory Foundation

Achievable by federal statute without constitutional amendment. Establishes the FEIC, the federal integrity floor, the state electoral choice menu, party recognition framework, candidate label infrastructure, campaign finance integration, election day holiday, and congressional caucus recognition. First past the post enters transition period. International advisory framework convenes.

### Phase 2 — State System Pilots

States select certified alternatives to first past the post. Cardinal voting method pilots run in willing jurisdictions. FEIC certifies initial state systems. SSI identity infrastructure begins rollout. DoDA publishes comparative electoral system performance metrics. International advisors publish first technical assessment.

### Phase 3 — Presidential Architecture

State-level presidential caucus structures pilot in willing states. Electoral College allocation alternatives evaluated. National Popular Vote Interstate Compact pathway assessed. Constitutional convention authorization considered through National Referendum Mechanism Tier 3 process.

### Phase 4 — Full Implementation

First past the post transition period concludes. Presidential selection architecture operational. SSI voting identity framework operational. International advisory period concludes.

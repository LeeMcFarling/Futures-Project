# Presidential Selection Architecture: Regional Credentialing and Three-Round National Mandate

## Purpose

This brief defines the three-round presidential selection architecture. It establishes how regional caucuses credential candidates through party primaries, how a national primary filters the field to the top finalists, and how a general election produces a president with layered democratic mandate. It connects the National Capability Tour and Execution Corps talent pipeline directly to presidential credentialing, defines the independent candidate qualification pathway, and establishes the phased constitutional implementation pathway.

---

## The Problem

The American presidential selection system has three compounding failures that the regional caucus architecture is designed to correct.

**Early-state distortion.** Iowa and New Hampshire have exercised outsized influence over presidential selection for decades despite being among the least demographically representative states in the country. Candidates who perform well in these states gain momentum, funding, and media attention that compounds through subsequent contests. The nominee is effectively shaped by a small unrepresentative slice of the electorate before most Americans have meaningfully engaged.

**Fragmented-field plurality dynamics.** When many candidates compete in a single national primary using first past the post or even ranked choice, plurality dynamics can allow a candidate opposed by a majority of primary voters to accumulate delegates while opposition splits among multiple alternatives. The 2016 Republican primary demonstrated this failure mode clearly. The problem is structural, not personal.

**The execution-record gap.** Presidential candidates are selected primarily on communication skill, ideological positioning, fundraising capacity, and media profile. Demonstrated capacity to actually deliver complex outcomes at scale — the core competency of the job — is almost never a primary credential. The selection process is optimized for campaigning rather than governing.

The three-round architecture addresses all three failures simultaneously. It eliminates early-state distortion by running all caucus primaries simultaneously on a unified Primary Day. It solves the fragmented-field problem by compressing the national primary field before the national scoring round. And it makes execution record a primary credential by designing the caucus system around regional interest coalitions with direct knowledge of and investment in their candidates' demonstrated performance.

## The Congressional Development Stage

Presidential caucus blocs do not form in a vacuum. Congressional caucus blocs are the natural proving ground where the coalitions that eventually coordinate presidential campaigns first demonstrate their coherence, test their compatibility, and build the working relationships that make a credible joint presidential bid possible.

As state parties establish themselves and win congressional seats, their members arrive in Washington and begin the practical work of legislative coalition-building. Members with overlapping governing philosophies naturally align. They form congressional caucus blocs, file joint coalition agreements, vote together on shared priorities, and accumulate aligned mandate contribution records. The candidate nutrition label makes this alignment visible in real time — the Tier 1, Tier 2, and Tier 3 data for every sitting member is publicly accessible, and coalition patterns emerge from that data before any formal presidential declaration has been made.

By the time a presidential cycle approaches, the congressional record has already done much of the field-compression work. A Rust Belt labor congressional caucus bloc and an Appalachian energy congressional caucus bloc that have spent two legislative cycles voting together, filing joint coalition agreements on industrial policy, and building shared mandate contribution records have demonstrated the alignment that their presidential caucus bloc formation will formalize. The presidential caucus bloc is the ratification of a proven relationship between state parties whose members have already governed together — not a speculative pre-commitment assembled for electoral convenience.

This has a direct effect on presidential field size. The five-to-twelve candidate range the presidential architecture targets is the organic output of however many durable congressional caucus blocs have formed and demonstrated coherence over prior cycles. Congressional caucus blocs that cannot sustain themselves through a legislative cycle are unlikely to sustain a viable presidential campaign. The congressional stage filters the field before the presidential primary does.

### Implementation Sequencing

This logic produces a natural and strategically sound implementation sequence. The statutory foundation — FEIC, party recognition framework, congressional caucus bloc rights, candidate nutrition label — is established first. State parties form and begin winning congressional seats. Congressional caucus blocs develop over one to two full legislative cycles. Presidential caucus blocs emerge from demonstrated congressional alignment. The first full presidential election under the complete architecture follows.

This sequencing also reduces the threat salience of the reform at each stage. State party recognition reform is a states' rights argument. Congressional caucus bloc rights are an internal chamber governance question. By the time presidential caucus blocs form and the constitutional layer is addressed, the system has already demonstrated itself in practice and built a constituency of elected officials who won under it and have strong incentives to defend it. The reform builds its own political support as it goes rather than requiring overwhelming upfront coalition to overcome all opposition simultaneously.

## Round 1 — Party Primary and Caucus Selection

### Structure

On unified Primary Day — established under the Election Day Reform framework as a federal public holiday one year before the general election — every voter participates in their own party's primary.

A Rust Belt Labor party member in Youngstown, Ohio votes among the candidates who have declared for the Rust Belt Labor caucus. A progressive party member in Cincinnati votes among the candidates who have declared for the Urban Progressive caucus. An agricultural party member in rural Kansas votes among the candidates who have declared for the Plains Agricultural caucus. Each voter uses the single approved scoring method declared in their caucus's formation agreement to rate the candidates in their party's primary.

Each caucus aggregates its member state party results using the weighting formula defined in its caucus agreement and produces one endorsed candidate — the candidate who scored highest across the presidential caucus bloc's member state primaries. This candidate becomes the bloc's endorsed presidential candidate and advances to the national primary.

The party primary round compresses the national presidential field to the number of viable presidential caucus blocs — anticipated between five and twelve nationally — plus any independent candidates who have cleared the national qualification threshold defined below.

### Voting Method Consistency

All member state parties within a caucus must use the same approved scoring method for the presidential primary round, as declared in the caucus formation agreement filed with the FEIC. A state may use a different approved method for its own state and congressional elections while using the caucus-declared method for the presidential primary round specifically. Methodological consistency within the caucus is required to produce an aggregate result that is meaningful rather than an artifact of incompatible systems.

### Execution Record as Primary Credential

The caucus primary is where execution record credentialing happens. A candidate seeking the Rust Belt Labor caucus endorsement cannot win on platform alone. The caucus's member state parties — whose members have direct experience with industrial policy, manufacturing corridor development, workforce coordination, and regional infrastructure — evaluate candidates on demonstrated capacity to deliver in their regional context.

Candidates with records in the National Capability Tour, the Execution Corps, or regional public service have DoDA-certified outcome records that can be evaluated against the caucus's stated credential criteria. These records appear in the candidate's Tier 2 nutrition label under the public execution record category, separated from legislative record but equally visible. Candidates without regional execution records must make a stronger platform case to overcome that disadvantage. The system does not require an execution record — it rewards one.

This connection is the mechanism by which the Futures Project's talent pipeline feeds into democratic selection. An NCT alumnus who ran housing overlay coordination across a multi-state region and has DoDA-certified delivery outcomes has verifiable regional credentialing that a national party figure parachuting in from Washington does not. The caucus system formalizes what that difference should mean to voters.

## Round 2 — National Primary

### Structure

Approximately three to four months after unified Primary Day, a national primary is held in which all voters participate regardless of party affiliation. The ballot shows all caucus-endorsed candidates plus any qualified independent candidates. Voters score or rate across the full field using STAR voting — Score Then Automatic Runoff — as the national standard for this round.

STAR voting proceeds in two stages. In the scoring stage, voters rate each candidate on a zero-to-five scale. In the automatic runoff stage, the two candidates with the highest total scores advance to a head-to-head comparison in which the candidate preferred by more voters wins. The result is a finalist who both scores broadly well across the electorate and wins a genuine head-to-head majority.

The top two or three candidates by national primary result advance to the general election. The specific number — two or three — is a design parameter resolved through the constitutional convention process, with three as the preferred option since it preserves more genuine choice in the general election while remaining administratively manageable.

### The Deliberation Window

The three-to-four month window between the party primary and the national primary is a structural deliberation feature, not incidental scheduling. During this window, caucus-endorsed candidates build cross-regional support, demonstrate national governing capacity, signal coalition preferences and potential running mate choices, and subject their execution records and platforms to national scrutiny. This is when genuine cross-regional coalition building happens.

The deliberation window also creates a natural evaluation checkpoint. A candidate whose caucus coalition proves narrow, whose conduct becomes disqualifying, or whose platform proves untenable in national exposure has time to withdraw before the national primary rather than after it.

### What the National Primary Tests

The national primary is where cross-regional appeal is evaluated. A candidate who won their regional caucus convincingly but has no appeal outside their home region will score low among voters from other caucuses and will not advance. A candidate who won a smaller caucus but scores highly across multiple regions can overtake a larger caucus winner.

Cardinal voting is essential here because it allows voters to express genuine cross-caucus preferences. A Rust Belt Labor caucus voter can give high scores to both their caucus champion and a candidate from a different caucus they find compelling — expressing authentic preference intensity across the full field rather than being forced into a single choice that eliminates useful signal.

### Independent Qualification Pathway

Candidates who did not participate in any caucus primary may qualify for the national primary ballot by demonstrating verified small-donor support from a minimum number of verified individual donors distributed across a minimum number of states — thresholds set at the federal level by the FEIC. The small-donor breadth requirement tests genuine national support without being gameable by a single wealthy patron.

The independent threshold should be set meaningfully higher than caucus-endorsed candidates, reflecting that caucus endorsement carries its own democratic legitimacy from the party primary round. Independent qualification is a genuine alternative pathway, not an easy bypass of the caucus system.

Independent candidates must file qualification documentation at least sixty days before the national primary. Qualification guarantees ballot access. It does not guarantee competitive performance.

## Round 3 — General Election

### Structure

The top two or three finalists from the national primary appear on the general election ballot on the first Tuesday after the first Monday in November — the federal public holiday established under Election Day Reform. Voters score or rate the finalists using STAR voting. The winner is the president-elect.

### Layered Mandate

A president elected through this system carries a mandate with three distinct democratic layers.

The first layer is regional credentialing. The candidate won their caucus primary among voters with direct knowledge of their regional execution record and governing philosophy. This is a community of informed evaluators, not a national brand recognition contest.

The second layer is national primary validation. The candidate demonstrated cross-regional appeal by scoring well among voters from outside their home caucus in a competitive multi-candidate national scoring round.

The third layer is general election majority. The candidate won a general election among all voters using a method that produces genuine majority support rather than plurality victory.

This layered mandate is substantially more robust than what the current system produces — where a candidate can win a party nomination through a fragmented primary and a general election through an Electoral College majority that does not correspond to a popular majority.

### The Vice Presidency

General election finalists declare their running mates before the general election ballot is finalized. Voters choose governing teams, not individual candidates. The running mate selection signals coalition intent — which other caucus's priorities and credentialed leadership the winning candidate intends to incorporate into their administration.

Running mate declarations are required at least thirty days before the general election. A finalist who becomes unable to serve after the running mate declaration but before the election has a defined succession procedure that preserves the coalition signal without forcing a full restart.

## Constitutional Pathway and Phased Implementation

### Statutory Phase — Immediate

The following elements can be implemented by federal statute without constitutional amendment and should proceed immediately:

State electoral system certification and the FEIC are statutory. Party recognition framework and caucus formation filing requirements are statutory. Candidate nutrition label infrastructure is statutory. Campaign finance integration is statutory. Election day holiday and unified Primary Day calendar are statutory. Congressional caucus recognition and rights are achievable through chamber rules and statute. The national primary structure and voting method recommendations for federal elections can be implemented by statute in most respects.

### Electoral College — The Constitutional Obstacle

The Electoral College presents the primary constitutional obstacle to the full presidential selection architecture. The three-round structure works best when the Electoral College allocates votes proportionally to reflect the national primary results rather than operating through the current winner-take-all state allocation that was designed for a two-candidate system.

Three pathways exist for addressing the Electoral College.

The National Popular Vote Interstate Compact pathway allows states to commit their electoral votes to the national popular vote winner once states representing a majority of electoral votes have joined. This is achievable without constitutional amendment and can implement much of the intended effect of the national primary architecture for the general election round.

State-level proportional allocation laws allow individual states to allocate their electoral votes proportionally rather than winner-take-all. Maine and Nebraska already do this. Expanding proportional allocation to more states reduces the distortion between national primary results and Electoral College outcomes without requiring a constitutional amendment.

Constitutional amendment through the Article V process — requiring two-thirds of both chambers and three-quarters of states — is the cleanest long-term solution but the most difficult to achieve.

### Constitutional Convention — Preferred Vehicle

The scoped constitutional convention authorized through the National Referendum Mechanism's Tier 3 pathway is the recommended vehicle for resolving the Electoral College question and ratifying the full presidential selection architecture with constitutional clarity.

The convention's scope is defined in the authorizing resolution and is limited to: the approved menu of certified electoral methods for federal elections, the presidential selection architecture including the three-round structure and approved voting methods, the Electoral College allocation mechanism, and the party recognition and caucus formation framework. Delegates are explicitly not authorized to address any constitutional question outside this defined scope.

Any amendment proposed by the convention must still be ratified by three-quarters of state legislatures or ratifying conventions under Article V. The convention establishes the design. Ratification establishes the legal force.

The phasing is important. The statutory phase proceeds immediately and demonstrates the system working in practice. The constitutional convention is convened after the statutory phase has produced observable results — making the convention's task ratifying a demonstrated system rather than authorizing an untested one.

## Open Questions

Should the national primary produce two or three finalists for the general? What is the right independent qualification threshold — and should it be higher for national primary access than for general election access? Should STAR voting be mandated nationally for the presidential selection rounds by statute, or is it recommended within the certified menu? How should the system handle a caucus-endorsed candidate who becomes unable to continue between the party primary and the national primary — does the caucus's second-highest scorer advance, or does the caucus hold an emergency selection? What is the right deliberation window length between party primary and national primary — three months or four?

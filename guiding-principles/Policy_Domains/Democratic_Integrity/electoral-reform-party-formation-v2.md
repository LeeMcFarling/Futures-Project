# State and Regional Party Formation and Recognition

## Purpose

This brief defines how new political parties form, achieve ballot recognition, and organize into regional caucuses for presidential primary purposes. It is the political market layer of the electoral reform package — establishing conditions under which genuine democratic competition can emerge without producing shell parties, billionaire toys, or spoiler vehicles designed to split opposition votes.

---

## The Problem

The American two-party system is not the inevitable product of voter preference. It is the product of institutional design choices that make entry prohibitively difficult for new parties while making exit from existing parties politically costly for individual members. Ballot access laws in most states are explicitly designed to protect the Democratic and Republican parties from competition. Recognition thresholds, signature requirements, and filing deadlines create barriers that require either massive financial resources or extraordinary organizational capacity — resources that established parties have and new parties almost never do.

The result is not a market of political ideas. It is a cartel. Two national brands jointly designed the system that excludes alternatives and jointly benefit from maintaining it. Voters who find neither brand acceptable — and polling consistently suggests this describes a large and growing share of the electorate — have no legitimate channel for their political energy.

The solution is not to abolish parties. Parties are legitimate and useful democratic institutions. The solution is to lower entry barriers enough that the political market can actually function, while preventing the equally serious problem of a ballot cluttered with vanity projects, single-issue protest vehicles, and shell entities created by wealthy interests to split opposition votes.

The design challenge is calibration. The metric of genuine support — breadth of civic engagement, not depth of donor pockets — must determine who qualifies.

## State Party Formation and Recognition

### Recognition Requirements

A political organization seeking recognized party status in a state must satisfy requirements across five categories. These requirements are designed to be achievable by a genuinely organized civic formation while remaining beyond the reach of a poorly funded vanity project or a well-funded shell operation.

**Verified membership breadth.** The party must demonstrate a minimum number of verified registered members distributed across the state's congressional districts. The minimum threshold is set at the state level within federal guidelines established by the FEIC, calibrated so that the threshold is achievable through genuine community organizing without requiring professional signature-gathering infrastructure. SSI-verified digital registration is the preferred method, which dramatically reduces the cost of membership verification for emerging parties and eliminates the signature-gathering industry that has colonized state initiative systems in many states.

**Small-donor breadth.** The party must demonstrate financial support from a minimum number of verified individual donors giving amounts within the small-donor tier defined under the Campaign Finance Reform framework. Donor breadth, not donor depth, is the operative metric. A party supported by ten thousand donors giving twenty dollars each demonstrates genuine civic base. A party supported by one donor giving two hundred thousand dollars demonstrates a patron, not a movement. This is the most important single recognition requirement because it directly tests whether the party has genuine distributed support that cannot be manufactured through concentrated wealth.

**Transparent governance.** The party must file publicly accessible bylaws defining its internal governance structure, candidate selection procedures, membership rights, leadership selection process, and amendment procedures. Bylaws must include anti-discrimination provisions consistent with federal civil rights requirements. Internal governance must be sufficiently democratic that the party cannot be captured by a single funder or leadership clique without member recourse through defined procedural pathways.

**Financial disclosure.** The party must comply with real-time financial disclosure requirements under the Campaign Finance Reform framework — all donations above fifty dollars disclosed immediately through the Democracy Fund Portal, no anonymous dark money, no corporate or PAC contributions, no foreign national contributions.

**Anti-collusion compliance.** The party must certify that it is not operating as a coordinated vehicle for another recognized party, that its leadership has no undisclosed financial relationships with other recognized parties, and that it is not sharing operational infrastructure with another recognized party in ways designed to circumvent contribution limits or disclosure requirements.

### Recognition Duration and Renewal

Party recognition is granted for a period aligned with the federal election cycle and must be renewed by demonstrating continued membership breadth and small-donor activity. A party that fails to field candidates, maintain membership, or sustain donor activity loses recognition at the next renewal point. Recognition is not permanent, cannot be inherited, cannot be sold, and cannot be transferred.

### Synthetic Party and Spoiler Vehicle Detection

The FEIC maintains an active detection function for organizations that nominally satisfy recognition requirements while functioning as coordinated spoiler vehicles, shell operations, or extensions of established party infrastructure. Detection criteria are structural rather than ideological — the FEIC has no authority to evaluate a party's political positions, only its organizational independence.

Structural independence tests include: donor overlap analysis comparing the party's donor base against established party donor networks; vendor and contractor overlap identifying shared operational infrastructure; staff and leadership cross-referencing identifying undisclosed coordination; communication pattern analysis identifying coordinated messaging inconsistent with organizational independence; financial dependency mapping identifying funding flows that suggest direction from external actors; timing analysis identifying unusual formation patterns relative to electoral cycles; and common legal counsel identification where shared representation suggests coordinated strategy.

A party found to exhibit significant structural dependence on an established party or a single concentrated funding source is referred for investigation. Confirmed coordination results in recognition revocation. This enforcement is framed and operated as anti-cartel enforcement — the same principle that prevents established economic players from using shell entities to circumvent competition law — not as ideological party policing.

## Presidential Caucus Formation — Emerging from Congressional Alignment

### Presidential Caucus Blocs as Electoral Coordination Vehicles

State parties may voluntarily form presidential caucus blocs with other state parties for the purpose of coordinating presidential primary selection. A presidential caucus bloc is a formal electoral coordination agreement among existing recognized state parties that share demonstrated interest alignment — ideally proven through prior congressional caucus behavior — to pool their primary weight behind a shared presidential candidate.

This is a critical terminological distinction throughout this package. **Congressional caucus blocs** are the first-stage Washington institutions formed by elected members based on demonstrated legislative behavior. **Presidential caucus blocs** are the later electoral coordination vehicles formed by state parties — ideally based on the alignment those parties' congressional members have already demonstrated. The two are related but distinct. A presidential caucus bloc should, wherever possible, reflect and ratify the congressional alignment that preceded it rather than inventing new coalitions from scratch for electoral convenience.

Formation is interest-based, not geography-mandated. Non-contiguous caucusing is explicitly permitted and expected. A Rust Belt labor party in Ohio and a coal-country energy party in West Virginia may find more in common with each other than either does with dominant parties in their geographic neighbors. A Pacific Coast urban progressive party and a Great Lakes environmental party may caucus across significant geographic distance because their policy priorities and candidate criteria align more closely than their map position suggests. What matters is demonstrated governing affinity, not cartographic proximity.

The presidential caucus system is designed to produce between five and twelve viable candidate pools nationally — enough to represent genuine political diversity without producing a fragmented field that no scoring system can resolve into a clear mandate. That range is not a design cap. It is the organic output of however many durable congressional coalitions have proven themselves through actual legislative behavior and survived the friction of real governance.

### Presidential Caucus Bloc Formation Requirements

A presidential caucus bloc must be formed by at least two recognized state parties. Formation requires a public caucus agreement filed with the FEIC at least one year before the presidential primary — preventing tactical last-minute coalition formation designed to game the field rather than represent genuine interest alignment. Where the forming parties can demonstrate prior congressional caucus alignment, that record should be referenced in the filing as evidence of demonstrated rather than speculative coalition coherence.

The caucus agreement must specify:

The interest identity the presidential caucus bloc represents — the regional, economic, cultural, or policy coalition around which member parties are organizing. This is a substantive declaration, not a formality. Voters need to understand why these parties have coordinated, and the filing should reference any congressional caucus alignment that preceded and informed the presidential coordination decision.

The policy priorities the bloc is organizing around — the governing objectives that member parties share and that will anchor their candidate evaluation criteria.

The candidate credentials the bloc will use to evaluate declared candidates — what demonstrated experience, regional record, and governing capacity the bloc is looking for. This is where execution record credentialing is formalized: a bloc that declares it is looking for candidates with demonstrated regional economic development experience is committing to evaluate candidates on that criterion.

The internal selection method the bloc will use in its primary round — which approved scoring method member state parties will use for the presidential primary. Critically, all member state parties must use the same approved scoring method for the presidential primary round, regardless of what method those states use for their other elections. A caucus agreement that specifies different methods for different member states produces methodologically incoherent aggregate results and will not be certified. The caucus agreement must declare a single method.

The vote weighting formula — how each member state party's primary results will be weighted in the bloc aggregate to produce the bloc-endorsed candidate.

The names of all member state parties and their contact information for public accountability.

The caucus agreement is published in full, machine-readable, and integrated into the candidate nutrition label infrastructure. Voters can see why these parties coordinated — what they stand for, what they are looking for in a candidate, what governing priorities they are pooling around, and what congressional alignment preceded the electoral coordination.

### Presidential Caucus Bloc Membership Rules

State parties may belong to only one presidential caucus bloc per election cycle. A party that wishes to exit a bloc must file notice at least six months before the primary and may not join a different bloc in the same cycle. This prevents tactical mid-cycle switching designed to boost a preferred candidate's field position rather than reflect genuine interest alignment.

A state party may choose not to join any presidential caucus bloc and instead support an independent presidential candidate who has qualified through the national independent qualification pathway defined in Brief 3.

### Presidential Caucus Bloc Internal Governance

Each presidential caucus bloc determines its own internal primary selection procedure within the constraints of the caucus agreement. The selected procedure must use the single approved scoring method declared in the agreement and must be published before the filing deadline. The procedure may not be changed after filing.

## Congressional Caucus Formation

State and regional parties that elect members to Congress may form recognized congressional caucus blocs in Washington. Congressional caucuses are interest-based and need not mirror the presidential caucus structure — a member elected under a Rust Belt labor party label may join a congressional labor-industrial caucus that includes members from states outside their presidential caucus alignment.

### Congressional Caucus Recognition

A congressional caucus bloc is recognized upon filing a caucus agreement signed by a minimum of five members that defines the caucus's governing priorities, its policy commitments, its coalition principles, and its internal decision-making procedures. The agreement is published in full and machine-readable.

Recognition requires registration with the Congressional Operating System Interface and compliance with the financial disclosure and anti-collusion requirements that apply to state party recognition.

### Formal Caucus Rights

Recognized congressional caucus blocs receive formal institutional rights proportional to their membership.

Committee assignments are distributed to reflect caucus membership, not just party affiliation. A caucus with sufficient membership to constitute a meaningful voting bloc receives committee seats and ranking positions proportional to its size relative to the full chamber.

Staff allocation support is provided to recognized caucuses for policy research, constituent services coordination, and Digital Democracy Platform interface management. Caucus staff resources are public funding subject to transparency requirements.

Leadership negotiation standing gives recognized caucuses formal rights to participate in speaker and majority leader negotiations, including the ability to file public coalition agreements with other caucuses defining the terms of legislative cooperation.

Floor procedural rights allow recognized caucuses to file motions, request recorded votes, and trigger certain procedural mechanisms as a bloc.

### Coalition Agreements

Caucuses may file public coalition agreements with other caucuses defining the terms of their legislative cooperation on specific issues or for a defined legislative session. Coalition agreements are machine-readable, published in real time on the Digital Democracy Platform, and linked from each participating member's candidate nutrition label profile.

Coalition agreements create accountability. A caucus that files a public agreement to support specific legislation and then votes against it has created a visible record of that divergence. Members whose votes consistently diverge from their caucus's coalition agreements accumulate contested-status flags in their Tier 2 label record.

## Congress as the Proving Ground for Presidential Coalitions

The presidential caucus bloc architecture works best when formation reflects demonstrated coalition alignment rather than speculative strategic pre-commitment. Congressional caucus blocs are where that demonstration happens.

State parties that win seats in Congress send their members to Washington, where they encounter members from other state parties and begin the practical work of legislative coalition-building. Members with overlapping governing philosophies, similar voting records, and compatible policy priorities naturally find each other. The candidate nutrition label makes this alignment visible before it is formally declared — any member, journalist, or civic organization can examine the label database and identify which members are already behaving as a coherent coalition based on Tier 1 governing theory, Tier 2 voting record, and Tier 3 mandate contribution patterns.

Congressional caucus bloc formation formalizes what the label data already shows. When members from a Rust Belt labor party in Ohio and an Appalachian energy party in West Virginia have spent one or two congressional cycles filing joint coalition agreements, voting together on industrial and workforce policy, and building aligned mandate contribution records, their presidential caucus bloc formation is the ratification of a demonstrated relationship rather than a speculative bet on future alignment.

This has a direct and important effect on presidential field compression. The five-to-twelve candidate range that the presidential architecture targets is not an arbitrary design parameter — it is the organic output of however many durable congressional caucus blocs have formed and proven themselves over prior cycles. A presidential caucus bloc filing that can point to two or three cycles of demonstrated congressional alignment is making a substantially stronger legitimacy claim than one assembled purely on strategic grounds before any of its parties' members have worked together in Washington.

### The Sequencing Implication

This logic implies a natural sequencing for the full electoral reform architecture. State parties form first and begin winning state legislative and congressional seats. Congressional caucus blocs form and demonstrate coherence through at least one full legislative cycle — ideally two. Presidential caucus blocs emerge from demonstrated congressional alignment. The first full presidential election under the new architecture is therefore likely two congressional cycles after the statutory foundation is in place.

This is a longer runway than a purely parallel implementation would require. It is also a substantially more legitimate system when it arrives — one in which presidential coalitions are ratified expressions of proven governing partnerships rather than manufactured alignments assembled for a single election. The patience required to let congressional caucus blocs develop before activating the presidential architecture is a feature, not a bug. It also substantially reduces the threat salience of the reform to entrenched actors at each stage: state party recognition is a states' rights argument, congressional caucus rights are an internal chamber governance question, and by the time presidential caucus blocs form they are ratifying demonstrated relationships rather than launching an untested national system.

The FEIC tracks congressional caucus bloc formation, coalition agreement filings, and voting record alignment on an ongoing basis. By the time presidential caucus bloc filing windows open, the data infrastructure already contains the coalition map that presidential candidates and state parties need to make informed formation decisions. The label does the discovery work. The caucus filing formalizes it.

## Relationship to Campaign Finance Reform

The party formation framework depends entirely on the Campaign Finance Reform architecture. Verified individual donations, small-donor breadth metrics, real-time disclosure through the Democracy Fund Portal, and the National Democracy Fund matching system are all prerequisites for the recognition requirements to function as intended.

A party recognition system that uses donor breadth as its primary legitimacy metric only works if the underlying donation verification infrastructure is reliable and manipulation-resistant. That infrastructure is established under Campaign Finance Reform and the SSI Self-Sovereign Identity Framework. This brief assumes both are operational before the recognition framework activates.

## Open Questions

What is the right minimum membership threshold for state party recognition — and should it vary by state population? Should caucus agreements be subject to substantive certification review beyond the procedural filing requirements, or is public filing and disclosure sufficient? Should congressional caucus blocs have formal vote-pooling rights on procedural questions, or only on substantive policy coalitions? How should the system handle a recognized party that splits internally — which faction retains the recognition? Should the synthetic party detection function have civil penalty authority, or only referral authority to the Department of Justice?

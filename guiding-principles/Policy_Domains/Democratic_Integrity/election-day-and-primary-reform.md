# Election Day Reform: National Holiday & Unified Primary Calendar

## Summary

This proposal establishes **Election Day as a federal public holiday** for both primary and general elections, and restructures the federal primary calendar so that all primaries occur on a **single unified national Primary Day, held on the first Tuesday after the first Monday in August** — approximately three months before the November general election.

Together these reforms address two compounding failures in the current electoral system: participation barriers that suppress turnout and systematically skew candidate selection toward activated partisan minorities, and a fragmented, rolling primary calendar that rewards early-state dominance, exhausts candidates and voters alike, and produces nominees before most Americans have meaningfully engaged. The three-month window between August primary and November general election structurally limits the fundraising period, reduces the total cost of running for office, and creates a focused deliberative campaign season without the calendar exhaustion of the current system.

---

## Rationale

### The Participation Problem

American voter turnout consistently underperforms peer democracies. Primary turnout is significantly lower still — often below 20% of eligible voters — meaning that candidates for federal office are effectively selected by a small, unrepresentative slice of the electorate. Voters who work hourly jobs, lack flexible schedules, or face long commutes are structurally disadvantaged by elections held on working Tuesdays.

The current Tuesday election tradition dates to the 19th century, when farmers needed a day's travel to reach polling places and could not vote on the Sabbath. It has no functional justification in a modern economy and imposes real costs on working Americans that are not evenly distributed.

Treating Election Day as a federal holiday removes the most basic structural barrier to participation. It signals that democratic participation is a civic priority, not a private inconvenience to be managed around employment obligations.

### The Candidate Selection Problem

Primary elections select candidates, but their electorate is systematically unrepresentative of the general population. Low-turnout primaries amplify the influence of highly activated, ideologically consistent voters — typically those with the most extreme views within each party. Candidates rationally optimize for this audience during the primary, adopting positions and tones that increase general election polarization and reduce the space for cross-partisan governance.

This dynamic is not a failure of individual candidates. It is a structural output of the current system. The system selects for extremity because its incentives reward it.

Maximizing primary turnout by holding primaries on a national holiday directly dilutes the activated-partisan advantage. When more of the electorate participates, the median primary voter moves closer to the median general election voter, reducing the gap candidates must bridge and moderating the selection pressure toward extremity.

### The Calendar Problem

The current primary calendar fragments candidate selection across months, amplifies the influence of a handful of early states whose demographics do not represent the national electorate, and produces nominees so early that the general election campaign becomes exhausting and disconnected from real deliberation.

A unified national Primary Day held in August — approximately three months before the November general election — solves several problems simultaneously. It eliminates early-state distortion. It creates a clean, legible two-stage electoral calendar that voters can actually track. It provides a focused, defined campaign window between primary and general election rather than the current year-long exhaustion. And by pairing Primary Day with a national holiday, it applies the participation benefits equally across all states simultaneously rather than rewarding early-state turnout. Critically, a three-month general campaign window structurally limits the total amount of money candidates need to raise — reducing the fundraising imperative that currently dominates a legislator's time in office.

## Proposal

### 1. Election Day as a Federal Public Holiday

The first Tuesday after the first Monday in November is designated a **federal public holiday** for general election years.

- Federal employees receive a paid day off.
- Federal contracts with private employers include incentive provisions for providing paid time off to hourly and non-exempt workers on Election Day.
- States are encouraged to align their own public holiday designations through performance-based federal incentive alignment mechanisms.
- Polling hours are standardized to a minimum 15-hour window to ensure access across time zones.

This reform requires no change to existing voting procedures, ballot design, or electoral administration. It removes a structural participation barrier without altering any other dimension of the electoral system.

---

### 2. Unified National Primary Day

All federal primary elections — for the House, Senate, and Presidency — are consolidated onto a **single national Primary Day**, held on the **first Tuesday after the first Monday in August**, approximately three months before the November general election.

- Primary Day is designated a **federal public holiday** under the same terms as general Election Day.
- All states hold their federal primaries simultaneously on this date.
- State primaries for state-level offices may be held concurrently at state discretion.
- Early voting and mail ballot windows apply equally to Primary Day under the same access standards as the general election.

---

### 3. The Three-Month Deliberative Window

The approximately three-month interval between August Primary Day and the November general election is a structural feature, not incidental timing.

The window serves several functions:

- Candidates who advance from the primary have sufficient time to build general election coalitions without the exhaustion dynamics of a year-long campaign.
- The campaign period is long enough for genuine policy comparison but short enough to structurally limit total fundraising requirements — reducing the money candidates need to raise and the time they spend raising it.
- Third-party and independent candidates who did not participate in major-party primaries have a defined and meaningful window to qualify for the general election ballot under standardized access rules.
- The media and civic information environment has time to move from primary horse-race coverage into substantive policy comparison.

The interval also creates a natural evaluation checkpoint. Candidates whose primary coalition proves narrow, whose conduct becomes disqualifying, or whose policy positions prove untenable have three months in which that becomes visible — before the general election, not after.

---

### 4. Ballot Access Standardization for the General Election Window

The three-month window between August Primary Day and the November general election creates a defined pathway for third-party and independent candidates to qualify for general election ballots.

- Standardized federal ballot access thresholds replace the current patchwork of state-by-state signature requirements, many of which are designed to suppress third-party entry.
- Candidates who demonstrate a defined threshold of small-dollar donor support — consistent with the National Democracy Fund matching architecture — qualify automatically for federal general election ballots in all states.
- This access pathway is open for a defined window following Primary Day, ensuring that third-party candidates have fair entry without disrupting primary deliberation.

---

### 5. Presidential Primary Sequencing

The unified Primary Day model applies directly to congressional primaries. Presidential primary sequencing requires additional design given the delegate-based nomination structure of major parties.

Under this framework:

- Presidential primaries and caucuses are encouraged to consolidate onto or near the unified Primary Day through federal incentive alignment.
- States that hold presidential primaries on the unified national Primary Day receive priority access to federal election administration support funds.
- The fragmented multi-month presidential primary calendar is not mandated away but is structurally discouraged through capital and administrative incentives.
- Party rules governing delegate allocation remain within party authority. The federal reform governs ballot access, holiday designation, and incentive structure — not internal party nomination mechanics.

---

### 6. Election Administration Modernization

Consolidating primaries onto a national holiday and maximizing turnout requires election administration systems capable of handling increased volume reliably.

- Federal election administration support funds are expanded and conditioned on states meeting defined standards for polling place availability, wait time maximums, provisional ballot processing, and results reporting timelines.
- Voter registration modernization — including automatic registration at point of government contact — is paired with this reform to ensure that expanded participation is not constrained by registration barriers.
- The Department of Data and Accountability publishes standardized turnout, wait time, and administration quality metrics across all jurisdictions following each election cycle.

---

## Expected Outcomes

- Materially higher turnout in both primary and general elections, particularly among hourly workers, younger voters, and lower-income households.
- Primary electorates that more closely reflect the general electorate, reducing structural selection pressure toward candidate extremity.
- A cleaner, more legible two-stage national electoral calendar.
- Elimination of early-state distortion in presidential primary dynamics.
- A defined, fair pathway for third-party and independent candidates to reach general election ballots.
- A focused three-month deliberative window between primary results and general election voting — long enough for genuine policy comparison, short enough to structurally limit total campaign fundraising requirements.

---

## Global Precedents

- **Australia** and **New Zealand** treat election days as public events with high civic salience, contributing to turnout rates consistently above 75%.
- **France** operates a two-round general election system with a defined interval between rounds that produces similar deliberative benefits to the proposed primary-general structure.
- **Germany** holds federal elections on Sundays, eliminating the work-day conflict entirely.
- **Canada** has moved toward fixed election date legislation that creates predictable, legible electoral calendars.

*Lesson:* Participation rises when structural barriers fall and electoral calendars are legible and predictable.

---

## Fiscal Impact

The primary costs of this reform are election administration support funds and the economic impact of a federal holiday. These are modest relative to the democratic legitimacy benefits and are substantially offset by reduced litigation costs, reduced campaign expenditure compression, and improved long-term civic engagement.

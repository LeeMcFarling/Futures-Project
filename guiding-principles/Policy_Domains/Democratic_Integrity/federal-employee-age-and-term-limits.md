# Federal Age and Term Limits

## Purpose

This brief defines a uniform framework for age and term limits across the legislative, executive, and judicial branches of the federal government. The objective is to restore institutional renewal, limit entrenchment, and rebalance governance toward execution, accountability, and democratic legitimacy.

---

# Guiding Principles:
	- Democratic renewal: No individual should hold formal state power indefinitely.
	- Reliability over seniority: Authority is conditioned on functional capacity rather than tenure. 
	- Uniform application: Rules apply across branches to prevent arbitrage.
    - Prospective enforcement: No mid-term removals or retroactive penalties.
	- Separation of authority and expertise: Advisory capacity is preserved without decision-making power.
	- Legibility and enforceability: Eligibility rules must be simple, visible, and difficult to game.

## Constitutional and Legal Pathway
	- Legislative age and term limits require a constitutional amendment
	- Executive and appointed-role limits may be enacted by statute
	- Judicial senior-status transition requires a constitutional amendment or narrowly tailored Article III-compliant restructuring

This framework is designed to be legally durable and resistant to rollback.
---

# Age Limits

Federal Retirement Age Anchor

Eligibility for formal federal office is tied to the federally defined full public-service retirement age, rather than a fixed numerical age.

The retirement age:
	- Is set by federal statute
	- Serves as the eligibility threshold for holding formal decision-making authority
	- Applies uniformly across branches

To prevent self-dealing or silent expansion, the following constitutional guardrails apply:
	- The retirement age may not exceed 72 under any statutory change
	- Any increase requires a two-thirds supermajority in both chambers of Congress
	- Any increase must be justified by publicly reported healthspan and workforce-participation metrics
	- Changes take effect no sooner than 10 years after enactment

This structure allows adaptation to genuine advances in longevity while preventing incumbents from extending their own tenure.

---

Elected Federal Office
	- Eligibility requirement: Individual must be below the federal retirement age at the start of a new term

Applies to:
	- Members of the House of Representatives
	- Members of the Senate
	- President and Vice President
    - Article III Judicial Nominees
    - Appointed Federal Leadership
        - Cabinet Secretaries and deputies
        - Agency heads and deputies 
        - Independent regulatory commission leadership

Individuals who exceed the retirement-age threshold may complete their current term but are ineligible for re-election or reappointment.

---

# Term Limits

Legislative Branch
– House of Representatives: Maximum of 3 terms (6 years)
– Senate: Maximum of 2 terms (12 years)

House service is capped intentionally to keep the chamber closer to the electorate and to prevent long-term entrenchment. Members seeking extended legislative service may run for the Senate, which is structured for longer horizons.

Service in the House and Senate counts cumulatively toward a lifetime maximum of 12 years of congressional service, regardless of breaks in service.

Eligibility is assessed at the start of a term. A candidate must be eligible to serve the full term at the time of election or appointment. Partial terms may not be served to exhaust remaining eligibility.

Congressional term limits apply only to service in Congress and do not compound with eligibility for other federal offices, including the Presidency or Vice Presidency.

---

# Executive Branch
	- President: Retains the existing two-term constitutional limit
	- Vice President: Subject to the same age and cumulative service constraints

# Cabinet and agency leadership:
	- Maximum of 12 cumulative years in senior appointed leadership roles

---

# Judicial Branch
	- Article III Judges:
	- Fixed term of 10 years
	- No lifetime appointments

Judges serve a single, non-renewable 10-year term to preserve independence while enforcing regular turnover.

Upon completion of a term, judges may transition to non-decision-making advisory roles, including:
	- Senior advisory or mentoring positions
	- Temporary case assistance without final decision authority
	- Service within FFRDCs, judicial training bodies, or execution-corps advisory units

Restrictions post-term:
	- No final rulings or binding opinions
	- No chief judge or administrative authority
	- No permanent judicial office

This structure preserves judicial independence during service while preventing indefinite concentration of judicial power.

---

Advisory and Consultant Pathways

Individuals who reach age or term limits may continue to serve in advisory capacities, including:
	- Consultants and fellows
	- Execution corps advisors (non-decision making roles)
	- GAO, CBO, or FFRDC staff
	- Commission members without voting authority

Restrictions:
	- No formal votes
	- No budgetary sign-off
	- No command authority
	- No direct regulatory issuance

Expertise remains accessible while authority remains time-bounded.

---

# Transition and Enforcement

	- All limits apply prospectively
	- No individual is removed mid-term
	- Eligibility is assessed at the start of the next term or appointment
	- Staggered implementation schedules may be used to avoid institutional shock

Public eligibility rules and automated compliance checks ensure consistent enforcement.

---

Expected Outcomes
	- Reduced gerontocracy and institutional capture
	- Increased leadership turnover without loss of expertise
	- Stronger incentives to deliver results within finite mandates
	- Clearer pathways for new talent to enter federal leadership
	- Improved public trust through visible renewal

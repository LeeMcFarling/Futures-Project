# Abolish Gerrymandering: Independent Redistricting & Algorithmic Fairness

## Summary
This proposal ends partisan gerrymandering in the United States by creating **independent redistricting commissions** in every state, supported by **transparent algorithmic tools** that guarantee fair and competitive districts.  
District maps will be drawn according to publicly verifiable criteria, eliminating the ability of political parties to entrench power by manipulating boundaries.

---

## Rationale
Gerrymandering is one of the most insidious forms of modern corruption.  
When politicians choose their voters, accountability disappears — safe seats breed extremism, and minority voices are systematically diluted.  
Independent redistricting, guided by objective data and public oversight, restores equality of representation.

---

## Proposal

### 1. Independent Redistricting Commissions
- Each state must establish a **nonpartisan commission** of nine members:
  - 3 members registered with each of the two largest political parties.  
  - 3 members unaffiliated or representing minor parties.  
- Members are selected by a **judicial lottery** overseen by a state court panel to prevent political capture.  
- Commissioners are barred from elected office or party employment for 10 years before and after service.

---

### 2. Transparent, Data-Driven Mapping
- District maps are created using **open-source algorithms** that prioritize:
  1. Equal population  
  2. Geographic compactness  
  3. Preservation of existing communities  
  4. Non-discrimination and protection of minority voting rights  
  5. Maximization of competitive districts  
- Every line-drawing decision and dataset must be **publicly available** for audit and simulation by citizens and researchers.

---

### 3. Algorithmic Fairness Verification
- A **National Redistricting Audit Platform (NRAP)** evaluates each state’s proposed maps using fairness metrics:
  - **Efficiency Gap**
  - **Partisan Symmetry**
  - **Compactness Index**
  - **Minority Representation Index**
- States failing to meet fairness thresholds must revise their maps until compliance is achieved.  

---

### 4. Enforcement
- The **Department of Justice Voting Rights Division** and **Federal Election Commission** jointly certify maps before elections.  
- Courts can order emergency redistricting using the last certified fair map if states fail to comply.  
- Citizens may challenge unfair maps through a **Public Redistricting Petition** system, requiring judicial review within 60 days.

---

### 5. Public Participation
- Every proposed map is posted online for **public comment and citizen map submissions**.  
- States must host at least **five open hearings** across diverse regions before finalizing any map.  

---

## Global Precedents
- **Canada** and **Australia** both rely on independent commissions for federal redistricting, producing more competitive elections and higher public trust.  
- **UK Boundary Commissions** redraw districts on census cycles using apolitical criteria.  
- **Germany** employs proportional systems that eliminate gerrymandering entirely.  

*Lesson:* Representation works when boundaries serve people — not parties.

---

## Fiscal Impact
Implementation cost per census cycle is minimal (<$1 billion nationwide) and largely offset by long-term gains in voter engagement, reduced litigation, and policy stability.

## Open Questions
- Should algorithmic thresholds (e.g., efficiency gap) be fixed nationally or adaptive by state demographics?  
- Should states be allowed to opt into **proportional representation** instead of geographic districts?  
- How should we handle at-large or multi-member districts under this reform?

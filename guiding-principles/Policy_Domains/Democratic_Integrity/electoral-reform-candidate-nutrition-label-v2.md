# Candidate Nutrition Label, Mandate Accountability, and Congressional Caucus Reform

## Purpose

This brief defines the candidate and party information architecture — the standardized, viewpoint-neutral schema through which voters can evaluate candidates not just on what they claim to believe but on what they have done, how their actions relate to democratically established national priorities, and whether their conduct in office matches their campaign commitments. It also defines the democratic mandate infrastructure and the congressional caucus rights structure that ensures local political identity survives contact with Washington.

The governing loop this brief establishes is: **campaign label to mandate commitments to district accountability to DoDA-certified performance record to updated label to next election.** That loop is what makes local political formation survive Washington, and what makes Washington politics answerable to local constituencies rather than national party command.

---

## The Problem

Voters currently make presidential and congressional choices with profoundly asymmetric information. Candidates control their own presentation. Campaign advertising is optimized for emotional impact rather than substantive comparison. Media coverage prioritizes conflict, personality, and horse-race dynamics over governing philosophy and policy record. The information environment systematically advantages candidates with strong brand recognition, large advertising budgets, and media profiles over candidates with better records and more representative platforms.

The problem is not that voters are insufficiently attentive. The problem is that the information architecture does not give voters what they need to make the comparison they are trying to make: which of these candidates is most likely to govern in the direction I want, based on evidence rather than promises?

The candidate nutrition label is the answer to that question. It does not tell voters who is good. It makes the candidate's governing theory, demonstrated behavior, mandate contribution, and integrity record legible in a standardized format that allows genuine comparison. It is metadata, not editorial judgment. It is infrastructure, not propaganda.

---

## Infrastructure Prerequisites

The candidate nutrition label depends on two distinct infrastructure layers that must be designed and governed with exceptional care.

### The Democratic Mandate — Open Source

The national mandate — the set of democratically identified long-horizon public objectives that anchors the Tier 3 mandate contribution measurement — is derived from structured public input through the Digital Democracy Platform. The algorithms, weighting systems, aggregation methods, and signal processing that convert public input into a structured mandate expression must be fully open source, publicly auditable, version-controlled, and forkable. Any researcher, journalist, civic organization, or citizen can inspect the methodology, challenge its design, propose alternatives, and run independent analyses.

The mandate's legitimacy derives entirely from radical transparency. A mandate derived through opaque or proprietary methodology is not a democratic mandate — it is a technocratic imposition wearing democratic clothing. Open source is not optional. It is the constitutional foundation of the instrument.

### The Digital Democracy Platform — Hardened Infrastructure

The platform through which citizens actually participate — the identity verification layer, data collection systems, signal aggregation infrastructure, and participation interfaces — must be treated as critical national security infrastructure. The platform is hardened against foreign interference, domestic manipulation, bot amplification, astroturf campaigns, and single-point-of-failure attacks with the same seriousness applied to electoral infrastructure and financial system security.

The distinction between open mandate methodology and hardened platform infrastructure is load-bearing. Open source methodology means anyone can audit how input is processed. Hardened platform means the input collection itself is secure against manipulation. Both conditions must hold simultaneously. Conflating them — making the platform open in ways that expose attack surfaces, or making the methodology opaque in ways that undermine trust — defeats both purposes.

### DoDA and FEIC Rotation and Nomination Hardening

The institutions responsible for compiling candidate records, certifying mandate contribution, and operating the label infrastructure carry significant power over the political environment. That power requires an institutional design that is resistant to capture through a single administration's appointments or a single congressional majority's preferences.

DoDA's leadership structure must include staggered fixed nonrenewable terms across a minimum span exceeding any single presidential administration. Nomination must proceed through a multi-body process — no single branch controls appointment. Recusal rules must be enforced with teeth, not discretion. Post-service cooling off periods must prohibit work for any political campaign, party, or advocacy organization. All methodological decisions must be published in advance and subject to public comment before implementation.

The FEIC follows the same design principles established in Brief 1. The two institutions must be explicitly prohibited from sharing leadership, exchanging personnel during active service, or coordinating methodology without public disclosure and comment.

These rotation and nomination structures are not administrative detail. They are the primary protection against the label system becoming a political weapon. They must be specified in authorizing statute with the same precision as the substantive mandate.

## The Four-Tier Schema

Every recognized candidate for federal office and every recognized party maintains a public profile structured around four tiers of information. The profile is hosted on the Digital Democracy Platform and linked from every ballot and voter guide. The FEIC compiles Tier 2, Tier 3, and Tier 4 data from public records. DoDA certifies Tier 3 mandate contribution methodology. No tier is self-reported without a corresponding record-derived comparison.

### Tier 1 — Self-Declared Governing Theory

This tier contains what the candidate says they believe. It is self-authored and self-declared. It is not evaluated, validated, or edited by any government body. It exists to give voters a structured starting point for understanding the candidate's stated philosophy before comparing it to their observed behavior.

The self-declaration schema includes standardized fields covering: economic orientation on the spectrum from market-led to public-led economic organization, including labor/capital balance and fiscal philosophy; institutional posture from status quo preservation through incremental reform to structural transformation; power distribution preference across the federal/state, executive/legislative, and public/private dimensions; ranked issue priorities drawn from a standardized taxonomy consistent across all candidates; theory of executive power including stated position on emergency powers and congressional oversight; and foreign policy posture including alliance systems, multilateral institutions, and diplomatic engagement.

The schema is standardized nationally with a defined core. Candidates may add additional fields beyond the core in a designated extension section. The core fields are non-negotiable — candidates cannot opt out of any core schema field.

### Tier 2 — Record-Derived Behavior

This tier contains what the candidate has actually done. It is not self-authored. It is compiled from public records by the FEIC operating under standardized methodology published in advance by DoDA.

For candidates with legislative records, Tier 2 includes: voting record with pattern analysis showing consistency or divergence from stated governing theory; sponsorship and co-sponsorship record showing which policy domains the candidate has actively advanced; caucus membership and coalition agreements; attendance and participation record; district townhall commitments and their fulfillment record; and budgetary behavior showing whether fiscal votes match stated fiscal philosophy.

For candidates without legislative records — including first-time candidates, NCT alumni, Execution Corps veterans, military service members, nonprofit leaders, business builders, and community organizers — Tier 2 includes a **Public Execution Record** category that displays verifiable records from non-legislative public roles. This category is explicitly separated from legislative record with clear visual distinction so voters understand the difference in record type, but it is given equal display prominence so the label does not become incumbent-biased.

DoDA-certified NCT and Execution Corps performance records feed directly into this category. A candidate who managed a regional housing coordination initiative with DoDA-certified outcome data has a verifiable public execution record that is displayed in Tier 2 alongside or in place of a legislative record. The absence of a legislative record is not treated as an absence of record.

Campaign finance patterns are also displayed in Tier 2: donor concentration visualization showing what share of funding comes from small donors versus large donors versus sectoral concentrations, and any enforcement actions taken by the FEIC or Federal Democracy Commission.

The Tier 2 record explicitly shows divergence from the Tier 1 self-declaration. If a candidate declares themselves a fiscal conservative but has consistently voted for deficit expansion, that divergence is visible. If a candidate declares themselves an institutional reformer but has consistently defended incumbent institutional arrangements, that divergence is visible. The system displays divergences. It does not editorialize about them.

### Tier 3 — Mandate Contribution

This tier measures how the candidate's actions in office have been associated with movement toward the democratically established national mandate.

**Critical design constraint:** mandate contribution is probabilistic and attribution-bounded. It does not claim causal certainty. Political outcomes are complex, multi-causal, and subject to confounding factors that no measurement system can fully isolate. A candidate who pursued housing supply expansion policy in their district may have contributed to improved housing affordability outcomes — or those outcomes may reflect broader market forces, regional migration patterns, or policy decisions by other actors. The label displays association, not attribution.

Specific language requirements for Tier 3 displays: all mandate contribution indicators are labeled with confidence bands, jurisdictional influence level, policy exposure score, and associated contribution designation rather than causal claims. A high confidence-band, high jurisdictional influence, high policy exposure result means the candidate had significant influence over policy in a domain where outcomes moved in the mandate direction — a meaningful signal without overstating causality. A low confidence-band result is displayed with explicit uncertainty notation.

The national mandate against which contribution is measured is derived from the open-source Digital Democracy Platform methodology. The mandate is pluralistic — it reflects the full distribution of public priorities, not a single agenda — and is publicly visible so any voter can evaluate whether they agree with its construction. Voters who disagree with the mandate's construction can see exactly how it was built and challenge the methodology through published comment processes.

Mandate contribution is updated on a defined annual cycle following DoDA certification of methodology and outcome data. Mid-cycle updates are not permitted to prevent the label from being used as a real-time political weapon during campaigns.

### Tier 4 — Integrity and Provenance

This tier contains information about the candidate's compliance with democratic integrity requirements.

Campaign finance compliance displays whether the candidate's fundraising complies with verified individual donation requirements, whether any enforcement actions have been taken, and a visual representation of donor concentration.

Conflict of interest disclosures show whether the candidate has complied with blind trust requirements, whether any stock trading violations have occurred under the Ban Stock Trading framework, and whether any disclosed financial interests overlap with legislative domains where the candidate has taken positions.

Synthetic media authentication shows whether the candidate's public communications are cryptographically authenticated under the Synthetic Media Provenance framework, and whether any unauthenticated content circulating under the candidate's name has been identified and flagged. In high-risk election contexts this field is particularly prominent — voters need to know whether what they are seeing is attributable to the candidate.

Claim sourcing displays the sourcing status of major factual claims in campaign materials: whether they are sourced to verifiable primary sources, whether they have been contested by the Referendum Factual Claims Panel or equivalent bodies, and the current status of any contested claims.

## Label Statuses

Every field in the candidate nutrition label carries one of three statuses displayed visually alongside the field content.

**Self-declared** indicates that the information in this field was provided by the candidate and has not been compared against observable record. This applies primarily to Tier 1 fields and to Tier 2 fields where the candidate is new to public life and has limited verifiable record.

**Record-validated** indicates that the information in this field has been compared to observable public record by the FEIC under published methodology and shows material consistency between declared position and observed behavior.

**Contested** indicates that observable record shows material divergence from declared position, that a claim has been challenged by a certified review body, or that conflicting evidence exists that has not been resolved. Contested status does not mean the candidate is dishonest — it means voters should examine this field more carefully before treating it as settled.

The label displays all three statuses neutrally. It does not recommend. It does not warn. It informs.

## The Accountability Loop

The candidate nutrition label is not a static voter guide. It is a living governance instrument that creates a continuous accountability loop between campaign commitments and governing behavior.

Before an election, the candidate declares their governing theory in Tier 1 and makes explicit mandate commitments — specific measurable outcomes they commit to pursuing — published on the Digital Democracy Platform and linked to their label.

After election, the candidate's Tier 2 record is updated continuously as they cast votes, sponsor legislation, make coalition agreements, and fulfill or fail to fulfill district townhall commitments. The semiannual district return requirement creates a structured session in every congressional district twice a year at which the data opens first: constituent signal from the Digital Democracy Platform, local outcome metrics, promise versus performance comparisons, and new commitment opportunities. These sessions are public, attended by verified constituents, and summarized in standardized format that feeds back into the label.

After each annual evaluation cycle, Tier 3 mandate contribution is updated by DoDA based on certified outcome data. The label shows whether the candidate's actions were associated with movement toward the outcomes they committed to, with confidence bands and attribution bounds.

At the next election, voters see the full loop: what this person claimed they would do, what they actually did, and what the evidence shows about whether it worked. That is a fundamentally different information environment than the one voters currently operate in — and it is the mechanism that makes the entire democratic reform package an operating system rather than a collection of good intentions.

## The Label as Coalition Discovery Infrastructure

The candidate nutrition label serves a function beyond individual candidate accountability — it is the data infrastructure through which congressional caucus bloc alignment becomes visible before presidential caucus blocs are formally declared.

As members from newly formed state parties arrive in Congress and begin voting, filing coalition agreements, and accumulating mandate contribution records, the label aggregates that behavior into a publicly readable alignment map. Members whose Tier 1 governing theories overlap, whose Tier 2 voting records cluster together, and whose Tier 3 mandate contribution patterns align are demonstrably behaving as a coherent congressional caucus bloc regardless of whether they have formally filed one. Any voter, journalist, civic organization, or prospective presidential candidate can examine the label database and identify those clusters.

This means the label does the discovery work that presidential caucus bloc formation requires. By the time state parties begin considering whether to pool their presidential primary weight, the congressional record already contains the evidence of which congressional caucus blocs have proven durable, which alignments are genuine, and which cross-party relationships have survived the friction of actual legislative work. Presidential caucus bloc formation becomes the formalization of what the label already shows rather than a speculative bet assembled for a single election cycle.

This is why the label's update cadence, methodology consistency, and anti-capture design are not merely technical requirements — they are structural prerequisites for the whole coalition architecture to function as intended. A label that is slow, inconsistent, or politically manipulated produces a distorted alignment map that misleads presidential caucus bloc formation rather than informing it. The label's integrity is the coalition architecture's foundation.

Members of Congress elected under state and regional party labels form recognized congressional caucus blocs in Washington. The caucus structure is the political input layer for the Congressional Operating System Interface — the mechanism through which locally grounded political identity translates into Washington coalition-building.

Congressional caucus recognition, rights, and coalition agreement structures are defined in Brief 2. This section addresses the specific connection between congressional caucus behavior and the candidate nutrition label accountability loop.

Every coalition agreement filed by a congressional caucus bloc appears in the Tier 2 label record of each member of that caucus. Every vote that diverges from a filed coalition agreement generates a contested-status flag in that member's Tier 2 record. Every fulfilled coalition commitment generates a record-validated notation.

This means that the label is not only measuring individual behavior — it is measuring whether the member's Washington coalition commitments match their local political identity. A member elected under a regional labor party label who joins a Washington caucus committed to labor-industrial policy and then consistently votes against that caucus's filed coalition agreements has created a visible accountability record that their constituents can see before the next election.

The semiannual district return requirement completes the channel. Twice a year, the member returns to their district and faces a structured public session where their label record is displayed first — what they committed to in Washington, how they voted, and what their constituents are currently signaling as priorities through the Digital Democracy Platform. New commitments made at these sessions appear in the label. Commitments made at prior sessions are tracked for fulfillment.

The loop is complete: the local party formation and caucus that sent the member to Washington can see whether Washington changed them, or whether they changed Washington.

## Open Questions

Who has standing to challenge a record-validated or contested label status — any voter, only verified constituents, or only certified advocacy organizations? What is the right update cadence for Tier 2 — continuous as votes are cast, or batch quarterly? Should the self-declared governing theory schema allow candidate-defined extensions beyond the standardized core, and if so what review process governs those extensions? Should the mandate contribution annual update cycle be suspended during active campaign periods to prevent the label from being weaponized in the final months before an election? What independent audit mechanism ensures that the FEIC's Tier 2 compilation methodology remains consistent and bias-free across candidates of different parties and regions? Should coalition agreements carry any formal enforcement mechanism beyond public visibility and label notation, or is the accountability loop sufficient?

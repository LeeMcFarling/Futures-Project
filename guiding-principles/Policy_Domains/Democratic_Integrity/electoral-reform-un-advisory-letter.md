# Open Letter to the UN Electoral Assistance Division

## Request for Technical Advisory Engagement on U.S. Electoral Reform

---

To the United Nations Electoral Assistance Division, and to our colleagues in the European Union Election Observation Mission, the Commonwealth Electoral Network, and the international community of electoral systems practitioners:

We are writing to request your technical advisory engagement on a proposed electoral reform package for the United States. We write not to outsource democratic responsibility, but because democratic seriousness requires technical humility. The United States has reached a point where the structural failures of its electoral system are no longer producing merely suboptimal governance — they are producing active institutional delegitimization. We believe the international community's experience with these problems, and with the systems that have proven effective in addressing them, is directly relevant to what we are trying to build. We would rather learn from your experience than repeat your mistakes.

We are asking for your honest technical assessment. We are not asking for validation.

---

## The Problem We Are Trying to Solve

The United States electoral system suffers from a set of compounding structural failures that have now progressed well beyond the normal pathology of democratic dysfunction. Understanding the nature of these failures is necessary context for evaluating the proposed remedies.

### Structural Duopoly and Cartel-Like Entrenchment

American politics is organized around two parties that jointly designed and maintain a system that excludes alternatives. This is not a conspiracy — it is the predictable output of institutional incentives operating over two centuries. Winner-take-all district elections, ballot access laws designed to prevent third-party entry, primary systems controlled by party insiders, and campaign finance structures that reward national brand loyalty over local political formation have combined to produce a political duopoly that is self-reinforcing at every level.

The consequences are well understood by anyone who has studied multiparty democracies. A two-party system compresses the full range of political opinion into two identity containers. It forces voters into binary choices that do not represent their actual preferences. It converts every election into an existential tribal contest rather than a competition among governing philosophies. And critically, it removes the coalition-building pressure that multiparty systems create — the requirement to actually negotiate, compromise, and build working majorities rather than simply outlasting the opposition.

European parliamentary systems avoid these pathologies not through superior civic virtue but through structural design. Proportional representation, multiparty coalition governments, and constructive vote-of-no-confidence mechanisms create strong incentives for parties to remain connected to functional governance rather than pure opposition. When two parties disagree, a coalition majority absorbs the disagreement and produces a working outcome. When no party holds a majority, parties must negotiate. Governance continues because the system is designed to continue rather than to gridlock.

We cannot import that system directly. The United States Constitution assigns election administration to states, which means that any federal electoral reform must work with, not against, the federal structure. Direct proportional representation at the federal level is constitutionally and politically unavailable. States where identity politics and local party entrenchment are most intense are precisely the states where federal mandate would generate the most resistance and the least implementation compliance. A reform that only works when the people who benefit from the current system choose to implement it is not a reform.

### Gerrymandering and Dark Money

Layered on top of the two-party structural problem are two mechanisms of active entrenchment that have metastasized beyond their original scope.

Partisan gerrymandering allows whichever party controls a state legislature after each census to draw congressional district maps that protect its incumbents and punish the opposition. The result is a large and growing share of congressional seats that are structurally noncompetitive — seats where the only meaningful election is the party primary, which is controlled by the most activated and ideologically extreme fraction of the electorate. Safe seats breed extremity. Extremity breeds dysfunction. Dysfunction breeds the conditions in which elections themselves are called into question.

We have drafted a gerrymandering reform brief that establishes independent redistricting commissions with transparent algorithmic fairness verification. That reform is necessary but not sufficient. It addresses a symptom of entrenched duopoly without addressing the underlying structural incentives that make partisan map-drawing so valuable in the first place.

Dark money and unlimited electoral donations have similarly metastasized. The Supreme Court's interpretation of political spending as protected speech has made it effectively impossible to limit the volume of money in American politics through ordinary legislation. The result is a system in which a small number of extremely wealthy donors and coordinated corporate networks exercise influence over electoral outcomes that is wildly disproportionate to their share of the population. We have drafted a campaign finance reform brief that creates a public matching system for small donors and imposes transparency requirements. Again, necessary but not sufficient.

Neither gerrymandering reform nor campaign finance reform addresses the underlying problem: a two-party system in which both parties have strong incentives to exploit every available mechanism of entrenchment because losing means not just losing power but losing the ability to shape the rules of the next election.

### The Legitimacy Crisis

What has moved this from ordinary democratic dysfunction into emergency territory is the final stage of the deterioration: partisan politics in the United States has progressed past the point of parties trying to outcompete each other and into parties calling the legitimacy of elections themselves into question. When one of the two major parties has demonstrated willingness to dispute the results of elections it loses, to construct legal theories under which the losing candidate retains power, and to mobilize supporters against the physical certification of electoral results, the system has crossed a threshold that ordinary reform cannot address.

This is not a problem of individual bad actors. It is the predictable terminal stage of a system that has removed all structural pressure toward democratic cooperation. When winning is existential and losing is catastrophic, and when the rules themselves are controlled by the winners, the incentive to accept losing disappears. The two-party system, taken to its logical conclusion, produces exactly this outcome.

We need a reform that changes the incentive structure, not just the rules within the existing structure.

## Our Proposed Architecture

We are not proposing to import European proportional representation into the American federal system. We are proposing something more targeted: a set of structural changes that create the conditions for genuine multiparty competition to emerge organically at the state level, aggregate upward into the federal system through a defined architecture, and produce a Washington that is organized around transparent coalitions rather than two national brands. We are also proposing complementary reforms to the information environment, the talent pipeline into public life, and the constitutional amendment process — because electoral reform alone cannot restore a system whose dysfunction is this deep.

### Opening the Political Market at the State Level

The duopoly's foundation is ballot access. New parties cannot compete if they cannot get on the ballot. The first move is therefore to lower state-level party recognition barriers to the point where genuine civic formations — organized around regional economic interests, local political cultures, and specific governing philosophies — can achieve recognized party status and compete in state elections.

We are proposing recognition requirements built around two metrics: verified membership breadth distributed across the state, and small-donor breadth demonstrating genuine distributed financial support. The key design choice is that donor breadth — not donor depth — is the operative metric. A party supported by ten thousand small donors demonstrates a civic base. A party supported by one large donor demonstrates a patron. This calibration is designed to make entry achievable for genuine political formations while making it unattractive for wealthy interests seeking to create spoiler vehicles.

Party recognition at the state level sets the foundation. But state-level multiparty competition without a pathway into federal governance simply produces fragmentation rather than pluralism.

### Regional Caucuses as the Federal Bridge

The constitutional assignment of election administration to states means we cannot mandate a single national electoral method. What we can do is create a voluntary coordination mechanism that allows state parties with shared interests to pool their primary weight and produce viable federal candidates.

We are proposing that recognized state parties may form regional caucuses — formal coordination agreements among parties that share sufficient interest alignment to support a common presidential candidate. Critically, these caucuses are interest-based rather than geography-mandated. A Rust Belt labor party in Ohio and an energy-producing state party in West Virginia may find more in common with each other than either does with the dominant parties in their geographic neighbors. A Pacific Coast urban progressive party and a Great Lakes environmental party may caucus across significant geographic distance because their policy priorities align more closely than their map position suggests.

The caucus system produces field compression. Rather than twenty presidential candidates competing in a national primary where plurality dynamics can produce a winner opposed by most voters, regional caucuses each produce one endorsed candidate through their own primary process. Five to twelve caucus-endorsed candidates then compete in a national primary using cardinal voting methods — specifically STAR voting, Score Then Automatic Runoff — that allow voters to express genuine preference intensity across the full field rather than being forced into a single binary choice.

The top finalists from the national primary advance to a general election, also using cardinal scoring methods. The result is a president who carries a layered mandate: regional credentialing from the caucus primary, cross-regional validation from the national primary, and a general election majority.

### A Three-Round Presidential Selection

The full presidential selection architecture operates as follows.

In Round One, on a unified national Primary Day established as a federal holiday one year before the general election, every voter participates in their own party's primary. Each caucus aggregates its member state results using a single declared voting method — methodological consistency within the caucus is required for the aggregate to be meaningful — and produces one endorsed candidate.

In Round Two, three to four months later, all voters participate in a national primary regardless of party affiliation. The full field of caucus-endorsed candidates plus qualified independents appears on a single ballot. Voters score candidates using STAR voting. The top two or three advance.

In Round Three, the general election, finalists compete on the November ballot. The winner carries all three layers of democratic mandate.

This architecture eliminates early-state distortion — all caucus primaries happen simultaneously. It solves fragmented-field plurality dynamics — field compression before the national primary means a manageable competitive field. And it makes demonstrated execution capacity a primary credential — regional caucuses evaluate candidates on regional records, not national brand recognition.

### Changing How Washington Caucuses

Multiparty entry at the state level and regional caucus aggregation into federal candidacies only produces the desired outcome if members elected under regional party labels retain their political identity once they reach Washington. The historical pattern in the United States is that third-party members who reach Congress quickly dissolve into one of the two major caucuses, because the two major caucuses control committee assignments, staff resources, floor time, and leadership positions.

We are proposing that recognized congressional caucus blocs — formed by members elected under state and regional party labels — receive formal institutional rights proportional to their membership: committee seats, staff resources, leadership negotiation standing, and floor procedural rights. Coalition agreements between caucuses are public and machine-readable. Members who vote against their caucus's filed coalition commitments generate visible accountability records in their public profile.

This does not guarantee coalition-style governance. It creates the conditions under which coalition-style governance becomes possible — and under which the pressure to dissolve into national party brands is reduced by giving regional political identity formal institutional standing in Washington.

### An Alternative Talent Pipeline

One of the reasons the duopoly is so resilient is that it controls the talent pipeline into public life. Political careers in the United States run through party structures. Candidates who have not risen through party networks, donor networks, or media networks have almost no path to competitive candidacy regardless of their actual governing capacity.

We are building a complementary pipeline. The National Capability Tour is a structured civic service program that deploys early-career citizens to public execution projects across the country — housing development, healthcare system expansion, industrial corridor activation, digital government modernization. The Execution Corps is the elite tier that emerges from demonstrated performance in that pipeline. Both generate DoDA-certified outcome records that are verifiable, comparable, and publicly accessible.

These records feed directly into the candidate information system. A candidate who managed a regional housing coordination initiative with DoDA-certified delivery outcomes has a verifiable public execution record that appears in their candidate profile with the same prominence as a legislative record. A caucus that declares it is looking for candidates with demonstrated regional economic development experience can evaluate that criterion against objective records rather than campaign rhetoric.

The talent pipeline is not designed to replace political experience as a credential. It is designed to make governing capacity — the ability to actually deliver complex outcomes at scale — a visible and verifiable credential alongside political experience.

### Restoring Information Integrity

A multiparty system with a more competitive talent pipeline still fails if the information environment through which voters evaluate candidates is as degraded as the current American environment. We are proposing three complementary reforms to the information layer.

The Digital Democracy Platform is a cryptographically secured civic participation infrastructure that allows verified citizens to signal their policy priorities and evaluate their representatives' performance against those priorities on a continuous basis rather than episodically at elections. The platform's mandate methodology — how public input is aggregated into a structured expression of national priorities — is fully open source and publicly auditable. The platform infrastructure itself is treated as critical national security infrastructure and hardened against foreign interference and domestic manipulation.

The Candidate Nutrition Label is a standardized, viewpoint-neutral information schema attached to every federal candidate. It displays four tiers of information: the candidate's self-declared governing theory, their record-derived behavior compiled from public records, their probabilistic contribution to democratically established national priorities, and their integrity and provenance record including campaign finance patterns and synthetic media authentication status. The label distinguishes between self-declared, record-validated, and contested information. It informs. It does not recommend.

The SSI Self-Sovereign Identity Framework and Synthetic Media Provenance standards together address the bot amplification and deepfake distortion that have severely degraded the American information environment. SSI-verified participation means that civic engagement cannot be manufactured at scale by automated actors. Cryptographic provenance for candidate communications means that voters can distinguish authenticated candidate speech from fabricated content. Neither system is censorship — both are attribution infrastructure.

### A Mandate for Constitutional Change — The Capstone, Not the Launch Vehicle

The elements of this architecture that require constitutional change — primarily the Electoral College allocation mechanism and the presidential selection structure — need a democratic mandate that is itself legitimate and difficult to attack. But constitutional change is the final stage of this architecture, not the entry point. The statutory reforms proceed first, prove themselves in practice, and build the democratic legitimacy that makes constitutional ratification achievable.

The implementation sequence is deliberate. We establish the FEIC and the federal integrity floor. We pilot certified voting methods in willing states. We demonstrate that party recognition frameworks produce genuine civic formations rather than shell operations. We show that candidate nutrition labels work in practice and that their due-process protections are sufficient. We build congressional caucus structures that survive contact with Washington. Only after that foundation is proven do we seek public ratification of the constitutional elements.

We are proposing a mechanism through which Congress enacts the full reform package with severable titles subject to public ratification votes on defined election day ballots. Titles that receive the required affirmative vote become operative. Titles that do not expire permanently. The ratification mechanism operates within the contingent-legislation logic recognized in Field v. Clark (1892) — the public vote is the triggering condition Congress wrote into law it already enacted, not a separate act of legislation.

Where constitutional amendment is required, a public ratification vote on whether to authorize a scoped constitutional convention creates a democratic mandate that places legislators on the record — supporting or opposing what their constituents have explicitly approved. The convention's scope is defined in the authorizing resolution and limited specifically to electoral architecture: approved voting methods, presidential selection structure, Electoral College allocation, and party recognition framework. Any amendment proposed by the convention still requires ratification by three-quarters of states under Article V.

The convention is the capstone of a demonstrated system, not a speculative authorization for an untested one. A public that has watched the statutory reforms operate through several election cycles is in a far stronger position to authorize constitutional change than a public being asked to ratify a theoretical design. We are building toward the convention, not leading with it.

## Why We Are Writing to You

We are writing to the UN Electoral Assistance Division and our international partners for several specific reasons.

You have operational experience we lack. Several of the systems we are proposing — cardinal voting methods, independent electoral commissions, multi-party coalition governance, digital civic identity infrastructure — have been implemented at national scale in allied democracies and have decades of real-world performance data attached to them. The United States is, in most of these areas, starting from scratch. We would rather learn from your experience with implementation failures, unexpected second-order effects, and design choices that looked good on paper and proved difficult in practice than discover those problems ourselves.

Estonia's experience with digital national identity infrastructure is directly applicable to our SSI framework build-out. Germany's experience with coalition government mechanics is directly applicable to our congressional caucus reform design. New Zealand and Australia's experience with cardinal and preferential voting systems is directly applicable to our certified voting method menu. Taiwan's Digital Democracy platform is the most sophisticated operational example of the participatory infrastructure we are trying to build. The EU's experience with election observation methodology is applicable to our domestic audit and certification framework.

You provide external legitimacy that domestic actors cannot provide themselves. The previous administration's demonstrated willingness to call election results into question has created a political environment in which any reform proposed by domestic actors is immediately characterized as partisan manipulation, regardless of its actual design. International technical advisory engagement — particularly from the UN Electoral Assistance Division and allied democratic institutions — provides a legitimacy layer that is structurally different from domestic endorsement. When internationally recognized electoral experts review a system design and find it consistent with democratic best practice, that assessment is substantially harder for domestic bad actors to dismiss.

We are also aware that the previous administration's conduct has damaged the United States' international credibility as a stable democratic partner. Rebuilding that credibility requires demonstrating that the United States is serious about addressing the structural failures that made that conduct possible. Subjecting our proposed reforms to rigorous international technical review — and publishing those findings in full — is part of that demonstration.

We are not asking you to endorse our political choices. We are asking you to stress-test our institutional design. Tell us where you have seen similar designs fail. Tell us what we have not thought of. Tell us which of our assumptions about implementation are most likely to be wrong. Tell us whether our SSI identity framework is consistent with best practice in digital civic infrastructure. Tell us whether our independent electoral commission design has the anti-capture architecture that comparable commissions have learned, sometimes painfully, is necessary.

## The Specific Areas Where We Most Need Your Input

**Cardinal voting method implementation.** We are recommending STAR voting as the national standard for federal elections. We are aware that method transitions are difficult and that voter comprehension is a significant implementation challenge. What does the international experience with cardinal method adoption tell us about transition sequencing, voter education requirements, and the design choices that most affect successful implementation?

**Independent electoral commission design.** Our Federal Electoral Integrity Commission is designed with staggered nonrenewable terms, multi-body nomination processes, and strict anti-capture rules. What failure modes have comparable commissions experienced that we have not yet addressed? What design choices have proven most important for long-term independence?

**Multi-party system transition from a two-party baseline.** Most countries with multi-party systems either inherited them or built them from the beginning. Very few have successfully transitioned from entrenched two-party duopoly. What does the international experience with party system transitions tell us about the most dangerous failure modes in the early years? What institutional supports have proven most important for new parties to survive their first electoral cycles?

**Digital civic identity infrastructure.** Our SSI framework is designed to enable verified civic participation without centralized identity databases. Estonia's X-Road and digital identity infrastructure is the closest operational analogue. What have been the most significant security vulnerabilities, adoption barriers, and governance challenges in comparable implementations?

**Digital democracy platform design.** Taiwan's vTaiwan and Pol.is implementations are the most directly relevant international precedents for the participatory infrastructure we are proposing. What have been the limits of these systems in practice? Where have they succeeded in producing genuine policy signal and where have they been captured by organized advocacy or distorted by participation gaps?

**Electoral integrity during system transitions.** The period of transition from one electoral system to another is typically the highest-risk period for electoral integrity failures. What monitoring, audit, and certification practices have proven most effective during comparable transitions?

## What We Are Proposing as an Advisory Structure

We propose a formal technical advisory engagement structured around three phases aligned with our implementation timeline.

In the design review phase, we request written technical assessments of the four component briefs of the electoral reform package, with particular attention to the areas identified above. We welcome assessments that identify design flaws, recommend modifications, and flag known failure modes from comparable international implementations. All assessments will be published in full.

One area we specifically flag for design review is the due-process architecture of the Federal Electoral Integrity Commission's candidate label compilation function. Candidate nutrition labels will be politically consequential — a contested-status flag or a record-derived divergence notation can materially affect a campaign. Candidates must have standing to challenge factual errors, methodological misclassification, and contested-status designations through a fast, public, and bounded review process. We have identified this as a requirement but have not yet fully specified the challenge pathway. We are requesting international comparative experience with comparable independent commission record-compilation functions and the due-process mechanisms that have proven most effective at resolving disputes quickly without politicizing the commission itself.

In the pilot observation phase, as state-level pilots of certified voting methods and party recognition frameworks begin, we request observer participation from designated international electoral experts who can provide real-time technical assessment of implementation against international best practice standards. Observation findings will be published in full and made available to the Federal Electoral Integrity Commission and to Congress.

In the constitutional convention phase, if and when a scoped constitutional convention is authorized through the public ratification process, we request advisory participation from international experts in constitutional design and electoral system architecture to provide technical review of proposed amendments before they are submitted to state ratification.

The advisory engagement is explicitly non-binding throughout. All design decisions remain with American democratic institutions. We are requesting expertise and honest assessment, not authorization or endorsement.

To be explicit about scope: international advisors receive no access to voter-identifiable data, internal campaign data, ballot infrastructure, or operational election systems. Advisory engagement is limited to reviewing public design documents, anonymized pilot outputs, audit methodologies, and institutional architecture. This boundary is not a courtesy — it is a structural firewall that protects both the integrity of the advisory process and the political viability of the engagement domestically.

## A Final Note on Framing

We are aware that international advisory engagement in American electoral reform will be characterized by some domestic actors as inappropriate foreign interference. We want to address this directly.

The United States routinely provides electoral assistance and technical advisory support to emerging and transitioning democracies around the world. We do so because we believe that democratic expertise is a public good that should be shared across national boundaries, and because we believe that strong democratic institutions globally serve American interests as well as the interests of the countries receiving assistance. We are now asking to receive the same kind of technical expertise we have long provided to others.

This is not a concession of sovereignty. It is an acknowledgment that other democracies have solved problems we have not yet solved, and that intellectual humility in the face of that reality is a strength rather than a weakness. The alternative — insisting that American democratic institutions are beyond the need for external technical review — is precisely the kind of institutional arrogance that has allowed the current problems to compound for decades without adequate response.

We also want to be transparent about the strategic logic of how this reform is sequenced, because it has a direct bearing on how your advisory engagement fits in. The architecture is deliberately staged to reduce threat salience at each step. State party recognition reform is a states' rights argument — states should be able to have the parties their voters want. Congressional caucus bloc rights are an internal chamber governance question — elected members should be able to organize as they choose. Candidate nutrition labels are a transparency measure — voters deserve standardized information. None of these individually triggers the full defensive mobilization of entrenched actors, because none of them individually threatens the presidential duopoly in isolation.

By the time the architecture has produced functioning state parties, demonstrated congressional caucus blocs, and a proven label system operating across several election cycles, the constitutional reform layer addresses a system that already exists and has already built its own constituency of elected officials who won under it. The reform builds its own political support as it operates rather than requiring a single overwhelming coalition to defeat all opposition simultaneously. Your advisory engagement fits into this sequencing precisely: your most critical input comes during the design and pilot phases, before the architecture is locked in, which is also the phase of lowest political salience and therefore lowest resistance. By the time the constitutional layer is reached, the design review your advisors provide today will have already shaped a system that demonstrates itself credibly before it asks for ratification.

We are trying to build something that works. We want your help doing that.

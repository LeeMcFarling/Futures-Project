# Representation Fairness and Electoral Method Choice

## Purpose

The existing congressional district system has produced a race to the bottom. Whichever party controls a state legislature controls district boundaries — and both parties, wherever they have held power, have used that control to choose their voters rather than let voters choose them. The result is not a partisan failure of one side. It is a structural failure of a system that makes choosing your own electorate the rational move for any party in power.

This brief establishes a federal democratic integrity floor that ends that dynamic. It defines the minimum conditions every state's House election system must satisfy, and a certified menu of electoral methods through which states may satisfy them. The floor does not mandate any particular representation architecture. It makes the race-to-the-bottom move — using electoral rules to entrench existing power against competitive challenge — illegal regardless of which party attempts it and regardless of which method a state has chosen.

The core doctrinal claim is this: **winner-take-all single-member districts create structural incentives for manipulation that survive even honest mapmakers.** So long as geography determines representation exclusively, parties that control geography control outcomes. Independent redistricting processes produce fairer maps, but they still operate inside a system where every district is a winner-take-all monopoly and minority political formations — whether partisan, regional, or ideological — can hold substantial statewide support while winning zero seats. This brief addresses the incentive structure, not just the map.

---

## The Problem

Gerrymandering is a symptom of a deeper structural condition, not the disease itself. The disease is **winner-take-all geographic monopoly plus party control over entry rules**.

When every district produces a single winner by plurality or majority in a geographically defined area, three structural pathologies regenerate regardless of how fairly the maps are drawn.

**Duverger pressure.** First-past-the-post single-member districts produce two-party systems through mathematical inevitability, not through voter preference. Voters rationally abandon parties they prefer when those parties cannot win their district. The result is not a market of political ideas — it is a duopoly whose structure is sustained by electoral arithmetic rather than voter satisfaction with either available option. Polling consistently documents massive latent demand for alternatives to both major parties. That demand has nowhere to go in a winner-take-all system.

**Safe-seat primary distortion.** Safe districts breed ideological extremity. When general election outcomes are predetermined by geography and partisan registration, the only meaningful election is the primary — which is controlled by a small, activated, ideologically consistent minority of voters. Candidates rationally optimize for that minority, not for the broader electorate. The system does not produce extreme candidates because voters are extreme. It produces extreme candidates because the selection mechanism is designed, structurally, to reward extremity.

**Anti-competitive entry rules.** Established parties control the rules governing their own competition. Ballot access thresholds, filing deadlines, party recognition requirements, and district magnitude are all subject to legislative adjustment by the very parties that benefit from keeping them restrictive. Even with honest maps, the entry rules can be calibrated to make third-party and independent success effectively impossible. This is cartel behavior in regulatory clothing.

Independent redistricting processes are a genuine improvement within the existing system. This brief changes the system.

## Design Principle

**The federal government establishes the democratic integrity floor. States choose the electoral method inside it.**

States are not required to adopt any particular representation architecture. They are required to satisfy a set of anti-entrenchment standards — and to choose a certified method for doing so from a federally maintained menu. The metric is democratic outcome quality, not system uniformity.

This is a decentralization of method under a centralization of democratic guarantees. It is federalism functioning as intended: genuine diversity of approach within a framework that prevents failure modes from being laundered as local preference.

## The Federal Democratic Integrity Floor

Every federal House delegation must be produced under an electoral system that satisfies all of the following standards, certified by the Federal Electoral Integrity Commission before each election cycle.

**No partisan self-entrenchment.** The method may not allow the party in power at the state level to design rules — through district boundaries, list structure, threshold setting, recognition requirements, or any other mechanism — that predictably preserve its own advantage against competitive challenge. This is the core anti-cartel standard. It applies to both legacy parties and any new parties that achieve dominance in a given state.

**Proportionality floor.** The method must produce a delegation whose composition bears a reasonable relationship to the actual distribution of voter preferences across the state. What counts as reasonable is evaluated relative to delegation size and method feasibility — the FEIC establishes quantitative proportionality tolerance bands calibrated to each state's seat count and certified method before each cycle, not a uniform national standard. A state with twenty seats operating MMP is held to tighter proportionality expectations than a state with three seats operating independent districting, where seat granularity alone limits achievable proportionality. Extreme seat-vote distortions that a certified method could have prevented — where a party wins 40 percent of statewide votes but zero seats under a system that should have produced at least one — are disqualifying. Distortions that are mathematically unavoidable given delegation size and method constraints are flagged for reporting and improvement but do not trigger decertification.

**Voter access.** The method must not impose barriers to participation that fall disproportionately on voters based on race, ethnicity, language, income, or disability. This standard is continuous with the Voter ID and Registration Reform framework and the Anti-Discrimination provisions of the Electoral System Standardization brief.

**Candidate and party access.** Entry thresholds, filing deadlines, signature requirements, list composition rules, and party recognition requirements must not be designed to protect existing parties from competitive challenge. The FEIC certifies these rules before each cycle. Changes to threshold or access rules made within twenty-four months of an election trigger automatic heightened review and require affirmative FEIC certification before taking effect.

**Auditability and transparency.** The method must produce results that are publicly verifiable and machine-readable under the Unified Law and Regulation Repository standards. Seat allocation formulas must be disclosed in advance. Results must be traceable from individual precinct counts through final allocation by any citizen or researcher.

**Constituency accountability layer.** The method must produce a legible relationship between voters and the representatives who serve them. For proportional systems, this accountability layer is established through the constituency-service assignment architecture defined in the Representation Heatmap section of this brief. The layer must enable defined townhall accountability sessions, constituent contact routing, and the semiannual accountability loop established under the Candidate Nutrition Label framework.

**Minority representation protection.** The method must affirmatively protect meaningful representation for communities that have historically faced systematic dilution of their voting power. This standard applies continuously — a method that passes it initially but drifts toward dilutive outcomes through subsequent rule adjustment triggers renewed certification review.

## The Certified State Electoral Method Menu

States satisfy the integrity floor by selecting one of the following certified methods. States may petition the FEIC to certify additional methods not on this menu. The FEIC certifies whether a proposed method meets the integrity floor — it makes no political judgment about which method is preferable. That judgment belongs to the states.

### Mixed-Member Proportional Representation

Mixed-member proportional representation — MMP — gives voters two votes: one for a constituency representative in a geographically defined local district, and one for a party list that determines statewide seat allocation. The party list vote is the primary proportional allocator. Constituency seats are filled first. Compensatory or "top-up" seats are then allocated from party lists to bring the statewide delegation into proportional alignment with the party vote.

MMP preserves place-based constituency representation while correcting for the distortions that single-member district geography produces in statewide outcomes. Voters retain a local representative with a defined geographic accountability relationship. The statewide delegation reflects actual statewide voter preferences. These two values coexist rather than trading off against each other.

New Zealand adopted MMP by citizen referendum in 1993 after systematic two-party distortion under first-past-the-post produced years of governments whose seat share dramatically misrepresented their vote share. Germany's Bundestag has operated under a comparable MMP architecture since the postwar constitutional design, and has sustained stable multi-party coalition governance across eight decades. Both systems preserved constituency voting while producing proportional delegations.

For U.S. House elections, an MMP structure at the state level would allocate a portion of each state's House seats as constituency seats — filled through local district elections — and the remainder as compensatory list seats allocated to achieve proportionality. The precise split between constituency and compensatory seats is a state design parameter within federally certified bounds.

Under the certified MMP option, party lists must be open or semi-open — voters must have a meaningful mechanism to influence which candidates from each party receive list seats. Closed lists controlled entirely by party leadership are not certified under this framework. This is consistent with the anti-machine design of the candidate nutrition label architecture: voters evaluate actual candidates, records, and governing behavior, not only party brands.

### Open-List Proportional Representation

Open-list PR allocates seats to parties in proportion to their statewide vote share, but gives voters influence over which specific candidates within each party receive those seats. Voters cast a ballot for a candidate rather than (or in addition to) a party, and candidates within each party are ranked for seat allocation by the number of votes they personally receive.

This method eliminates the closed-list problem — the concentration of list-ordering power in party leadership — while preserving the proportionality that makes diverse political formations viable. A voter whose political home is a labor-industrial party in a manufacturing corridor can support that party proportionally while also signaling preference for the specific candidate whose regional record they find most credible.

Open-list PR is particularly well-suited to the Futures Project's representational theory. The Candidate Nutrition Label is designed around the premise that voters should evaluate individuals, not just brands. Open-list PR operationalizes that premise in the seat-allocation mechanism itself.

Several Nordic countries and the Netherlands operate open-list PR for national elections, producing multi-party legislatures that represent genuine voter distribution without party-machine capture of candidate selection.

### Proportional Ranked-Choice Voting in Multi-Member Districts — PR-STV

Proportional ranked-choice voting — formally called the Single Transferable Vote or STV when used to achieve proportional outcomes — allows voters to rank candidates across parties in multi-member constituencies. Seats are filled as candidates reach a proportional quota through first-preference votes and transfers. The result is a delegation that reflects voter preferences proportionally while giving voters candidate-centered choice rather than party-list choice.

Ireland uses PR-STV for every major election — general, local, European, presidential — and has done so since independence. The system produces proportional outcomes through direct voter ranking rather than party-determined lists, making it arguably the most voter-controlled of the proportional methods.

For U.S. states, PR-STV would require multi-member districts — regions that elect two to five representatives each through the ranked and transferable process. This preserves geographic accountability at a regional level (though not at single-district granularity) while producing proportional outcomes and eliminating party-machine control over candidate ranking.

PR-STV is the most complex of the certified methods from a ballot-design standpoint. States selecting it must demonstrate voter comprehension through a certified public education and ballot design review before implementation.

### Independent Nonpartisan Districting — Transitional and Fallback Certified Option

States may satisfy the integrity floor through a genuinely independent redistricting process meeting all federal certification standards. This option is available as a transitional pathway for states that have not yet implemented a proportional method, and as a permanent option for states where delegation size makes proportional methods impractical and the small-delegation cardinal primary option is not preferred.

The certification standard includes: commission independence with judicial-lottery member selection and conflict-of-interest restrictions barring elected office or party employment for defined periods before and after service; public map data in machine-readable format; open-source or publicly auditable mapping tools; public hearings distributed across diverse regions; minority representation protection; compactness and community-preservation criteria; partisan fairness testing using certified metrics including efficiency gap and partisan symmetry; a judicial challenge pathway with defined timelines; and emergency fallback maps where states fail to produce compliant maps before an election.

This option does not satisfy the proportionality floor through seat allocation. It satisfies it through competitive district design and partisan fairness testing. States selecting nonpartisan districting must demonstrate that their certified district maps produce delegation outcomes bearing a reasonable relationship to statewide vote distribution — not merely that the maps were drawn without partisan intent.

The structural limits of this option must be acknowledged plainly. In geographically polarized states, even good-faith independent commission maps cannot reliably produce proportional outcomes. Single-member districts concentrate representation in geographic majorities, and where partisan sorting has made geography and party affiliation nearly coextensive, seat-vote distortions will persist regardless of mapmaker integrity. This is a property of the method, not a failure of the commission.

Accordingly, states operating under independent nonpartisan districting are subject to heightened FEIC review when two or more consecutive certified election cycles produce seat-vote distortions exceeding the tolerance band for their delegation size. Heightened review does not automatically decertify the state's method — it triggers a structured transition assessment in which the FEIC evaluates whether a proportional method is feasible for that state's delegation size and produces a public report with findings. That report is advisory. The state retains method choice. But repeated distortion cycles under a certified nonpartisan districting system create a documented record that the method is structurally failing the proportionality floor, which informs both the FEIC certification decision and the state's own electorate.

### Statewide Cardinal Primary with Proportional Runoff Threshold — Small-Delegation Option

States whose total U.S. House delegation falls at or below a seat count the FEIC certifies as too small for multi-member proportional methods to produce meaningful proportionality may use a two-round statewide method designed to achieve comparable democratic outcomes within those constraints.

The underlying logic is straightforward: as delegation size shrinks, the minimum statewide support needed to earn a single seat rises, and seat granularity becomes too coarse for traditional proportional allocation to track actual voter distribution. The FEIC determines the delegation size at which this constraint becomes binding and publishes that threshold before each redistricting cycle. The threshold is not a fixed number written into statute — it is an FEIC-certified finding derived from seat count, delegation composition, and proportionality modeling, and it is subject to public comment before certification.

As an illustration of the underlying math: in a delegation of *n* seats, a rough viability threshold per seat is in the neighborhood of `1/(n+1)` of statewide support — meaning a single-seat state has a natural threshold around 50 percent, a two-seat state around 33 percent, and so on. The FEIC uses this relationship as a starting point for its viability modeling, not as a constitutional formula. The specific certified threshold for any state in any cycle is the FEIC's determination, informed by that math but not mechanically dictated by it.

The two-round method operates as follows.

In the primary round, all recognized parties and qualifying independent candidates compete in a single statewide election using an approved cardinal scoring method — STAR voting is the recommended default. Voters score all candidates across the full field.

Advancement to the general election is determined by an FEIC-certified viability threshold rather than a fixed top-*n* cutoff. Any candidate whose statewide support clears the certified threshold for that state's seat count advances automatically. If no candidate clears the threshold — possible in competitive fields — the top-scoring candidates by FEIC-certified tiebreak criteria advance, with a minimum of two finalists. The number of general election finalists is variable and derived from voter expression, not legislatively fixed. This prevents a fixed cutoff from arbitrarily eliminating a candidate with genuine majority-level support simply because two others happened to score slightly higher.

In the general election, all advanced candidates appear on the ballot and voters score or rank among them using the state's certified method. The winner or winners — one per seat — are determined by that method's allocation rules.

States above the FEIC-certified size threshold must use one of the full proportional methods or independent nonpartisan districting. States at or below it may use this option or, if their delegation is large enough to support it, may choose any method from the full menu instead.

## Anti-Entrenchment Architecture

The integrity floor prevents existing parties from using the rules governing elections to preserve their own advantages. Anti-entrenchment enforcement is the most operationally demanding element of this framework, because established parties will seek to satisfy the letter of certification requirements while designing threshold structures, access rules, and timing provisions that effectively maintain duopoly control.

The FEIC's anti-entrenchment mandate extends across the following specific vectors.

**Threshold calibration.** Every proportional system has either formal thresholds or effective mathematical thresholds produced by district magnitude and seat count. A state with eight House seats has a natural threshold of roughly 12.5 percent before allocation formula effects. Formal thresholds at or below this natural level serve genuine fragmentation-prevention purposes. Formal thresholds significantly above the natural level serve entrenchment purposes. The FEIC sets maximum permissible formal thresholds calibrated to each state's seat count. No formal threshold may be set at a level that would have prevented any party from receiving representation in any of the prior three election cycles under actual statewide vote share results.

**Ballot access timing.** Filing deadlines and petition requirements may not be structured to give established parties a meaningful head start on ballot placement. Standardized filing windows apply uniformly to all recognized parties and qualifying independent candidates under the framework established in the Electoral System Standardization brief.

**List composition rules.** For methods that involve party lists, list composition rules — minimum candidate requirements, ordering procedures, gender or demographic representation requirements — must be applied uniformly across all recognized parties. Established parties may not use list rule requirements as barriers against newly recognized parties that lack established infrastructure.

**District magnitude manipulation.** For MMP and PR-STV implementations, the ratio of constituency seats to compensatory seats, and the size of multi-member districts, must be set before the first implementation cycle and subject to a defined minimum stability period before adjustment. Changes to district magnitude that would predictably reduce the proportionality of outcomes trigger heightened review.

**Recognition threshold gaming.** Party recognition requirements — membership thresholds, small-donor breadth requirements, governance filing requirements — are set under the State Party Formation and Recognition framework. The FEIC monitors whether recognition requirements are being adjusted in ways that target newly emerging parties while established parties are grandfathered. Any recognition requirement change proposed by a state legislature that contains a carveout or transitional provision favoring currently recognized parties is automatically referred for heightened review.

**Allocation formula gaming.** The choice of seat allocation formula is itself an attack surface. D'Hondt tends to favor larger parties relative to Sainte-Laguë; overhang rules can inflate majority-party seat counts beyond their vote share; leveling seat structures can be calibrated to benefit or penalize specific party sizes; apparentement and fusion rules can be written to advantage established parties capable of coordinating list linkages; and independent candidate treatment rules can be structured to suppress unaffiliated competition. All seat allocation formulas and associated rules — including overhang treatment, leveling seat design, list linkage rules, and independent candidate allocation — must be pre-certified by the FEIC before each cycle. Certification requires public simulation of the proposed formula against the prior three election cycles' actual vote share data, stress-testing for systematic advantage to parties currently holding seats, and public comment before the formula is finalized. A formula change that would have materially improved outcomes for the party proposing it in any of the prior three cycles triggers automatic heightened review. The FEIC must affirmatively certify that the change does not materially advantage established parties before it takes effect. Absent affirmative certification, the prior certified rules remain in effect for that election cycle.

## Constituency-Service Assignment and the Representation Heatmap

The most persistent concern about proportional representation in the American context is the loss of place-based accountability. In a single-member district system, voters know who represents them. The representative holds a defined geographic constituency. The townhall, the constituent services office, the semiannual accountability session — all of these are geographically anchored.

Proportional systems are often understood to dissolve this anchor. They do not have to.

This brief establishes **constituency-service assignment** as the mechanism through which proportional delegation retains a geographic accountability layer. It operates as follows.

After proportional seat allocation determines which parties and candidates have won seats in a state's House delegation, a constituency-service geography is assigned to each representative based on where that party's electoral support is most concentrated. If a Green party formation earns a seat in Colorado through MMP or open-list PR, and its strongest support concentration is in Boulder, Denver, and surrounding Front Range precincts, those geographies become the representative's designated constituency-service territory. If a labor-industrial formation earns a seat whose support is most concentrated in Pueblo, Adams County, and the southern manufacturing corridor, that geography becomes its representative's constituency-service territory.

This assignment is **not a second election and has no effect on seat allocation**. It is a post-allocation accountability mechanism — a way of making visible where each representative's democratic mandate is geographically rooted and ensuring that the Candidate Nutrition Label's constituency accountability architecture remains operational under proportional delegation.

The constituency-service geography is published as a representation heatmap — a public map showing vote share distribution by party, seat allocation results, representative assignment, and underserved or low-representation clusters across the state. The map is informational, not exclusionary. A representative assigned to a specific accountability geography is not prohibited from serving constituents elsewhere — but their designated townhall locations, constituent services presence, and semiannual accountability sessions under the Candidate Nutrition Label framework are concentrated in that geography.

The assignment algorithm operates under a defined objective function: minimize statewide constituent-service distance across all assignments simultaneously, maximize alignment between each representative's vote concentration geography and their assigned service territory, and preserve assignment continuity across cycles unless a party's electoral support has meaningfully shifted in geographic distribution. These three objectives are weighted in that order. The algorithm is run as a constrained optimization across all seats in the state's delegation simultaneously — not assigned seat by seat — so that no single representative's geography is determined without accounting for the full assignment problem.

The algorithm must also resolve three specific conflict conditions. When two representatives from the same party draw support from overlapping geographies — possible when a party earns multiple seats through open-list PR and its candidates concentrated their campaigns in the same region — assignment is determined by relative vote concentration at the precinct level, with each representative assigned the geography where their personal vote share is highest relative to their co-partisan. When a party's support is diffuse rather than geographically clustered — distributed broadly without clear density peaks — the representative is assigned a geographic anchor centered on the state's highest-density support precinct cluster, with a service territory radius set to include a minimum defined constituent population. When a representative is elected through personal open-list votes that cut across their party's geographic support pattern — their individual vote geography diverges from their party's aggregate geography — the assignment follows the individual candidate's vote geography rather than the party's, since the open-list mechanism reflects a personal rather than party mandate.

Assignment outputs are published in full with supporting precinct-level data so that any citizen, researcher, or party can verify that the objective function was correctly applied.

For members elected through proportional systems, their Candidate Nutrition Label profile includes both party and caucus affiliation and their assigned constituency-service geography, displayed as a map layer alongside their governing theory, voting record, and mandate contribution data. The Digital Democracy Platform routes constituent signals by representative assignment geography, ensuring that a citizen in Pueblo who contacts the platform about a manufacturing policy issue reaches the representative whose assigned geography includes their location, not whichever representative happens to respond fastest.

The representation heatmap is maintained by the FEIC and updated after each election cycle. It is machine-readable, publicly auditable, and integrated directly into the Digital Democracy Platform's constituent-routing architecture. It is not maintained by any political party. The algorithm that assigns constituency-service geographies from precinct-level support data is open source, publicly documented, and subject to public comment before each cycle.

## Voluntary Adoption and the National Political Market Opening Act

States are not compelled to adopt any particular certified method. Compulsion is neither constitutionally necessary nor politically viable as a first-order move. What is necessary is a mechanism that makes adoption of certified proportional methods genuinely attractive — and a clear statement of what is and is not voluntary.

**Method choice is voluntary. Floor compliance is mandatory.** States may choose any certified method from the menu. They are not required to choose proportional representation. What they are required to do is operate a system that passes the integrity floor's anti-entrenchment standards. A state that repeatedly fails certification — producing systematic seat-vote distortions, manipulating thresholds, or structuring access rules to protect incumbents — is not being compelled to adopt PR. It is being compelled to stop running a system that demonstrably fails the floor. Those are different claims, and the distinction matters both legally and politically. The integrity floor does not select a winner among methods. It defines the conditions under which any method remains certified.

The voluntary adoption pathway works through two mechanisms: incentives for states that opt into certified proportional methods, and a national mandate activation mechanism that can convert voluntary adoption into a broader democratic commitment.

### State Adoption Incentives

States that adopt a certified proportional delegation method receive priority access to federal election modernization infrastructure. Specific incentive mechanisms include:

Federal election administration support funds at an enhanced rate for the first two implementation cycles, covering ballot design, voter education, audit infrastructure build-out, and results reporting system upgrades.

Priority access to SSI voter-roll modernization under the Voter ID and Registration Reform framework, which is being sequenced for deployment and whose rollout priority is set by the FEIC in consultation with DoDA.

Open-source election software and ballot design support developed under the DoDA technical assistance program, reducing the implementation cost of new methods for state election administrators who currently lack the technical capacity to design compliant systems independently.

Integration into the Digital Democracy Platform's representation heatmap and constituent-routing infrastructure from the first election cycle under the new method, rather than after a waiting period.

Pilot status designation for candidate nutrition label integration, giving states that adopt proportional methods early access to the integrated label-heatmap-accountability loop before it is fully deployed nationally.

These incentives are not trivial. Election administration modernization is expensive. States operating under legacy systems face real implementation costs when adopting new methods. Federal support that covers those costs in exchange for adoption of certified methods creates a genuine incentive without mandating the method.

### The National Mandate Pathway — Activation Without Compulsion

The National Referendum Mechanism brief establishes a structure in which Congress may pass legislation that becomes operative only through subsequent public activation votes. That structure was designed primarily for democratic integrity reforms facing institutional capture. It applies directly here.

Congress may pass a **Representation and Political Market Opening Act** whose titles activate separately through public ratification votes. The act's structure would include some titles that take immediate effect on passage — the FEIC anti-entrenchment certification mandate, the threshold calibration rules, the twenty-four-month pre-election rule-change review requirement — and other titles whose activation is contingent on public ratification.

The public ratification question is framed precisely. It does not ask: "Do you want more political parties?" That framing is too abstract, too easily demagogued, and does not accurately describe the reform.

The question is: "Should states be permitted and supported to adopt certified electoral systems that make legislative representation more closely reflect voter preferences, while preserving local accountability standards?"

This framing is accurate. It is also considerably harder to oppose than "proportional representation" as an abstraction. It asks whether states should have the option, not whether every state must exercise it. It ties the reform to the Futures Project's consistent principle — state choice within a federal floor — rather than requiring voters to endorse a specific electoral system they may not fully understand.

Titles covering the state adoption incentive package, the FEIC proportional certification process, and the representation heatmap infrastructure are natural candidates for activation-contingent treatment. Their activation follows a demonstrated public mandate rather than legislative determination alone, giving the reform a democratic legitimacy record that is very difficult for future bad actors to dismiss or legislate away.

States that are most likely to adopt early are those where one-party geographic dominance has produced frustrated minorities with no viable representation pathway, and where intra-majority factions have been suppressed by safe-seat primary dynamics. Both conditions are common across the political spectrum — there are meaningful populations in Massachusetts, Maryland, and Oregon who cannot effectively access their own delegation, just as there are in Tennessee, Oklahoma, and South Carolina. Proportional delegation lets geographic minorities in all of these states achieve representation proportional to their actual statewide support. That is a strange-bedfellows reform coalition that crosses every conventional partisan line.

## Relationship to Party Formation and the Congressional Proving Ground

The representation fairness architecture is not independent of the party formation and congressional caucus frameworks. It is the mechanism through which party formation produces actual representation.

The State Party Formation and Recognition brief establishes a genuine political market entry pathway. A party that satisfies the recognition requirements — breadth of membership, small-donor base, transparent governance, financial disclosure, anti-collusion compliance — is a legitimate political actor. But recognition without a viable path to representation is symbolic market entry, not real political competition.

Under a winner-take-all single-member district system, a newly recognized party with 15 percent statewide support can field candidates in every district and win zero seats. That is not a political market. That is a market where entry is permitted and competition is structurally precluded.

Under a certified proportional method, 15 percent statewide support produces roughly proportional representation in the state's delegation. The party earns seats. Its members arrive in Washington. Congressional caucus blocs form. Presidential caucus blocs emerge from demonstrated congressional alignment. The chain that the Party Formation brief and Presidential Selection Architecture brief describe becomes operational.

This is why the representation fairness reform is architecturally prior to the full development of the congressional caucus and presidential selection systems. Those systems require a proportional representation layer to generate the diverse congressional membership that makes genuine multi-party caucus formation possible. Without it, the political market opening that party formation establishes is theoretical. With it, party formation converts directly into representation and then into legislative coalition formation.

The implementation sequencing therefore follows this order: FEIC establishment and integrity floor enforcement operate immediately on passage. State adoption of certified proportional methods begins with early adopters in the first two election cycles after the statutory foundation is in place. Congressional caucus diversity develops as proportional delegations begin producing members from new parties. Presidential caucus blocs emerge from that congressional alignment. The full architecture becomes operational over two to three cycles rather than on day one — but it becomes operational because each layer was built in sequence rather than assumed simultaneously.

## Relationship to the Candidate Nutrition Label

The constituency-service assignment system is the electoral counterpart to the Digital Democracy Platform's constituent-signal routing architecture. The platform is designed to capture constituent signal, route concerns to the appropriate level of government, and support semiannual accountability sessions grounded in certified data. The representation heatmap is what makes that routing work under proportional delegation.

A representative elected through open-list PR in a state that does not draw single-member districts still needs a legible accountability geography. Their Candidate Nutrition Label profile needs to show constituents where they are anchored. The heatmap provides that anchor. The platform routes signals using it. The semiannual townhall sessions are concentrated in the designated geography. The accountability loop is operational.

For members elected through constituency seats in MMP states, the existing Nutrition Label constituency accountability architecture applies directly — they have a defined single-member district and the standard accountability obligations of that structure. For members elected through compensatory list seats, the heatmap assignment provides the equivalent geographic anchor.

The Nutrition Label must be updated to include a representation geography field — a map layer showing the member's assigned constituency-service territory alongside their party and caucus affiliation, governing theory declaration, voting record, and mandate contribution data. This is an additive update to the Label schema, not a structural revision.

## Implementation Phasing

This brief supersedes the prior Anti-Gerrymandering brief. Its implementation follows the phasing established in the Electoral System Standardization brief, with the following specific additions.

**Phase 1 — Statutory Foundation.** Congress establishes the FEIC and its anti-entrenchment certification mandate. The federal integrity floor takes effect for all federal House elections. The threshold calibration rules and pre-election rule-change review requirements activate immediately. The certified method menu is published. States currently operating under independent nonpartisan redistricting processes satisfying the certification standards in this brief are in compliance with the integrity floor and need not transition unless they choose to.

**Phase 2 — Voluntary Adoption and Pilot Operation.** States opt into certified proportional methods. The FEIC certifies first-cycle proportional delegation designs. Federal support funds flow to adopting states. The Digital Democracy Platform deploys representation heatmap integration for adopting states. Voter education infrastructure is built and deployed through DoDA's technical assistance program. The National Referendum Mechanism is used to refer the Representation and Political Market Opening Act's activation-contingent titles to public ratification if Congress has passed the act.

**Phase 3 — Congressional Caucus Development.** As proportional state delegations produce diverse congressional membership, congressional caucus blocs form and develop legislative track records. The FEIC tracks caucus formation, coalition agreement filings, and voting alignment on an ongoing basis. The candidate nutrition label database accumulates the coalition map that presidential caucus bloc formation will eventually require.

**Phase 4 — Presidential Architecture Integration.** Presidential caucus blocs emerge from demonstrated congressional alignment. The full architecture — state party formation, proportional representation, congressional caucus development, presidential caucus bloc formation, national primary, general election — operates as an integrated system.

## Open Questions

Should the FEIC's proportionality tolerance bands be set by statute or by regulatory rulemaking with public comment?

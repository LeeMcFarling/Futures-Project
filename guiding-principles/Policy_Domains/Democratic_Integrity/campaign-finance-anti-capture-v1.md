# Campaign Finance Anti-Capture: Statutory Closure, Public Mandate, and Constitutional Repair

## Purpose

This brief defines the three-track strategy for closing the post-*Citizens United* campaign finance regime: first through ordinary statute, then through NRM public mandate, and finally through constitutional amendment where current doctrine prevents statutory repair.

This brief is a companion to the Campaign Finance Reform brief, which establishes the National Democracy Fund, the Democracy Fund Portal, the tiered individual donation architecture, and the small-donor matching system. Those instruments are the positive architecture — building a citizen-funded system. This brief is the anti-capture architecture — systematically closing the avenues through which concentrated money bypasses, undermines, or dominates that system.

The core claim is this: money-in-politics reform should not depend on pretending existing doctrine is friendly. The reform should legislate as far as current doctrine permits, force a clear litigation record where doctrine blocks it, generate a documented public mandate, and use that mandate to pursue constitutional repair. Each track is independently valuable. Together they become a compounding strategy.

---

## The Problem

The campaign finance system no longer merely funds elections. It creates dependency markets between officeholders, donors, outside spending networks, PACs, nonprofits, consultants, platform advertisers, and regulated industries. The problem is not only corruption in the classic sense of explicit quid pro quo. It is structural dependence: elected officials operate inside a financing environment where access, viability, message amplification, and political survival depend on concentrated money. The policy consequences follow from that dependence even without any explicit exchange.

*Citizens United v. FEC* (2010) and its doctrinal descendants established that political spending is protected speech under the First Amendment and that the government's anti-corruption interest does not justify limiting independent expenditures by corporations and other entities. The Court held that independent expenditures — money spent separately from campaigns without formal coordination — cannot corrupt because there is no coordination and therefore no quid pro quo. This reasoning makes ordinary statutory reform incomplete and vulnerable.

The result is a two-tier system. Inside the formal campaign structure, contribution limits apply and disclosure is required. Outside it, in the independent expenditure and dark money ecosystem, unlimited sums can flow through layered nonprofit and corporate structures with minimal disclosure, no contribution limits, and no accountability to the voters being targeted.

If you want to influence the government through money, the current system lets you do it at industrial scale through artificial entities designed for that purpose. The reform premise is simple: if you want to shape American governance, be a citizen and participate as one. Run for office. Volunteer. Donate within the individual limits the Campaign Finance brief establishes. What you may not do is use a corporation, union, nonprofit, or other artificial entity as a vehicle to amplify your financial power beyond what any individual citizen could deploy, or to obscure who is actually behind the spending.

---

## Design Principle

**Legislate where possible. Mandate where blocked. Amend where doctrine prevents democratic repair.**

The three tracks are connected but severable. The statute does not wait for the amendment. The amendment does not wait for litigation to be exhausted. The public mandate is prepared in advance so that obstruction — whether congressional or judicial — can be converted into constitutional momentum rather than becoming a dead end. Each track failure creates the conditions for the next track's success.

## Lawful Advocacy and Corrupt Influence — Rules of Construction

This Act is governed by a clear distinction between lawful political advocacy and corrupt influence architecture. That distinction is pathway-based, not viewpoint-based. The law does not regulate what is said. It regulates the concealed funding structures, artificial-entity amplifiers, and hidden access markets through which concentrated money converts itself into governing leverage.

**Lawful advocacy includes:** natural-person political speech; unpaid volunteer activity; bona fide press activity; public testimony; written comments in regulatory proceedings; ordinary civic association and organizing; registered lobbying through disclosed channels; participation in recorded public policy forums under the lobbying credit system; certified NRM committee activity; and campaign or party spending through regulated accounts funded by verified individual contributions processed through the Democracy Fund Portal.

**Corrupt influence is not defined by viewpoint, ideology, party alignment, or policy preference.** It is defined by structure and method: concealed funding; beneficial-owner laundering through intermediary entities; artificial-entity amplification outside authorized pathways; coordinated spending disguised as independence; private access purchased through hospitality, financial consideration, or bundled arrangements; undisclosed third-party intermediaries; ex parte regulatory pressure where public channels are required; and any attempt to convert financial power into preferential government access outside the pathways expressly authorized by law.

This Act shall not be construed to prohibit political opinion, ideological advocacy, criticism of public officials, independent journalism, unpaid individual expression, volunteer organizing, or ordinary civic petitioning. It shall be construed to prohibit shadow influence systems that conceal who is speaking, who is paying, who is coordinating, and what access or official action is being sought.

### Legislative Findings on Corruption Pathways

Congress finds that corruption in modern campaign finance and lobbying does not occur only through explicit quid pro quo exchange. It also occurs through dependency markets, concealed sponsorship, coordinated outside spending, access brokerage, revolving-door compensation, platform amplification, private hospitality networks, and artificial-entity spending structures that make elected officials dependent on concentrated money for political survival.

Congress further finds that distinguishing lawful advocacy from corrupt influence requires attention to the pathway through which influence is exerted. Public, attributable, reportable, nonexclusive participation is lawful. Concealed, preferential, financially conditioned, or artificially amplified access is corrupt or corruption-adjacent and may be regulated to protect democratic self-governance.

Congress further finds that the lobbying credit system, Democracy Fund Portal, and beneficial-owner disclosure requirements established by this Act create a complete set of lawful pathways for institutional policy input. Institutional actors who use those pathways are engaged in lawful advocacy. Institutional actors who circumvent those pathways through shadow channels are engaged in conduct this Act is designed to prevent.

The public statement of this doctrine is simple: **Say what you want. Advocate for what you want. Petition government. But do it in public, through lawful channels, without laundering money or buying hidden access.**

## The Regulated Pathways Doctrine

Before the statutory tracks are described individually, the operating rule of the entire framework should be stated plainly.

**No money, in-kind support, data service, advertising, electioneering communication, coordinated activity, discounted vendor service, platform amplification purchase, nonprofit pass-through, donor-advised transfer, or artificial-entity expenditure may be used to influence a federal election except through a pathway expressly authorized by this Act.**

The permitted pathways are:

- Verified individual contributions processed through approved campaign committee accounts under the tiered limits established in the Campaign Finance Reform brief
- Candidate committee expenditures funded through those verified contributions
- Recognized party accounts funded by verified individual contributions under the party formation framework
- National Democracy Fund small-donor matching disbursements
- Certified NRM public mandate committees operating under the disclosure and spending architecture of this Act
- Protected unpaid individual political expression, volunteer activity, and bona fide press activity

Any spending, coordination, or in-kind support that does not fit within a permitted pathway is presumptively prohibited and subject to the enforcement and penalty structure of this Act. This architecture is not a list of things that are banned. It is a definition of what is allowed, with everything else defaulting to prohibited. That inversion of the default is the structural core of the anti-capture regime.

## Track One — Statutory Anti-Capture Reform

The statutory track passes the strongest anti-capture legislation Congress can plausibly defend under current doctrine. It is not drafted under the fantasy that every provision survives judicial review. It is drafted to make as much law as possible operative immediately while forcing courts to identify, with precision, which anti-capture tools current doctrine forbids. That precision is valuable: it clarifies exactly what the constitutional amendment must restore.

### Coordination Redefinition

The most important single statutory move is expanding the legal definition of coordination between campaigns and outside spenders. Under current FEC regulations, coordination is defined narrowly by reference to formal communication and prior approval. The statutory track redefines it as a rebuttable presumption triggered by a conjunction of factors.

An outside expenditure is presumptively coordinated — and therefore subject to contribution limits regardless of its formal structure — when two or more of the following factors are present: shared vendors or data infrastructure with the candidate committee; timing that closely parallels the campaign's own advertising activity; message content substantially similar to campaign-produced materials; use of material non-public information about campaign strategy; overlap between the outside spender's organizational leadership and campaign fundraising or advisory circles; or any request, suggestion, or signal from the candidate or their agents regarding the expenditure.

The presumption is rebuttable. The spending entity may demonstrate independence by showing that the expenditure was made without knowledge of the campaign's strategic plans, through entirely separate vendors and data systems, without any overlap in personnel, and in a manner that reflects the spender's own independent political judgment rather than strategic integration with the campaign.

This structure is designed to read as anti-evasion rather than thought-policing. No single factor — shared demographics, ideological alignment, general support for a candidate's platform — triggers the presumption. The trigger requires a pattern of factors that, taken together, indicate strategic integration rather than parallel independent activity. This narrows the litigation target while substantially shrinking the space for nominally independent expenditures that are functionally campaign extensions.

### Real-Time Disclosure and Beneficial Ownership

All political expenditures in federal elections — including independent expenditures, electioneering communications, and dark-money pass-throughs — must be disclosed through the Democracy Fund Portal within 24 hours of commitment. Disclosure covers the true beneficial owner of the funds: the natural persons whose money ultimately funds the expenditure, traced through any number of intermediary entities. Layered nonprofit and LLC structures that obscure the original source of funds are treated as coordination evasion and subject to the contribution limit framework.

This provision is constitutionally solid. *Citizens United* and *Buckley v. Valeo* both explicitly upheld disclosure requirements. The novel element here is requiring disclosure of the beneficial owner through the full ownership chain, rather than stopping at the immediate donor to the expenditure vehicle.

### Natural-Person Requirement for Political Expenditures

All independent expenditures and electioneering communications in federal elections must be funded through verified individual-source accounts and regulated committees — meaning the funds must be traceable to natural persons who are U.S. citizens, processed through pathways authorized under the Regulated Pathways Doctrine. No corporate treasury funds, no union treasury funds, no nonprofit organizational funds, no foreign national funds may be used directly or indirectly to fund political expenditures.

This provision distinguishes funding source from association and speech. It does not say organizations may not hold political views, endorse candidates, or engage in political activity. It says that federal election spending by artificial entities must be funded through verified individual-source accounts, not through pooled organizational treasury funds that aggregate financial power beyond what any individual contributor could deploy. The associational and speech interests of corporations and other entities are acknowledged; the argument is that those interests do not extend to the use of treasury funds — accumulated for non-political commercial or organizational purposes — as financial amplifiers in elections.

PACs and other expenditure vehicles must certify that all funds used for political expenditures derive from verified individual contributions processed through approved channels.

### Dark-Money Pass-Through Ban

Nonprofit organizations, trade associations, and other tax-exempt entities may not serve as pass-through vehicles for political expenditures. An organization that makes political expenditures must disclose the full funding chain to the Democracy Fund Portal, including all donors above a defined threshold whose contributions funded the political activity. Shell-entity and intermediary structures designed to obscure the original source of funds are treated as evasion and subject to criminal referral.

### Foreign-Money Firewall

The absolute foreign-money ban established in the Campaign Finance brief applies with full force: no foreign national, foreign government, foreign corporation, or foreign-controlled entity may contribute to, coordinate with, or fund independent expenditures in any federal election. This provision is the strongest constitutionally, faces the least litigation risk, and establishes the clearest moral foundation for the rest of the framework.

### Contractor and Regulated-Entity Restrictions

Federal contractors and entities under active federal regulatory supervision may not make political expenditures in excess of defined limits during periods when their contracts or regulatory proceedings are active. The government's interest in preventing the quid pro quo corruption of its own procurement and regulatory processes is the strongest available basis for this restriction.

### Democracy Fund Portal as Universal Infrastructure

The Democracy Fund Portal established under the Campaign Finance brief becomes the mandatory reporting and routing infrastructure for all political expenditures, not just campaign contributions. Independent expenditures, electioneering communications, and dark-money organization spending must flow through or be reported to the Portal in real time. The Portal publishes a public influence network map showing which natural persons, through what chains of entities, are funding which political expenditures on which races.

### Severability and Expedited Judicial Review

The statute includes explicit severability: if any provision is struck, the remainder operates independently. It also includes an expedited judicial review provision establishing a fast-track 90-day review pathway in the D.C. Circuit with direct appeal and expedited certiorari petition procedures to the Supreme Court under procedures Congress is constitutionally authorized to prescribe. The goal is forcing judicial clarity before the next election cycle rather than allowing provisions to operate in legal limbo across multiple cycles — not compelling the Supreme Court to grant merits review, which Congress cannot do, but creating procedural incentives that make expedited resolution more likely.

### Comparative Design Note

Democracies with lower money-in-politics distortion generally rely on public party financing, transparent reporting requirements, party-centered finance structures, lower dependence on candidate-specific advertising, and stricter treatment of anonymous or foreign influence. The U.S. version of campaign finance reform cannot simply import that model because current First Amendment doctrine protects independent expenditures in ways that most comparable constitutional frameworks do not. This brief therefore combines public-finance logic — the National Democracy Fund and small-donor matching architecture — with a regulated-pathways anti-evasion statute and a constitutional repair track. The comparative lesson is not that the Scandinavian or German model is directly transplantable; it is that functioning democracies have found ways to prevent financial capture without suppressing political speech, and that the U.S. can do the same within its own constitutional tradition once that tradition is updated to reflect current realities.

## Track One-A — Regulated Lobbying Access Market

Closing the shadow channels of institutional influence is necessary but not sufficient. Corporations, unions, trade associations, nonprofits, and other institutional actors retain legitimate interests in petitioning government, providing technical information, and advocating for policy positions. Those interests do not disappear when private access markets are restricted — they find new channels, some of which are harder to detect and regulate than the ones being closed. The regulated lobbying access market is the replacement pathway: a lawful, transparent, publicly priced, nonexclusive, auditable channel for institutional policy input that converts private influence-seeking into public, recorded participation.

The doctrine: **institutional actors may petition government, provide technical information, and advocate for policy. They may not purchase private influence. The lobbying credit system converts institutional access into a public, priced, recorded, nonexclusive, auditable channel.**

This belongs inside the statutory anti-capture track — not as a separate enforcement mechanism, but as the affirmative replacement for the shadow channels being closed. The anti-capture statute restricts; the lobbying credit system redirects. Together they eliminate the current shadow access economy and replace it with something visible, structured, and democratically accountable.

### How the System Works

Corporations, unions, trade associations, nonprofits, professional associations, and other artificial entities seeking structured policy input purchase **Lobbying Access Credits** through the Democracy Fund Portal. Credits do not fund candidates, parties, caucuses, or campaigns. They are paid into a public Civic Deliberation Fund — a dedicated account within the Democracy Fund architecture — and entitle the purchaser only to defined forms of public participation: recorded policy forums, technical roundtables, written submissions, legislative staff briefings, agency consultation sessions, and implementation hearings.

Every credit purchase, participating entity, beneficial owner, policy topic, forum date, attendee list, written submission, and follow-up contact is publicly logged, machine-readable, and linked to the relevant bill, rulemaking, or oversight matter in the Democracy Fund Portal. The portal publishes a real-time influence-activity map showing which entities are purchasing access to which policy domains, how much they are spending, and what submissions they are making.

### What Credits Buy and Do Not Buy

Credits buy **participation**, not outcomes.

They do not authorize private one-on-one meetings with elected officials or their staff outside the recorded forum structure. They do not authorize preferential treatment in procurement, enforcement, licensing, grants, or regulatory decisions. They do not permit ex parte regulatory influence outside documented channels. They do not allow campaign contributions, party donations, or PAC transfers. They do not displace citizen, labor, small-business, or public-interest participation. They do not purchase exclusive access windows — any entity may participate in a forum regardless of whether another entity has purchased credits for the same session.

### Revenue and Countervailing Capacity

Revenue from the Lobbying Access Credit system is dedicated exclusively to funding public-interest counterweight capacity. The stronger the institutional demand for access, the more the system funds the public's ability to scrutinize that access.

Funded uses include: technical counsel for citizen groups and public-interest organizations; small-business and labor participation grants; community policy clinics; independent public-interest research staff; FEIC and Democracy Fund Portal transparency infrastructure; travel and participation stipends for non-corporate participants; DoDA-reviewed policy impact briefs covering competing claims; open public hearing technology and simultaneous translation; and plain-language summaries of technical submissions made available to all participants and the public simultaneously.

### Sectoral Caps

In any given policy domain, no entity or affiliated group of entities may purchase more than a defined maximum share of total available access credits per legislative or regulatory cycle. This prevents the credit system from becoming "richest sector dominates the hearing."

Sectoral caps operate at the domain level. In a healthcare policy forum, hospital systems, insurers, pharmaceutical companies, physician groups, labor unions, patient advocates, rural providers, and public-interest researchers each operate within defined participation lanes. A hospital system may purchase credits to participate, but it cannot purchase enough credits to crowd out other voices. The **Public Lobbying Access Office** publishes sector definitions and cap levels before each cycle through public rulemaking with comment, subject to FEIC review where the forum concerns election law or democratic-integrity legislation. This keeps the FEIC within its electoral mandate while ensuring an independent body administers the access market across all other policy domains.

Small entities, labor groups, civic associations, and public-interest organizations receive free or subsidized participation credits funded through the Civic Deliberation Fund revenue stream. This is structural, not discretionary — the subsidy is a fixed fraction of credit revenue, not a grant program subject to annual appropriations pressure.

### Petition-Right Safe Harbor

Nothing in the lobbying credit system prohibits unpaid petitioning, public testimony, written comments, ordinary civic advocacy, or registered lobbying through disclosed channels. Credits apply only to structured institutional access channels created by statute. They do not condition the constitutional right to petition government on payment. An individual citizen, community organization, or small civic association may appear at any public policy forum, submit written comments, and contact elected officials and their staff through ordinary channels without purchasing credits and without restriction under this Act.

The lobbying credit system is lawful and legitimate only if it prevents, rather than formalizes, pay-to-play. The following rules are structural and non-negotiable:

No private meetings may be purchased through credits. No campaign, party, PAC, or candidate receives any portion of credit revenue. No procurement, enforcement, licensing, grant, or regulatory decision may be discussed outside publicly recorded channels. No exclusive access windows may be purchased. No entity may purchase more than the capped share of total access credits in a policy domain. Small entities, labor groups, civic associations, and public-interest organizations receive free or subsidized participation credits by right, not at the discretion of any official. All forums must include public-interest counterparty representation where feasible. All submissions are public, machine-readable, and linked to the relevant bill, rulemaking, or oversight matter. Violations trigger loss of lobbying access rights, civil penalties, referral for ethics enforcement, and mandatory public disclosure of the violation and the entity responsible.

### Relationship to Existing Lobbying Disclosure Law

The Lobbying Disclosure Act and related registration requirements remain in effect and are strengthened, not replaced. The credit system adds a priced, recorded participation channel; it does not eliminate registration, contact reporting, or the revolving-door restrictions that existing law provides. Entities participating in credit-funded forums must be registered under disclosure law. Unregistered lobbying contact outside the credit system is treated as a violation of both the credit system rules and the underlying lobbying disclosure requirements.

### The Attack and the Answer

The obvious attack is that this legalizes buying access to government. The answer is direct: access already exists. Today it is private, bundled, hidden, and routed through money networks, hospitality arrangements, paid retreats, dark-money intermediaries, and consulting pipelines. This system abolishes private access markets and replaces them with public, priced, nonexclusive, recorded participation — while funding the public's capacity to scrutinize the participants in real time.

The state is not pretending institutional actors have no legitimate technical input to offer. It is preventing that input from becoming private government capture. The lobbying credit system does not criminalize lobbying. It civilizes it.

### Litigation Posture

The statutory track expects litigation and treats the litigation record as a strategic asset. Surviving provisions create immediate anti-capture effect. Invalidated provisions become part of the documented public record — identifying with precision which anti-capture tools current doctrine forbids. Court opinions clarifying the constitutional boundary inform the NRM mandate language and the constitutional amendment design. A statutory loss is not success by another name. It is only useful if the fallback path is prepared. The brief acknowledges this explicitly: litigation becomes a pressure multiplier only when the public mandate and amendment strategy are ready before the first challenge is filed.

## Track Two — NRM Public Mandate

The NRM track creates a documented public mandate to remove dark money and concentrated institutional spending from federal elections. It does not directly overturn *Citizens United*. It does not itself amend the Constitution. It establishes that the American public, given a clear and fair opportunity to express their view, wants Congress and the states to have the authority to regulate election spending and prevent financial capture of democratic institutions.

### The Ballot Question

The ballot question must be framed precisely. It does not ask voters to agree with a legal theory. It does not ask them to endorse a specific statute. It asks whether they want the government to have authority it currently lacks.

The recommended framing:

> Should Congress and the states be authorized to establish viewpoint-neutral limits and disclosure rules governing dark money, corporate and institutional election spending, independent expenditures, coordinated spending, and political spending by artificial entities — while fully preserving individual political speech, press freedom, and volunteer activity?

This framing is accurate, non-partisan, and hard to characterize as censorship. It explicitly protects natural-person speech and press freedom. It focuses the mandate on artificial entities — corporations, unions, nonprofits, PACs, and other non-human organizational structures — rather than on political speech generally.

### Severable NRM Titles

The NRM legislation contains five severable titles, each independently ratifiable:

**Title I — Dark-Money Disclosure and Beneficial Ownership.** Establishes the mandatory beneficial-owner disclosure requirement through the Democracy Fund Portal for all political expenditures in federal elections.

**Title II — Artificial-Entity Political Spending Limits.** Governs **who may fund election spending** — establishing the authority of Congress to require that political expenditures by artificial entities be funded exclusively through verified individual-source accounts and regulated committees, not through organizational treasury funds.

**Title III — Independent Expenditure and Coordination Authority.** Governs **when outside spending is functionally campaign spending** — establishing the authority of Congress to define and regulate coordination between campaigns and outside spenders, and to subject expenditures that meet the expanded coordination definition to contribution limits regardless of their formal structure. This is distinct from Title II: Title II addresses the funding source; Title III addresses the strategic relationship between the spender and the candidate.

**Title IV — Public Small-Donor Financing Mandate.** Establishes the National Democracy Fund as a permanent federal institution with dedicated funding and a mandate to amplify small-donor participation through public matching in all federal elections.

**Title V — Constitutional Amendment Instruction.** A Tier 3 NRM title establishing a public mandate for constitutional amendment to restore democratic authority over campaign finance. This title requires 60% support on the ratification question, placement on an election meeting the Tier 3 participation floor, and passage in states representing at least 60% of the national eligible voting population.

### Implementation Logic

If ratified, each title triggers its own implementation pathway. Congress re-passes or strengthens statutory provisions where the mandate provides additional constitutional grounding. The Candidate Nutrition Label records each member's position on implementing the mandate — making obstruction visible. The Digital Democracy Platform's public dashboard tracks implementation progress and member accountability. State-level campaigns use the mandate to pressure state legislatures and state parties. The Title V mandate becomes a core element of the Article V convention application strategy.

The campaign line that makes the political case is direct: **If you want to influence the American government, be an American citizen. Run for office. Organize. Donate within your individual limits. What you may not do is use a corporation or a dark-money network as a financial amplifier that no individual citizen could deploy.**

## Track Three — Constitutional Amendment

The permanent closure of the post-*Citizens United* regime requires a constitutional amendment restoring democratic authority over campaign finance. The amendment should not be framed merely as "overturn *Citizens United*." That framing is reactive, legally specific, and easier to attack. The amendment should affirmatively define public authority over election spending while explicitly protecting the speech freedoms that the existing doctrine rightly values.

### Amendment Design Principles

The amendment must establish that Congress and the states may regulate political contributions and expenditures by corporations, unions, nonprofits, PACs, and other artificial entities. It must confirm that this authority extends to independent expenditures, electioneering communications, coordinated spending, and dark-money pass-through structures. It must be explicitly viewpoint-neutral — the government may regulate the financial architecture of political spending, not the ideological content of political speech. And it must affirmatively protect natural-person speech, press freedom, and volunteer political activity from any interpretation that would treat the amendment as licensing government censorship.

### Draft Amendment Language

> Congress and the states may establish reasonable, viewpoint-neutral limits and disclosure requirements governing contributions, expenditures, independent expenditures, electioneering communications, coordinated spending, and political spending by artificial entities — including corporations, unions, nonprofit organizations, political committees, and other juridical persons whether created under federal law, state law, or their equivalent — for the purpose of protecting democratic self-government, preventing corruption or the appearance of corruption, ensuring the political equality of citizens, and preserving transparent elections.
>
> Nothing in this article shall authorize censorship of the press, restriction of unpaid individual political expression, limitation of volunteer political activity, or discrimination based on political viewpoint.

### Relationship to Term Limits and SCOTUS Reform

The campaign finance amendment, the term limits amendment, and the SCOTUS active-service limits amendment expose the same underlying failure mode: institutions and interests controlling the rules of their own accountability. Campaign finance gives concentrated money structural access to officeholders. Term limits give incumbents control over renewal rules. SCOTUS tenure gives the judiciary the power to freeze anti-capture doctrine indefinitely.

These reforms are not the same amendment and should not be bundled into a single mega-proposal. Each has its own ratification logic, its own political coalition, and its own constitutional grounding. But they compound narratively and politically. Campaign-finance obstruction strengthens the case for term limits. Term-limit obstruction strengthens the case for constitutional mandate politics. Judicial blockage of anti-capture law strengthens the case for SCOTUS active-service reform. The reform coalition should make this compounding explicit: every obstruction point is evidence that the system is protecting itself, which is exactly the argument for the next layer of reform.

## Anti-Abuse Safeguards

The strongest attack on this framework is that it authorizes government censorship of political speech. Every element of the framework must be designed to make that attack false, not merely to rebut it rhetorically.

The safeguards are structural. Viewpoint neutrality is non-negotiable: the framework regulates the financial architecture of political spending, not the ideological content. The press exemption is explicit and broad: no provision applies to bona fide press activity. The individual speech safe harbor is explicit: natural-person political speech, including unpaid expression and volunteer activity, is protected regardless of the funding rules that apply to artificial entities. Enforcement authority is administrative and civil, not criminal for ordinary political activity. Rulemaking is public and subject to judicial review. No licensing of political speech is created at any point in the framework.

The anti-abuse design also addresses the media-shell loophole: a corporation cannot claim press-freedom exemption for political advertising or spending that is not bona fide journalism. The press exemption protects editorial content, reporting, and commentary. It does not protect an industrial corporation that registers a newsletter to run unlimited political advertising under a press-freedom shield.

## Red-Team Attack Surface

**"This criminalizes political speech."** The framework does not create criminal liability for speech. It creates civil and administrative penalties for operating influence pathways that fall outside authorized channels — concealed funding structures, beneficial-owner laundering, sham-independence spending, and private access arrangements. The rules of construction in the Act state this distinction explicitly: the prohibited activity is structural and pathway-based, not viewpoint-based. Ordinary political expression, civic advocacy, and registered lobbying remain fully lawful and unrestricted.

**"This is censorship."** Censorship restricts what may be said based on content or viewpoint. This framework restricts how concentrated money may flow through political channels. It does not restrict any opinion, any policy position, any criticism of public officials, or any political message. A corporation that wishes to advocate for lower corporate taxes may do so through natural-person speech, public testimony, registered lobbying, and the lobbying credit system. It may not do so through a dark-money nonprofit that conceals the funding source. The message is unrestricted; the financial architecture is regulated.

**"This is incumbent protection."** The framework applies symmetrically to all parties and all artificial entities. It is paired with a public financing system — the National Democracy Fund — that specifically amplifies challengers who lack donor networks. Incumbents with established fundraising infrastructure are relatively disadvantaged by a system that amplifies small-donor breadth over large-donor depth.

**"Corporations are people."** The framework accepts the Court's holding that corporations have First Amendment interests. It argues that those interests do not extend to unlimited independent expenditures in elections, and that the government's interest in democratic self-governance is a compelling interest that justifies regulation of artificial entities in the electoral context. The amendment settles this argument by constitutional text.

**"This will be selectively enforced."** The Democracy Fund Portal's real-time public disclosure architecture makes enforcement visible and auditable. The FEIC's anti-capture mandate applies symmetrically. The severability and fast-track review provisions force judicial clarity before selective enforcement can entrench itself.

**"This bans political speech by corporations."** It does not ban corporate political speech. It requires that political spending by artificial entities flow through authorized, disclosed, attributable channels funded by verified individual contributions — not through organizational treasury funds that aggregate financial power beyond what any individual citizen could deploy. A corporation retains First Amendment interests. Those interests do not extend to the use of treasury funds as unlimited financial amplifiers in elections outside the authorized pathway architecture.

## Sequencing Integration

This brief integrates into the Democratic Integrity sequencing stack as follows.

**Layer 0.** Draft statutory language, beneficial-owner disclosure architecture, coordination-rule expansion, amendment language, litigation strategy, and NRM title framing are all prepared before office.

**Layer 3.** The statutory anti-capture reform package is enacted alongside the National Democracy Fund and Democracy Fund Portal in the core infrastructure layer. The statutory track activates immediately. The litigation record begins accumulating.

**Layer 4.** If statutory provisions face immediate injunction or invalidation, NRM mandate titles covering dark-money disclosure and artificial-entity limits go to the first public mandate ballot. The Title V constitutional amendment instruction is a Tier 3 title targeted for a later cycle once the Tier 2 mandate architecture is established.

**Layer 6/7.** The constitutional amendment is pursued through the Article V convention pathway, with the campaign finance amendment either bundled into a broadly scoped "democratic integrity and political accountability" convention application or pursued as a parallel constitutional track depending on political conditions. The NRM Title V ratification result provides the mandate weight for state legislature applications.

## Success Conditions

**Short-term:** Statutory disclosure, public financing, anti-evasion, coordination-rule expansion, and foreign-money rules survive judicial review or generate a clear litigation record. The Democracy Fund Portal is operational and publishing real-time influence network data. The NRM mandate ballot language is drafted and reviewed.

**Medium-term:** Member positions on the mandate are visible through the Candidate Nutrition Label. Dark-money opposition is politically costly in election cycles where the mandate result is documented. State-level campaigns are aligned with the national mandate.

**Long-term:** Constitutional amendment restores full public authority over campaign finance. Small-donor financing and party formation are structurally viable. Political competition depends on civic breadth of support rather than donor network depth. The dependency market between officeholders and concentrated money is replaced by a public financing system that amplifies the preferences of ordinary citizens.

## Open Questions

How aggressive should the first statute be, given expected litigation? Should the NRM mandate be specific to dark money or broader campaign finance authority? Should the constitutional amendment include unions alongside corporations and nonprofits — and if so, how should the labor-political relationship be addressed without creating an asymmetric framework that protects corporate spending while banning union spending? How should press protections be drafted without creating an exploitable media-shell loophole? Should public financing be mandatory for federal candidates or optional with strong incentives? How should independent-expenditure coordination be defined without sweeping in ordinary ideological alignment between organizations and candidates who share beliefs? Should the campaign-finance amendment language be bundled with term limits in a convention application or kept fully separate but narratively linked? How should state campaign-finance authority interact with federal election rules? Where should core campaign finance enforcement live — FEIC, FEC successor, Democracy Fund Commission, or a new Campaign Finance Integrity Commission? Should the Lobbying Access Credit system be administered by a standalone Public Lobbying Access Office, a Democracy Fund Commission, an FEC successor, or a broader Public Integrity Commission that consolidates campaign finance and lobbying oversight under one anti-capture mandate?

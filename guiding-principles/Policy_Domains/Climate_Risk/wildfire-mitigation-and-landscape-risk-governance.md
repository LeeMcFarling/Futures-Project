# Wildfire Mitigation and Landscape Risk Governance

## Executive Summary

Wildfire risk is a structural, fat-tailed hazard: a small fraction of events drive a dominant share of total loss. This makes “average-year” planning insufficient. The objective of this policy is to reduce catastrophic tail risk by treating landscapes and the wildland–urban interface (WUI) as managed risk systems, using continuous monitoring, fuel-load reduction, prescribed fire at scale, and enforceable mitigation standards tied to insurance eligibility.

The federal role is not to centralize land control; it is to build the execution capacity, liability structure, data infrastructure, and intergovernmental compacts required for consistent, large-scale risk reduction. Transparency and version-controlled standards are treated as governance primitives aligned with a governance-as-code approach. [See: Regulation Overhaul]

---

## 1. Problem Statement — What’s Broken

Wildfire losses are rising because exposure is compounding: fuel loads accumulate, drought and heat stress expand burn windows, and development spreads into the WUI. Current mitigation is fragmented across agencies and jurisdictions, and prescribed fire is constrained by a liability and permitting environment that makes risk-reducing burns rationally unattractive.

Insurance markets respond to the tail by withdrawing or sharply raising premiums, which creates political pressure to suppress rates or socialize losses after disasters. The result is repeated rebuilding into hazard while the underlying tail risk worsens.

---

## 2. Policy Objective — What This System Optimizes For

This policy optimizes for measurable reduction in catastrophic wildfire tail risk while preserving property rights and state/tribal authority.

Success is defined by: reduced extreme-loss probability in mapped corridors; increased annual treated acreage (mechanical thinning + prescribed fire) in priority zones; improved structure survival rates in defensible-space compliant communities; and improved insurance availability in compliant areas.

---

## 3. Core Principles

Wildfire is a power-law problem. Catastrophic events dominate expected losses, so the correct objective is tail containment, not marginal improvement.

Wildfire fuel treatment is critical infrastructure. The landscape is treated as a managed system with maintenance schedules, not as an unmanaged externality.

Insurance is a compliance lever. Eligibility for federal catastrophe reinsurance and related stabilizers is conditioned on mitigation standards and verified implementation.

Transparency is mandatory. Risk maps, treatment plans, compliance status, and post-event performance are published and version-controlled to prevent political drift and capture. [Regulation Overhaul]

---

## 4. National Wildfire Risk Map Layer

This policy establishes a continuously updated wildfire risk map layer used for eligibility, planning, and accountability. Inputs include NASA and NOAA remote sensing (vegetation stress, soil moisture, burn scars, heat anomalies), plus topography, fuel models, wind corridors, and structure density. The output is not a single score; it is a set of operational layers:

Landscape hazard layers (fuel load, drought stress, slope/wind alignment), exposure layers (WUI density, critical infrastructure), and response layers (evacuation capacity, access constraints). Updates occur on a fixed cadence, with published change logs and parameter diffs to keep the system legible and contestable without being politicized.  [Regulation Overhaul]

## Data Governance and Wildfire Risk Mapping (DoDa)

The national wildfire risk map layer is maintained by the Department of Data and Accountability (DoDa).

DoDa aggregates remote sensing and environmental datasets from:

- NASA satellite monitoring
- NOAA drought and atmospheric data
- United States Forest Service fuel models
- Department of Interior land management telemetry
- State wildfire and forestry agencies

DoDa standardizes these datasets into a continuously updated wildfire risk layer that includes:

- fuel load density
- vegetation stress
- burn scar history
- wind corridor amplification
- wildland–urban interface exposure

The resulting risk maps are published through a national dashboard and updated on a fixed cadence with transparent model documentation.

Land management agencies, tribal governments, and state forestry departments remain responsible for operational mitigation programs such as prescribed burns and fuel reduction. DoDa provides the standardized risk measurement layer used to guide those interventions.

---

## 5. Treatment and Mitigation Architecture

### 5.1 Priority Treatment Corridors

The system designates priority corridors where interventions produce the largest tail reduction per dollar: WUI rings around communities, dominant wind corridors, ridgelines above settlements, and critical infrastructure corridors (power, water conveyance, evacuation routes). Interventions are selected for tail reduction impact, not aesthetics.

### 5.2 Prescribed Fire at Scale

Prescribed fire becomes a core capacity with standardized training, certification, seasonal staffing, and multi-jurisdictional burn teams. The program pairs federal execution capacity with state and local implementation under opt-in compacts.

### 5.3 Mechanical Thinning and Biomass Strategy

Where prescribed fire is constrained by proximity, smoke constraints, or fuel conditions, mechanical thinning is used to reduce ladder fuels and create defensible treatment zones. Biomass removal is treated as a logistics problem; offtake strategies (where viable) are designed to reduce disposal bottlenecks without depending on fragile economics.

---

## 6. Tribal Partnerships and Co-Management Compacts

Tribal co-management is a structural feature, not symbolic inclusion. Compacts support tribal leadership in culturally rooted land stewardship, including controlled burns and landscape restoration practices, while expanding regional execution capacity. Compacts define: authority boundaries, operational coordination, data-sharing terms, and funding flows. The goal is to scale proven stewardship practices while respecting sovereignty and local governance.

---

## 7. Liability Reform and Permitting Fast Lanes

Scaling prescribed burns requires changing the incentive surface.

This policy establishes a certified burn liability framework: if burns are conducted by certified teams under approved plans and weather windows, liability shifts from strict or ambiguous standards to a negligence standard with clear safe harbors. The objective is not immunity; it is predictable risk so actors can operate at scale.

Permitting is streamlined via predefined burn windows, standardized plan templates, and interagency fast lanes for priority corridors. This reduces delays that push burns into riskier conditions.

---

## 8. WUI Standards Linked to Insurance Eligibility

To preserve market signals while preventing collapse, the system links eligibility for the federal catastrophe reinsurance layer to minimum WUI standards at both property and community levels.

Property-level requirements include defensible space, ember-resistant vents, roof and eave hardening, and access clearance. Community-level requirements include fuel break maintenance schedules, enforcement of setback rules in the highest-risk microzones, and evacuation/serviceability planning.

Areas that do not meet standards can remain inhabited, but they lose access to subsidized pooling and face private pricing and availability outcomes.

---

## 9. Governance, Measurement, and Accountability

Every treatment corridor and participating jurisdiction publishes: baseline risk metrics, annual treatment targets, compliance status, and post-event performance reviews. Independent audits verify treated acreage, maintenance adherence, and WUI compliance.

Standards and compacts are maintained as version-controlled governance artifacts to prevent quiet dilution after high-loss years or political turnover. [Regulation Overhaul] 

---

## 10. Pilot Strategy

Pilots begin where: hazard is high, data is strong, and governance willingness exists. Initial pilots focus on a limited number of corridors to prove operational throughput and compliance integrity.

Pilot evaluation measures: treated acreage per season, escaped-burn incidence, tail-risk reduction proxies (fuel continuity breaks, modeled extreme spread reduction), structure survival rates, insurance availability changes, and cost per unit risk reduction.

Scaling occurs only after pilots demonstrate both safety performance and material tail reduction.

---

## System-Level Outcome

This policy converts wildfire from a recurring fiscal and human catastrophe into a managed tail-risk system. It stabilizes insurance markets by reducing the probability of megafire losses and by conditioning pooling eligibility on verifiable mitigation. It creates a durable execution model for fuel management and prescribed fire, expands capacity through tribal and state partnerships, and forces long-horizon honesty about where the WUI can remain viable under tightening climate conditions.

---

## Public Facing Draft — Plain Language

Wildfires are getting worse, and a few huge fires cause most of the damage. The solution is not just faster firefighting after the fact. The solution is reducing the fuel and exposure that make catastrophic fires possible.

This plan uses modern satellite data to map risk, expands controlled burns and fuel treatment at scale (including partnerships with tribes), and sets clear standards for homes and communities in high-risk areas.

No one is forced to move. But if a community refuses basic safety steps and keeps building into danger, the national system will not keep subsidizing the risk forever.

# Node Typologies & Re-Urbanization Triggers

## Purpose

Node typologies define **where re-urbanization should occur first** and under what conditions the public infrastructure spine is deployed. The objective is to focus growth on land that is already underperforming socially and economically, while minimizing displacement, political resistance, and infrastructure risk.

Rather than debating density parcel by parcel, this framework establishes **default redevelopment candidates** with pre-aligned zoning, infrastructure, and permitting pathways under Special Transit Zones (STZs).

---

## Core Principle: Build Where the City Is Already Broken

Re-urbanization should prioritize land that:
- Already disrupts urban continuity  
- Produces minimal social value relative to its footprint  
- Can absorb density without displacing existing communities  
- Benefits disproportionately from transit and utility upgrades  

---

## Primary Node Typologies

### 1. Big-Box Retail & Power Centers

**Examples:** Locally underperforming large-format retail stores, shopping centers, abandoned malls, superstores, and venues with surface parking.

**Why these work:**
- Single ownership or small ownership groups
- Large, contiguous parcels
- Excess surface parking
- Typically located on arterial corridors

**Default re-urbanization pattern:**
- Consolidate parking into structures
- Integrate spine segments along parcel edge or median
- Introduce mid-rise residential and mixed-use blocks
- Retain or reconfigure ground-floor retail


These sites are among the lowest-risk, highest-upside candidates.

---

### 2. Strip Malls & Commercial Dead Zones

**Examples:** Linear retail strips, declining commercial corridors, aging plazas.

**Why these work:**
- Underperforming land value
- Fragmented but shallow parcels
- Existing access to arterials and utilities

**Default re-urbanization pattern:**
- Parcel aggregation or coordinated redevelopment
- Replacement with mid-rise, walkable blocks
- Spine integration as a linear civic corridor
- Transition zones to adjacent neighborhoods

These corridors often become **early proof-of-concept successes**.

---

### 3. Stadium & Event Parking Fields

**Examples:** Sports venues with large surface parking areas.

**Why these work:**
- Massive land area used intermittently
- Public or quasi-public ownership
- Existing transit demand during events

**Default re-urbanization pattern:**
- Retain event parking in structured form
- Introduce permanent residential, commercial, and civic uses
- Deploy spine as primary transit and public-space backbone
- Enable year-round activation

These sites convert episodic use into continuous urban life.

---

### 4. Highway-Adjacent & Arterial Edges

**Examples:** Oversized arterials, frontage roads, highway buffer zones.

**Why these work:**
- High mobility demand
- Environmental and noise buffers already exist
- Poor pedestrian experience today

**Default re-urbanization pattern:**
- Deploy spine along or above arterial
- Reduce surface lanes post-transit deployment
- Create green boulevard and civic colonnade
- Build mid-rise density set back from traffic

This reframes mobility corridors as **urban spines** rather than barriers.

---

### 5. Industrial Transition Zones

**Examples:** Light industrial areas no longer operating at capacity.

**Why these work:**
- Large parcels and flexible zoning
- Often near rail or freight corridors
- Lower displacement risk

**Default re-urbanization pattern:**
- Mixed employment and residential reuse
- Retention of compatible light industry
- Spine deployment as shared access corridor
- Gradual densification over time

---

## Re-Urbanization Triggers

A node becomes eligible for STZ designation and spine deployment when one or more of the following triggers are met:

- Proximity to existing or planned rail infrastructure  
- Sustained underutilization of land (e.g., low FAR, excess parking)  
- Public ownership or cooperative ownership structure  
- Corridor-level congestion or mobility demand  
- Housing supply constraints within the surrounding region  

Triggers are **objective and published in advance** to avoid discretionary debate.

---

## Default Density Envelope

Nodes designated under this framework receive a **pre-aligned mid-rise density envelope**, typically including:
- Mid-rise height ranges appropriate to context
- Mixed residential, commercial, and civic uses
- Reduced or eliminated parking minimums
- By-right density tied to transit and spine integration

This envelope is adjustable only at the margins once adopted.

---

## Relationship to the Infrastructure Spine

The public infrastructure spine is:
- Required for nodes above defined size thresholds
- Optional but incentivized for smaller nodes
- Treated as shared civic infrastructure, not project-specific amenity

Spine deployment is synchronized with utility upgrades and public realm investment.

---

## Governance & Sequencing

Node designation follows a staged process:
1. Preliminary eligibility assessment  
2. Local opt-in and STZ designation  
3. Selection of regional pattern book  
4. Spine segment selection and alignment  
5. Phased development rollout  

This sequencing prevents speculative upzoning without infrastructure delivery.

---

## Why This Works

Node typologies:
- Remove uncertainty from site selection
- Focus growth where resistance is lowest
- Enable faster approvals under STZs
- Align housing, transit, and utilities
- Produce visible early wins

They make re-urbanization **procedural, not ideological**.

---

## Relationship to Other Framework Components

Node typologies link:
- STZ legal authority  
- Infrastructure spine deployment  
- Regional architectural pattern books  
- Housing and density policy  

Together, these form a complete re-urbanization operating system.

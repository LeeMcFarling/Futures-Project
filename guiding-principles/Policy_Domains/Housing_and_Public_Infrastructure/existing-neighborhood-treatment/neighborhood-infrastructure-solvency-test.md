## Purpose

The Local Infrastructure Funding Test (LIFT) is a simple, rule-based check used alongside Neighborhood Civic Overlays to ensure that new neighborhood-scale development does not create unfunded infrastructure liabilities. Its purpose is to answer one question clearly and consistently before approval:

Will the new activity help pay for the infrastructure it uses?

LIFT is not a full accounting system, not a retroactive tax mechanism, and not a barrier to modest development. It is a guardrail that ensures local governments stop approving small changes that quietly worsen long‑term maintenance shortfalls.

---
## Problem Statement

Local governments routinely approve incremental development without evaluating whether it can support the long-term costs these development create. Roads, sidewalks, lighting, drainage, and utilities are easy to build but expensive to maintain. When approvals ignore this reality, maintenance obligations accumulate faster than revenue, leading to deferred repairs, degraded public space, and fiscal stress.

The failure is not density itself but the absence of a solvency check. LIFT closes that gap with a lightweight, repeatable rule.

---
## Core Principle

New development should not make existing infrastructure harder to maintain.

If a project increases local tax revenue more than it increases infrastructure maintenance costs, it is fiscally healthy. If it does not, it should not proceed without redesign. 

The test applies only to new projects moving forward. 

It does not:
- Reassign responsibility for existing roads or utilities
- Require neighborhoods to fix legacy infrastructure
- Penalize areas for past planning decisions

---
## The Test (Plain Language)

Each project must answer the following question:

“Will the local tax revenue generated by this new use help cover the cost of maintaining roads and other infrastructure in the surrounding area?”

If yes, the project proceeds.
If no, the project does not proceed in its current form.

---
## Inputs (Standardized)

The test uses inputs that cities already maintain:

- Assessed value of the proposed use
- Local property and sales tax rates
- Standard per‑unit maintenance costs for:
  - Local roads
  - Sidewalks and lighting
  - Stormwater and drainage
  - Utilities serving the site

No bespoke studies or consultant reports are required.

---
## Geography

Each project is evaluated within a defined service area, typically a short walking radius or block group surrounding the site. This keeps the test local, understandable, and resistant to gaming.

---
## Outcomes & Decision Rules

- If projected recurring revenue ≥ projected incremental maintenance cost: approval proceeds
- If projected recurring revenue < projected incremental maintenance cost: approval is denied or redesigned

There is no discretionary override.

---
## Relationship to Zoning & Relationship to Transit and Density

LIFT does not dictate land use. It conditions approval on fiscal sustainability. Zoning overlays define what *may* be built; the solvency test determines whether it is responsible to do so.

Passing LIFT at the neighborhood scale ensures that walkability improvements strengthen, rather than weaken, municipal finances. As multiple projects pass the test and aggregate demand, they create fiscally sound candidates for higher-capacity transit and future density.

---
## Bottom Line

This measure is easy to explain, easy to administer, and hard to argue against. By applying it consistently, cities can support walkable neighborhoods while finally aligning day‑to‑day approvals with long‑term infrastructure reality.

Build what you can afford to maintain. Grow where it pays for itself.

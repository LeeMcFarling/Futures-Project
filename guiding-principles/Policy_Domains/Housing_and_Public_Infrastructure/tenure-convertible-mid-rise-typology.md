# Tenure-Convertible Mid-Rise Typology  
## Catalytic Density & Capital Recycling Model

## Purpose

The Tenure-Convertible Mid-Rise Typology establishes a standardized, pattern-book-aligned building form designed to preserve tenure flexibility across housing cycles. It integrates structural optionality with catalytic public capital participation in order to expand affordable and workforce housing supply without permanent grant dependency.

This typology is intended as an optional pilot deployment within designated development corridors. It is not a universal mandate. Its objective is to prove that density, affordability, and capital recycling can coexist within a disciplined execution framework.

---

## Strategic Premise

Traditional housing development locks tenure at the point of construction. Rental buildings are designed for rental. Condominiums are designed for ownership. Co-living units are optimized for a single operating model. This rigidity creates stranded assets during market cycles and limits affordability pathways.

The Tenure-Convertible Mid-Rise model embeds structural flexibility at the pattern-book level. Buildings are designed so that interior configuration may shift between co-living, rental apartments, condominiums, or segmented town-style ownership without structural demolition.

The long-lived asset is the envelope, structural grid, and vertical service spine. The interior unit configuration remains adaptable.

This flexibility supports affordability by reducing lifecycle risk and allowing tenure sequencing based on market conditions.

---

## Physical Design Principles

The typology includes:

A regularized structural grid that avoids load-bearing demising walls, enabling unit recombination or subdivision.

Centralized vertical mechanical and plumbing risers positioned to allow multiple interior layouts without core system reconstruction.

Non-load-bearing modular interior partitions to permit conversion between:

- Co-living suites and shared common units  
- Standard rental apartments  
- Fee-simple condominium units  
- Ground-level town segments where site interface permits  

Floor-to-floor heights sufficient to accommodate evolving mechanical standards without envelope alteration.

Corridor and egress geometry designed to comply with both rental and ownership code pathways.

These standards are embedded within the pattern book as an optional “Tenure-Convertible” assembly set.

---

## Affordability & Lifecycle Sequencing

Affordability is addressed through lifecycle sequencing rather than permanent subsidy dependence.

Phase One: Initial Development  
The building may open as co-living or smaller rental units to improve early absorption and revenue stability.

Phase Two: Stabilization  
Units may convert to traditional rental apartments with embedded workforce or income-restricted allocations as defined by corridor need assessment.

Phase Three: Ownership Conversion  
Portions of the building may convert to condominium or limited-equity ownership once market conditions support transition.

This sequencing reduces financial fragility and expands pathways to ownership without requiring new construction.

---

## PCA Catalytic Participation Model

Under the broader Execution Corps framework, the Public Capital Authority (PCA) may participate early in development through catalytic capital deployment.

Eligible participation may include:

- Subordinated equity
- Preferred equity with capped return
- Convertible debt instruments tied to appreciation
- Infrastructure pre-financing
- Land assembly support

The PCA does not operate buildings directly. It participates as catalytic capital to de-risk early phases and ensure density delivery.

---

## Clawback & Capital Recycling Mechanism

PCA participation is governed by defined internal rate of return (IRR) bands.

If project IRR exceeds the published threshold band for the corridor, excess return above the band repays PCA participation through structured clawback mechanisms.

If project underperforms within defined risk parameters, PCA absorbs loss proportionally to its subordinated position.

Recovered capital is recycled into future corridor deployments or additional mixed-income housing phases.

No permanent grant stream is embedded within this model. Public capital is structured as catalytic and recoverable.

---

## Execution Corps Role

Execution Corps may, in defined pilot corridors, assemble land, coordinate entitlement, and structure initial capital stacks where private coordination failure prevents density deployment.

Such participation is time-bound and project-specific.

Execution Corps does not retain long-term development ownership and may not expand beyond corridor-specific mandates without legislative authorization.

---

## Relationship to Affordable Housing Policy

The Tenure-Convertible Mid-Rise Typology addresses affordability through:

- Increased supply
- Reduced lifecycle rigidity
- Capital recycling
- Structured mixed-income integration

It does not replace targeted supportive housing models or healthcare-integrated housing programs.

Homelessness interventions, particularly for high-acuity populations, require parallel healthcare-enabled housing pilots under a distinct policy framework.

This typology may coexist with such programs but does not substitute for them.

---

## Rural Housing Distinction

This model is designed for corridor-based mid-rise development. Rural housing access presents distinct capital feasibility and infrastructure extension challenges and is addressed under a separate policy track.

---

## Strategic Outcome

If piloted successfully, the Tenure-Convertible Mid-Rise Typology demonstrates that:

- Density can be adaptable rather than tenure-locked.
- Public capital can catalyze development without permanent subsidy.
- Affordability can be embedded through sequencing and clawback rather than rigid mandate.
- Market cycles can be absorbed without asset obsolescence.

The result is durable, flexible housing stock aligned with long-term affordability and capital recycling principles.

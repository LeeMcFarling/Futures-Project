# Overview

As an overview, the tax code should be interpreted as a capital map, not a legal artifact. Every tax expenditure (be that  tax credit, deduction, exemption, or preferential treatment) should be classified as an implied investment into an economic sector. Instead of this investment map, our tax code is a 70,000+ page graveyard of legacy incentives which represents one of the largest and least transparent uses of government capital. This expenditure operates off-budget, is difficult to audit, and tends to persist long after it's original justification has expired.  

The core objective of reform is to convert opaque, perpetual incentives into explicit, accountable capital instruments. Rather than influencing behavior indirectly through the tax code, the government deploys capital directly through ultra-low-interest loans, repayable credits, equity-like stakes, and milestone-based grants aligned to clearly defined public goals.

Capital allocation prioritizes recyclability over consumption. Wherever feasible, public funds are structured to return to the system—through repayment, revenue participation, or sunset clauses—allowing capital to be reused and scaled over time. Non-repayable support is reserved for true public goods, early-stage research, or national-interest capabilities where market failure is demonstrable.

Incentives are designed at the sector and system level, not as bespoke carve-outs for individual firms or activities. This reduces rent-seeking, simplifies compliance, and shifts competition toward performance rather than tax engineering. Eligibility, terms, and performance metrics are standardized, transparent, and time-bounded.

Risk is allocated deliberately. The state absorbs early-stage or coordination risk that private markets underprice, while avoiding permanent exposure to mature, low-risk activities. As sectors stabilize, support automatically steps down or converts to market-rate financing, preventing dependency and crowd-out.

All capital instruments are subject to continuous evaluation. Outcomes are measured against predefined benchmarks, underperforming incentives are restructured or terminated, and successful mechanisms are scaled. The tax code ceases to be a graveyard of legacy incentives and becomes a narrow interface for collection, not a substitute for investment strategy.

The end state is a tax system that raises revenue cleanly and a capital system that deploys public money intentionally—each doing one job well, rather than one system failing at both.

# **Methodology**


Step 1: We direct the treasury to interpret each tax expenditure as an implied investment into an economic sector. The treasury (along with the IRS if necessary) will produce a live inventory that assigns each credit, deduction, exemption, and preferential rate both to a:

1) Primary Sector (energy, housing, healthcare, manufacturing, finance, labor, R&D, agriculture, etc.) and

2) a secondary objective (capacity expansion, risk reduction, price stabilization, innovation, regional development, etc.)

Anything that cannot be cleanly mapped fails the first test. From there, we test sector-level aggregation. We stop evaluating incentives line by line and instead ask: 'What is the government implicitly investing in this sector each year, and what's the return profile?' For each sector we quantify total forgone revenue, beneficiaries, concentration effects, duration, and behavioral elasticity. The objective is to expose redundancy, rent capture, and zombie incentives that no longer provide outcomes. 

Step 2: Once each tax structure is broken down in this manor, we evaluate each one for convertability into a financial instrument. For each line item, we ask: 

1) Does this incentive represent risk capital, operating support, or consumption relief? 
2) Is the activity time-bounded or perpetual by design? 
3) Could the same outcome be achived with an explicit capital instrument? If the answer is yes, we default to conversion. 

Step 3: Converstion

Conversion follows standardized instrument templates, not bespoke deals. For each item: 

a) If it is to facilitate risk taking, it becomes an ultra low-interest loan or revenue-linked repayment instrument. 

b) If it is growth centered, it becomes a milestone based grant, equity partnership (with measured drawdown if successful), or milestone based grants. 

c) If this is for long term industrial support, it gets converted into a capped equity like stake, or revolving outcome based funds. Structural disadvantages and inefficiencies should be resolved via coordination with other agencies as defined in manufacturing. 

**Important Notes**
Conversion is phased by sector, and prospective (not retroactive). Companies in the sector recieving tax benefits are grandfathered into the new system over a set amount of time, while new activity in the sector uses this new capital framework. 


#### **Public Capital Authority**

A new executive-branch agency will be developed which will be responsible for deploying public capital as disciplined and recyclable investments rather than opaque subsidies (see PCA). Its mandate is to allocate capital in ways that expand national productive capacity, reduce systemic risk, and generate measureable long term returns -- whether those be financial, economic, or strategic -- within the parameters set by congress. 

The PCA does not set tax policy, define national priorities, or regulate industries. It converts signal obtained from other executive agencies and objectives from congress and Digital Democracy into financial instrument investment.

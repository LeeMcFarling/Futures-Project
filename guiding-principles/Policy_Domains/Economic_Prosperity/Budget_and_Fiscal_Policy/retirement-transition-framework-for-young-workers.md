# Retirement Transition and Inheritance Entry Framework

## Purpose

This brief defines how legacy retirement balances and inherited family assets enter the Universal Superannuation System.

The goal is to make the transition generous for ordinary households, simple for younger workers, and disciplined at dynasty scale. Younger workers should not owe a tax bill merely because the country migrates to a better retirement architecture. Older workers should not be forced to rewrite retirement plans built under prior law. Families below the dynasty threshold should be able to transmit homes, savings, businesses, and proceeds into the new structure. Above the threshold, inherited wealth must not use the new account system to erase embedded gains, fragment assets across descendants, or convert dynasty capital into invisible protected balances.

The governing doctrine is:

**Young workers get migration relief. Ordinary families get continuity. Dynasty-scale capital gets realization.**

---

## Relationship to the Universal Superannuation System

The Universal Superannuation System establishes the four-bucket architecture: Bucket A secures retirement, Bucket B funds formation, Bucket C preserves legitimate family continuity, and Bucket D disciplines dynasty-scale capital above the CPI-indexed $50 million total-asset threshold.

This brief governs entry into that architecture.

The transition rules do not create a freestanding tax shelter. They determine how legacy balances, inherited assets, and inherited proceeds enter the system. Once inside, assets are governed by the same bucket sequencing, lock rules, use restrictions, anti-pledging rules, inheritance rules, beneficial-control attestation, source metadata, and dynasty-threshold aggregation that govern all protected superannuation capital.

The transition pass-through changes the tax treatment of migration only. It does not convert retirement savings into liquid wealth. It does not create a general-purpose investment shelter. It moves legacy retirement capital into the new system under the new system’s rules.

---

## Younger-Worker Transition Pass-Through

During the four-year transition window, individuals under age 40 may transfer the first **$100,000** of aggregate pre-tax legacy retirement balances into the Universal Superannuation System without recognizing conversion income.

The exclusion is marginal, not cliff-based.

A worker with $90,000 of eligible pre-tax retirement savings may transfer the full amount tax-free.

A worker with $160,000 may transfer the first $100,000 tax-free. The remaining $60,000 may remain in the legacy system or transfer under ordinary tax-settlement rules.

A worker with no retirement balance receives no retroactive cash benefit.

The pass-through applies only to balances legally owned by the participant as of the statutory measurement date. It does not apply to inherited balances, related-party transfers, post-measurement artificial contributions, or disguised compensation.

Transferred balances enter **Bucket A first** and remain subject to the system’s ordinary lock, anti-pledging, anti-borrowing, inheritance, attribution, and threshold rules.

The rule is:

**The waiver follows the worker’s own legacy retirement savings. It does not follow opportunistic capital moved into the worker’s name after enactment.**

---

## Transition Window

The transition pass-through is available for four years following enactment.

Eligibility is determined by age at enactment. An individual who is under age 40 on the enactment date remains eligible for the full four-year window, even if they turn 40 during that period.

The four-year window serves three purposes.

First, it gives employers, custodians, recordkeepers, payroll systems, and account administrators enough time to execute trustee-to-trustee transfers without operational chaos.

Second, it avoids creating a permanent tax-preference channel. The pass-through exists to migrate the system, not to create a recurring shelter.

Third, it gives households time to consolidate accounts, receive advice, correct legacy recordkeeping issues, and move assets intentionally.

After the transition window closes, remaining pre-tax legacy balances are governed by ordinary tax rules.

---

## Eligible Accounts

Eligible legacy retirement balances include pre-tax balances held in defined-contribution retirement accounts specified by statute, including traditional 401(k), 403(b), traditional IRA, Thrift Savings Plan, governmental defined-contribution plans, and similar accounts.

Existing Roth balances may transfer cleanly because their tax status has already been settled. Roth balances do not consume the $100,000 pre-tax transition exclusion, but they remain subject to bucket routing, lock rules, source metadata, and threshold aggregation after entry into the Universal Superannuation System.

Inherited retirement accounts are not eligible for the under-40 transition pass-through. They are governed by the inheritance entry rules.

Defined-benefit pension rights, Social Security claims, and annuity contracts are not automatically converted under this framework. They require separate actuarial and contractual treatment.

---

## Bucket Routing for Transition Balances

Qualified transition balances enter Bucket A first.

The transition pass-through does not allow participants to route tax-free converted capital directly into more flexible buckets while retirement sufficiency remains unmet.

The routing rule is:

**Bucket A first. Bucket B only after Bucket A sufficiency. Bucket C only after Bucket B conditions are satisfied. Bucket D treatment applies above the dynasty threshold.**

This sequencing preserves the policy purpose of the transition benefit. The pass-through exists to secure retirement and simplify system migration. It does not create a general-purpose tax-free investment account.

If a participant’s Bucket A is already certified as sufficient, converted balances may route to the next available bucket under ordinary bucket rules. The transition exclusion changes the tax cost of entry, not the internal governance structure.

---

## Older Workers and Voluntary Rothification

Workers age 40 and older remain in the existing retirement-account architecture unless they voluntarily elect to transfer assets into the new system.

For older cohorts, pre-tax retirement balances may enter the Universal Superannuation System only through ordinary tax settlement. The transfer is a Rothification event: tax status is settled first, then post-tax capital may enter the protected account structure.

This preserves existing retirement expectations while giving older households a clean way to support children or heirs inside the new system.

Older workers may keep their existing retirement accounts. They may continue under legacy withdrawal rules. They may maintain existing beneficiary structures subject to generally applicable tax law. They may voluntarily transfer assets into the new system, but pre-tax balances must settle their tax status before receiving protected post-tax treatment inside the superannuation architecture.

The rule is:

**Younger workers receive migration relief. Older workers receive legal continuity. Older households may support the next generation with tax-settled capital.**

---

## Inheritance Entry Below the Dynasty Threshold

For total attributable family assets below the CPI-indexed **$50 million** threshold, inherited assets and inheritance proceeds may enter the protected family-capital architecture after ordinary tax treatment, source certification, and bucket routing.

This includes inherited cash, brokerage assets, retirement balances, homes, farms, closely held businesses, real estate interests, private-company interests, and proceeds from the sale of inherited assets.

Below the threshold, inheritance is treated as legitimate family continuity. The system should make ordinary intergenerational transfer easier, not force families into bespoke trusts, family LLCs, and attorney-designed structures merely to pass capital responsibly.

Inherited assets remain tagged by source. A sold inherited house, business, farm, or private-company interest does not become ordinary wage savings merely because it has been converted into cash. The proceeds may enter the protected architecture, but they retain inheritance-proceeds character for attribution, bucket routing, and threshold analysis.

The rule is:

**Below $50 million, inheritance may capitalize the next generation.**

---

## Treatment of Inherited Illiquid Assets

Inherited illiquid assets may enter the family-capital architecture below the dynasty threshold, but they retain their inherited-asset character.

Illiquid assets include homes, farms, closely held businesses, real estate portfolios, private company equity, family operating companies, restricted partnership interests, mineral rights, and other productive or hard-to-value assets.

For below-threshold families, inherited illiquid assets may be retained by the heir, sold and converted into cash proceeds, contributed into the protected architecture where permitted, routed into family-account structures, or used to satisfy Bucket B or Bucket C purposes where allowed.

The system does not treat ordinary inheritance of a house, farm, or family business as dynasty avoidance merely because the asset is valuable.

But the asset must be tagged.

The receiving system must know whether the contribution came from ordinary earnings, retirement savings, inherited brokerage assets, a sold inherited home, a sold family business, or another inheritance-proceeds source.

The core rule is:

**Asset form cannot cleanse tax history. A house, business, farm, or private-company interest does not become ordinary contribution capital merely because it is sold for cash.**

---

## Inherited Illiquid Asset Sale Proceeds

Inherited illiquid assets do not escape inheritance-entry rules merely because they are sold before contribution into the superannuation system.

If an heir inherits a house, farm, closely held business, real estate interest, private-company interest, partnership interest, or other illiquid asset and later sells that asset, the sale proceeds are treated as inheritance-proceeds capital for purposes of superannuation entry.

Inheritance-proceeds capital may enter the protected family-account architecture only after certification of the original inherited asset, date-of-death valuation, sale price, tax basis, capital-gains treatment, estate or inheritance-tax treatment where applicable, beneficial ownership, family-network attribution, dynasty-threshold status, and whether the asset was below-threshold family capital or above-threshold dynasty capital.

The conversion of an inherited illiquid asset into cash does not convert the proceeds into ordinary savings. The proceeds retain their inheritance character until tax status and threshold status are certified.

Post-certification proceeds may enter the heir’s superannuation account, child future account, or family-capital bucket according to ordinary bucket-routing rules. Bucket A sufficiency must be filled first. Bucket B and Bucket C access remain governed by the recipient’s existing bucket status. Above-threshold proceeds remain subject to the dynasty framework.

This rule prevents inherited homes, businesses, farms, and private assets from becoming backdoor tax-free superannuation fuel through a sale-and-contribute sequence.

---

## Dynasty Treatment Above the Threshold

Where the decedent, estate, or attributable family-control group exceeds the CPI-indexed **$50 million** threshold, the excess is governed by the Dynasty Tax and Generational Transfer Framework.

The threshold is marginal, not cliff-based.

A family with $48 million in total attributable assets remains within the protected family-capital architecture.

A family with $80 million in total attributable assets receives protected family-capital treatment on the first $50 million, while the $30 million excess faces dynasty realization.

Sale does not cleanse the exposure.

Trust routing does not cleanse the exposure.

Entity conversion does not cleanse the exposure.

Contribution into descendant accounts does not cleanse the exposure.

Transfer into the Universal Superannuation System does not cleanse the exposure.

The dynasty framework applies economic ownership rather than legal form. Assets held through trusts, partnerships, family offices, foundations, holding companies, or other entities are attributed to the beneficial holder or family-control network for threshold purposes.

The rule is:

**Below-threshold inheritance may capitalize the next generation. Above-threshold inheritance must first settle the dynasty obligation.**

---

## Illiquid Asset Settlement Above the Threshold

Above-threshold inherited illiquid assets are governed by the Dynasty Illiquid Asset Realization Framework.

The governing doctrine is:

**Illiquidity changes the calendar, not the obligation.**

An above-threshold family business, farm, real estate portfolio, or private-company interest is not forced into immediate liquidation merely because a triggering event occurs. The triggering event measures the obligation. The drawdown schedule pays it.

At the triggering event, the system aggregates assets, applies the threshold, credits prior payments, values the remaining above-threshold exposure, recognizes the obligation, assigns a statutory settlement schedule, and records the obligation in the ledger.

The family may retain ownership and operational control by complying with payment schedules, valuation requirements, reporting obligations, and anti-borrowing rules.

This preserves productive businesses while preventing illiquidity from becoming a permanent escape hatch.

---

## Source Certification

All privileged entry into the superannuation or family-account architecture requires source certification.

The system records whether capital entered as wages, employee contributions, employer contributions, public seed capital, community deposits, family contributions, Roth-settled retirement balances, transition-excluded retirement balances, inherited cash, inherited securities, inherited retirement balances, inherited property proceeds, inherited business proceeds, trust distributions, estate distributions, controlled-entity transfers, or other certified sources.

For each entry, the account records source identity, beneficiary identity, relationship to beneficiary, timestamp, amount, tax status, asset type, valuation method, bucket allocation, contributor type, and threshold relevance.

This is not public exposure of family finances. It is private administrative metadata required to prevent privileged accounts from becoming anonymous dynasty shelters.

Legal form can vary.

Privileged treatment requires attestable source and beneficial-control information.

---

## Anti-Abuse Rule

The transition waiver follows the worker’s own legacy retirement savings. It does not follow opportunistic capital moved into the worker’s name after enactment.

Inheritance follows source character. Sale proceeds from inherited assets retain inherited-proceeds character until tax status and threshold status are certified.

Dynasty exposure follows economic ownership. A family cannot reduce above-threshold exposure by fragmenting assets across descendants, child accounts, trusts, family LLCs, holding companies, or estate vehicles.

For dynasty-threshold purposes, assets transferred from related parties, estates, trusts, controlled entities, and family-control networks remain attributable where economic ownership, retained influence, or coordinated control remains present.

The rule is:

**You can capitalize your children. You cannot use their accounts as fragments of a hidden dynasty balance sheet.**

---

## Fiscal Logic

The transition framework creates a visible middle-class benefit without creating an open-ended tax shelter.

The under-40 transition pass-through waives conversion tax on modest early-career retirement balances. Because these balances are generally smaller and would otherwise generate tax revenue decades later, the cost is primarily a present-value cost rather than a large immediate cash cost.

The inheritance-entry framework is generous below $50 million because that is the system’s intended family-continuity zone. Ordinary families can inherit, sell, retain, or contribute assets into governed accounts without being forced into complex estate engineering.

The revenue discipline comes above the threshold.

Dynasty-scale assets cannot use basis step-up, trusts, illiquidity, family-control entities, or descendant accounts to permanently eliminate embedded gains. Above-threshold assets face realization at the generational transfer, with illiquid assets receiving schedule relief rather than obligation relief.

The fiscal bargain is:

**Forgive migration friction for younger workers. Permit ordinary family continuity below the threshold. Recover deferred embedded gains above the dynasty line.**

## Public Explanation

This framework should be explained as two linked transition rules.

First, younger workers get a **Legacy Retirement Conversion Credit**. If you are under 40, the first $100,000 of your old pre-tax retirement savings can move into the new national retirement account without a tax bill. The money stays retirement capital. It cannot be cashed out, pledged, or borrowed against.

Second, families get a **Family Capital Entry Rule**. If total family assets are below the dynasty threshold, inherited assets can enter the family-capital system after ordinary tax treatment and source certification. If total family assets are above the threshold, the excess must settle the dynasty obligation first.

The public sentence:

**Young people get a clean start. Ordinary families get continuity. Dynasty-scale avoidance gets closed.**

## Draft Statutory Language Concept

During the transition period, an eligible individual below the statutory transition age may elect to transfer qualified legacy retirement balances into a certified universal superannuation account. The first $100,000 of aggregate pre-tax qualified legacy retirement balances legally owned by the eligible individual as of the statutory measurement date shall not be included in gross income solely by reason of conversion, provided such balances are transferred trustee-to-trustee, remain subject to the universal superannuation account’s bucket rules, and are not distributed to the individual.

The conversion exclusion shall be marginal and shall not apply to inherited balances, related-party transfers, post-measurement artificial contributions, disguised compensation, or assets transferred for the principal purpose of securing conversion exclusion treatment.

Qualified transition balances shall enter Bucket A until retirement sufficiency is certified. Thereafter, balances may be allocated according to ordinary bucket-routing rules. Transition treatment shall not alter withdrawal restrictions, anti-pledging rules, inheritance rules, beneficial-ownership attribution, contribution limits, or dynasty-threshold aggregation.

Individuals above the statutory transition age may transfer qualified retirement balances into the universal superannuation system only under ordinary tax-settlement rules. Pre-tax balances transferred by such individuals shall be treated as conversion income except as otherwise provided by statute.

For total attributable family assets below the CPI-indexed $50 million threshold, inherited assets and inheritance proceeds may enter the protected family-capital architecture after ordinary tax treatment, source certification, and bucket routing. Such entry shall not erase source character, alter bucket sequencing, or exempt the assets from threshold aggregation.

Where the decedent, estate, family-control group, or attributable asset pool exceeds the CPI-indexed $50 million threshold, the above-threshold portion shall be governed by the Dynasty Tax and Generational Transfer Framework and any applicable illiquid-asset realization rules. Sale, trust routing, entity conversion, or contribution into descendant accounts shall not convert above-threshold dynasty capital into ordinary contribution capital.

Inherited illiquid assets and proceeds from the sale of inherited illiquid assets shall retain inheritance-proceeds character until source, tax status, valuation, and threshold status are certified.

## Open Questions

The final design must specify the statutory measurement date for under-40 balances, whether age 40 is the correct transition line, whether the $100,000 exclusion should be indexed, and which legacy account types qualify.

The inheritance rule also requires a final choice on below-threshold tax treatment: whether current ordinary tax treatment remains in place during transition, whether step-up basis is preserved below the dynasty threshold, or whether some inherited liquid assets receive partial settlement before family-account entry.

The implementation layer must define annual contribution limits, source-certification standards, state tax conformity, and the interaction between estate-level, heir-level, and family-network threshold attribution.

---

## Through Line

This framework completes the universal superannuation architecture.

It does not punish younger workers for saving in the old system.

It does not force older workers into a new architecture late in life.

It does not treat ordinary inheritance as aristocratic avoidance.

It does not let dynasty-scale capital hide inside child accounts, trusts, or post-sale proceeds.

**Young workers get migration relief. Ordinary families get continuity. Dynasty-scale capital gets realization.**

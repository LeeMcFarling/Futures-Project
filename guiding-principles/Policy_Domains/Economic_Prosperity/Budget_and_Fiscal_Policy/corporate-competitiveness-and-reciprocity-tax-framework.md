## Executive Summary

America should be easy to build in, easy to invest in, and hard to game.

The United States should be the best place in the world to start, scale,
and sustain great companies. American firms should have access to deep
capital markets, advanced infrastructure, world-class talent, strong legal
protections, public research spillovers, abundant energy, faster permitting,
and targeted strategic support when national competitiveness requires it.
This framework commits to providing those things. It is not a posture of
suspicion toward business. It is a commitment to making productive enterprise
genuinely easier in America than anywhere else.

In return, large profitable corporations that benefit from the American
economic environment at extraordinary scale should make a baseline
contribution to the public system that enables their success. The same
principle that governs the rest of this fiscal architecture applies here:
actors who benefit from the American system at extraordinary scale incur
structured obligations to sustain it. For households, capital holders, and
corporations alike, scale changes the social contract. The mechanisms
differ because the actors differ. The principle is the same.

This framework rejects two failed models simultaneously. The free-riding
model, in which large profitable firms extract value from American markets
and institutions while using loopholes, offshore structures, or accounting
timing to avoid meaningful contribution, undermines the legitimacy of the
market system itself. The anti-business drag model, in which productive
firms face so much friction, uncertainty, and complexity that formation and
domestic expansion become less attractive than competing jurisdictions,
surrenders the growth capacity that makes everything else possible. Neither
model is acceptable. Both are currently in operation.

The objective is a high-performance reciprocity regime: the easiest advanced
economy in which to build a legitimate great company, and one of the hardest
in which to game the public system.

## Core Principle

A corporation that benefits materially from the American operating
environment at scale should contribute to the maintenance and renewal of
that environment. The environment in question is not abstract. It includes
the rule of law and contract enforcement that make long-term investment
possible, the capital markets that enable formation and growth at speed,
the public research base whose spillovers underwrite private innovation,
the infrastructure and logistics networks that move goods and people, the
defense umbrella and trade architecture that secure access to global markets,
the workforce formed through decades of public education investment, and
the domestic consumer purchasing power that generates the demand large firms
depend on. None of these are free. None of them are self-sustaining. All of
them require continuous public investment and institutional maintenance.

Corporate success remains encouraged, protected, and rewarded. Permanent
free-riding on the system that enables that success does not.

The United States should compete aggressively to attract builders, founders,
manufacturers, and innovators. It should not compete to attract accounting
gamesmanship or tolerance for zero-tax outcomes among highly profitable
firms operating at national scale. Those are not the same competition, and
conflating them has cost the system its legitimacy without gaining the
growth it was supposed to buy.

## What the Current System Gets Wrong

The current corporate tax architecture fails in five specific and
interconnected ways.

The first is the divergence between where value is created and where profits
are recognized. A firm may rely entirely on American markets, labor,
infrastructure, legal protections, and consumer demand while routing its
profits through legal entity structures, transfer pricing arrangements, or
intercompany debt to jurisdictions where they face minimal taxation. The
economic activity occurs in the United States. The tax obligation does not.
This is not primarily a compliance failure — it is a structural feature of
a system that allows paper location to determine taxable geography regardless
of where economic substance resides.

The second is the legitimacy damage inflicted by persistent zero-tax outcomes
at scale. When corporations that report billions in annual profits to
shareholders consistently pay negligible federal income tax, the public
correctly concludes that the rules apply differently to different actors.
That conclusion is correct, and it erodes confidence in the market system
itself — not just in government. A market economy whose tax architecture
visibly rewards optimization over production cannot sustain the political
support it requires to function over time.

The third is the use of the tax code as a substitute for industrial policy.
Sector-specific credits, timing preferences, and carve-outs have accumulated
over decades into a shadow investment system that is off-budget, weakly
accountable, difficult to evaluate, and nearly impossible to sunset. This
shadow system does not do industrial policy well — it does it invisibly,
which is worse than doing it badly in the open, because invisible policy
cannot be measured, corrected, or terminated.

The fourth is the competitive disadvantage this creates for firms that
actually build things. A manufacturer competing against a financial
engineering operation faces a structural disadvantage if the engineering
operation can reduce its effective tax rate to near zero through structures
unavailable to the manufacturer. The tax system should not reward
optimization skill over production skill. Currently, at sufficient scale,
it does.

The fifth is the regressive complexity premium. Large multinational firms
maintain teams of lawyers and accountants whose primary function is
navigating and exploiting tax complexity. Small and mid-sized domestic
businesses cannot. The result is that complexity itself functions as a
subsidy for scale, compounding the competitive advantages that large firms
already possess through capital access, brand, and market position.

## The State's Commitment: What America Provides

This framework is structured as a reciprocal bargain, not a unilateral
obligation. Before stating what corporations owe to the system, it is
necessary to state clearly what the system commits to providing in return.

The United States commits to building and maintaining the operating
environment that makes domestic enterprise genuinely competitive. This
means modern infrastructure — freight, logistics, grid, and broadband —
that moves goods and data at globally competitive cost and speed. It means
abundant, reliable, and increasingly affordable energy from a diversified
domestic supply base. It means faster and more predictable permitting, so
that a firm that decides to build in America can execute that decision on
a timeline that competes with peer jurisdictions rather than stretching
across a decade of administrative review. It means workforce pipelines
through the apprenticeship, community college, and reskilling architecture
built elsewhere in this platform, so that firms expanding domestic capacity
can find trained workers rather than importing them or relocating. It means
strategic financing through the Public Capital Authority for investments
in sectors where private capital is insufficient to absorb early risk alone.
It means export assistance and market access support through the strategic
trade architecture. It means R&D commercialization support that converts
public research investment into domestic production rather than allowing
it to be commercialized elsewhere.

These are not small commitments. They represent a genuine restructuring of
the state's relationship to productive enterprise — not as a passive
regulator waiting to penalize failure, but as an active partner in building
the conditions for success. The reciprocity floor described below is the
corporate side of that bargain. Neither side is optional. A state that
demands baseline contribution without providing genuine competitive support
is extracting, not partnering. A state that provides genuine competitive
support without expecting baseline contribution is subsidizing free-riding.
This framework requires both sides to hold.

## Baseline Reciprocity Floor

The Inflation Reduction Act's corporate alternative minimum tax established
the correct principle: large profitable corporations should not consistently
report substantial earnings to shareholders while making negligible federal
tax contributions. This framework retains that principle and modernizes the
mechanism into a clearer, more durable, and more precisely targeted
reciprocity floor.

A corporation enters the reciprocity floor regime when it meets both of the
following conditions on a rolling three-year basis. Multi-year averaging is
essential. Single-year measurement creates an obvious engineering opportunity:
a firm can structure one loss year in three to escape the floor's application
regardless of its sustained profitability. The rolling three-year window
closes that opportunity while smoothing the impact of genuine business cycle
variation.

For purposes of this framework, a covered corporation is a C-corporation
or consolidated C-corporation group — including U.S. subsidiaries of foreign
multinationals with material U.S. economic presence — whose adjusted book
profit and effective tax rate are measured on a consolidated group basis.
Multinational groups are assessed on their worldwide consolidated financial
statements, with U.S.-sourced income determined under the value creation
alignment rules. Pass-through entities — S-corporations, partnerships, and
sole proprietorships — are excluded from the reciprocity floor regime unless
a covered corporation uses a pass-through structure primarily to avoid floor
application, in which case the anti-abuse doctrine treats the arrangement
as a covered consolidated group for measurement purposes. The test is
economic substance, not legal form — consistent with every other provision
in this framework.

The scale gate is crossed when average adjusted book profit exceeds $100
million over the prior three fiscal years. This threshold is set deliberately
above the level of ordinary successful enterprise — profitable regional
businesses, growing mid-market firms, family companies with strong earnings
— and below the level at which sophisticated tax architecture is both
accessible and economically dominant as a planning strategy. A firm with
$100 million in average annual book profit is operating at genuine national
scale and has both the resources to engage in complex tax planning and the
obligation to contribute meaningfully to the system it operates within.

The contribution gate is crossed when the effective federal cash tax
contribution falls below 15 percent over the same three-year period. This
aligns the framework with the OECD global minimum tax standard, providing
international defensibility and preventing the United States from being
either more aggressive or more permissive than the emerging international
norm. A 15 percent effective floor is not a high bar for a profitable
firm operating primarily in the United States. It is a bar that persistent
zero-tax engineering is specifically designed to clear, which is why the
gate exists.

When both conditions are met, a baseline reciprocity liability applies
sufficient to restore the corporation to the 15 percent minimum contribution
on its three-year average adjusted book profit. Liability is calculated
on the cumulative shortfall from the floor over the measurement period,
not on any single year's outcome.

Corporations may reduce baseline reciprocity liability through qualified
domestic investment, workforce expansion, strategic capacity creation, and
approved PCA-linked activity. Reciprocity credits reduce liability toward
the floor. They do not reduce it below the floor. A covered corporation
that makes substantial genuine domestic investment pays less than it would
otherwise owe under the reciprocity mechanism, but it does not return to
a zero-tax outcome. The floor is a floor, not a ceiling it is optimized
to stay at.

## Value Creation Alignment Rules

The most consequential reform in this framework is not the floor itself
but the sourcing rules that determine where taxable corporate income is
recognized. A reciprocity floor applied to paper-location profits is
only as effective as the rules that determine which profits count. Without
value creation alignment, a sophisticated firm can maintain the appearance
of meeting the floor while routing the economic substance of its profits
to lower-tax jurisdictions.

Value creation alignment rules establish that the sourcing of taxable
income for covered corporations is determined by where economic value is
genuinely created, not by where legal entities are registered or where
intercompany contracts are nominally executed. The relevant indicators are
the location of workforce and payroll, the location of R&D activity and
innovation, the location of production and physical productive assets, the
location of final consumer markets and revenue recognition, the reliance
on public infrastructure and logistics networks, and the use of publicly
supported strategic ecosystems including defense, trade security, and
publicly funded research spillovers.

These indicators function as a weighted sourcing formula applied to covered
corporations above the scale gate. A firm whose workforce, production,
R&D, and consumer markets are predominantly domestic cannot source the
majority of its profits offshore through intercompany arrangements without
a demonstrable economic basis for that sourcing. The economic substance
doctrine — already established in existing tax law as the foundation for
challenging transactions lacking genuine economic rationale — provides the
legal framework on which value creation alignment rests. This framework
extends that doctrine from individual transactions to the sourcing of
profits across the enterprise, applied to covered corporations on an
annual reporting basis.

Pure paper location decisions carry no weight in the alignment formula.
A legal entity registered in a jurisdiction where the firm has no employees,
no productive assets, no customers, and no genuine operations cannot
function as a profit recognition vehicle for income that economically
originates in the United States. The alignment rules make that explicit
in statute rather than leaving it to case-by-case litigation under general
economic substance doctrine.

## Offshore and Accounting Arbitrage Closure

The value creation alignment rules address the structural sourcing problem.
The arbitrage closure provisions address the specific instruments through
which profit shifting and tax base erosion currently operate.

Artificial profit shifting to low-substance jurisdictions — the transfer
of nominal ownership of intellectual property, financial assets, or
contractual rights to entities in low-tax jurisdictions where those assets
are not genuinely managed or developed — is treated as a sourcing violation
under the value creation alignment rules. The substance of the asset
determines its sourcing, not the domicile of the holding entity.

Transfer pricing disconnected from economic reality — the pricing of
intercompany transactions at rates that would not be agreed to between
unrelated parties at arm's length — is addressed through enhanced
comparability standards that weight actual economic contribution over
legal form. A subsidiary that performs routine functions does not receive
residual profit allocations that would only be appropriate for a principal
entity assuming genuine risk and providing genuine value.

Earnings stripping through related-party debt — the loading of domestic
entities with intercompany debt at above-market rates to generate deductible
interest that reduces U.S. taxable income — is addressed through deductibility
limits for related-party interest that scale with the firm's domestic
earnings and genuine third-party debt ratios.

Stock compensation distortions that produce persistent zero liability —
in which large option and restricted stock deductions taken for tax purposes
far exceed the compensation expense recognized in financial statements,
generating tax losses that eliminate liability for years — are addressed
by requiring that stock compensation deductions for purposes of the
reciprocity floor calculation be capped at the amount recognized in
financial statement expense. This closes the gap between book profit
and taxable income that this specific structure creates for otherwise
highly profitable firms.

Timing games using accelerated depreciation, loss carryforwards, and
other deferral mechanisms are not targeted where they represent genuine
productive investment — accelerated depreciation for real capital
expenditure is a legitimate policy instrument and is preserved. They are
addressed where they are deployed primarily as deferral mechanisms
disconnected from the productive activity they nominally reflect.

## Reciprocity Credits for Genuine Domestic Contribution

The reciprocity framework does not operate as a pure tax increase. Firms
that make genuine domestic investments — creating real economic capacity,
employing workers, building productive assets, and deepening the domestic
supply chains that make the American economy more resilient — receive
credits that reduce their reciprocity liability. The credit structure
converts the floor from a minimum tax into an incentive structure: the
more genuinely a firm builds in America, the lower its reciprocity
obligation, subject to the floor.

Qualifying investments include new productive facilities and plant
expansion, strategic manufacturing capacity in sectors identified through
the corridor and industrial planning architecture, apprenticeship and
workforce training programs that produce credentialed workers, domestic
supply-chain deepening that reduces import dependence in strategic sectors,
R&D activity tied to domestic commercialization rather than offshore
exploitation, energy, grid, freight, and logistics co-investment that
improves the shared infrastructure all firms depend on, and resilience
investments in critical sectors designated through the PCA portfolio
framework.

Credits are calculated on a formulaic basis against documented qualifying
expenditure, are available only in the year incurred or carried forward
for a defined period, and are subject to sunset review at defined intervals.
No credit category is permanent by default. A credit that cannot demonstrate
a positive outcome against the investment it represents does not survive
renewal. This discipline mirrors the broader capital allocation framework:
public support is measurable, time-bounded, and contingent on results.

As noted, no combination of credits reduces a covered corporation below
the reciprocity floor once both gates are triggered. The floor exists
precisely for firms whose structure or history would otherwise produce
zero-contribution outcomes regardless of investment activity.

## Replacing Hidden Subsidies with Explicit Instruments

Where the government seeks to accelerate strategic sectors, investment,
or industrial development, explicit capital instruments deployed through
the Public Capital Authority are superior to tax code carve-outs in every
dimension that matters for accountability and effectiveness.

A PCA growth loan is visible, traceable, repayable, and subject to defined
performance expectations. A tax credit for the same activity is off-budget,
difficult to audit, politically entrenched, and nearly impossible to sunset.
Both instruments transfer public value to the firm. Only one of them is
accountable for what that value produces.

Public support under this framework is rule-based, published, and
performance-linked — not discretionary favoritism. The criteria for
receiving PCA financing, reciprocity credits, or other forms of public
competitive support are established in statute or published regulatory
guidance, applied uniformly to qualifying activity, and subject to DoDA
outcome certification. No firm receives preferential treatment through
undisclosed arrangements, regulatory accommodation, or politically
motivated exception. The discipline that applies to the tax side —
broad rules, clean enforcement, no bespoke carve-outs — applies equally
to the support side. A government that demands transparent contribution
while dispensing opaque support has not solved the legitimacy problem.
It has moved it.

The conversion doctrine established in the capital allocation reform
framework applies here: tax expenditures that function as sector-specific
investment instruments are candidates for conversion into explicit PCA
instruments on a prospective basis. New qualifying activity in converted
sectors defaults into PCA instruments. Legacy tax preferences are grandfathered
on defined timetables and phased out as the PCA framework matures in each
sector.

The tax code's role in corporate investment support narrows over time to
the reciprocity credit structure — formulaic, transparent, and directly
linked to qualifying domestic investment — rather than the current
accumulation of bespoke carve-outs whose aggregate cost is measured in
hundreds of billions annually and whose aggregate benefit is largely
unmeasured.

## Small and Mid-Sized Business Distinction

This framework applies to corporations operating at the scale defined by
the reciprocity floor gates. It is not designed for, and does not apply to,
ordinary domestic businesses, family firms, growth-stage companies, or
closely held enterprises whose profitability derives from active management
and operation rather than from financial engineering at national scale.

Below the $100 million average book profit threshold, normal tax rules
apply and the reciprocity floor mechanism does not engage. Closely held
firms whose book profit derives primarily from pass-through operations or
active owner management are assessed on economic substance grounds
consistent with the rest of the framework — the determination is made by
the nature of the income, not merely by the legal structure of the entity.

A startup safe harbor applies explicitly: no reciprocity floor obligation
attaches until a firm has sustained profitability above the scale gate
threshold for a full three-year rolling period. A firm that crosses the
book profit threshold in a single year does not enter the regime. A firm
that crosses it in two of three years does not enter the regime. Sustained
profitability is the trigger, not episodic success. This protects founders
and growth-stage companies during the years when capital is being deployed,
markets are being built, and profitability is uneven — the period when
certainty of treatment matters most and where the reciprocity floor would
be most disruptive to genuine enterprise formation.

The ambition for small and mid-sized business is the opposite of the
reciprocity floor: simpler filing, clearer depreciation rules, lower
compliance burden, easier access to growth capital through the PCA, faster
approvals for legitimate expansion, and an overall administrative experience
that is less burdensome than comparable jurisdictions. The complexity that
rises for gaming should fall for building. This is not a rhetorical
aspiration. It is a specific design requirement for the administrative
modernization agenda.

## Administrative Modernization

The reciprocity framework requires administrative infrastructure capable
of processing multinational corporate reporting at the scale and complexity
the covered population represents. The IRS and Treasury systems that would
administer these provisions must be rebuilt for the purpose, not patched
onto legacy infrastructure designed for a simpler tax environment.

Standardized multinational reporting enables the value creation alignment
analysis on a consistent, comparable basis across covered corporations.
Real-time reconciliation of credits and liabilities reduces the gap between
what firms report and what they owe. Clearer beneficial ownership mapping
addresses the opacity that allows nominal legal structures to obscure
economic substance. Cross-border information integration — through FATCA,
the OECD Common Reporting Standard, and bilateral exchange agreements —
creates the data environment in which offshore profit shifting is detectable
rather than invisible. Automated anomaly detection identifies patterns in
reported figures that diverge from economic indicators in ways consistent
with arbitrage rather than genuine business variation.

For compliant firms, modernized administration means faster certainty,
lower audit burden, and more predictable outcomes. For aggressive structures,
it means shorter detection lag and higher exposure probability. The
DoDA monitoring function — already established as the system-wide
measurement layer for the institutional stack — applies to enforcement
velocity in the corporate domain as it does in the capital holder domain:
tracking the rate at which novel avoidance structures are identified and
addressed, and publishing that rate publicly as an accountability mechanism
on Treasury's classification and guidance function.

## Regulatory Certainty and Appeals

A reciprocity framework that creates unpredictable classification risk
is a framework that imposes a hidden compliance tax on legitimate enterprise.
Firms operating in good faith need timely, binding answers about how the
rules apply to their specific structures before committing to major
investment and organizational decisions. Regulatory uncertainty is itself
a form of friction — one that falls hardest on firms without large
in-house tax departments, which is precisely the population this framework
is designed to protect.

Treasury operates an expedited binding ruling process for covered
corporations and entities approaching coverage thresholds. A firm that
submits a complete ruling request — including full disclosure of its
consolidated structure, income sourcing, and the specific transaction or
arrangement for which certainty is sought — receives a binding ruling
within 90 days. A request that receives no ruling within 90 days is
treated as approved as filed, subject to audit review within a defined
subsequent window. Silence is not a safe harbor for aggressive structures,
but it is not an indefinite hold on legitimate planning either.

Binding rulings apply to the specific facts disclosed. Material changes
to structure, operations, or sourcing require a new ruling request. Rulings
do not bind Treasury with respect to arrangements that achieve the same
economic outcome through undisclosed steps — the step-transaction doctrine
applies to ruling requests as it does to all classification determinations.

Firms that disagree with a classification determination — including a
determination that the reciprocity floor applies, that a specific structure
constitutes an anti-abuse arrangement, or that a credit claim does not
qualify — may pursue administrative appeal through a dedicated corporate
reciprocity appeals unit within IRS, with a target resolution window of
180 days from filing. Unresolved appeals proceed to Tax Court under
existing judicial review procedures. The burden of demonstrating that
a classification is incorrect rests with the firm for floor-trigger
determinations and with Treasury for credit disallowances where the firm
has provided complete documentation of qualifying expenditure.

This two-track certainty architecture — advance rulings for prospective
planning, defined appeals for disputed determinations — ensures that
firms are not navigating open-ended regulatory ambiguity on decisions
that determine where they build, hire, and invest.

## Transition Strategy

Reform proceeds in three phases matched to institutional capacity and
political feasibility.

In the first twenty-four months, the existing IRA minimum tax floor is
preserved while the upgraded reciprocity mechanism is designed and the
administrative infrastructure required to operate it is built. Treasury
conducts a comprehensive inventory of major corporate tax expenditures by
sector, beneficiary, cost, and measurable outcome. Anti-arbitrage
modernization rules are published for notice and comment. Reporting
modernization systems begin deployment. Reciprocity credit standards are
established in regulatory guidance. Startup and growth-stage compliance
pathways are simplified as a parallel track, so that the administrative
investment in enforcement capacity visibly produces benefits for productive
firms as well as obligations for large ones.

From years two through five, the conversion of selected tax expenditures
into PCA instruments begins in sectors where conversion is most feasible
and PCA capacity is most mature. Value creation alignment rules take full
effect for covered corporations. Legacy deductions are simplified and
sunset schedules are established for provisions that fail the outcome test.
The upgraded reciprocity floor replaces the IRA minimum tax mechanism. The
framework is benchmarked against peer jurisdictions annually on business
formation friction, effective tax rates for genuine producers, and
compliance burden for small and mid-sized firms.

From year five forward, the mature system operates with a stable
competitiveness regime, a narrow and updated anti-avoidance architecture,
and an explicit strategic investment toolkit outside the tax code that
has largely replaced the hidden subsidy system that preceded it. The
goal is a corporate tax environment that is globally recognized as
rewarding production, punishing gaming, and providing genuine support
for the firms that choose to build in America.

## Annual Competitiveness Benchmarking

The reciprocity framework commits the state to making America genuinely
easier to build in, not merely to asserting that it is. That commitment
requires measurement. DoDA publishes an annual competitiveness benchmark
report covering the United States against a defined peer set of advanced
economies across five indicators.

The first is permitting and approval speed — median time from application
to construction authorization for industrial, commercial, and energy
projects above defined scale thresholds, compared against peer jurisdictions.
The second is compliance burden — estimated annual hours and cost required
for a covered corporation to meet federal tax filing, reporting, and
audit obligations, compared against peer jurisdictions. The third is
energy cost — average industrial electricity and natural gas prices by
region relative to peer jurisdictions, updated quarterly. The fourth is
workforce pipeline speed — median time to fill skilled technical roles
in manufacturing, energy, and technology sectors domestically versus
peer economies. The fifth is effective producer tax burden — average
effective federal tax rate for corporations whose revenue, employment,
and physical assets are predominantly domestic, compared against the
nominal reciprocity floor and against peer jurisdictions.

If any of the five indicators deteriorates for two consecutive annual
benchmark periods relative to the peer set, an automatic review is
triggered. The review requires Treasury and the relevant agencies to
publish a cause assessment within 90 days and a corrective action plan
within 180 days. The review does not automatically modify policy. It
creates a public record that a competitive commitment has not been met
and a mandatory government response to that record. Congressional
oversight is activated by the published trigger. The corrective action
plan is subject to DoDA outcome certification on the same timescale as
any other federally authorized intervention.

This mechanism converts the state's competitive commitments from
aspirational language into measurable obligations with defined
consequences for non-performance.

## Bottom Line

This framework rejects the false choice between pro-business growth and
fair corporate contribution. Those are not competing values. They are
complementary requirements of a functioning market economy.

America can be the best place in the world to build a great company. Great
companies can contribute to the nation that made their success possible.
The state can provide genuine competitive support and expect genuine
reciprocity in return. None of these propositions requires abandoning the
others.

The corporate tax system should make one thing easy and one thing hard.
Building should be easy. Gaming should be hard. Right now the balance is
inverted at sufficient scale. This framework restores it.

Reward production. Reward innovation. Reward investment. Help builders.
End persistent free-riding. That is the complete doctrine — and each part
of it depends on the others to hold.

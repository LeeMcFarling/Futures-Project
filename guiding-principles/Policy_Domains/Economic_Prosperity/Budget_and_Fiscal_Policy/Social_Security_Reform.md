# Problem Statement

Social Security retirement benefits are financed through a pay-as-you-go model that depends on stable worker-to-retiree ratios. Persistent below-replacement fertility, rising longevity, and slower labor-force growth have placed the system under long-term strain, requiring repeated political interventions to maintain solvency. At the same time, the U.S. supplemental retirement system is fragmented, voluntary, and unevenly adopted, leaving many workers underprepared for retirement despite decades of economic growth.

Incremental adjustments can delay insolvency but do not resolve the structural mismatch between demographic dependency and retirement financing.

---

# Policy Objective

Transition the United States from a demographic transfer-based retirement system to a universal, prefunded superannuation system for future cohorts, while fully honoring all accrued Social Security promises. The reform seeks to stabilize long-run public finances, raise national saving, and provide every worker with a simple, portable, and owned retirement asset.

---

# Core Design

## Cohort-Based Transition

- Individuals born on or after a statutory cutoff year (e.g., 2030) do not accrue Social Security retirement benefits.
- All current retirees and all workers born before the cutoff remain fully covered under existing Social Security retirement rules.
- Social Security Disability Insurance and survivor benefits remain intact and are addressed separately.

Following the cutoff, Social Security transitions into a closed-book, self-amortizing system: payroll taxes continue to finance a shrinking pool of legacy obligations, with no new retirement liabilities added.

---

## Universal Superannuation Accounts

- Each eligible citizen receives a nationally standardized superannuation account.
- Accounts are individually owned, portable across employers, and independent of firm-specific plan design.
- Assets are legally private property and protected from employer or government claims except as explicitly defined in statute.

This system replaces the existing patchwork of 401(k), 403(b), and similar employer-sponsored retirement plans.

---

## Mandatory Payroll Contributions

- Employers contribute a fixed statutory percentage of wages directly into each worker’s superannuation account via payroll.
- Employee contributions beyond the base rate remain optional but are automatically facilitated.
- These contributions replace discretionary employer matches and voluntary enrollment regimes.

Payroll tax rates for Social Security remain in place during the transition to finance legacy obligations. Upon actuarial exhaustion of those obligations, payroll tax rates automatically step down pursuant to actuarial triggers defined in statute.

---

## Tax Treatment (Single-Tax Principle)

Superannuation assets are taxed once, at contribution, and receive no further income taxation within defined limits.

- Contributions are made from after-tax income.
- Investment growth is untaxed.
- Retirement withdrawals up to the indexed cap are untaxed.

This structure eliminates late-life tax shocks, improves revenue certainty, and simplifies compliance. Superannuation assets constitute private property. Changes to tax treatment or withdrawal rules apply prospectively only and may not retroactively alter accrued balances or contributions.

---

## Lifetime Cap (Bucket A) and Above-Cap (Bucket B) Treatment

#### Bucket A — Protected Superannuation (Retirement Capital)
- Each individual receives a lifetime superannuation special-treatment cap of $15 million, indexed annually to CPI-U.
- Mandatory payroll contributions accrue to Bucket A until the indexed cap is reached.
- Contributions to Bucket A are made from after-tax income.
- Investment growth within Bucket A is untaxed.
- Withdrawals from Bucket A are tax-free when made at or after the statutory retirement age.
- Bucket A assets are retirement-locked and may not be pledged, borrowed against, or used as collateral.
- Balances within Bucket A transfer to heirs tax-free, roll into the heir’s superannuation account, and remain subject to retirement-lock and cap aggregation rules.

Bucket A exists solely to provide secure retirement income and bounded intergenerational continuity.

#### Bucket B — Above-Cap Capital (Taxable Capital)
- Once the indexed Bucket A cap is reached, mandatory payroll contributions cease.
- Any additional elective contributions and any investment returns attributable to balances above the indexed cap accrue to Bucket B.
- Bucket B assets are held within the same custodial structure and portfolio allocation as Bucket A, preserving unified portfolio management.
- Bucket B assets are treated as ordinary taxable capital.
- Capital gains attributable to Bucket B are subject to capital-gains taxation, including mandatory realization at death under the unified estate taxation framework.
- Bucket B assets may be pledged or borrowed against under standardized loan-to-value limits; borrowing does not eliminate ultimate capital-gains tax liability, including realization at death.

#### Clarifying Principle

For clarity, the cap limits preferential tax treatment, not ownership or portfolio size.
Balances up to the indexed cap function as protected retirement capital (Bucket A), while balances above the cap function as ordinary taxable capital (Bucket B), governed by the same tax and estate rules applicable to other large capital holdings

#### Anti-Avoidance Rule

Borrowing, leverage, or liquidity access associated with Bucket B assets may not be used to defer, eliminate, or recharacterize capital-gains tax liability. Unrealized gains attributable to Bucket B are realized and taxed at death regardless of interim borrowing activity.

---

### Withdrawals

- Withdrawals attributable to balances at or below the indexed cap are tax-free.
- Withdrawals attributable to balances above the cap are taxed at capital-gains rates.

### At Death

- Balances up to the indexed cap transfer to heirs tax-free.
- Unrealized gains attributable to balances above the cap are realized and taxed at capital-gains rates at death, aligned with the national estate taxation framework.

---

## Inheritance and Intergenerational Rules

- Inherited superannuation balances roll into the heir’s superannuation account.
- Inherited balances remain retirement-locked for the heir, subject to standard retirement-age rules and limited hardship exceptions.
- Inherited balances count toward the heir’s lifetime indexed cap.

This preserves intergenerational compounding while preventing early consumption or dynastic cashouts.

---

## Investment Defaults, Advisor Integration, and Guardrails

- Default investment is a low-fee, diversified lifecycle (target-date) portfolio designed to deliver adequate retirement outcomes for the majority of participants.
- The default fund menu is intentionally limited to prevent excessive complexity, high fees, and predatory practices.
- Prohibited activities include borrowing against superannuation assets, pledging them as collateral, self-dealing, or related-party transactions.

The default investment menu constitutes a statutory safe harbor. Participants who remain in the default are not required to engage advisors or make active investment decisions to achieve reasonable retirement outcomes.

## Optional Off-Menu Investment Election

Participants may elect to invest superannuation assets outside the default investment menu, subject to affirmative opt-in, enhanced disclosure, and fiduciary oversight.

- Off-menu investment does not alter contribution limits, tax treatment, retirement lock, inheritance rules, bucket structure, or cap aggregation.
- Off-menu assets may be managed by the participant directly or by a licensed fiduciary financial advisor operating under a statutory superannuation fiduciary standard.
- Commission-based compensation, proprietary product steering, revenue sharing, and undisclosed conflicts of interest are prohibited.
- Advisors may charge transparent asset-based, flat, or retainer fees for off-menu management and related planning services.

This framework preserves consumer protection by default while maintaining a legitimate, honest role for financial advisors in portfolio customization, taxable capital coordination, estate planning, and liquidity management.

## Retirement Age and Longevity Adjustment

- The normal retirement age is indexed to cohort life expectancy using a statutory formula with smoothing and maximum annual adjustment limits.
- This age governs superannuation withdrawals, annuitization requirements, and Social Security drawdown for legacy cohorts.

This alignment prevents age arbitrage and repeated legislative renegotiation.

---

## Responsibility and Liability Boundary

Outcomes associated with default investment options are governed by statutory safe-harbor standards. Outcomes associated with off-menu investment elections remain the responsibility of the participant and any designated fiduciary advisor.

This separation ensures accountability without reintroducing systemic risk or public liability.


## Retirement Conversion and Longevity Protection

- At retirement, a defined portion of accumulated balances must be converted into a regulated lifetime income product (public or tightly regulated private annuity or pooled longevity fund).
- This preserves protection against outliving assets, replicating the core insurance function of Social Security retirement benefits.

---

# Pre-Seeding and Capital Formation

The superannuation system includes a one-time government seed contribution at birth or first eligibility.

- Initial seed levels may be symbolic and increase gradually.
- Seed levels scale toward a statutory cap of $10,000 per individual.
- Increases are tied to objective fiscal conditions, including primary budget balance, declining debt-to-GDP ratios, and reduction of legacy Social Security obligations.

Pre-seeding is a conditional national investment, not an open-ended entitlement.

---

# Financial Literacy Integration

- Mandatory financial literacy education is required for high school graduation.
- Curriculum standards include compound growth, fees, inflation, taxes, insurance, debt, and fraud prevention.
- Students interact with a simulated view of their superannuation account to reinforce applied understanding.

---

# Macroeconomic Effects

- Raises household and national saving.
- Converts demographic liabilities into owned financial assets.
- Reduces long-run fiscal risk and political pressure on retirement financing.
- Improves labor mobility and retirement adequacy for workers underserved by the current system.

---

# Conclusion

This reform completes the transition from transfer-financed retirement to ownership-financed retirement. By closing Social Security retirement accruals to future cohorts and replacing them with a universal superannuation system, the United States can stabilize retirement outcomes, simplify payroll infrastructure, and build durable, intergenerational capital without breaking promises to current retirees.

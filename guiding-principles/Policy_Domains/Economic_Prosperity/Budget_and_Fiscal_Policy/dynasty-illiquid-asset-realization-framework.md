# Dynasty Illiquid Asset Realization Framework

## Purpose

The Dynasty Tax and Generational Transfer Framework establishes that
above-threshold accumulated gains must be recognized at the generational
transfer. It does not require immediate liquidation of the assets in
which those gains are embedded.

This brief is the operational layer. It defines how dynasty realization
works for illiquid, closely held, operating, and hard-to-value assets:
how the obligation is calculated, how it is scheduled, how prior payments
are credited, how the triggering event functions as a checkpoint rather
than a forced sale, and how the prepayment election gives families a
planning tool they can use before the trigger arrives.

The governing doctrine:

**Illiquidity changes the calendar, not the obligation.**

---

## The Problem with Immediate Liquidation

A well-designed dynasty tax that required immediate cash settlement at
death or at the 100-year holding mark would be both economically
destructive and politically unsustainable.

A family business that employs hundreds of people, a real estate
portfolio that houses thousands of tenants, a mineral rights position
that underpins regional energy infrastructure, a private equity stake
that represents decades of operating control — none of these can be
liquidated in the weeks following a death without destroying value,
harming counterparties who had no role in the triggering event, and
creating exactly the "government forced us to sell the farm" narrative
that would undermine the entire framework.

The anti-dynasty regime does not need immediate cash. It needs a
recognized obligation, a durable payment structure, and prevention of
illiquidity as a permanent escape hatch. Those are compatible with
long-duration settlement. They are incompatible with firesales.

The framework is not "sell everything." It is "you owe a known amount,
and you have a structured schedule to pay it."

---

## Core Doctrine

A dynasty-triggering event is a valuation and reconciliation checkpoint,
not a forced liquidation event.

At death or the 100-year holding mark, the system aggregates attributable
assets, applies the CPI-indexed threshold, credits prior recognized gains
and prepayments, values the remaining above-threshold exposure, and
assigns a statutory drawdown schedule. The family is not required to
firesell assets. The obligation must be satisfied on the schedule through
cash, distributions, partial sales, refinancing, or orderly disposition.

Death measures the obligation. The drawdown schedule pays it.

The framework does not require immediate liquidation of illiquid or
closely held assets. Above-threshold dynasty realization creates a
recognized obligation, not a forced sale. The obligation is satisfied
through a statutory drawdown schedule calibrated by asset class,
liquidity profile, and operating-company risk. Illiquidity may alter
timing, payment structure, and disposition method. It does not eliminate
the obligation or permit artificial valuation discounts created by
family-control structures.

---

## The Triggering Event as Checkpoint

At the triggering event — death or the 100-year holding mark — the
system performs a standardized valuation and reconciliation sequence.

**Step 1 — Asset aggregation.** All assets attributable to the
beneficial holder or family-control network are aggregated. SSI-backed
beneficial-control attestation maps paper entities — trusts, family
LLCs, family limited partnerships, family offices, foundations, holding
companies — back to the underlying control persons. Fragmentation across
legal entities does not reduce the aggregated threshold calculation.

**Step 2 — Threshold application.** The CPI-indexed $50 million
threshold is applied. Only the portion of total beneficial assets above
the threshold is subject to dynasty realization. A holder with $80
million in attributable assets faces realization on $30 million, not
on the full $80 million.

**Step 3 — Prior payment credit.** Gains previously recognized through
borrowing-triggered realization events are credited. Basis adjustments
from those events reduce the remaining embedded gain. Prepayment credits
from voluntary prior settlement are applied. The system does not tax
the same gain twice. Every dollar of gain is recognized exactly once.

**Step 4 — Obligation recognition.** The remaining above-threshold
embedded gain — after threshold application and prior-payment credits
— is recognized as a tax obligation at the triggering event. Recognition
occurs at fair market value of the underlying assets, not at net equity
after debt. A holder who borrowed against above-threshold assets to
reduce apparent equity cannot use that leverage to reduce the recognized
tax base.

**Step 5 — Schedule assignment.** The recognized obligation is assigned
a statutory drawdown schedule calibrated by asset class, liquidity
profile, and operating-company status. The schedule is not discretionary.
It is assigned by rule based on the asset composition of the above-
threshold pool.

**Step 6 — Ledger entry.** The recognized obligation, schedule, asset
composition, prior credits, and valuation methodology are recorded in
the immutable government ledger. Subsequent payments, valuations, and
credits are appended. The record persists.

## The Three Settlement Lanes

### Lane 1 — Ordinary Drawdown

The default lane. Triggered automatically at death or the 100-year
holding mark for above-threshold assets that have not been prepaid.

The recognized obligation is assigned a statutory payment schedule
calibrated by asset class. Payment may be made from any combination
of cash, dividends, distributions, partial asset sales, proceeds from
refinancing, or other liquid sources. The estate is not required to
use any specific payment method. It is required to satisfy the schedule.

**Asset-class calibration:**

Publicly traded securities: drawdown over a defined short-to-medium
schedule sufficient to allow orderly market absorption without material
price distortion. The schedule is set in statute with asset-class
parameters. It is not subject to discretionary extension.

Private equity and closely held business interests: longer schedule
calibrated to allow operating continuity, planned distribution of
earnings, or orderly third-party sale without destroying going-concern
value. Minimum annual payment obligations apply. The estate may
satisfy its obligation from dividends, distributions, or earnings
without requiring a controlling-interest sale in the early years of
the schedule.

Real estate portfolios: structured disposition schedule calibrated to
allow orderly marketing rather than distressed auction. The schedule
distinguishes between stabilized income-producing assets — where
distributions can fund the obligation over time — and vacant, speculative,
or development-stage assets — where a sale schedule may be required.

Mineral rights and natural resource positions: schedule calibrated to
production income, royalty flows, and operator control status.

Art, collectibles, and non-income-producing assets: shorter schedule
than operating assets because these positions do not generate income
to service the obligation. Structured auction or private sale required.
No perpetual deferral because the asset cannot pay for itself.

Diversified private fund interests: treated by reference to the
underlying asset composition of the fund. Limited partnership interests
in funds with defined sunset provisions follow the fund's own wind-down
rather than requiring early redemption.

### Lane 2 — Prepayment Election

The voluntary lane. Available at any point before a triggering event
for holders who anticipate crossing the threshold, have already crossed
it, or prefer to settle their exposure on their own terms.

The prepayment election is a planning tool, not a discount mechanism.
The rate is the same as it would be at the triggering event. The
election provides certainty: known valuation methodology, known
applicable rate, known payment schedule. It eliminates the dynasty
obligation for the designated assets at the triggering event —
prepaid assets do not go through the estate drawdown because the
liability has already been settled.

Prepayment is irrevocable once elected for the designated assets. It
locks in the capital gains rate at the time of election, which benefits
holders who expect rates to rise or who simply prefer to control the
process. Prepaid assets transfer to PCA administration on the elected
schedule, moving them out of the estate on the holder's own terms.

For holders with large illiquid positions — private company founders,
major real estate operators, controlling shareholders in closely held
businesses — the prepayment election allows settlement without requiring
a controlling-interest sale during the holder's lifetime. The holder
designates assets for prepayment, agrees to the drawdown schedule,
locks in the rate, and converts an uncertain future estate obligation
into a settled current liability.

The prepayment election also functions as a rolling planning tool across
the threshold. A family that has $60 million in attributable assets and
expects further appreciation can elect to prepay the current above-
threshold exposure — $10 million — while that amount is still manageable,
rather than waiting until the estate is $200 million and the obligation
is proportionally larger. The system is designed to make early voluntary
settlement attractive and administratively smooth.

**Prepayment credit mechanics:** All amounts paid through the prepayment
election are credited against the obligation recognized at the triggering
event. Basis is adjusted upward for each prepaid tranche. The ledger
records the prepayment, the valuation, the rate, and the credit so that
reconciliation at death is a clean accounting exercise rather than a
contested valuation dispute.

### Lane 3 — Hardship and Operating-Company Schedule

The extended lane. Available for assets where ordinary drawdown would
cause material harm to going-concern value, employees, creditors,
tenants, or regional economic stability — and where that harm is
independently verifiable, not merely asserted by the estate.

The hardship and operating-company schedule is not a discretionary
escape hatch. It is a calibrated extension of the ordinary drawdown
with additional requirements.

**Eligibility.** To qualify for an extended schedule under this lane,
the estate must demonstrate that the above-threshold asset is an
operating business with active employees, ongoing customer or tenant
relationships, or critical supply or service function in a defined
geography; that immediate or accelerated drawdown would materially
harm those parties; and that the extension is necessary to preserve
rather than merely convenient to the estate.

Eligibility is determined by the designated settlement authority under
published criteria. It is not negotiated estate by estate. Systemic-
risk or community-impact arguments are independently assessed, not
accepted at the estate's characterization.

**Extended schedule terms.** The extended schedule provides a longer
payment calendar — potentially over decades for a multi-generational
operating business — in exchange for stricter reporting, minimum annual
payment floors, independent valuation at defined intervals, and
prohibition on new borrowing against the relevant asset pool while the
obligation remains outstanding.

The estate on an extended schedule may not pledge, encumber, or borrow
against the above-threshold operating assets to fund consumption,
distributions to non-operating heirs, or any purpose unrelated to the
operating business. The extended schedule exists to protect the business
and its stakeholders, not to give the estate access to low-cost leverage
while the dynasty obligation is deferred.

**Going-concern protection mechanism.** For operating businesses where
a change in control sale would be genuinely destructive, the extended
schedule may permit the estate to satisfy the obligation from earnings,
dividends, or distributions over the schedule period rather than from
asset sale proceeds. The business continues operating. The obligation
is satisfied from the income the business generates. The estate does
not need to sell control to a third party in order to settle.

This is the design principle that makes the framework compatible with
genuine family business continuity. The obligation is not a forced sale
of the business. It is a claim on the income the business produces.
Over a long enough schedule, most profitable operating businesses can
satisfy a dynasty obligation from earnings without requiring a change
in control.

## Valuation Principles

### Fair Market Value Standard

Dynasty realization occurs at fair market value of the underlying asset
at the triggering event. This is the same standard used throughout the
capital-gains system.

Fair market value is the price at which the asset would change hands
between a willing buyer and a willing seller, neither under compulsion
to buy or sell, both having reasonable knowledge of the relevant facts.

### No Artificial Discounts for Family Control

Illiquidity is a real economic characteristic. Family-control structures
are a legal choice.

The framework distinguishes between the two.

A genuine third-party market discount — the documented haircut that
unrelated buyers apply to minority positions in private markets, verified
by independent appraisal and consistent with arms-length transaction
data — may be recognized where it reflects real economic value
reduction.

An artificial control discount — a valuation reduction created by the
estate's own legal structure, such as a family LLC, family limited
partnership, or layered trust arrangement that concentrates nominal
minority interests while preserving effective family control — is not
recognized. The valuation aggregates beneficial control, not legal form.
SSI-backed beneficial-control attestation provides the enforcement
mechanism: the control graph is mapped at the triggering event, and
valuation is applied to the economically equivalent position rather
than to the artificially discounted legal fragments.

Illiquidity may affect the payment calendar. It does not erase the
tax base.

### Independent Valuation Requirement

Above-threshold assets subject to dynasty realization must be valued
by an independent qualified appraiser under published methodology
standards. The estate may not use its own preferred valuation
methodology without independent corroboration.

For assets with active third-party markets — publicly traded securities,
exchange-traded funds, widely held real estate investment trusts — market
price is the valuation. For assets without active markets — private
equity, closely held businesses, private real estate, mineral rights,
art — independent appraisal is required.

The IRS or designated settlement authority publishes approved appraisal
methodology standards by asset class. Appraisers are subject to
independence requirements, and appraisals are subject to review.
Systematic undervaluation or appraiser conflicts of interest are subject
to penalty and professional sanction.

### Periodic Revaluation Under Extended Schedules

For assets on the hardship and operating-company schedule, independent
revaluation occurs at defined intervals — no less frequently than every
five years, and at any event that materially changes the asset's value
or the family's control structure. Revaluation does not change the
recognized obligation calculated at the triggering event, but it
informs the remaining payment schedule and ensures the settlement
authority can assess the estate's capacity to satisfy the obligation.

## Payment Mechanics

### Interest and Accretion During Drawdown

A recognized dynasty obligation that is paid over time must carry an
accretion rate. Without one, a 30-year drawdown schedule is a hidden
discount relative to immediate settlement — the time value of money
effectively reduces the real obligation in proportion to the schedule
length. That would make the longest schedules the most attractive, which
inverts the intended incentive structure.

The accretion rate is set at the applicable federal rate for long-term
obligations plus a modest administrative spread, reviewed annually and
published by the settlement authority. The intent is not a punitive
rate. Extended time is not free, but it is not a penalty. The rate
should approximate the cost of government borrowing, reflecting the
public's opportunity cost from deferred receipt — not a commercial
lending rate that would make long schedules operationally impossible
for genuine going-concern estates.

Interest accrues on the outstanding recognized obligation balance,
reduced by payments made. Payments are applied first to accrued
interest, then to principal reduction. This prevents the obligation
from growing faster than the estate can service it under a well-
structured schedule, while ensuring the settlement authority is
compensated for time.

The prepayment election carries no interest premium but also no
discount: prepaid obligations are settled at the recognized rate on
the elected schedule, with no accretion penalty for early settlement
and no discount for immediacy. The benefit of prepayment is certainty
and control, not a lower obligation.

Minimum annual payment floors are calibrated to ensure that the
principal balance declines meaningfully over the schedule period, not
merely that interest is serviced while the principal persists. An
extended schedule that services only interest while the principal
remains static is not a drawdown — it is a permanent deferral with
a fee. That is not permitted.

### Sources of Payment

The estate may satisfy its drawdown obligation from any combination of:

- cash or liquid assets held outside the above-threshold pool
- dividends, earnings distributions, or operating income from the
  above-threshold business or asset
- proceeds from partial sales of non-controlling interests
- proceeds from refinancing of non-above-threshold assets
- proceeds from voluntary arms-length sales of above-threshold assets
- prepayment credits previously recorded

### Prohibited Payment Sources

The estate may not satisfy the obligation by:

- pledging above-threshold operating assets to fund payments while
  deferring actual settlement
- creating new borrowing against the above-threshold asset pool that
  reduces apparent equity without reducing the tax base
- distributing above-threshold assets to below-threshold heirs to
  artificially fragment the obligation
- claiming credit for payments that were not actually received by the
  settlement authority

### Payment Recording

All payments are recorded in the immutable government ledger against
the recognized obligation. The ledger records payment date, amount,
source category, accrued interest applied, principal reduced, remaining
obligation, and current compliance status. The ledger is the
authoritative record for reconciliation at subsequent checkpoint events.

## The Recurring Checkpoint Model

Death and the 100-year holding mark are not the only possible checkpoint
events. The framework should also accommodate mid-schedule checkpoints
for estates on extended payment schedules.

A recurring checkpoint — scheduled at defined intervals, no less
frequently than every five years for estates on the hardship and
operating-company lane — performs a compliance and trajectory review:
payment progress against schedule, current collateral sufficiency,
updated capacity assessment, and schedule revision where justified.

Checkpoints update the payment trajectory. They do not revalue the
original taxable event or retroactively increase the recognized principal
obligation. The tax base was determined at the triggering event and is
fixed. Post-trigger appreciation in the underlying asset is not a new
taxable event under this framework unless a separate triggering event
independently applies — a new death, a new 100-year mark, or a new
borrowing-triggered realization event under the companion framework.
Treating ordinary asset appreciation during the drawdown period as
grounds for upward revaluation of the original obligation would convert
a structured settlement regime into an ongoing wealth tax. That is not
the design.

What checkpoints do govern is payment capacity and compliance status.
If asset value has increased materially, the settlement authority may
require higher minimum annual payments on the grounds that the estate's
capacity to service the obligation has improved. If asset value has
declined materially through genuine third-party market conditions —
not through insider-controlled transactions or artificial restructuring
— the settlement authority may adjust the payment schedule to reflect
reduced distributable income, subject to a floor that prevents the
obligation from effectively disappearing during a down cycle.

The checkpoint is also a fraud and misstatement review point. If the
original triggering-event valuation is found to have been materially
understated — through appraisal fraud, concealment of assets, or
misrepresentation of beneficial control — the recognized obligation may
be restated upward to reflect the correct tax base. Restatement at
checkpoints is a remedy for misstatement, not a routine revaluation
mechanism.

The checkpoint is an accounting and compliance event, not an opportunity
to renegotiate the obligation. Minimum floors apply. Default on minimum
payments triggers acceleration to ordinary drawdown schedule.

## Anti-Abuse Architecture

### Fragmentation Prevention

The anti-fragmentation rules established in the Dynasty Tax and
Generational Transfer Framework apply with full force to illiquid
asset structures.

A family cannot reduce its above-threshold exposure by distributing
illiquid assets across children's and descendants' super accounts,
family trusts, entity structures, or gift programs in the years
preceding the triggering event. The ten-year lookback covers all such
transfers. For dynasty-threshold purposes, the attribution follows
beneficial control, not legal form.

### Valuation Timing Manipulation

The estate may not defer asset valuation to a date when the asset is
temporarily depressed below fair market value. Valuation occurs at the
triggering event at fair market value determined under standard
methodology. The settlement authority may reject an appraisal that
appears to reflect timing manipulation rather than genuine market
conditions.

### Schedule Gaming

Extended-schedule status does not permit the estate to convert the
drawdown obligation into a perpetual deferral. Minimum annual payment
floors apply under all schedule categories. An estate that fails to
meet minimum payment floors — without documented operating-company
hardship — reverts to an accelerated ordinary drawdown schedule.

An estate that satisfies its minimum payments for several years and
then ceases payment without documented cause is subject to the full
remaining obligation on an accelerated basis, with interest accruing
from the date of default.

### Refinancing Prohibition

While an above-threshold obligation is outstanding, the estate may not
use borrowing against the relevant asset pool to generate distributions
to non-operating heirs, fund lifestyle expenditures, or acquire new
assets outside the operating business. The borrow-and-defer strategy
that the borrowing-triggered partial realization framework closes during
the holder's lifetime is also closed as a payment-avoidance mechanism
during the drawdown schedule.

## PCA Role in Settlement

### Administration vs. Custody

The Public Capital Authority or a designated PCA-administered estate
settlement facility administers above-threshold dynasty obligations.
Administration and custody are distinct functions, and the distinction
matters politically and operationally.

**By default, the family retains ownership and operational control
of the above-threshold asset.** The recognized dynasty obligation is
recorded as a security interest and enforcement lien against the
relevant asset pool. The family continues to own, manage, operate,
and receive distributions from the asset subject to schedule compliance
and reporting obligations. PCA does not take the asset. It holds a
structured claim against it.

This is the default rule for both the ordinary drawdown lane and the
hardship and operating-company lane. The estate is not dispossessed.
It retains control in exchange for schedule compliance, periodic
reporting, independent valuation, and the borrowing prohibition on the
above-threshold pool while the obligation is outstanding.

**PCA custody or asset transfer occurs only in three circumstances.**
First, the family elects the prepayment lane and voluntarily transfers
designated assets to PCA administration as part of that election.
Second, the estate defaults on minimum payment obligations without
documented hardship grounds, at which point PCA may seek an enforcement
remedy including receivership or asset transfer to satisfy the
accelerated obligation. Third, the estate enters a structured settlement
arrangement — distinct from both ordinary drawdown and prepayment —
in which specific assets are transferred to PCA in satisfaction of a
defined tranche of the obligation on agreed terms.

This architecture prevents the framing of dynasty settlement as
government seizure. The family is not dispossessed at death. The
business does not change hands when the triggering event occurs.
The government holds a recorded claim, enforces a payment schedule,
and receives payments over time. Asset transfer is a remedy or an
election — not the default consequence of crossing the threshold.

### PCA Administrative Role

Within the default lien-and-schedule model, PCA's administrative
functions are: recording the recognized obligation and security
interest in the immutable ledger; receiving and recording payments;
commissioning independent valuations at triggering events and
scheduled checkpoints; enforcing schedule compliance and minimum
payment floors; administering prepayment elections; coordinating with
IRS and estate administration authorities; and assessing hardship and
operating-company lane eligibility under published criteria.

The PCA does not have discretionary authority to reduce, negotiate,
or forgive recognized obligations. Its administrative role is
ministerial: record the obligation, receive payments, enforce the
schedule, and deploy received capital.

### Capital Deployment

Capital received through dynasty obligation settlement flows through
PCA portfolio discipline: defined deployment objectives, performance
measurement, and sunset provisions. It does not disappear into the
general fund. It is deployed toward the public systems — infrastructure,
workforce development, research capacity, and the universal
superannuation seeder — that contributed to the appreciation being
recognized. The fiscal through line is explicit: the capital recovered
above the democratic wealth threshold is reinvested in the systems
that make the next generation's wealth formation possible.

## Relationship to Borrowing-Triggered Realization

The borrowing-triggered partial realization framework and this framework
are designed to be internally consistent.

Borrowing-triggered realization events during the holder's lifetime
adjust basis upward. When the triggering event arrives, only the
remaining embedded gain — the portion not previously recognized through
borrowing-triggered events — is subject to dynasty realization.

A holder who managed their exposure proactively during their lifetime,
triggering proportional realization events through the borrowing
framework, faces a smaller dynasty obligation at the triggering event
because the basis has been stepped up through those lifetime events.
A holder who borrowed extensively but never triggered realization under
that framework faces the full embedded gain at the triggering event.
The two frameworks close different paths. Neither creates a double
recognition on the same gain.

All borrowing-triggered basis adjustments are recorded in the immutable
government ledger and credited automatically at the triggering event
reconciliation. The reconciliation is a ledger operation, not a
contested factual inquiry.

## Failure Modes

The framework fails if valuation becomes a contest of dueling appraisers
that systematically disadvantages the settlement authority.

It fails if the hardship and operating-company lane becomes a de facto
permanent deferral for any estate that can characterize its assets as
operationally sensitive.

It fails if minimum payment floors are set too low to create meaningful
annual settlement progress.

It fails if the prepayment election is structured in a way that discourages
early voluntary settlement rather than incentivizing it.

It fails if PCA settlement administration acquires discretionary reduction
authority that allows political or relational negotiation of recognized
obligations.

It fails if the anti-fragmentation rules and SSI-backed beneficial-control
attestation are not enforced with sufficient rigor to prevent pre-death
fragmentation strategies.

It fails if the recurring checkpoint model is treated as an opportunity
to renegotiate obligations rather than to update the payment trajectory.

## Consolidated Doctrine

The illiquid asset realization framework rests on four principles that
should be carried consistently across implementing regulations, PCA
administration guidance, and IRS enforcement practice.

**Illiquidity changes the calendar, not the obligation.** The recognized
dynasty obligation is determined at the triggering event and does not
diminish because the underlying assets are hard to sell quickly.
Illiquidity affects how and when payment is made; it does not reduce
what is owed.

**Death measures the obligation. The drawdown schedule pays it.**
The triggering event is an accounting and realization checkpoint, not
a forced liquidation. The estate has time to satisfy the obligation
through cash, distributions, partial sales, refinancing, or orderly
disposition. It does not have the option to use that time to make the
obligation disappear.

**Legal form is irrelevant. Beneficial control is determinative.**
Fragmentation across trusts, LLCs, family limited partnerships, holding
companies, or child accounts does not reduce the threshold calculation.
SSI-backed attestation maps the control graph. Privileged treatment
requires attestable beneficial ownership.

**Prepayment is a planning tool, not a discount.** Families that engage
with the framework proactively — electing to settle exposure before the
triggering event, on a known schedule, at a known rate — receive certainty
and administrative simplicity in exchange. They do not receive a reduced
obligation. The rate is the same. The obligation is the same. The
planning benefit is knowing what it is and controlling when and how
it is paid.

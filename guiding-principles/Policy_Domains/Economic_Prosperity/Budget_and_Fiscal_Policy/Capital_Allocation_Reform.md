# **Overview**

As an overview, the tax code should be interpreted as a capital map rather than a legal artifact. Every tax credit, deduction, exemption, or preferential treatment should be classified as an implied investment into an economic sector to align with how these instruments function in practice.

Instead of this investment map, however, our current tax code is a 70,000+ page graveyard of legacy incentives collected over the past 100 years and represents one of the largest and least transparent uses of public capital. While every dollar out the door on the other end of the ledger is meticulously scrutinized, this expenditure operates off-budget, is difficult to audit, and tends to persist long after its original justification has expired.

The core objective of this reform is to convert old, opaque, and perpetual tax ‘incentives’ into explicit and accountable capital instruments. Rather than attempting to influence behavior indirectly through the tax code, the government should deploy capital directly through ultra-low-interest loans, repayable credits, equity-like stakes (with mandatory sunset and drawdown clauses), and milestone-based grants aligned to clearly defined public goals.

This philosophy of capital allocation prioritizes recyclability over consumption. Wherever feasible, tax incentives are restructured into instruments that return public capital to the system, allowing it to be reused and scaled over time. Non-repayable support is reserved for true public goods, early-stage research, or national interests, especially where market failure is demonstrable. 

This conversion of bespoke carve-outs into explicit investments is designed to reduce the following: 

* Rent-seeking behaviors, where corporations seek profit from the government or a broken system rather than actual competition in the free market. 
* Lobbyists proposed tax incentives that distort the markets they claim to protect by disproportionately benefiting one company or firm. 
* Tax Engineering - where incentives are gamed to artificially minimize corporate liability, while completely bypassing the policy intent those incentives were written to advance. 

Instead of these failures, the state under this proposal will also allocate funds directly. Risks that markets traditionally avoid (long-term aquifer depletion in agriculture, new antibiotic development, conversion from fossil fuels to renewables) are directly targeted; early risk can be absorbed by the state, and support is automatically drawn down once sectors stabilize. 

Outcomes of each investment are measured against predefined benchmarks, underperforming incentives are restructured or terminated, and successful mechanisms are scaled. Under this system, the tax code ceases to be a graveyard of legacy incentives and becomes a narrow interface for collection rather than a substitute for investment strategy.

The end state is a tax system that raises revenue cleanly paired with a legislative execution instrument (the public capital authority) that invests that capital well. Splitting the system into these two parts allows each half to do one job well, rather than one system failing at both.

# **Methodology**

**Step 1:**

We direct the treasury to interpret each tax expenditure as an implied investment into an economic sector. The treasury (along with the IRS if necessary) will produce a live inventory that assigns each credit, deduction, exemption, and preferential rate both to a:

1. Primary Sector (energy, housing, healthcare, manufacturing, finance, labor, R&D, agriculture, etc.) and
2. a secondary objective (capacity expansion, risk reduction, price stabilization, innovation, regional development, etc.)

Anything that cannot be cleanly mapped fails the first test. From there, we test sector-level aggregation. We stop evaluating incentives line by line and instead ask: ‘What is the government implicitly investing in this sector each year, and what’s the return profile?’ For each sector we quantify total forgone revenue, beneficiaries, concentration effects, duration, and behavioral elasticity. The objective is to expose redundancy, rent capture, and zombie incentives that no longer provide outcomes.

**Step 2: **

Once each tax structure is broken down in this manner, we evaluate each one for convertibility into a financial instrument. For each line item, we ask:

1. Does this incentive represent risk capital, operating support, or consumption relief?
2. Is the activity time-bounded or perpetual by design?
3. Could the same outcome be achieved with an explicit capital instrument? If the answer is yes, we default to conversion.

**Step 3:**

Conversion into capital instruments follows standardized instrument templates, rather than bespoke deals lobbied by individual firms. For each item:

a) If it is to facilitate risk-taking, it becomes an ultra-low-interest loan or revenue-linked repayment instrument.

b) If it is growth-centered, it becomes a milestone-based grant, equity partnership (with measured drawdown if successful). 

c) If this is for long-term industrial support, it gets converted into a revolving outcome-based fund or equity-like stake. Structural disadvantages and inefficiencies should be resolved via coordination with other agencies, as defined in manufacturing.



**Important Notes**

All sector portfolios and associated capital deployments will be administered by the Public Capital Authority (PCA). Conversion to this framework is phased by sector and applies prospectively rather than retroactively. Firms currently receiving sector-specific tax benefits transition into the new structure over a defined timetable, while all new qualifying activity is financed through the updated capital framework.

**Public Capital Authority**

As outlined in institutional-modernization/public-capital-authority, the Public Capital Authority is established as a legislative–executive instrument with a charter explicitly authorizing it to administer public funds in pursuit of congressionally defined objectives.

The PCA will operate under the legislative branch and is fully subject to congressional oversight. Its role is to deploy public capital as disciplined, recyclable investments rather than as open-ended subsidies, functioning as a specialized execution body with the technical, financial, and operational expertise required to structure, evaluate, and manage large-scale public investments.

Its mandate is to allocate capital in ways that expand national productive capacity, reduce systemic risk, and generate measureable long term returns – whether those be financial, economic, or strategic – within the parameters set by Congress.

**Scope and Constraints:**

The PCA does not set tax policy, determine national priorities, or regulate industries. Instead, it translates legislative objectives and validated executive-branch signals into structured financial instruments, ensuring that public capital is deployed with clear performance expectations, accountability, and recoverability where appropriate.

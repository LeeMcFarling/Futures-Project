# National Catastrophe Reinsurance System

## Executive Summary

This system creates a federal catastrophe reinsurance layer that stabilizes property insurance markets against extreme tail events while preserving geographic risk signals. The federal role is explicitly limited to “catastrophe layers,” not routine losses, and access is conditioned on mitigation compliance and transparent risk modeling. The objective is to prevent insurance market collapse and fiscal panic-bailouts by replacing discretionary disaster politics with a pre-capitalized, rule-driven structure that prices risk, rewards mitigation, and sunsets support in areas that become structurally non-viable.

This is designed as public infrastructure for risk transfer, not a permanent subsidy. Like other resilience investments, it is governed with lifecycle accounting, published criteria, and explicit triggers for scaling, revision, or termination. [Agricultural Finance]

---

## 1. Problem Statement — What’s Broken

Catastrophic losses are rising and becoming more correlated across regions. Private insurers and reinsurers respond rationally by raising rates, tightening underwriting, or exiting markets, especially where wildfire, flood, and hurricane risks concentrate. Political systems then respond by suppressing rates, backfilling losses via disaster aid, or creating ad hoc state pools. That sequence destroys signal integrity, accelerates underpricing, and increases federal contingent liabilities while making coverage availability volatile for households and lenders.

The failure mode is predictable: underpriced risk accumulates, a large event hits, the market retreats, and political pressure forces socialization without mitigation requirements. The result is higher long-run costs and repeated rebuilding in high-loss locations.

---

## 2. Policy Objective — What This System Optimizes For

The reinsurance system is built to: maintain continuous availability of catastrophe coverage; preserve risk-adjusted pricing and local mitigation incentives; reduce federal “panic bailout” exposure by pre-capitalizing tail risk; and create a disciplined off-ramp for geographies that cross into structural non-viability.

Success is defined by fewer insurer exits, lower variance in availability across cycles, measurable adoption of mitigation standards in eligible regions, and a declining share of discretionary federal disaster rebuilding for repeat-loss properties.

---

## 3. Core Design Principles

**Pool the tail, price the baseline.** Federal support attaches only at high loss layers. Primary insurance remains private and risk-rated.

**Eligibility is conditional.** Communities and properties must meet mitigation and land-use standards to access federal reinsurance capacity.

**Automatic, model-driven updates.** Risk bands, attachments, and capital requirements update on a fixed cadence from published models, reducing political discretion.

**No financing of new exposure.** Federal backing cannot be used to enable growth in clearly defined high-risk zones.

**Sunset where non-viability persists.** If structural risk remains above thresholds after mitigation, federal reinsurance eligibility phases out in a pre-declared lifecycle.

---

## 4. System Architecture — How It Works

### 4.1 Coverage Layering

The system defines three layers:

**Layer A (Private primary layer).** Routine losses and moderate events remain fully private. States may regulate conduct, but not suppress solvency pricing.

**Layer B (Market reinsurance layer).** Traditional private reinsurance operates normally.

**Layer C (Federal catastrophe layer).** A federal facility provides reinsurance only above a defined attachment point. It is priced, capitalized, and limited. It exists to prevent “market disappearance” after tail events, not to lower ordinary premiums.

### 4.2 Attachment and Exhaustion (Statutory Concept)

Attachment is defined by one or more of the following, selected per peril and region:

- A per-policy loss threshold (property-level attachment)
- A per-event industry loss threshold (event-level attachment)
- A per-insurer annual aggregate loss threshold (aggregate attachment)

Exhaustion is capped to prevent unlimited federal exposure; once reached, additional losses revert to private markets and state/local responsibility, with disaster aid constrained by repeat-loss rules.

### 4.3 Pricing Bands

Federal reinsurance pricing is risk-adjusted and published as bands. Pricing reflects both hazard and compliance: the same region is cheaper if it meets mitigation and land-use standards, more expensive if it does not. The pricing schedule is updated automatically on a fixed timeline.

Political rate suppression is explicitly barred within the federal facility.

## Data Governance and Risk Modeling (DoDa)

The Department of Data and Accountability (DoDa) maintains the national catastrophe risk data layer used for pricing bands, eligibility standards, and structural exposure zone designations.

DoDa aggregates hazard and loss modeling inputs from multiple federal and state agencies, including:

- NOAA storm and climate hazard models
- NASA remote sensing data
- FEMA disaster loss databases
- USGS geological and hydrological risk layers
- State insurance and claims reporting systems

These datasets are standardized into a national catastrophe risk dashboard used to calculate:

- expected annual loss estimates
- tail-event probability thresholds
- regional insurance risk bands
- eligibility criteria for federal catastrophe reinsurance

The pricing and eligibility rules described in this system reference these standardized indicators.

Insurance regulators and federal financial authorities maintain authority over insurance markets. DoDa’s role is strictly measurement and model transparency, ensuring that catastrophe risk pricing is grounded in publicly verifiable data.

---

## 5. Eligibility and Mitigation Compliance

Access to Layer C requires meeting a minimum “Resilience Eligibility Standard,” with three components:

**Property-level standards.** Building codes, defensible space, elevation/floodproofing, roof and ember-resistant design, and other peril-specific requirements.

**Community-level standards.** Land-use constraints in the highest-risk microzones, WUI fuel management plans, drainage and flood control standards, and evacuation/serviceability plans.

**Data transparency standards.** Participation in risk mapping, claims reporting, and mitigation verification audits, using published, non-punitive data boundaries consistent with trust-preserving design.

Eligibility is binary for the federal facility: either the locality is in compliance or it is not. Non-compliance is not punished; it simply prices itself out or loses access.

---

## 6. Structural Exposure Zones and the Off-Ramp

The reinsurance system is explicitly integrated with the Climate Exposure Lifecycle. Once a geography is designated as structurally high-risk, it enters a staged path:

**Years 0–10:** Access remains available but is conditioned on mitigation adoption with tightening standards.

**Years 10–20:** Federal reinsurance may remain for existing exposure, but new federally-backed mortgage origination is phased out and infrastructure expansion that increases exposure is ineligible.

**20+ years:** Federal reinsurance eligibility sunsets for persistently non-viable zones. Repeat-loss rebuilding subsidies are barred. Remaining exposure becomes private decision-making.

This is the “middle tone”: no forced displacement, but the national balance sheet stops underwriting compounding exposure after decades of notice.

---

## 7. Capitalization, Fiscal Discipline, and Funding

The federal facility is capitalized ex ante to prevent post-disaster appropriation spirals. Capitalization combines:

- Risk-based premiums paid by participating insurers (passed through to policyholders)
- A standing federal capital reserve appropriation with explicit exposure limits
- Optional catastrophe bond issuance with statutory caps
- Experience-rated adjustments that increase contributions after loss years within bounds

The facility operates with lifecycle accounting and published contingent liability reporting, consistent with the broader “public capital as disciplined investment” approach. [Aggricultural Finance]
---

## 8. Governance and Anti-Capture Safeguards

Governance is designed to prevent the facility from turning into a political subsidy machine.

**Independent board with statutory mandate.** Clear objectives: solvency, continuity of coverage, tail-risk only, and mitigation-linked eligibility.

**Model transparency and version control.** Risk models, parameter changes, and pricing band updates are published with “diffs,” aligned with the governance-as-code approach. [Regulation Overhaul]

**Audit and clawback authority.** Fraud, misrepresentation of mitigation status, or non-compliance triggers pricing surcharges, restitution, and loss of eligibility.

**Recorded-meeting and conflict rules.** Mandatory disclosure of stakeholder meetings; cooling-off periods for key roles; published decision memos for major changes.

---

## 9. Override Mechanics and Political Interference Resistance

No statute can perfectly bind future Congresses procedurally. The durability strategy is to make interference explicit, scored, and costly.

Any override of: attachment points, exhaustion caps, pricing bands, or eligibility standards requires:

- A standalone recorded vote on the override provision
- Explicit fiscal offsets (pay-fors) reflecting the increase in contingent liability
- Public reporting of the implied subsidy and distributional incidence

This pushes “socialize the loss” from an implicit maneuver to an explicit fiscal choice.

---

## 10. Interlocks With Mortgages and Municipal Finance

The system is ineffective if federal housing finance continues expanding exposure in zones already designated as structurally high-risk.

Accordingly:

- New federal mortgage guarantees phase out in designated high-risk zones on the same clock as eligibility tightening.
- Municipal bond disclosures must include exposure zone status and projected serviceability impacts, improving capital market discipline.

---

## 11. Pilot Strategy

Pilots should begin in a limited set of perils and geographies where private market retreat is already visible and data quality is high, with clear evaluation metrics.

Pilot evaluation focuses on:

- Carrier participation and market continuity
- Rate stability versus availability (availability is the priority)
- Mitigation adoption rates and compliance integrity
- Changes in repeat-loss rebuilding reliance
- Federal contingent liability trajectory

Scaling occurs only if pilots demonstrate improved market stability without collapsing risk signals.

---

## 12. System-Level Outcome

This reinsurance system stabilizes insurance availability while preserving the core function of insurance: risk signaling. It reduces chaotic insurer exits, limits fiscal exposure through pre-capitalized tail coverage, and forces long-horizon honesty about where continued development is sustainable. Over time, it shifts the U.S. from reactive disaster socialization to predictable, mitigation-linked risk governance.

---

## Public Facing Draft — Plain Language

Disasters are getting more expensive. When insurers pull out, people get stuck with no coverage and then the federal government ends up paying anyway after the next big event.

This system keeps insurance available by having the federal government cover only the most extreme “worst-case” disasters, the way reinsurance works, but with strict rules.

Insurance prices still reflect local risk. Communities that reduce risk get better terms. Communities that keep building into danger lose access over time.

No one is forced to move. But we stop asking the entire country to endlessly subsidize places that become structurally unsafe, especially after decades of warning.

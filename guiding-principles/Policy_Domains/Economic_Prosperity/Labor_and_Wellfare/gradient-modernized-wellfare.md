## Problem Statement

Federal means-tested programs such as SNAP and Medicaid rely on income thresholds and stacked phase-outs tied to the Federal Poverty Level (FPL). When households cross eligibility limits, benefits can fall sharply or phase out simultaneously across programs, producing high localized effective marginal withdrawal rates (EMTRs).

These cliffs create rational income suppression behavior, administrative churn, and volatility in disposable income. They also obscure the true marginal structure of public support by embedding implicit tax rates within eligibility rules rather than transparent schedules.

The current system discourages additional work at specific income bands while increasing administrative burden on states due to enrollment cycling.

## Policy Objective

Replace cliff-based eligibility with a coordinated, continuous taper structure that guarantees:

1. Post-tax-and-transfer disposable income increases with every additional dollar earned for working-age, non-disabled households.

2. Combined federal benefit withdrawal rates remain below a statutory EMTR ceiling within the primary phase-out band (e.g., 100–250% FPL).

This reform converts threshold-based eligibility into a gradient-based incentive system.

## Core Structural Reform

### Continuous Phase-Out Formula

For covered programs:

Benefit = MaxBenefit × (1 − TR × (EarnedIncome − EntryPoint))

Where:
- TR = taper rate
- EntryPoint = income at which phase-out begins

Benefits decline smoothly to zero rather than terminating at discrete thresholds.

### EMTR Coordination

Taper rates across SNAP and Medicaid must be coordinated so that the combined withdrawal rate does not exceed a federally defined ceiling within a defined income band. Unified income definitions and standardized treatment of deductions are required to prevent stacking artifacts.

### Indexing and Drift Prevention

Phase-out entry points and bands are indexed to CPI or median wage growth to prevent the reemergence of cliffs due to inflationary drift.

### Income Smoothing

Implement rolling income averaging (e.g., quarterly smoothing) to reduce churn caused by short-term volatility and to lower administrative cycling.

## Scope and Boundary Conditions

This reform applies to working-age, non-disabled households.

Disability-based eligibility categories — including SSI, SSDI, and medically determined Medicaid pathways — remain governed by existing statutory frameworks and are not subject to work-linked taper restructuring.

## Federal–State Integration

SNAP and Medicaid administration requires federal-state coordination. States retain discretion over baseline generosity but must comply with the federally defined EMTR ceiling. Federal oversight includes annual marginal-rate compliance testing incorporating state supplements and waivers.

## Fiscal and Economic Effects

Budget neutrality is achievable through calibrated taper extensions. However, imposing an EMTR ceiling may require modest fiscal expansion depending on design.

Expected impacts:
- Reduced income suppression behavior
- Increased labor supply in near-threshold income bands
- Reduced administrative churn
- Improved income stability

## Evaluation Metrics

- Distribution of effective marginal withdrawal rates
- SNAP and Medicaid churn rates
- Labor participation changes in 100–200% FPL band
- Variance of monthly disposable income
- Administrative cost per enrollee
- Fraud and misreporting incidence

## Strategic Framing

This reform modernizes the safety net by eliminating poverty traps without reducing protection. It ensures that work always increases net income while preserving coverage for vulnerable populations.

The system transitions from binary eligibility to continuous incentives.

## Healthcare-Specific Scope and Trigger

This Act is strictly limited to healthcare benefit financing during the national healthcare reform transition.

It activates only in jurisdictions participating in the federal healthcare reform pilot and phases in concurrently with healthcare system modernization.

The per-hour employer contribution applies solely to Healthcare Transition Accounts (HTAs) and is not a generalized portable benefits mandate. It does not apply to retirement, paid leave, or non-healthcare benefit domains.

Upon full deployment of the national SSI/UPI payment rail architecture and permanent healthcare financing structure, HTAs may be consolidated, converted, or sunset pursuant to the healthcare transition statute.

This Act exists exclusively as a healthcare transition mechanism.

## Problem Statement

Employer healthcare benefit mandates are frequently tied to discrete hour thresholds. This creates artificial incentives for hour-splitting and part-time structuring to avoid benefit obligations.

Workers experience coverage instability during employment transitions. Employers respond rationally to threshold-based healthcare cost structures rather than operational efficiency.

The result is a labor market distorted by healthcare-specific regulatory cliffs.

## Policy Objective

Replace threshold-based healthcare benefit mandates with a standardized per-hour healthcare contribution that:

1. Applies uniformly across full-time and part-time workers.
2. Eliminates financial incentives for hour-splitting related to healthcare coverage.
3. Provides portable healthcare financing stability during the reform transition.

## Core Structural Reform

### Per-Hour Healthcare Contribution Requirement

All covered employers remit a standardized per-hour healthcare contribution (flat dollar or percentage of wages) for each employee into a Healthcare Transition Account (HTA).

The contribution:

- Accrues proportionally with hours worked.
- Vests immediately or near-immediately.
- Is independent of full-time status.

This neutralizes the economic advantage of dividing a 40-hour role into two 20-hour roles for healthcare mandate avoidance.

### Healthcare Transition Accounts (HTAs)

HTAs are worker-owned, portable accounts that:

- Follow the worker across employers and employment classifications.
- Accumulate standardized healthcare-specific employer contributions.
- Provide healthcare coverage stabilization during the transition period.

Approved uses during transition include:

- Healthcare premium support.
- Coverage continuity during employment gaps.
- Authorized healthcare cost stabilization functions defined in the healthcare reform statute.

HTAs operate on interim infrastructure and migrate into the permanent SSI/UPI architecture upon system readiness.

## Pilot Implementation Framework

This Act is deployed only in jurisdictions participating in the federal healthcare reform pilot.

Pilot regions implement:

- The per-hour healthcare contribution requirement.
- HTA capitalization and usage rules.
- Integrated reporting infrastructure aligned with healthcare reform systems.

Pilot duration: 24–36 months.

Independent evaluation measures:

- Hour distribution changes.
- Employer scheduling behavior.
- Healthcare coverage continuity.
- Administrative burden.
- Integration performance with healthcare systems.

National scaling occurs only following pilot evaluation and SSI/UPI readiness certification.

## Scope and Coverage

Applies to employees covered under federal labor law within participating healthcare reform jurisdictions.

Independent contractor treatment is governed separately under Worker Classification Parity reform.

Aggregation rules apply across affiliated entities under common control to prevent payroll fragmentation.

## Strategic Framing

This reform is a healthcare modernization mechanism, not a broad labor mandate.

It removes healthcare-specific regulatory cliffs, stabilizes coverage during transition, and aligns employer contribution structure with the long-term healthcare financing architecture.

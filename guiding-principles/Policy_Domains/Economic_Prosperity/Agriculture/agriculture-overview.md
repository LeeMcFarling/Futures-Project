# Agricultural Innovation in America — Overview


American agriculture remains highly productive, but it is increasingly brittle. Farmers are caught in the middle of extractive economic dynamics both upstream and downstream. Upstream, they are forced into cost-taking agreements driven by restrictive seed IP protections that, rather than fostering competition among seed producers for superior traits, have concentrated market power and locked farmers into accepting set prices. These dynamics are reinforced by farm equipment practices that impose proprietary servicing, software lock-ins, and recurring subscription costs layered on top of already high capital expenditures. Downstream, farmers remain price takers, over-reliant on single-crop commodity markets and exposed to volatile global demand they cannot influence. All of this sits atop an unsustainable physical foundation: widespread over-extraction of groundwater aquifers that, on current trajectories, face partial or total collapse within the next half-century. The cumulative effect is a farming sector that is structurally over-leveraged, over-reliant on commodities whose prices can shift on a whim, increasingly targeted by conglomerates and private equity seeking to consolidate land into new monopolistic mega-projects, and forced to operate atop an increasingly unstable economic and environmental foundation.

To reverse these dynamics and restore resilience at the farm level, this initiative focuses on lowering structural risk while expanding farmer agency. It deploys integrated agricultural data systems that give farmers real-time visibility into domestic crop demand, regional supply constraints, and emerging market signals, reducing blind overproduction and price shocks. It opens structured multinational collaboration on agricultural research to expand the pool of seed innovation, accelerate trait development, and prevent innovation bottlenecks driven by closed or exclusionary IP regimes. It reduces seed price pressure by limiting exclusionary IP practices through public buyouts and open licensing, restoring competition among seed producers based on performance rather than access. It breaks equipment lock-ins by restoring farmers’ rights to repair, service, and use the operational data generated by their own machinery, lowering lifetime equipment costs and improving on-farm decision-making. It enables farmers to de-risk production by supporting crop planning across both domestic food security needs and diversified export markets, reducing dependence on single commodities or volatile foreign demand. Finally, it treats water security as foundational national infrastructure, using federal coordination, scientific monitoring, and long-term recharge and conservation programs to stabilize and restore groundwater aquifers and ensure agricultural viability over the coming decades.

# PUBLIC FACING DRAFT: Fixing American Agriculture for the Long Term

## What’s the Problem?

American farmers grow a lot of food, but the system they work in is getting harder to survive.

Costs keep rising. Seed prices are high. Equipment is expensive to fix. Farmers often don’t know where demand will be until it’s too late. ALL while water supplies are shrinking in many areas.

At the same time, farmers are often stuck in the middle. Big companies control inputs on one side and markets on the other. When prices change or things go wrong, farmers usually take the hit.

This system produces food today, but it creates serious risks for the future.

---

## What Is the Goal?

The goal is simple: **make farming more stable, more affordable, and more resilient**.

That means:
- Lowering costs farmers can’t control  
- Giving farmers better information 
- Making markets more competitive upsteam and downstream of the farm 
- Protecting land and water for the next generation  

Under our system, the farmers stay in charge. The system just works better.

---

## What Is the Plan?

This effort focuses on fixing the system, not micromanaging farms.

It includes:
- Making better seed technology available to everyone  
- Giving farmers clear market information before they plant  
- Reducing financial risk so farmers don’t have to bet everything on one crop  
- Letting farmers repair their own equipment and use their own data  
- Protecting groundwater so farms don’t run dry  
- Expanding processing and storage so farmers have real buyers  
- Paying for improvements as long-term investments, not endless subsidies  

Importantly, everything is voluntary. Programs are tested in small pilots and they are only scaled when there is a proven benefit.

---

## What This Is *Not*

This is not central planning.

It does not tell farmers what to grow.

It does not nationalize farms or companies.

It does not rely on permanent handouts.

---

## Why This Matters

If nothing changes, farming will keep getting more expensive and more fragile. More farms will disappear. More land will be consolidated. Water problems will get worse.

If the system is fixed, farmers can:
- Take less risk  
- Keep more value from their work  
- Plan for the future  
- Pass farms on to the next generation  

This is about keeping American agriculture strong—not just this year, but for decades.

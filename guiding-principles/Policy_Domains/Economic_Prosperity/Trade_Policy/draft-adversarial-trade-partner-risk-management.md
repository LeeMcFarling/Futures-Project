# Adversarial Trade Partner Risk Management Framework

## Executive Summary

This framework defines how the United States engages with **systemically adversarial trade partners**—countries where:

- State policy actively seeks technology extraction  
- Industrial policy is used to displace foreign firms  
- Market access is conditioned on IP transfer or forced partnerships  
- Strategic sectors are targeted for state-subsidized competition  

The objective is not immediate economic separation.

The objective is:

> **Structured de-risking of critical dependencies while preserving economic stability**

The system replaces reactive decoupling with:

- Predictable risk classification  
- Phased exposure reduction  
- Firm-level guidance and enforcement  
- Coordinated domestic and allied capacity buildout  

---

## 1. Strategic Principle — De-Risk, Don’t Shock

The framework operates on a core principle:

> Sudden disengagement creates economic damage  
> Unmanaged exposure creates strategic vulnerability  

Policy must balance both.

This system therefore:

- Reduces dependency over time  
- Protects firms during transition  
- Builds alternative supply chains before restrictions escalate  

---

## 2. Adversarial Classification Criteria

Countries are designated as **Adversarial Trade Partners (ATP)** based on:

- Documented forced or coerced technology transfer practices  
- Systematic industrial targeting of foreign firms  
- State-subsidized market displacement behavior  
- Export controls or resource weaponization risk  
- Lack of reciprocal market access  

Classification is:

- Evidence-based  
- Periodically reviewed  
- Publicly documented  

---

## 3. Exposure Mapping (DoDA Layer)

DoDA maintains a continuous map of:

- U.S. dependency on ATPs by sector  
- Supplier concentration risk  
- Time-to-replacement estimates  
- Critical vs non-critical exposure  

Exposure is classified into tiers:

**Tier A — Critical Dependency**
- No immediate substitutes  
- National security or core industrial relevance  

**Tier B — Transitional Dependency**
- Substitutes exist but require scaling  

**Tier C — Non-Critical Exposure**
- Easily replaceable or low-risk  

---

## 4. Firm-Level Risk Notification System

Firms operating in or sourcing from ATPs receive:

- Formal risk designation  
- Sector-specific exposure analysis  
- Recommended transition pathways  

This replaces:

> Informal awareness with formal, actionable guidance

---

## 5. Industrial Transition Pathways

Firms are given structured options to reduce exposure:

### A. Domestic Relocation
- U.S.-based production with full support stack  

### B. NORAM Nearshoring
- Canada or qualified Mexico states  

### C. IDP Reallocation
- Trusted allied production  

### D. Hybrid Supply Chain Diversification
- Multi-country sourcing to reduce concentration  

---

## 6. Phased De-Risking Timeline

Exposure reduction occurs over defined windows:

- Short-term: Stabilization (0–2 years)  
- Mid-term: Diversification (2–5 years)  
- Long-term: Strategic independence (5–10 years)  

Timeline varies by:

- Sector  
- Dependency tier  
- Replacement feasibility  

---

## 7. Tariff & Restriction Escalation Ladder

Restrictions increase gradually:

### Phase 1 — Transparency & Monitoring
- Reporting requirements  
- Risk disclosure  

### Phase 2 — Incentivized Transition
- Tax advantages for relocation  
- Preferential treatment for compliant firms  

### Phase 3 — Targeted Tariffs
- Applied to ATP-sourced goods in strategic sectors  

### Phase 4 — Strategic Restrictions
- Export/import controls on critical sectors  
- Procurement exclusions  

---

## 8. Rare Earths & Critical Materials Strategy

Critical materials (e.g., rare earths) are treated differently.

- Domestic and NORAM capacity is scaled first
- Strategic reserves are built  
- Recycling and alternative sourcing expanded  

Only after:

> Replacement capacity exists

Do stronger restrictions apply.

---

## 9. IP Protection & Anti-Extraction Measures

Firms operating in ATP environments must comply with:

- Enhanced disclosure of JV structures  
- Restrictions on sensitive technology transfer  
- Mandatory risk acknowledgment for high-risk operations  

In extreme cases:

- Prohibition on participation in specific sectors  

---

## 10. Anti-Circumvention Controls

To prevent system gaming:

- Transshipment detection systems  
- Supply chain tracing requirements  
- Ownership transparency enforcement  

ATP-origin goods cannot:

> Re-enter through neutral or third-party jurisdictions undetected

---

## 11. Integration with Manufacturing Stack

This framework connects directly to:

- Demand coordination system (supplier replacement)
- Critical materials strategy (domestic-first sourcing)
- Tariff governance (enforcement mechanism)  
- NORAM alignment (relocation pathways)  

---

## 12. What This Policy Is Not

This is not:

- Immediate decoupling  
- Blanket bans on trade  
- Anti-globalization policy  
- Punitive economic isolation  

It does not attempt to:

> Shut down trade overnight

It ensures:

> Trade does not create strategic dependency

---

## 13. System-Level Outcome

The United States transitions from:

> High-risk, concentrated dependency on adversarial systems  

To:

> Diversified, resilient, and controlled exposure  

Firms retain flexibility.

The system retains stability.

Strategic vulnerability is reduced over time.

---

# PUBLIC FACING DRAFT: Reducing Risk Without Breaking the Economy

Some countries use trade to gain an advantage in ways that hurt American businesses.

They pressure companies to give up technology.  
They support domestic competitors to push foreign companies out.

We can’t ignore that.

But we also can’t just cut everything off overnight without hurting our own economy.

This plan takes a smarter approach.

We reduce our reliance step by step.  
We build alternatives here at home and with trusted partners.  
And we only increase restrictions once those alternatives exist.

The goal is simple:

- Protect American businesses  
- Strengthen supply chains  
- Avoid unnecessary disruption

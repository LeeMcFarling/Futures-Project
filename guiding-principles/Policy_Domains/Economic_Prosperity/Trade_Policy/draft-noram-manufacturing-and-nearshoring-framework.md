# NORAM Manufacturing Alignment & Nearshoring Framework

## Executive Summary

This framework establishes a **North American manufacturing alignment strategy** that integrates the United States, Canada, and Mexico into a coordinated industrial system.

The objective is not to force all production into the United States.

The objective is to ensure that:

> Production serving the American market occurs within a **trusted, resilient, and geographically proximate system**

Where domestic production is not efficient or feasible, production is directed toward:

- Canada  
- Mexico (under defined security and governance conditions)  
- IDP-aligned partners (as fallback)

This creates a **tiered manufacturing geography** that balances:

- National competitiveness  
- Supply chain resilience  
- Cost efficiency  
- Security integrity  

---

## 1. Strategic Principle — Regional, Not Global

The system operates on a clear hierarchy:

1. **Domestic Production (United States) — Priority Tier**
2. **NORAM Production (Canada & Mexico) — Secondary Tier**
3. **IDP Production — Tertiary Tier**
4. **Non-aligned Global Production — Residual**

This reflects a core reality:

> Not all manufacturing should be domestic  
> But critical manufacturing should not be globally exposed

---

## 2. Canada Integration

Canada is treated as a **fully trusted industrial partner** for:

- Energy systems  
- Critical materials  
- Heavy industry  
- Advanced manufacturing inputs  

Integration includes:

- Supply chain interoperability  
- Joint corridor planning  
- Shared standards and certification  
- Coordinated capital deployment where appropriate  

Canada functions as a **capacity extension of the domestic system**, not an external node.

---

## 3. Mexico Integration — Conditional Industrial Expansion

Mexico represents the **largest near-term opportunity for scaled manufacturing expansion**, but requires structured governance.

### Core Approach

Manufacturing expansion into Mexico is:

- **Targeted (sector-specific)**
- **Geographically selective (state-level)**
- **Security-conditioned (cartel activity gating)**

---

## 4. State-Level Industrial Targeting (Mexico)

Rather than treating Mexico as a single unit, the system evaluates **states individually**.

### State Classification System

States are categorized based on:

- Cartel activity and violence levels  
- Rule-of-law indicators  
- Infrastructure readiness  
- Workforce availability  
- Local governance reliability  

---

### Tier Structure

**Tier 1 — Secure Industrial States**
- Low cartel presence  
- Stable governance  
- Eligible for full industrial deployment  

**Tier 2 — Transitional States**
- Moderate risk  
- Eligible for limited or monitored investment  

**Tier 3 — Restricted States**
- High cartel activity  
- Ineligible for industrial deployment  
- Subject to capital withdrawal  

---

## 5. Investment Signaling & Enforcement

### A. Positive Signal (Incentives)

Tier 1 states receive:

- Priority manufacturing investment  
- Corridor-style development support  
- Supplier ecosystem integration  
- Workforce development partnerships  

---

### B. Negative Signal (Withdrawal)

If cartel activity increases:

- Investment pipelines are paused  
- New projects are redirected  
- Existing capital deployment is reduced  

This creates:

> A direct economic incentive for local stabilization

---

## 6. Firm-Level Nearshoring Activation

This extends your tariff logic beyond domestic production.

### Step 1 — Firm Notification

Firms dependent on global production are notified:

- Opportunity to relocate production to:
  - United States  
  - Canada  
  - Qualified Mexican states  

---

### Step 2 — Transition Window

Firms are given a defined timeline:

> X months to initiate relocation or capacity shift

---

### Step 3 — Support Deployment

If firms opt in:

- Execution Corps assists deployment  
- Supplier coordination system activates  
- Workforce pipelines align  

---

### Step 4 — Enforcement (Tariff Trigger)

If firms do not respond:

- Tariffs are applied to goods produced outside:
  - NORAM  
  - IDP-aligned partners  

---

## 7. Security-Linked Industrial Policy (Mexico)

This is the differentiator.

Industrial policy is explicitly linked to **security outcomes**.

### Mechanism

- Investment flows correlate with state-level security metrics  
- Security deterioration → economic consequences  
- Security improvement → increased industrial participation  

---

### Outcome

Local governments face a clear tradeoff:

> Improve security → gain jobs and investment  
> Lose control → lose economic growth

This is not:

- Ignoring domestic production

## 11. Industrial Participation & Capability Alignment Requirements (Draft)

### Purpose

This section establishes a **participation-based market access framework** for firms serving the U.S. and broader NORAM market.

The objective is to ensure that:

> Firms benefiting from access to the American market contribute to the development of the North American industrial system.

This replaces blunt localization mandates with a **flexible, multi-path participation model** that aligns private incentives with public industrial capacity goals.

---

## 11.1 Core Principle

Market access is conditioned on:

> **Meaningful participation in NORAM industrial capacity, supply chains, or workforce development**

Participation is defined functionally, not politically.

Firms are not required to follow a single structure (e.g., joint venture), but must meet **clear, measurable participation thresholds**.

---

## 11.2 Participation Pathways

Firms may qualify for compliant market access through one or more of the following pathways:

---

### Pathway A — Joint Venture or Partnership (Optional)

- Partnership with a NORAM-based firm  
- Shared ownership, production, or facility development  
- May include technology collaboration or co-manufacturing  

This pathway is **permitted but not required**.

---

### Pathway B — Domestic Supplier Integration (Primary Pathway)

Firms must source a defined share of inputs from:

- U.S. suppliers  
- Canadian suppliers  
- Qualified Mexican suppliers (Tier 1 states)

Requirements:

- Integration into the Industrial Supplier Registry  
- Participation in demand coordination system  
- Verified supplier relationships and volume thresholds  

This pathway prioritizes development of the **intermediate industrial layer** rather than final assembly alone. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

### Pathway C — Workforce & Capability Investment

Firms may qualify by:

- Funding or co-developing apprenticeship pipelines  
- Investing in technical training programs  
- Establishing process capability within NORAM (tooling, machining, etc.)  

Focus is on **durable capability creation**, not temporary employment.

---

### Pathway D — Local Capital Participation

- Minority ownership by NORAM entities  
- Participation from domestic industrial firms, pension funds, or public capital vehicles  
- Structured to align long-term incentives with regional growth  

---

## 11.3 Qualification Thresholds

Each firm must meet a **minimum participation score** based on:

- Share of inputs sourced within NORAM  
- Level of workforce investment  
- Degree of supplier ecosystem integration  
- Capital committed to regional operations  

Thresholds are:

- Sector-specific  
- Time-phased  
- Transparent and published  

---

## 11.4 Integration with Tariff Enforcement

This system operates as a **precondition layer** to tariff activation.

### Sequence:

1. Firm identified in strategic sector  
2. Participation pathways communicated  
3. Defined compliance window (X months)  
4. Support stack offered (Execution Corps, PCA, supplier coordination)  
5. Participation assessed  

---

### Enforcement Trigger

If firm does not meet participation thresholds:

- Tariffs are applied to non-compliant production  
- Tariff intensity scales with degree of non-compliance  

This creates:

> A rules-based incentive structure rather than discretionary enforcement

---

## 11.5 Mexico-Specific Conditions

Participation in Mexico is subject to **state-level qualification rules**:

- Only Tier 1 states qualify for full participation pathways  
- Tier 2 states may have restricted pathway eligibility  
- Tier 3 states do not qualify for participation-based tariff exemptions  

This prevents:

> Circumvention through low-security regions

---

## 11.6 Anti-Circumvention Safeguards

To ensure genuine participation:

- Final assembly alone does not qualify  
- Supplier integration must be verifiable  
- Ownership and sourcing structures are audited  
- Transshipment and reclassification schemes are prohibited  

Participation must reflect:

> Real economic integration, not superficial compliance

---

## 11.7 Circuit Breakers

Participation requirements are adjusted if:

- Domestic or regional supply is insufficient  
- Input shortages emerge  
- Price distortions exceed defined thresholds  

This ensures:

> Industrial policy remains adaptive and non-destructive

---

## 11.8 Strategic Rationale

This framework ensures that:

- Market access drives industrial development  
- Supply chains deepen rather than hollow out  
- Workforce capabilities scale alongside capital investment  

It avoids:

- Rigid mandates  
- Forced partnerships  
- Market exit by high-value firms  

Instead, it creates:

> A flexible system where firms choose how to integrate — but cannot avoid integration entirely

---

## 11.9 System-Level Outcome

Firms operating in the U.S. market become:

- Embedded in NORAM supply chains  
- Invested in workforce development  
- Aligned with long-term regional capacity growth  

The system transitions from:

> Open access without obligation  

To:

> Access linked to participation and contribution

# PUBLIC FACING DRAFT: Build It Closer to Home

We don’t need to make everything in the United States.

But we do need to make sure the things we rely on are built somewhere we trust.

This plan focuses on building more in:

- The United States  
- Canada  
- Safer, stable parts of Mexico  

Instead of relying on distant and unstable supply chains.

It also sends a clear message:

Areas that are safe and stable will get investment.  
Areas that are not will lose it.

That means:

- More jobs closer to home  
- Stronger supply chains  
- Real incentives for stability  

We’re not pulling back from trade.

We’re rebuilding it smarter.

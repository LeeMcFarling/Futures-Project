## Problem Statement

U.S. trade policy has oscillated between two dysfunctional poles: uncritical free-trade orthodoxy that hollowed out domestic industrial capacity and underpriced strategic dependency, and reactive protectionism that imposes costs without building durable alternatives.

Neither approach reflects the current strategic environment: a world of contested supply chains, allied industrial coordination requirements, adversarial economic statecraft from China, and domestic manufacturing capacity deficits that market mechanisms alone will not resolve.

Trade policy must be redesigned as a strategic instrument — not merely a tariff schedule or a vehicle for political signaling.

---

## Core Frame

Trade policy should serve three simultaneous objectives:

1. **Domestic industrial base development** — trade rules that create predictable demand signals enabling U.S. and allied manufacturing investment
2. **Alliance coordination** — trade architecture that strengthens economic integration with allied partners and creates durable separation from strategic adversaries
3. **Supply chain security** — reducing single-point dependencies in critical sectors without creating autarkic inefficiency

The central tension: domestic protection can enable industrial build-out but risks ossifying inefficiency; openness enables competition and efficiency but can hollow out strategic capacity. The framework must distinguish sectors by strategic criticality and design trade rules accordingly.

## Strategic Principles

### 1. Sector-Differentiated Trade Posture
Not all sectors require the same trade posture. A tiered framework:

- **Tier I: Strategic-Critical** — sectors where supply disruption constitutes a national security risk (critical minerals, semiconductors, advanced batteries, defense-relevant components, pharmaceutical active ingredients). Domestic-first sourcing, allied-preference structures, adversarial exclusion.
- **Tier II: Industrial Anchor** — manufacturing sectors with high multiplier effects and workforce depth (steel, chemicals, heavy equipment, shipbuilding). Managed competition: performance-based protection with sunset provisions, allied coordination, anti-dumping enforcement.
- **Tier III: Competitive** — sectors where U.S. competitive advantage is sustainable and trade openness produces net gains. Standard market rules with strong enforcement.

### 2. Allied Trade Architecture
Trade strategy must integrate with the Integrated Defense Partnerships (IDP) framework. Allied nations should face preference structures that reward defense-industrial cooperation:
- Preferential market access for partners meeting IDP industrial coordination commitments
- Joint supply chain development and mutual recognition frameworks
- Coordinated adversarial exclusion (Huawei precedent → generalized principle)

### 3. Adversarial Decoupling — Disciplined and Staged
China decoupling requires disciplined staging to avoid self-inflicted disruption:
- Identify critical chokepoints (rare earth refining, battery cells, APIs) and build domestic/allied alternatives before decoupling, not after
- Avoid broad tariffs that raise consumer and producer costs without strategic payoff
- Enforce against genuine economic statecraft violations (currency manipulation, subsidized overcapacity, forced technology transfer) through WTO-consistent mechanisms where possible, and unilateral mechanisms where not

### 4. Industrial Policy Coherence
Trade policy must be coherent with domestic industrial policy. Protectionist measures without complementary supply-side investments (workforce, infrastructure, capital) create rents without capacity. The sequencing principle: build domestic alternative capacity first, then restructure trade exposure.

## Key Policy Instruments

### Market Access Architecture
- **Conditional market access agreements** — bilateral frameworks linking access to labor, environmental, and industrial practice standards
- **Mutual recognition frameworks** with allied partners on product standards, certifications, and regulatory regimes (reduces non-tariff barriers without harmonization costs)

### Tariff and Duty Structure
- Replace ad-hoc tariff escalations with a structured sector-classification system based on the Tier framework above
- Automatic sunset provisions on protective tariffs conditioned on domestic capacity benchmarks
- **Anti-dumping and countervailing duty modernization** — streamlined enforcement, faster timelines, expanded capacity to respond to subsidized overcapacity flooding

### Currency and Financial Trade Integrity
- Coordinate with Treasury and IDP financial-trade-integrity framework on currency manipulation monitoring and enforcement
- Prevent adversarial use of currency policy to offset tariff structures

### Export Control Architecture
- Strengthen export control regimes for strategic technologies — aligned with allied partners to prevent circumvention
- Entity list maintenance and expansion based on security review, not political signaling

### WTO and Multilateral Reform
- Engage WTO reform process to address: dispute resolution backlog, treatment of state-owned enterprises, digital trade rules, and subsidy disciplines
- Build plurilateral coalitions with allied partners where WTO consensus is blocked

## Non-Goals

- Not a protectionist manifesto — the goal is strategic trade management, not mercantilist autarky
- Not a WTO abandonment framework — the goal is reform and coalition-building, not unilateral withdrawal
- Not a substitute for domestic industrial policy — trade architecture and supply-side investment must be built in parallel

## Success Criteria

- U.S. Tier I sector dependencies on strategic adversaries reduced by defined percentage within defined timeframe
- Allied trade architecture operational: preferential access frameworks active with IDP partners
- Anti-dumping enforcement timelines cut by 50%+
- Supply chain mapping completed for all Tier I sectors
- No strategic decoupling action taken before domestic/allied alternative capacity is certified ready

# 21st Century Anti-Capture and Fair Competition Framework

## Executive Summary

Modern market power often does not appear first as higher prices or traditional monopoly structure. It appears as control over the operating conditions of participation. Firms can shape market outcomes through ranking systems, payment rails, app distribution, standards governance, identity layers, algorithmic pricing tools, data asymmetries, and technical lock-in. In these environments, competition policy must evolve beyond narrow price tests and merger review alone.

This framework defines fair competition for the digital era. Its purpose is to preserve open entry, genuine consumer and business choice, competitive independence, and institutional pluralism without resorting to blanket hostility toward scale, innovation, or successful firms. The objective is not to punish large companies for being large. The objective is to prevent any firm or coordinated bloc from becoming the de facto governor of market access, pricing, verification, or participation.

The framework therefore focuses on anti-capture rules: constraints on chokepoint power, algorithmic coordination, forced ecosystem dependence, standards capture, and discriminatory control of critical infrastructure. Where scale creates real value, it should remain lawful. Where scale enables private rule-setting over public-facing economic life, structural safeguards are warranted.

## The Problem

Twentieth-century competition law was built around an industrial economy in which monopoly typically showed up through direct price increases, exclusive control over physical distribution, or formal merger concentration. In digitally mediated markets, power often emerges earlier and through less visible mechanisms.

A platform may control discovery without owning the goods being sold. A payment or wallet layer may control access without holding market share in the downstream service. A pricing software vendor may coordinate market behavior without any explicit cartel agreement. A dominant operating system, app store, cloud platform, or identity provider may convert technical dependence into economic and political leverage. These dynamics can suppress competition even when nominal consumer prices remain low or zero.

The result is a new form of market concentration: not only monopoly over products, but control over the rules of interaction.

## Core Objective

Preserve competitive independence in markets where digital infrastructure, algorithms, and platform intermediation shape the terms of participation.

## Fair Competition Standard

A market is not meaningfully competitive when one actor, or a small coordinated set of actors, can determine the effective terms of entry, visibility, pricing, payment access, identity verification, or reputational legitimacy for others in that market.

Fair competition in the digital era requires that participants can enter, interoperate, switch, and compete without surrendering strategic dependence to an unavoidable intermediary.

## Core Principles

### Competition must be evaluated at the layer where power is actually exercised

The relevant source of power may not be the final product market. It may sit in rankings, operating systems, payment routing, identity verification, recommendation engines, cloud dependencies, or standards certification. Competition analysis must therefore examine infrastructural leverage, not just end-user pricing.

### Competitive independence matters as much as market share

A market can appear fragmented while still being effectively coordinated through common pricing engines, common optimization vendors, common data brokers, or common app-distribution chokepoints. The question is whether firms are actually making independent competitive decisions.

### Lock-in can be coercive even when participation is formally voluntary

When switching costs are extreme, interoperability is weak, and core credentials or user history cannot move, users and businesses become captive even without explicit exclusivity contracts. Portability and contestability are therefore central competition concerns.

### Critical digital infrastructure carries heightened obligations

Actors that operate unavoidable layers of market participation should face stronger duties of interoperability, neutrality, portability, and non-discrimination than ordinary firms in ordinary markets.

## Priority Risk Areas

### Algorithmic price-setting and machine-mediated coordination

In concentrated or transparent markets, common pricing tools and optimization systems can produce stable supra-competitive outcomes without explicit human conspiracy. Competition law should treat systematic algorithmic coordination as a live anticompetitive risk, especially where firms rely on shared vendors, real-time competitor data, or predictive systems designed to suppress deviation.

### Platform chokepoints

App stores, operating systems, dominant marketplaces, cloud services, ad exchanges, and recommendation systems can become gatekeepers that determine downstream viability. When these actors preference their own services, extract rents from dependency, or use access control to shape competition above the layer they operate, they act less like market participants and more like private regulators.

### Standards and protocol capture

Open standards can preserve competition, but standards bodies can also be captured by incumbents. Where identity, payments, provenance, or communications standards become critical infrastructure, governance must remain plural, transparent, and resistant to closed control.

### Bundling and cross-layer dependence

The combination of identity, payments, hosting, distribution, ranking, and analytics can create forms of dependency that exceed ordinary product tying. A firm should not be allowed to turn control in one layer into unavoidable dependence across the full stack.

## Policy Tools

### Interoperability and portability rights

In markets where lock-in is a core barrier to competition, firms operating critical infrastructure may be required to support standardized interoperability and user-controlled export of core data, credentials, and transaction histories. The goal is not forced commodification of proprietary products, but prevention of captivity.

### Anti-tying rules for critical infrastructure

Operators of dominant infrastructure should be restricted from requiring users, merchants, developers, or counterparties to adopt affiliated services as a condition of access. This includes tying payment access to identity systems, app distribution to proprietary billing, or wallet access to affiliated provenance or credentialing services.

### Structural separation where conflicts are systemic

Where a single actor controls both a critical layer and a downstream competitor, structural remedies may be warranted if conduct remedies prove insufficient. The aim is not reflexive break-up, but prevention of durable self-preferencing and governance through dependency.

### Auditability in concentrated algorithmic markets

Firms operating pricing, ranking, or allocation systems in concentrated markets may be required to maintain auditable records sufficient to assess whether their systems are suppressing competitive independence or enabling tacit coordination.

### Anti-capture review of standards governance

Critical protocol and standards bodies should be subject to transparency requirements, conflict-of-interest rules, rotating representation, and periodic review to prevent incumbents from converting standards control into market foreclosure.

## Relationship to Existing Antitrust

This framework supplements rather than replaces traditional antitrust tools. Merger review, monopolization doctrine, cartel enforcement, and consumer-protection law remain important. The change is analytical and institutional: modern competition policy must account for infrastructural control, algorithmic coordination, and dependency-based market power earlier and more explicitly.

## What This Is Not

This framework is not a generalized war on size, scale, or technological success. It does not assume that every large platform is illegitimate, that all network effects are harmful, or that innovation should be slowed through permanent legal siege.

It is not an invitation to bury firms in endless lawsuits, politicized enforcement, or vague public-interest balancing. It is not a program of punitive dismantling for its own sake. It does not seek to replace private-sector innovation with bureaucratic control, and it does not presume that government agencies should directly operate most digital services.

It is also not a backdoor censorship or industrial policy regime. The objective is to define fair rules of competition and prevent private governance by chokepoint control, not to dictate lawful speech, prohibit scale, or micromanage ordinary business strategy.

The framework is pro-market in the strongest sense: it seeks markets where firms actually compete rather than inherit advantage from dependency, opacity, and control over the rails.

## Implementation Logic

The first priority is to define digital market power in terms that reflect current technical reality. The second is to identify the narrow set of markets where infrastructural leverage creates constitutional-level risk to competition and participation. The third is to apply proportionate remedies matched to the actual source of power: interoperability where lock-in dominates, anti-tying where bundling dominates, auditability where algorithmic coordination dominates, and structural separation where cross-layer conflicts are durable.

The framework should favor clear rules, fast review, and predictable obligations over sprawling litigation. Enforcement should aim to restore contestability and independence, not to create permanent uncertainty.

## Conclusion

Fair competition in the digital era requires more than preventing classical monopoly pricing. It requires preserving the conditions under which people, businesses, and institutions can participate without becoming dependent on a single gatekeeper or coordinated control system. The task is not to punish scale. It is to prevent scale from hardening into private rule.

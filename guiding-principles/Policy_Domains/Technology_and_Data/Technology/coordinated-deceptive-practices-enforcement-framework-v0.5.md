# Coordinated Deceptive Practices Enforcement & Transition Framework (CDE Framework)

## Executive Summary

This framework establishes a new **market integrity and fraud enforcement** regime targeting coordinated, concealed, and materially deceptive conduct by capitalized actors in public health, safety, and market domains.

This is a market integrity and product liability framework. It is not an information regulation framework. It does not adjudicate truth, regulate speech, or target good-faith scientific disagreement. It targets the same category of conduct addressed in tobacco litigation and analogous fraud cases: enterprise-level concealment of known harm, funded through concealed channels, contradicted by internal knowledge.

The framework integrates:
- **Strict enterprise liability for coordinated deception**
- **Non-linear economic penalties**
- **High-powered whistleblower incentives with defined race-condition rules**
- **A standing amnesty and disclosure track with a defined epistemic threshold schema and decay function**
- **PCA-backed transition pathways**
- **DoDA-driven detection and audit triggers**
- **Special provisions for new leadership inheriting prior misconduct**
- **Heightened safeguards for government institution application**

The objective is to make systematic deception economically irrational and structurally unstable — and to ensure that firms and institutions which discover emerging harms have a clear, penalty-free path to disclose them before they compound.

---

## I. Problem Statement

Market-scale deception in high-impact domains is not primarily organic. It is often coordinated across entities, funded through concealed channels, contradicted by internal knowledge, and designed to manipulate public behavior.

At the same time, firms operating in good faith will inevitably discover — through post-market data, internal research, or external findings — that products or practices they believed were safe are not. The current legal environment creates a perverse incentive: voluntary disclosure carries similar liability exposure to being caught later. Rational actors bury the problem.

Both failure modes produce the same outcome: harms that could have been contained early instead compound over years or decades.

This framework addresses both by changing the incentive structure so that disclosure dominates concealment as a strategy — whether the firm is coming clean about past coordinated deception or reporting a safety signal discovered in good faith.

---

## II. Core Definition: Coordinated Deceptive Enterprise (CDE)

A **Coordinated Deceptive Enterprise (CDE)** is established when **all five** of the following conditions are met:

### 1. Enterprise Coordination
Demonstrable coordination across multiple actors (firms, NGOs, media entities, or intermediaries), including shared funding sources, strategic alignment documents, or communication linkages.

### 2. Internal Knowledge Contradiction
Material internal evidence that contradicts public-facing claims or narratives.

### 3. Pattern of Conduct
Sustained, repeated actions rather than isolated incidents.

### 4. Material Impact Domain
Activities affecting public health, financial markets, or environmental or safety-critical systems. Material impact requires demonstrable or reasonably foreseeable harm exceeding domain-specific thresholds defined and published in the relevant sector's safety and market integrity standards. Trivial, speculative, or de minimis impacts do not satisfy this element. Domain-specific thresholds are published before enforcement begins and versioned in the ULRR.

### 5. Concealment Mechanism
Use of structures designed to obscure origin or intent: undisclosed funding, proxy organizations, or fabricated independence.

**All five elements must be present. No single element, and no combination of fewer than five, triggers CDE liability.**

### Jurisdictional Scope

CDE liability attaches to any entity operating within the jurisdiction regardless of where coordination occurred, provided material impact occurs within the jurisdiction. Coordination routed through foreign subsidiaries, offshore entities, or international intermediaries does not create a jurisdictional gap if the conduct produces material impact domestically. Firms cannot avoid CDE liability by structuring coordination activities outside the jurisdiction while targeting domestic markets, consumers, or public health systems.

## III. Enforcement Mechanism

### A. Liability Structure

CDE designation triggers enterprise-level liability:
- Joint and several liability across participating entities
- Attribution of coordinated actions to the enterprise as a whole

**Organizational knowledge aggregation:** Organizational knowledge may be established through aggregated internal records even where no single individual possessed complete knowledge of the coordinated conduct. Deliberate compartmentalization — structuring information flows to ensure that no individual holds complete knowledge while the organization as a whole operates on that knowledge — does not constitute a defense against CDE designation. The evidentiary question is whether the organization, considered as a whole across its documented communications and decision-making processes, possessed the internal knowledge contradiction required by Element 2. This provision prevents compartmentalization as a structural defense strategy while preserving the requirement that the aggregated evidence meet the clear and convincing standard.

### B. Evidentiary Standard and Burden of Proof

CDE designation requires **clear and convincing evidence** that all five elements are met. This standard applies universally — to private firms, government institutions, and all tiers of violation. Criminal components of enforcement require proof **beyond a reasonable doubt** for individual criminal liability, including the perjury-equivalent attestation violations that create personal executive exposure.

These standards are not aspirational. They are statutory requirements. No CDE designation may proceed on a lower standard regardless of the severity of the alleged conduct or the identity of the actor.

Enforcement is anchored in provable facts:
- Internal documents (emails, memos, research suppression)
- Financial tracing (funding flows, intermediaries)
- Coordination artifacts (shared messaging strategies)

The framework explicitly avoids adjudicating abstract truth claims. The evidentiary question is not "was the public claim false" but "did the firm's internal record contradict its public-facing claims while it ran an active effort to suppress that contradiction."

### C. No Retroactive Thresholding

Epistemic thresholds, disclosure windows, and liability standards are defined ex ante and published before enforcement begins. They cannot be retroactively applied to conduct that predates their publication. Firms are evaluated against the standards that were in effect at the time of the conduct, not standards developed afterward.

This provision is absolute. It applies equally to private firms and government institutions.

### D. False Positive Accountability for Enforcement Bodies

The framework imposes reciprocal accountability on enforcement bodies to deter overreach and protect institutional credibility.

If a formally initiated CDE proceeding fails to meet the evidentiary standard — meaning a case is brought and does not result in designation because the clear and convincing standard was not met — the initiating body must publish a full public justification explaining why the case was brought and where the evidentiary basis fell short. This justification is filed in the public enforcement record and is not subject to redaction except for narrowly defined national security grounds.

Enforcement bodies that accumulate repeated unsupported actions — defined as a pattern of formally initiated proceedings that fail the evidentiary standard within a defined review period — are subject to mandatory internal review, with findings reported to the relevant congressional oversight committee.

Individual officials who initiate CDE proceedings without a good-faith evidentiary basis, as determined through the internal review process, are subject to the same disciplinary and legal consequences that apply to violations of the no-partisan-trigger provision in government institution cases.

This provision does not penalize enforcement bodies for bringing difficult cases that fail on the merits. It penalizes bad-faith or politically motivated case initiation. The distinction is established through the internal review process, which evaluates whether the evidentiary record at the time of initiation was sufficient to support a good-faith belief that the clear and convincing standard could be met.

## IV. Penalty Framework (Convex Structure)

### Tier I: Minor Violations
- Civil fines
- Mandatory disclosures

### Tier II: Coordinated Misrepresentation
- Profit disgorgement (campaign-period revenue)
- 2–3x damages multipliers
- Compliance monitoring requirements

### Tier III: Coordinated Deceptive Enterprise (CDE)
- Treble damages tied to modeled downstream harm
- Full internal document disclosure
- Executive penalties (clawbacks, disqualification)
- Procurement exclusion (temporary or permanent)

**Harm modeling standard:** Downstream harm modeling for Tier III penalties must use standardized methodologies that are published in advance, independently auditable, and subject to adversarial challenge in enforcement proceedings. No penalty may be calculated using a methodology that has not been published and made available for public comment prior to its first use. Harm estimates must include confidence intervals and sensitivity analyses. Courts must treat implausible or non-reproducible harm estimates as grounds for penalty reduction.

**Design Principle:** Penalties must exceed expected returns from deception by a significant margin across all plausible detection probability assumptions.

## V. Whistleblower Incentive System

### Structure
- Award: **10–30% of total recovered penalties**
- First-mover advantage: highest payout to earliest validated submission
- Evidence requirement: primary documentation demonstrating coordination and internal contradiction

### Protections
- Legal immunity for disclosure participants (conditional on full cooperation)
- Anonymity guarantees
- Financial and physical protection mechanisms where necessary

### Race Condition Rule — Firm vs. Whistleblower

The interaction between the standing disclosure track and the whistleblower system is governed by a defined race condition that accelerates disclosure from both directions simultaneously:

**If the firm discloses first through the DoDA portal:** The firm receives standing track immunity terms. Whistleblower claims arising from the same matter are reduced proportionally — the firm's voluntary disclosure is credited against the whistleblower's contribution to enforcement. Whistleblowers who provided the internal signal that prompted the firm's disclosure retain partial awards reflecting their causal contribution.

**If a whistleblower files first:** The firm loses eligibility for full standing track immunity for that matter. The firm may still disclose and cooperate fully — which reduces penalties relative to being caught through enforcement alone — but the full immunity terms of the standing track do not apply once a whistleblower has beaten the firm to disclosure.

This race condition creates simultaneous pressure from two directions: firms want to disclose before employees defect, and employees want to file before the firm discloses. Both pressures accelerate the moment of public knowledge. Concealment becomes an increasingly unstable equilibrium as the number of people who know about the harm grows.

## VI. Amnesty & Disclosure — Launch Window and Standing Track

### A. Launch Window (12–18 Months)

When the CDE framework is established, a time-bounded launch window opens. Eligible entities may:
- Submit full internal records of past campaigns or concealed safety information
- Disclose coordination structures and funding pathways
- Commit to forward compliance standards

First-mover advantage applies: earliest validated disclosures receive the most favorable terms. The window closes on a published date and does not reopen.

Benefits:
- Reduced financial penalties
- Conditional immunity from criminal prosecution for conduct during the concealment period
- Eligibility for PCA transition support

Conditions:
- Full and truthful disclosure
- Ongoing cooperation with audits
- Adoption of transparency standards going forward

### B. Standing Permanent Disclosure Track

After the launch window closes, the amnesty and disclosure mechanism remains permanently open. Harms are discovered continuously — the standing track ensures firms always have a clear path to disclose rather than a binary choice between voluntary disclosure and concealed suppression.

**Epistemic Threshold Schema**

The disclosure obligation activates when internal evidence crosses the epistemic threshold. The threshold is crossed when internal evidence meets **two or more** of the following trigger classes:

**Trigger Class 1 — Statistical signal:** An adverse event rate, safety metric, or harm indicator that exceeds the baseline by a margin that would prompt investigation in a standard post-market surveillance protocol. The specific statistical threshold is defined by domain and published in the relevant sector's safety monitoring standards.

**Trigger Class 2 — Internal study with adverse findings:** A completed internal study, analysis, or review that produces findings inconsistent with public-facing safety claims, regardless of whether the study was intended to evaluate safety.

**Trigger Class 3 — Replication or corroboration:** An external study, regulatory finding, or credible third-party report that corroborates an internal signal the firm was already tracking, even if the external finding alone would not have triggered the threshold.

**Trigger Class 4 — Leadership-level awareness:** Evidence that the adverse signal reached decision-making leadership — C-suite, board, or equivalent — through any channel, including informal communication.

**Trigger Class 5 — Suppression action:** Any internal action taken with knowledge of the adverse signal that materially impairs further investigation or disclosure of that signal. The action must be both knowing — taken by someone aware of the adverse finding — and material — meaningfully reducing the organization's ability to investigate, escalate, or disclose the signal. Normal internal prioritization decisions, budget adjustments unrelated to the adverse signal, and personnel changes with no connection to the safety finding do not meet this trigger class. The intent-plus-materiality pairing is required; neither element alone is sufficient.

Meeting any two of these five trigger classes establishes that the epistemic threshold has been crossed. The DoDA portal timestamp of the firm's disclosure establishes whether disclosure occurred within the defined response window.

The trigger class schema is published in advance, versioned in the ULRR, and cannot be retroactively modified to apply to prior conduct.

**Grace Period Decay Function**

Immunity terms are not binary — they decay continuously as a function of time elapsed after the epistemic threshold is crossed. This prevents cliff effects, reduces gaming around exact timing, and produces a proportional response curve that is more defensible in court.

The decay function operates as follows:

- **Disclosure within T₀ (immediate window, defined by domain):** Full immunity for pre-threshold harms. Operational remediation costs only. No civil or criminal liability for the concealment period.

- **Disclosure between T₀ and T₁ (extended window):** Partial immunity on a declining schedule. Civil liability for harms during the concealment period is reduced proportionally to how quickly the firm disclosed within the T₀–T₁ band. Criminal liability immunity remains available if disclosure is complete and cooperation is full.

- **Disclosure after T₁:** Treated as concealment. The standing track remains available — firms can still disclose and receive reduced penalties relative to being caught through enforcement — but immunity from civil and criminal liability for the concealment period no longer applies.

T₀ and T₁ are defined by domain and published in advance. They reflect the severity of the harm category, the rate at which the harm compounds without intervention, and the time reasonably required for a firm to organize a complete disclosure. Domains with faster-compounding harms have shorter windows.

**What Disclosure Triggers**

Upon verified disclosure:
- Immunity from civil and criminal liability for harms occurring before the epistemic threshold was crossed
- Protection from CDE designation for the disclosed conduct
- Entry into the remediation process under the relevant domain authority
- Access to PCA transition capital for product modification, reformulation, or market withdrawal

The firm bears operational consequences — product modification, recall coordination, consumer notification, potential market withdrawal — but not punitive liability for the pre-threshold period.

**What the Standing Track Does Not Cover**

The standing track does not apply to firms that engaged in active CDE conduct — coordinated deception with internal knowledge contradiction and concealment mechanisms. A firm cannot use the standing track to launder Category 1 concealment by reframing it as an honest mistake. DoDA's verification process distinguishes between genuine uncertainty and active suppression. If internal documents show the firm ran an active campaign to suppress safety findings while publicly claiming safety, CDE enforcement applies regardless of the disclosure attempt.

**Standing track immunity does not extend to harms that independently satisfy CDE criteria.** If the disclosed conduct meets all five CDE elements — coordination, internal contradiction, pattern, material impact, and concealment mechanism — the standing track's immunity terms do not apply regardless of when disclosure occurs. This is the hard wall between the uncertainty track and the fraud track. A firm that discovers it has been running a coordinated deceptive enterprise cannot access standing track immunity by disclosing the enterprise. The standing track is for honest mistakes that resolved badly. The CDE framework is for coordinated deception. The two tracks are mutually exclusive for any given course of conduct.

### C. New Leadership Inheriting Prior Misconduct

A firm that is actively subject to CDE enforcement for Matter A may still access the standing track for Matter B — a distinct harm discovered in good faith by new leadership that had no involvement in the Matter A concealment.

This provision exists because the alternative — barring all standing track access for firms under CDE enforcement — would create a perverse incentive to manage through enforcement rather than clean house. New leadership that discovers and discloses new harms should be protected. That protection is also what makes bringing in clean leadership a rational corporate strategy during CDE proceedings, which is the outcome the framework wants to encourage.

Eligibility conditions for the new-leadership provision:

- The disclosed harm must be demonstrably distinct from the conduct triggering CDE liability, established by meeting all three of the following distinctness criteria: (1) non-overlapping product line or exposure pathway — the disclosed harm involves a different product, substance, or practice than the Matter A conduct; (2) non-overlapping time window relative to threshold — the epistemic threshold for the new harm was crossed after the new leadership assumed relevant oversight responsibility, not during the prior leadership's tenure; (3) no shared decision-making chain — the disclosed harm was not known to, directed by, or concealed by any individual who also participated in the Matter A concealment
- The disclosing leadership must have joined the firm or assumed relevant oversight responsibility after the Matter A concealment period, and must have had no involvement in the concealment
- The disclosure must be made promptly after the new leadership's discovery — the epistemic threshold and decay function apply from the moment of the new leadership's awareness, not from when prior leadership first had reason to know
- Full cooperation with the ongoing Matter A enforcement is required as a condition of standing track access for Matter B

All three distinctness criteria must be met. Meeting two of three is insufficient. This prevents the obvious gaming strategy of reframing the same harm as a distinct matter by describing it at a different level of specificity.

## VII. Government Institution Application

The CDE framework applies to government institutions and agencies. Coordinated deception by government actors in public health, safety, and market domains causes the same harms as private-sector deception and is governed by the same market integrity principles.

However, the political weaponization risk for government institution applications is materially higher than for private firms. The following heightened safeguards apply exclusively to investigations of government institutions.

### Heightened Evidentiary Requirements

All five CDE elements must be met to the same standard as private-sector cases. Additionally, for government institution cases:

- The internal knowledge contradiction must be established through contemporaneous documentary evidence — not inferred from subsequent policy changes, personnel decisions, or outcomes
- The concealment mechanism must involve active structural efforts to suppress or misrepresent information, not normal bureaucratic classification, interagency communication protocols, or deliberative process protections
- The pattern of conduct must span a defined minimum period and involve multiple distinct actors, not a single policy decision or a single official's actions

Normal disagreements about policy priorities, scientific uncertainty handled through standard regulatory processes, interagency disputes about methodology, and decisions made under genuine uncertainty that later proved incorrect are explicitly excluded from CDE eligibility regardless of outcome.

### Independent Review Panel

Before any formal CDE investigation of a government institution proceeds, an independent review panel must determine that the five-element threshold is met by clear and convincing evidence. The panel consists of:

- A retired federal judge appointed through a process insulated from the current administration
- A former inspector general from a federal agency unrelated to the subject institution
- A senior academic with relevant domain expertise, nominated through a defined process that prevents partisan selection

The panel determination requires unanimity. A split panel result does not authorize the investigation to proceed. The panel's reasoning is published in full.

### Separation of Powers Constraints

CDE investigations of executive branch agencies require coordination with the relevant congressional oversight committees and the independent inspector general of the subject agency. The investigation cannot proceed over the objection of the relevant inspector general except through judicial authorization.

Investigations of regulatory agencies that made good-faith scientific determinations later found to be incorrect are not authorized under this framework. The framework targets coordinated deception, not scientific error. Regulatory decisions made through transparent processes with contemporaneous public documentation of the evidentiary basis are presumptively excluded from CDE eligibility.

### No Partisan Trigger

CDE investigations of government institutions cannot be initiated based on:

- Policy disagreements between the current and prior administration
- Changes in regulatory interpretation or enforcement priority
- Scientific findings that contradict prior agency determinations, absent evidence that those determinations were made in bad faith
- Political or electoral motivations of any kind

Violations of the no-partisan-trigger provision by investigators or officials initiating the investigation are themselves subject to disciplinary and legal consequences.

### Standing Track for Government Institutions

Government institutions may access the standing track under the same conditions as private firms. New agency leadership that discovers evidence of prior coordinated deception may disclose through the DoDA portal and receive the same protections as new corporate leadership inheriting prior misconduct. The political sensitivity of such disclosures does not alter the immunity terms — if anything, the framework creates a stronger incentive for new agency leadership to disclose promptly rather than managing through the prior administration's misconduct.

## VIII. Transition Commission & PCA Integration

### Purpose
To prevent systemic disruption while enabling industry transition.

### Functions
- Evaluate disclosed firms for transition eligibility
- Allocate PCA funding for product reformulation, safer alternatives, and supply chain restructuring

### Incentive Alignment
Firms that disclose early and cooperate fully receive preferential access to capital, regulatory fast-tracking, and reputational reset mechanisms.

## IX. DoDA Detection & Audit Layer

DoDA operates as a **market surveillance system**, not a truth arbiter. It also operates the standardized disclosure portal through which all voluntary disclosures are submitted, timestamped, and verified.

### Capabilities
- Detection of synchronized narrative emergence across entities
- Mapping of funding networks
- Identification of anomalous amplification patterns
- Receipt, timestamping, and verification of voluntary disclosures
- Maintenance of the disclosure timestamp record that establishes immunity boundaries
- Trigger class assessment at verification — determining which trigger classes are met and when the epistemic threshold was crossed

### Output
- Risk flags and audit triggers
- Sector-level monitoring reports
- Disclosure verification notices with timestamped immunity boundary determinations
- Remediation coordination referrals

DoDA does not make final CDE liability determinations. It produces verified findings that inform enforcement proceedings. Final determinations are made through the enforcement process with full due process protections.

**Hard constraint on DoDA authority:** DoDA outputs — risk flags, audit triggers, disclosure verifications, trigger class assessments — are evidentiary inputs only. They have no independent legal force absent adjudication through the enforcement process. DoDA cannot impose penalties, designate entities as CDEs, or restrict market access through its own authority. Any enforcement consequence requires a separate adjudication proceeding in which the DoDA finding is treated as evidence, not as a determination. This constraint is statutory and cannot be waived by administrative action.

## X. Safeguards & Constraints

### 1. Market Integrity Frame
This framework regulates market fraud and product liability — not information, not speech, not opinion. Good-faith scientific disagreement, policy disputes, and normal regulatory uncertainty are explicitly outside scope.

### 2. High Evidentiary Threshold
All five CDE conditions must be met. No retroactive thresholding. Trigger class schema published ex ante.

### 3. Transparency of Enforcement
Public documentation of all enforcement actions. Independent review mechanisms. Full auditability of decisions. Remediation plans for standing track participants are published.

### 4. Safe Harbor
Entities are protected when funding sources are disclosed, uncertainty is clearly communicated, no internal contradiction exists, and disclosure is made promptly upon crossing the epistemic threshold.

### 5. Government Institution Heightened Safeguards
Independent review panel with unanimity requirement. No-partisan-trigger provision. Separation of powers constraints. Explicit exclusion of policy disagreement and scientific error.

### 6. No Retroactive Thresholding
Absolute. Applies to all actors including government institutions.

---

## XI. Failure Modes & Mitigations

### Risk: Political Weaponization of Government Institution Provisions
Mitigation: Independent unanimity-required review panel. No-partisan-trigger provision with legal consequences for violations. Separation of powers constraints. High evidentiary threshold with contemporaneous documentary evidence requirement.

### Risk: Industry Resistance / Litigation
Mitigation: Launch window. Transition incentives. Standing track reduces litigation incentive. Market integrity framing reduces constitutional exposure.

### Risk: Innovation Chilling
Mitigation: Safe harbor for disclosed and good-faith claims. Standing track explicitly protects firms that act on safety signals promptly. No-retroactive-thresholding protection.

### Risk: Standing Track Gaming
Mitigation: DoDA verification distinguishes honest-mistake from active concealment. CDE designation applies regardless of disclosure attempt where five elements are met. Race condition rule prevents using the standing track as a litigation strategy after enforcement begins.

### Risk: New Leadership Provision Gaming (Firms Cycling Leadership to Reset Liability)
Mitigation: The new leadership provision applies only to harms that are demonstrably distinct from the Matter A conduct. Cycling leadership to reset liability on the same harm is not covered — the trigger class schema establishes when prior leadership crossed the threshold, and that determination does not reset with leadership change.

## Bottom Line

This is a market integrity and fraud framework.

It targets the same category of conduct addressed in tobacco litigation and analogous product liability cases: enterprise-level concealment of known harm. It does not regulate speech, adjudicate truth, or target good-faith scientific disagreement.

The choice architecture is simple: disclose now and get the amnesty track, or conceal and face the full enforcement regime when it surfaces — because the whistleblower incentives, DoDA surveillance, and peer comparison mechanisms across the ecosystem make concealment an increasingly unreliable strategy over time.

The standing track makes early disclosure the rational choice for firms that discover problems in good faith. The new-leadership provision incentivizes boards to clean house during enforcement rather than manage through it. The government institution safeguards prevent the framework from becoming a partisan tool while preserving its application to genuine government misconduct.

The launch window creates urgency. The standing track makes the logic permanent. The decay function makes the response proportional. The race condition makes the system temporally coherent.

Systematic deception becomes a dominated strategy. Early disclosure of emerging harms becomes the rational default. The timing of truth revelation is re-wired — not just for the past, but as a steady-state feature of how markets and institutions operate going forward.

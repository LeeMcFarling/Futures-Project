## Summary

This brief proposes a standardized, platform-integrated **Information Nutrition Label** for digital content.

The system does **not** determine whether content is right or wrong, true or false, or how left or right it is. No government agency makes these judgments, and no central authority controls classification.

Credibility signals are produced through a transparent, auditable process that combines:
- Peer evaluation by diverse civic participants serving in a jury-duty–style role
- Independent third-party institutions across allied democracies
- Open-source machine learning models subject to public scrutiny

The role of the state is limited to setting interoperability standards and transparency requirements. All evaluative judgments are aggregated, averaged, and published as metadata attached to content at scale.

Participation from **aligned democracies** is intentional. Disinformation, captured media organizations, or high-view-count influencers within any single country can distort the media environment. Distributing participation across allied democracies reduces the risk of manipulation caused by failure, capture, or politicization in any one government or media ecosystem.

The objective is to make information quality legible at the point of consumption—similar to how nutrition labels inform food choices—without suppressing speech or delegating truth to the government.

## Problem Statement

Digital information systems suffer from three structural failures:
1. Opacity around sourcing, evidence, and confidence
2. Asymmetric amplification of low-confidence or extreme claims
3. Large-scale abuse via automation and coordinated inauthentic behavior

Existing moderation approaches are opaque, discretionary, and politically fragile.

## Policy Objective

Create a non-coercive, standards-based truth instrumentation layer that improves epistemic clarity while preserving free expression, anonymity, and democratic legitimacy.

## Core System Components

### 1. Digital Nutrition Label

High-reach content is accompanied by a standardized label displaying:
- Factual Confidence Band (high / medium / low)
- Source Quality (primary, secondary, anonymous, circular)
- Evidence Status (verified, incomplete, contested, unverified)
- Ideological Framing Indicator (relative position, not judgment)
- Content Type (news, analysis, opinion, satire)
- Political Relevance Flag (non-political / civic / political)

Non-political content is explicitly labeled as **non-political or neutral**.

Labels are descriptive, not prescriptive.

### 2. Open Verification & Rating Pipeline

Assessments derive from:
- Third-party institutions across allied democracies
- Time-bounded civic raters serving in a jury-style capacity
- Open-source ML classification models

All methods and aggregation rules are public and auditable.

### 3. Blind-Spot Instrumentation (Optional)

An opt-in feature shows users systematic coverage gaps in their feed relative to globally salient topics. Blind spots are descriptive, capped in frequency, and never forced.

### 4. Post-Hoc Correction Visibility

If content is later downgraded or annotated, users who viewed it receive a notification indicating the change, without erasing the original content.

### 5. SSI-Enabled Amplification Integrity (Phase 2)

Large-scale amplification may require cryptographic proofs:
- Proof of unique human
- Proof of jurisdictional eligibility for civic content

Proofs disclose narrow facts via zero-knowledge mechanisms. Identity disclosure is never required.

## Governance & Safeguards

- No centralized truth authority
- No integration into ranking algorithms by default
- Per-platform DIDs with rotation
- Independent audits and appeals

## Expected Outcomes

- Reduced virality of low-confidence claims
- Suppressed bot-driven amplification
- Improved public trust through transparency

## Phase Strategy

Phase 1: Voluntary pilots  
Phase 2: SSI-backed amplification integrity  
Phase 3: Cross-platform and international standardization

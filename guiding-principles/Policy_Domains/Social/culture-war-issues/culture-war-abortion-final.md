# Abortion

## What the fight is actually about

Abortion has been compressed into a single binary issue that hides the fact that multiple distinct harms are being argued through the same legal and moral framework at the same time. That compression has made the country less humane, less clear, and less governable — and it has done something worse than produce bad policy. It has made it structurally impossible to solve any individual problem, because every problem is treated as inseparable from the whole metaphysical dispute.

Some Americans believe personhood begins at conception and that abortion is morally equivalent to murder from the moment of fertilization. Others believe personhood or moral status emerges later — tied to viability, consciousness, neurological development, or some other threshold. Those disagreements are real and they are not going away. A pluralistic democracy does not resolve them by force. It builds systems that can hold across the disagreement.

But not every issue currently being governed through abortion law is actually the same issue. Emergency maternal care is not the same as elective termination in the second trimester, which is not the same as fertility medicine, which is not the same as a pregnancy resulting from rape, which is not the same as a late-term complication with a wanted pregnancy. Treating all of them as one legal category and one moral debate has produced a system that handles none of them well.

This framework is not designed to adjudicate the right choice. It is designed to protect the woman regardless of which outcome occurs. The current system — on both sides — is structured around policing the decision. The pro-life regime says: make the preferred choice and we are done with you. The pro-choice regime says: make the preferred choice and we are done with you. Neither one asks what happens to the woman inside the circumstances that produced the pregnancy, and neither one follows her into the consequences of whichever outcome occurs. This framework does.

The framework proposed here disaggregates the issue into four distinct categories, each governed by its own logic. These categories should be separately settled through nationally ratified standards, so that the edge harms producing cruelty, chaos, and spillover into unrelated forms of reproductive care can be addressed directly rather than collapsed into a single all-or-nothing fight.

After that, states can retain bounded discretion over elective abortion within a defined national framework, allowing democratic variance where moral disagreement is deepest while preventing regimes so vague or extreme that they collapse back into emergency-care failure or adjacent bans on contraception and fertility treatment.

## The four categories and proposed referendums

### 1. Emergency maternal care

Pregnancy can involve complications that are not elective abortions in any meaningful sense: miscarriage management, ectopic pregnancy, preterm rupture of membranes, hemorrhage, sepsis risk, incomplete pregnancy loss, and other acute medical emergencies. These cases arise in hospitals, under time pressure, often without warning. They are not scheduled in advance. They are not chosen.

Yet in practice, abortion bans and vague exception regimes have repeatedly failed to cleanly distinguish these emergencies from elective terminations. The result has been delay, legal confusion, physician hesitation, and women being forced to deteriorate further before treatment is provided. In some states, the combination of criminal penalties, licensing risk, and ambiguous statutory language has created conditions where doctors and hospitals wait until a woman is at the edge of collapse before intervening. That is not pro-life governance. It is institutional failure that kills and harms women, and it is happening because legislators wrote emergency medicine into a culture war statute without thinking through what that means in an actual hospital at two in the morning.

**Proposed national referendum:** A federal floor clearly and narrowly protecting treatment for defined emergency pregnancy complications — conditions that women do not choose and cannot control, and that require immediate medical intervention. The purpose is not to settle the entire abortion debate. It is to ensure that no woman is denied stabilizing care because the law is unclear, because a physician is afraid, or because a hospital's legal team told the ER to wait. States that have failed to protect this through their own statutes would be bound by the national standard; states that wish to exceed it may do so.

### 2. Fertility medicine (IVF)

IVF is a fertility treatment used by women and families who are trying to have children. It is not a dispute about elective termination. More than 100,000 babies are born through IVF in the United States each year. It is mainstream medicine, and the families who use it are not asking for anything unusual. They are trying to become parents.

IVF has been pulled into abortion politics because fetal personhood logic can extend outward and treat embryo creation, storage, selection, or disposal as legally suspect. The result is a second harm that has nothing to do with elective abortion: women and families trying to conceive can lose access to fertility treatment because the law refuses to distinguish between a fertilized embryo in a clinic and a pregnancy in the body. Alabama demonstrated how fast this can happen, and how unprepared legislators were for the consequences when it did.

**Proposed national referendum:** Explicit protection for IVF and related fertility care as a distinct category, insulated from abortion restrictions and embryo-personhood spillover. This does not require resolving when personhood begins. It requires recognizing that a framework designed to restrict elective terminations should not accidentally disable fertility medicine for families trying to grow. Those are different things and they should be governed differently. States that wish to exceed the national protection may do so.

## Counterfactual safeguards for the same pregnancy

The next two categories govern opposite branches of the same underlying reality. In one branch, the pregnancy ends in abortion. In the other, it ends in birth. A humane framework must impose obligations on the state in both cases. Care does not end when the abortion is performed, and responsibility does not end when the child is born.

### 3. Care, safeguarding, and support for women receiving abortions

Even where abortion is legally available, the system should not treat the encounter as morally weightless or clinically complete the moment a procedure is scheduled. Some pregnancies result from rape, incest, coercion, trafficking, or abusive relationships. Some women may be under direct threat from a partner or family member. Others may be experiencing acute psychological distress, panic, isolation, or severe material instability requiring immediate support.

A humane framework should therefore require that abortion care include a basic safeguarding and support protocol — not as a punitive gate, but as a protective screen. Its purpose is to identify threat, coercion, or crisis, not to create a performative obstacle designed to generate delay.

The woman should be privately assessed for signs of abuse, pressure from a partner or family member, trafficking risk, criminal conduct related to the pregnancy, and acute mental health needs. Where risk is identified, the provider should connect her to appropriate services, document the concern, and trigger the legally required reporting pathways where they apply. The screening should be brief, private, standardized, and medically administered, with strict confidentiality protections and clear limits on delay. It should not authorize indefinite holds, discretionary moral counseling, or open-ended second-guessing of the patient's decision absent evidence of coercion, incapacity, trafficking risk, or immediate danger. The purpose is to identify abuse and crisis, not to recreate obstruction through softer language.

This is not a waiting period. It is not a mandatory ultrasound or a scripted speech about alternatives. Those interventions were designed to obstruct care while maintaining plausible deniability, and they should be recognized as what they are. What is being proposed here is a genuine clinical safeguard — the same kind of basic duty of care that any serious medical encounter involves. The standard is that care is human, serious, and supportive without converting support into disguised denial.

**Proposed national referendum:** A federal clinical safeguarding standard for abortion care, requiring private assessment for coercion, abuse, and crisis, with a mandatory referral pathway to services where risk is identified. States may exceed this standard but not fall below it.

### 4. Maternal, child, and placement support where pregnancies end in birth

This fourth category does not govern who can access abortion. It governs what the state owes when a pregnancy ends in birth, especially where abortion is restricted, denied, or practically unavailable.

If the law prohibits or meaningfully limits abortion, the state cannot treat birth as the end of its concern. Women will carry pregnancies under circumstances that are often difficult, destabilizing, or involuntary in a practical sense: adolescence, poverty, partner abandonment, housing insecurity, educational disruption, job loss, or family breakdown. And in some cases, the mother will not be ready or able to parent after birth. A serious framework cannot stop its moral reasoning at delivery. The state assumes an affirmative duty not only to the mother, but to the child whose life it has insisted must be carried forward.

A humane framework therefore requires each state to maintain real systems of support for mothers and for children born under conditions of hardship or non-readiness. That includes prenatal and postnatal care, nutritional support, childcare assistance, educational continuation for teenage mothers, income stabilization, and housing pathways where needed. It also includes functioning child-placement systems — adoption support, foster and guardianship capacity, temporary residential care where necessary, and direct child welfare support when a mother cannot safely raise the child herself.

This obligation should not be symbolic. It should be real enough to alter outcomes. If a teenager carries a pregnancy to term, the state should help ensure she can continue school. If a woman gives birth while housing insecure, the state should have a funded pathway for shelter or transitional housing. If a child is born into conditions where the mother cannot parent, the state should be prepared not merely to remove the child into chaos, but to support stable placement and long-term development.

Pro-life governance that ends at delivery is not pro-life. It is pro-birth, which is a different and considerably smaller thing. The principle is straightforward: where the state compels the birth, the state shares responsibility for the downstream burden. States that restrict abortion below the gestational threshold they have set should be required to demonstrate funded support systems meeting defined adequacy standards before those restrictions take effect or remain operative.

**Proposed national referendum:** A national per-child adequacy standard, implemented at the state level, requiring each state to provide a minimum level of maternal, child, and placement support for births carried to term under conditions of hardship or non-readiness. The standard should be indexed to a defined amount per child and audited for actual adequacy rather than nominal compliance.

## Elective termination: state-set gestational thresholds within national floors

Once the four national floors are established, the remaining question — under what conditions and at what gestational stage elective termination is legal — is governed at the state level. This is the part of the debate where the country genuinely disagrees along lines of moral philosophy, religious conviction, and community values. It is the part that should not be resolved by total federal mandate, because national uniformity in either direction has repeatedly proven unstable.

The model proposed here is state-set gestational thresholds within the national floor. A state may permit abortion on request through twelve weeks, or twenty, or up to viability, or with defined exceptions thereafter. States may also establish their own versions of the safeguarding and support standards above the national minimum. What states may not do is fall below the national floors on emergency care, fertility medicine, clinical safeguarding, or maternal, child, and placement obligations.

This approach does several things the current binary cannot. It allows states to reflect actual community values rather than forcing a statewide coalition into an all-or-nothing position. It creates predictability — people know what the rules are in their state, rather than living inside a legal fog where the answer depends on which hospital, which judge, or which attorney general is involved. And it distributes the hardest moral question to the level of democratic governance where community consensus is most accurately expressed.

There is a real equity concern with geographic variation: access to abortion in a state with a twelve-week threshold is materially different from access in a state with a twenty-four-week threshold, and women in restrictive states with fewer resources will have less ability to travel. That concern is legitimate. It does not, however, resolve into a total federal settlement, because the country does not actually share one settled moral view on gestational limits. The framework instead addresses that inequity by requiring meaningful maternal, child, and placement support where pregnancies end in birth under conditions of hardship or restricted access.

## A note on implementation

The national referendum framework and state-set gestational thresholds are both contingent on broader structural changes — digital democracy infrastructure that makes direct democratic participation at scale feasible and trustworthy, and in some cases constitutional reform that clarifies the appropriate boundary between federal and state authority. Those frameworks are addressed elsewhere in this project. The abortion framework proposed here is designed with those structures in mind. Some of its components — particularly direct national referendums — are not fully operational without them.

The principles, however, stand independent of the implementation mechanism: the four categories should be governed separately, the national floors should reflect broad democratic mandate rather than thin legislative majorities, and the gestational question should be settled at the state level with real obligations attached to real outcomes.

## What we need to give up to get there

Getting to something durable on abortion requires each side to let go of something real.

The anti-abortion side has to accept that emergency maternal care, fertility medicine, and clinical safeguarding cannot be culture war casualties. The women who have been denied timely treatment for miscarriages, ectopic pregnancies, and life-threatening complications are not abstractions. A national floor protecting that care is not a concession to abortion-on-demand. It is a basic acknowledgment that the law cannot be permitted to kill women to maintain rhetorical consistency. The anti-abortion side also has to accept that a serious framework does not end at birth. If the state insists that a pregnancy be carried to term, the state inherits responsibility for the downstream well-being of both mother and child.

The pro-choice side has to accept that democratic settlement at the state level is not the same as the end of reproductive rights. The argument that abortion access can only be protected through a single national legal regime has produced decades of judicial politics, polarization, and institutional damage. States governing abortion within meaningful national floors is not total victory, but it is closer to what democratic pluralism looks like on questions where the country genuinely disagrees.

Both sides have to accept that the people in the middle — who support access in early pregnancy and emergency care, want some limits as pregnancy progresses, oppose coerced abortion and coerced pregnancy alike, and are genuinely uncertain about the harder cases — are not fence-sitters or moral failures. They are the majority. A framework that cannot accommodate them is not a framework. It is a permanent occupation waiting for the next reversal.

## The questions worth asking

Rather than asking "are you pro-life or pro-choice," the questions that reveal where people actually stand are more specific:

Should a woman experiencing an ectopic pregnancy be able to receive immediate treatment without the hospital waiting for legal clearance?

Should families using IVF be protected from liability for embryos that do not survive or are not implanted?

Should abortion providers be required to screen for coercion, abuse, and trafficking before performing a procedure?

Should states be required to maintain real maternal, child, adoption, foster, and placement support systems for pregnancies that end in birth under conditions of hardship or non-readiness?

Should gestational age be a primary factor in what legal standard applies — and should states be able to set that threshold themselves within national floors?

Should these questions be settled by separate national referendums rather than by courts or party-line legislative votes?

On most of these questions, the country is significantly less divided than the binary suggests. That is where a workable framework begins — not with the metaphysical dispute about personhood that will never be resolved by law, but with the specific, concrete questions where agreement is actually possible and where the harm of getting it wrong falls on real people.

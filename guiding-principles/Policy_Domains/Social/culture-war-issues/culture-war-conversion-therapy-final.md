# Conversion Therapy

## What the fight is actually about

Conversion therapy should be treated as a narrower issue than the broader fights over LGBT acceptance, trans identity, schools, parental rights, or transition medicine. Those are separate disputes with different harms, different institutions, and different legal standards. The issue here is more specific: whether minors may be subjected to coercive, abusive, or professionally irresponsible efforts to force identity suppression or identity change — in either direction.

That narrowing matters because the current public debate is collapsing several different questions into one. On one side, major medical organizations continue to find that sexual orientation and gender identity change efforts lack evidentiary support and are associated with risk of harm, including depression, hopelessness, and suicidality in youth exposed to such efforts. On the other side, attempts to regulate these practices as speech bans have now run directly into a major First Amendment problem. In *Chiles v. Salazar*, decided March 31, 2026, the Supreme Court held that Colorado's law, as applied to a counselor's talk therapy, regulated speech based on viewpoint and required more rigorous First Amendment scrutiny.

That means a durable framework cannot be built around the claim that the state may ban disfavored counseling speech whenever it dislikes the goal of the therapy. But it also cannot ignore the underlying harm to minors. The way through is to distinguish belief from coercion, speech from malpractice, and parental disagreement from abuse.

This brief therefore does not attempt to settle the broader LGBT or trans acceptance debate. It does not try to make the state the final arbiter of family morality, religious doctrine, or every private conversation about sexuality or gender. And it does not create a framework under which a minor's identity disclosure to a school counselor or other adult — in the absence of documented abuse or coercion — triggers state intervention in the family. The trigger throughout is documented coercion and harm, not identity. That line is not incidental to this framework. It is the framework.

---

## The harms are not all the same problem

The public debate often treats conversion therapy as though it were one thing. It is not.

There is licensed clinical conduct, where a therapist or counselor claims professional authority and directs treatment toward suppressing, changing, or extinguishing a minor's sexual orientation or gender identity. Current state laws have mainly focused on this category, typically by prohibiting licensed mental-health practitioners from subjecting minors to conversion therapy. Those laws are uneven across the country and generally do not reach clergy or other unlicensed actors.

There is coercion outside formal therapy, where the harm may occur in camps, ministries, informal counseling settings, or family-controlled environments. Most existing laws do not reach religious or non-state-licensed individuals, which leaves a large gap even where licensed-provider bans exist.

And there is the counterfactual fear that many conservatives hold: not that minors will be forced to suppress identity, but that institutions — schools, clinics, licensed providers — may push them toward an identity outcome they would not otherwise adopt. A workable framework should not pretend that this fear does not exist. It should instead guard against coercive identity-direction in either direction for minors under dependency conditions — which means the malpractice standard runs both ways, and the institutional safeguards apply to both failure modes.

The goal is not to empower the state to enforce one moral anthropology. The goal is to prevent coercive identity-directed treatment of minors, discipline licensed malpractice, and create tightly defined child-triggered safeguards when abuse thresholds are crossed — not when identity is disclosed.

## The two national referendums

### 1. Licensed-provider malpractice and professional misrepresentation toward minors

The first national referendum should establish a narrow professional standard for licensed mental-health providers working with minors. The state should not try to ban every disfavored conversation. It should instead define a professional boundary: licensed providers may not use a course of treatment directed toward changing, suppressing, or extinguishing a minor's sexual orientation or gender identity when that treatment departs from accepted standards of care, is misrepresented as evidence-based when it is not, or is associated with documented psychological deterioration under treatment.

This is a malpractice framework, not a speech-policing framework. That distinction matters after *Chiles*. The legal target is not belief, prayer, or traditional moral views. The legal target is the use of professional licensure to conduct identity-directed treatment on minors in a way that is clinically unsupported, coercive, or harmful.

The standard is explicitly symmetrical. A licensed provider may not use coercive, predetermined therapy to force identity suppression. Equally, a licensed provider may not use coercive, predetermined therapy to drive a minor toward a preselected identity outcome. For minors, the professional obligation is exploratory, noncoercive, and evidence-based. A therapist who uses dependency, shame, social pressure, or ideological scripting to move a child toward a predetermined result is violating the same professional standard regardless of which direction the pressure runs. The trigger is the coercive method and the predetermined goal, not the ideology behind it.

To make this workable, the statute should rely on a bundle of factors rather than one noisy indicator. The trigger should not be a single report of distress. It should be a pattern of identity-directed treatment coupled with one or more of the following: material departure from accepted professional standards, documented coercive methods, misrepresentation of efficacy or evidentiary basis, or documented psychological deterioration such as self-harm risk, suicidality, severe depressive symptoms, trauma symptoms, or comparable clinically significant decline.

Licensed-provider status should be verifiable through a standardized, privacy-preserving credentialing system that incorporates state licensure, accredited professional status, and relevant disciplinary standing without making any single state board the sole public trust anchor.

**Proposed national referendum:** A national malpractice and misrepresentation standard for licensed mental-health providers working with minors, prohibiting coercive or clinically unsupported identity-directed treatment and false evidence-based claims in either direction, while requiring exploratory, noncoercive, and professionally supportable care.

### 2. Coercion and custodial abuse outside licensed settings

The second national referendum should address the large area that current licensed-provider bans usually miss: coercive conduct outside formal therapy. This is where the framework must be most tightly written, because it is also where the risk of state overreach is highest.

The state should not criminalize ordinary parental disagreement, religious teaching, pastoral counseling, or traditional moral belief as such. A parent who tells a child that their religious tradition considers same-sex relationships sinful is not committing abuse. A pastor who counsels a teenager about sexual ethics is not committing abuse. A family that does not affirm a child's stated identity is not, by that fact alone, committing abuse. The framework should not be readable as authority for state intervention in any of those situations.

The line is conduct, not viewpoint, and coercion, not disapproval. The law targets compelled attendance at abusive programs, confinement, threats tied to sexuality or gender nonconformity, degrading or humiliating treatment, sleep or food deprivation, physical abuse, and other coercive methods that would be unacceptable regardless of the ideological goal they serve.

The child-triggered safeguard must be equally precise. A minor should have access to a confidential reporting channel and a licensed child advocate or school-based mental-health professional — but the trigger for any state review is documented coercive abuse or credible threat, not identity disclosure. A minor who tells a school counselor "my parents don't accept me" or "my family holds traditional religious views" has not triggered a state intervention. That is a disclosure of family disagreement, not evidence of abuse, and treating it as the latter is exactly the kind of institutional overreach that has generated the most damaging backlash and that this framework is explicitly designed to prevent.

The trigger for child-protective review is specific and narrow: credible evidence of forced attendance at an abusive program, physical confinement, documented threats of harm tied to sexuality or gender nonconformity, degrading treatment, or other coercive methods meeting the abuse threshold. The review should be conducted by a trained child advocate operating under strict confidentiality and with a mandate to distinguish abuse from disagreement. A child-triggered report initiates confidential review, not presumptive state intervention, and any escalation beyond that point requires corroborated evidence meeting the abuse threshold. The standard is custodial coercion — not mere family conflict, not parental disapproval, and not identity itself.

**Proposed national referendum:** A national anti-coercion and child-protection standard prohibiting forced attendance, confinement, degrading practices, threats, or other coercive identity-directed treatment of minors outside licensed settings, paired with tightly defined, abuse-triggered — not identity-triggered — child reporting and review protections.

## Adults and the limit of state intervention

Once a person is an adult, the state should be much more reluctant to police voluntary belief-directed counseling as such. Adults retain the freedom to hold traditional religious beliefs, to decline same-sex relationships, to remain closeted, to seek pastoral advice, or to make choices others regard as unwise.

The state's role for adults is narrower. Licensed providers should still be subject to fraud, misrepresentation, and malpractice rules. Programs or counselors should not be allowed to market identity-change treatment as evidence-based clinical care where that claim is false. Coercion, confinement, abuse, and exploitation remain punishable under ordinary law. But adult voluntary association and belief-directed conduct should not be the primary target of this framework.

That limit matters because it keeps the framework anchored to child protection and licensed malpractice rather than turning it into a general campaign to police adult ideology.

## Why this framework is narrower than the broader LGBT and trans fight

This brief is intentionally narrow. It is not a general framework for LGBT acceptance. It is not a school curriculum policy. It is not a parental-rights theory. It is not a transition-medicine brief. It is not an effort to let the state decide every moral disagreement about sexuality and gender.

It is a child-protection and malpractice brief.

That narrowness is a strength. It allows the framework to focus on the specific harms most people can actually reason about: whether minors can be subjected to coercive identity-directed treatment, whether licensed providers can claim therapeutic authority for unsupported and harmful practices, and whether the state can protect children without turning every family dispute into a custody battle or every counseling conversation into a speech offense.

The transition-medicine question — what medical interventions are appropriate for minors with gender dysphoria, under what consent standards, with what review processes — is a separate and genuinely contested empirical and ethical debate. It deserves its own careful treatment and is addressed elsewhere in this series. The framework here does not prejudge that debate. It only requires that whatever a licensed provider does with a minor, it be done through noncoercive, exploratory, evidence-grounded practice rather than ideological scripting aimed at a predetermined outcome.

## What we need to give up to get there

The social-conservative side has to give up the idea that parental, religious, or therapeutic authority extends to coercive identity-directed treatment of minors. Authority is not a blank check. When a child is subjected to forced attendance, degrading treatment, threats, confinement, or professionally irresponsible efforts to force a predetermined identity outcome, the law is entitled to intervene. The fact that broader culture-war disputes exist does not erase that narrower harm.

The progressive side has to give up the idea that every disagreement about sexuality or gender should be routed through expansive institutional intervention, or that speech regulation is a stable substitute for tighter malpractice and abuse rules. A framework that collapses disagreement into abuse, or identity disclosure into a trigger for state action, will not protect minors durably. It will destroy the legitimacy needed to distinguish real coercion from ordinary conflict.

Both sides have to give up the temptation to treat children as sites of ideological resolution. The purpose of this framework is not to force one moral anthropology through the law. It is to prevent coercion, punish abuse, discipline licensed malpractice, and preserve enough institutional trust that real child protection remains possible.

## The questions worth asking

Rather than asking whether someone is simply for or against "conversion therapy," the more useful questions are these:

Should licensed providers be allowed to use coercive, predetermined identity-directed treatment on minors — in either direction — when it departs from accepted standards of care and is associated with documented harm?

Should the law distinguish between belief and coercive conduct, so that religious views and family disagreement are not criminalized, but forced attendance, threats, confinement, and degrading practices toward minors are prohibited?

Should minors have tightly defined, abuse-triggered — not identity-triggered — reporting and review protections, so that a minor disclosing family disagreement does not automatically become a state intervention case?

Should the malpractice standard apply symmetrically, so that coercive pressure toward suppression and coercive pressure toward a predetermined identity outcome are treated as the same professional failure?

Should adult voluntary belief-directed conduct remain largely outside the prohibition except where fraud, abuse, or licensing misconduct is present?

Those questions get much closer to the real issue than the usual slogans do. The country does not need a total state settlement of sexuality and gender to address this narrower harm. It needs a framework that can protect minors from coercive treatment and licensed malpractice without turning the law into a general regulator of belief — and without building institutional machinery that can be weaponized against families simply because they disagree.

# Undocumented Settlement, Childhood Arrivals, and One-Time Status Regularization

## What the fight is actually about

The immigration debate has been compressed into a binary that makes honest resolution impossible. On one side: they are human beings, full stop, and anyone who notices the scale of unauthorized presence or asks how it got this large is revealing their bigotry. On the other side: they broke the law, full stop, and the only legitimate response is removal. Both positions are performing moral clarity while avoiding moral responsibility. Neither one is a serious account of what actually happened.

A major part of what created the modern shadow population began in the 1960s, when Congress moved to reduce the seasonal migrant labor flows that had long existed on the southern border. Bracero and related programs had formalized a cycle of temporary movement that served American agricultural demand and provided income for Mexican laborers. Congress eliminated those legal channels without replacing them with anything that could absorb the same demand. The demand did not go away. The movement did not stop. It simply became illegal.

When you criminalize a labor flow the economy depends on, you do not end the flow. You push it underground. Employers found that unauthorized workers were cheaper and less likely to complain about conditions precisely because they had no legal standing to complain. The incentive structure rewarded non-enforcement as much as it rewarded entry. For decades, the law on paper said illegal while the economy on the ground said necessary, and the gap was filled by people living without status, without protection, and without any realistic path forward. The system created the shadow population. The system then declined to resolve it.

When the shadow population grew too large to ignore, the political response was not an honest accounting of how it had formed — it was a morality play. Legitimate concerns about labor market effects, housing costs, school overcrowding, wage suppression for low-income workers, and the basic coherence of a legal system that said one thing and did another were treated not as policy inputs but as evidence of racism. Simultaneously, the enforcement-only faction weaponized the chaos the first failure created without offering anything that would resolve it. Both responses made honest governance harder. The accumulated result is a public told that noticing the problem disqualifies them from participating in solving it — and a political class that has found the situation more useful unresolved than addressed.

The framework proposed here begins with that origin story, names the harms as they actually exist, and proposes a democratic resolution that is genuinely one time — because the whole political bargain depends on that word meaning what it says.

## The five harms

### Harm one — The institutional origin

The first harm is the one that created every subsequent harm: the 1960s legislative decision to criminalize existing labor flows without replacing them with functional legal channels. That decision did not reduce unauthorized entry. It converted legal, temporary, demand-driven labor movement into a permanently illegal, permanent-settlement-generating shadow economy. The employers who benefited were not held accountable. The workers who were recruited into the shadow economy were not protected. The government that maintained selective non-enforcement was not honest about what it was doing. The harm is institutional in origin and its effects have compounded over sixty years. Any framework that treats the shadow population as a spontaneous problem rather than a created one is not engaging with the actual history.

### Harm two — The moralization trap

The second harm is what happened when the shadow population became too large to ignore. Rather than reckoning honestly with the institutional failure that produced it, the political system converted a policy question into a moral character test. The range of legitimate concerns — about labor market effects, housing costs, school overcrowding, wage suppression for low-income Americans including earlier immigrant communities, and the basic coherence of a legal system that says one thing and does another — were treated not as policy inputs but as evidence of racism or nativism. Simultaneously, the enforcement-only faction weaponized the chaos the first failure created without offering anything that would actually resolve it. Both responses made honest governance harder. The moralization trap is its own harm because it has systematically prevented the country from having the conversation that the actual facts require.

### Harm three — Childhood arrivals in permanent limbo

The third harm is specific and has a specific population: people brought to the United States as minors who did not choose to enter without authorization, have often lived the majority of their lives here, attended American schools, formed American social networks, and in many cases know no other country as home. Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals was an executive response to congressional failure — it was never a statutory settlement, and it has never been stable. The program currently exists in a suspended, partial form: USCIS accepts initial requests but is not processing them, while renewal requests may be granted under the current litigation posture. That is not governance. It is a holding pattern that has now lasted over a decade, during which hundreds of thousands of people have organized their educations, careers, and family plans around a status that could be terminated by the next court ruling. The harm is real and it is ongoing.

### Harm four — A large non-criminal settled population living outside full legal order

The fourth harm affects a different population: adults who entered without authorization and have lived and worked in the United States for years or decades, raised children, paid taxes informally, built community ties, and become functionally integrated into the American economy and social fabric — while remaining in a state of permanent legal precarity that makes them vulnerable to exploitation, unable to fully participate in civic life, and unable to plan a future with any stability. This population is not homogeneous. Some have serious criminal histories that disqualify them from any regularization consideration. The majority do not. Treating the entire population as a monolith — either for the purposes of blanket amnesty or blanket removal — is not a policy. It is the avoidance of one.

### Harm five — Public distrust that any settlement becomes permanent incentive

The fifth harm is different in kind: it is not a harm experienced by the undocumented population, but a harm experienced by the body politic. The legitimate fear — held by a significant portion of the American public, including many legal immigrants who followed the authorized path — is that one-time regularization is never actually one time. That fear is historically grounded. The 1986 Immigration Reform and Control Act was also described as a one-time settlement. It was not followed by the enforcement reforms that were supposed to pair with it, the employer accountability provisions were not seriously enforced, and the unauthorized population continued to grow for decades afterward. The distrust this generated is not paranoid. It is a reasonable inference from the pattern. A framework that does not directly address and structurally constrain that distrust will not achieve democratic legitimacy regardless of its other merits, because a sufficient portion of the public will correctly identify it as a repeat of 1986.

## The four severable titles and proposed national votes

### Title One — DACA and childhood arrivals: statutory lawful status and defined pathway

The first title addresses the childhood arrivals population and moves them from administrative limbo to statutory ground.

The case for this title is the strongest in the stack and the least contested by the public when the question is asked directly rather than collapsed into a general amnesty frame. People brought to the United States as minors did not choose the violation. They have often spent more of their lives in the United States than in any other country. Their formative years, their education, their social relationships, their language, and in many cases their entire sense of self are American in every meaningful sense. Holding them indefinitely in a deferred-action status that cannot be relied upon is a form of institutional cruelty that serves no legitimate enforcement purpose and simply extends the original institutional failure onto people who bear no responsibility for it.

The settlement proposed here is more generous than deferred action and more clearly bounded than immediate citizenship. Eligible individuals must demonstrate continuous presence in the United States since a defined cutoff date, entry before the age of eighteen, completion of or current enrollment in secondary education or higher education or military service or documented employment, no violent felony or serious criminal record, enrollment in the SSI/UPI identity verification system, and tax compliance or tax reconciliation for prior years. The status granted is conditional lawful status — not deferred action, not immediate permanent residence, but a defined legal standing with a real path forward rather than endless administrative renewal. Loss of status upon conviction of a serious criminal offense is explicit and automatic.

The pathway to permanent residence and eventual citizenship is available but defined and gated: a minimum period of conditional status, demonstrated compliance with all conditions throughout that period, tax reconciliation, and the completion of integration requirements scaled to the individual's duration of residence. There is no fast track and no guarantee of outcome, but there is a real and reliable path rather than a permanent legal horizon that never gets closer.

Eligibility is retrospective only. Continuous presence before the statutory cutoff date is a hard requirement. Entry after that date does not establish eligibility under this title, and no administrative exception or equitable argument creates eligibility where the cutoff date has not been met.

**National vote:** Should people who were brought to the United States as minors before a defined cutoff date, who meet continuous presence, educational or employment, identity verification, criminal history, and tax compliance requirements, receive conditional lawful status and a defined pathway to permanent residence? Yes or No.

### Title Two — Long-settled non-criminal undocumented adults: one-time conditional registration and lawful presence

The second title addresses the broader legacy undocumented population and is a harder political question that deserves its own vote because voters may answer it differently from the first.

The framing here is not amnesty. Amnesty pretends the violation did not occur. This framework does not. The violation occurred. The system that incentivized it also occurred. The selective non-enforcement that sustained it for decades also occurred. A one-time registration settlement acknowledges all of that, imposes real compliance conditions, extracts an honest accounting from everyone involved, and resolves the legacy stock in a way that is paired with — and structurally dependent upon — the enforcement and employer accountability architecture that makes future unlawful entry reliably costly rather than reliably ignored.

Eligibility requires continuous presence before the statutory cutoff date, no violent felony or serious criminal record, verified identity enrollment, tax compliance or tax reconciliation for prior years, demonstrated integration into the community, and no participation in fraudulent or criminal schemes in connection with entry or residence. The specific thresholds and verification requirements are defined in the underlying immigration architecture. The status granted is conditional registered presence — not immediate permanent residence, not citizenship, not a guarantee of any further status — with work authorization and protection from removal during the period of compliance. Loss of status upon conviction of a serious criminal offense is subject to mandatory termination and expedited removal procedures as defined by statute.

The one-time character of this settlement must be stated explicitly, repeatedly, and in terms that cannot be administratively extended without congressional action. This title is a singular transition mechanism enacted to resolve the undocumented population present before the cutoff date during the transition to the new immigration enforcement architecture. It is not a program. It does not recur. It does not establish any expectation, precedent, or entitlement for any person who enters without authorization after the cutoff date. No subsequent administration may interpret it as a rolling or renewable offering. Re-enactment for any future population requires affirmative action by Congress, and this title explicitly states that such re-enactment requires a new national activation vote under the same mechanism that enacted the original settlement.

The enforcement pairing is not rhetorical. The new immigration architecture — lawful entry channels calibrated to actual labor demand, employer accountability with real consequences, ISA lifecycle administration, and border certainty — is what makes this politically survivable. Without the enforcement side of the bargain, regularization looks like permissive drift. With it, regularization looks like what it actually is: a reset. The legacy stock is resolved once, the future system functions honestly, and the country stops maintaining a permanent shadow economy on the theory that the alternative is impossible.

**National vote:** Should non-criminal undocumented adults who have been continuously present in the United States before a defined cutoff date, and who meet registration, identity verification, criminal history, and tax compliance requirements, receive conditional registered presence under a one-time national settlement tied to the activation of the new immigration enforcement architecture? Yes or No.

### Title Three — Family unity: provisional caregiver status for qualifying parents and primary caregivers

The third title exists because of a second-order harm created by the first two. If the country separately ratifies protection for childhood arrivals under Title One but rejects conditional registration for long-settled adults under Title Two, the result can be a system that legally recognizes the equities of the child while destabilizing the household the child depends on. A person who has lived in the United States since childhood, who has conditional lawful status, who is completing their education or working, who is building a life — that person's life is not separable from the family structure around it. A parent who would be subject to removal under a failed Title Two vote is not an abstraction in that child's life. They are the household.

Forced family separation as an unintended byproduct of how the vote came out is not governance. It is category collapse through severability, and it produces legal cruelty by accident rather than by intent. The framework must provide for it directly rather than hoping the conflict does not arise.

The family unity title is narrow and conditional. It applies to parents or primary caregivers of a qualifying childhood arrivals beneficiary under Title One, or of a U.S. citizen minor child, where the parent or caregiver meets all of the following: continuous presence in the United States before the same cutoff date applicable to other titles, no violent felony or serious criminal record, enrollment in the SSI/UPI identity verification system, tax compliance or tax reconciliation, documented ongoing primary care responsibility for the qualifying minor or qualifying childhood-arrival beneficiary, and no participation in fraudulent or criminal schemes related to entry or residence. The status granted is provisional family-unity authorization — not permanent residence, not conditional registered presence under Title Two, but a specifically bounded and separately defined protective status whose existence is contingent on the qualifying dependency relationship.

Provisional family-unity authorization is renewable for as long as the dependency condition exists and compliance is maintained, and terminates automatically when the qualifying minor reaches adulthood or when the qualifying childhood-arrival beneficiary no longer holds that status, subject to a defined transition window. It does not automatically convert to any other status. Any further status pathway requires separate enactment by Congress. The cutoff date requirement applies with the same force as in Titles One and Two — a parent or caregiver continuously present only after the cutoff date does not qualify regardless of the qualifying status of the minor in the household.

The purpose of this title is limited and specific: to prevent the legal framework from inadvertently fracturing households in which the law has already chosen to recognize a protected interest. It is not a backdoor general regularization path, and the drafting should reflect that discipline at every point.

**National vote:** Should non-criminal parents or primary caregivers of qualifying childhood arrivals beneficiaries or U.S. citizen minor children receive provisional family-unity authorization so that a legal framework protecting the child does not simultaneously force the dissolution of the household that child depends on? Yes or No.

### Title Four — Status-to-citizenship conversion: permanent residence and naturalization eligibility

The fourth title separates what the first three titles intentionally hold apart: the question of whether conditional lawful status may eventually convert to permanent residence and naturalization eligibility.

Titles One through Three grant real and meaningful statuses. They are not deportation deferrals. But they are also not permanent residence, and permanent residence is not citizenship, and the public's differentiated views on each of those thresholds deserve differentiated democratic expression. A voter who supports conditional lawful status for long-settled non-criminal adults may or may not support their eventual eligibility for naturalization. Forcing those questions together reproduces the same binary trap the whole framework is designed to avoid.

The ratification logic here is the same as throughout this series, but it bears stating specifically for this title: the question of permanent membership in the political community is categorically different from the question of lawful presence and work authorization. One resolves a humanitarian and economic problem. The other confers standing in the full sense — the right to vote, to naturalize, to hold citizenship in perpetuity. Those are not the same decision, and a democratic framework that collapses them into one vote is not being honest about what it is asking the public to ratify. Separate votes produce clearer democratic signal on each. They also prevent the fear of permanent mass naturalization from poisoning public support for the more limited regularization that most Americans are willing to accept when the question is asked directly.

Eligibility for conversion under this title requires: completion of a defined minimum period of conditional status under Title One or Title Two, full compliance with all conditions throughout that period, demonstrated English proficiency, completion of a civics requirement, tax reconciliation current through the year of application, no serious criminal history during the conditional period, and a separate application through USCIS with standard processing. Approval is not automatic. It is a threshold determination made on individual compliance record.

The timeline is real, not aspirational. A minimum period in conditional status — defined by the implementing statute, not administrative discretion — must be completed before a conversion application may be filed. Conversion to permanent residence begins a standard naturalization clock under existing law. There is no fast track and no expedited pathway. The principle is that the country is offering a genuine resolution, not a nominal one, but the resolution proceeds at a pace that allows compliance records to be established and reviewed.

**National vote:** Should holders of conditional lawful status or conditional registered presence under the childhood arrivals or adult regularization titles become eligible, after a defined compliance period, to apply for permanent residence and, thereafter, naturalization? Yes or No.

## The cutoff date and the one-time-only clause

These two structural elements are not details. They are the load-bearing architecture of the entire settlement, and they must be stated with the same clarity and force throughout every title.

The cutoff date is the line between the legacy population this settlement is resolving and every person who enters without authorization after the new enforcement architecture is in place. The dates used throughout Titles One through Four must be identical, must be defined precisely in statute, and must be unambiguous to anyone reading any title in isolation. Continuous presence before that date is required. Entry or commencement of unlawful presence after that date creates no eligibility under any title. No administrative exception, equitable tolling, or discretionary waiver may extend eligibility beyond the cutoff to persons who do not meet it.

The one-time-only clause must be drafted with equal clarity. This settlement is a singular transition mechanism, enacted once, to resolve the undocumented population that formed during sixty years of institutional failure preceding the new enforcement architecture. It is not a program. It does not signal that a future Congress will enact the same settlement for a future population. The statute should say directly: no person who enters without authorization after the cutoff date shall be eligible for relief under any title of this Act; this Act creates no continuing program, precedent, expectation, or entitlement for future unlawful entrants; and any regularization program for any future population requires separate, affirmative enactment by Congress and separate activation through a new national vote.

The 1986 Act is the cautionary precedent. Its enforcement provisions were not implemented. Its employer accountability mechanisms were not meaningfully used. The amnesty was real; the enforcement reset was not. What followed was three more decades of shadow-economy growth and political avoidance. This settlement is paired with a new immigration architecture that is itself the subject of separate enacted law — employer accountability, lawful-channel calibration, ISA lifecycle administration, and border certainty. The enforcement side of this bargain is not a promise. It is already law. The one-time clause means what it says because the enforcement architecture is already in place to make it real, not because the statute says so.

## How the activation mechanism applies

The four titles are separately severable and separately voted. The same enactment structure that governs the rest of this series applies here. Congress writes and passes the full Act — including all four titles, their findings, definitions, enforcement provisions, cutoff dates, compliance conditions, and activation conditions — and the President signs it. The Act is enacted at that point. Each severable title becomes operative only if it receives the required affirmative vote in a national activation referendum held on a defined Election Day.

A title that does not receive the required affirmative vote by the activation deadline sunsets and expires. It does not become dormant law. It ceases to exist. Re-enactment requires returning to Congress. This matters structurally for immigration settlement in particular: a failed Title Two vote does not create a shelf of unlived-in law that a future administration can activate without public consent. The failed title is gone, and its failure does not become the basis for any executive interpretation of the remaining active titles.

The national vote is not a legislative act. It is the triggering condition that Congress wrote into law. Lawmaking remains with Congress and the President. The public ratifies or sunsets law already made. The constitutional logic is the same as throughout this series.

## What we need to give up to get there

Getting to a durable settlement requires honest concessions from every direction. None of them are comfortable.

Those who have framed unauthorized presence as purely a law-and-order question must reckon with the institutional history that created it. The shadow population is not a spontaneous violation. It is the predictable consequence of eliminating legal labor channels without replacing them, sustained for decades by employers who benefited and administrations that looked away. A framework that demands legal accountability from workers while refusing institutional accountability from Congress and the employer class is not being consistent. It is being selective.

Those who have framed the undocumented population as purely a humanitarian question must accept that the communities absorbing the costs of unmanaged entry had legitimate grievances — suppressed wages, overcrowded schools, strained public services, and the institutional insult of a legal immigration system that told one population to wait in line while another moved ahead of it. Those concerns are not bigotry. A framework that treats them as disqualifying will be rejected, correctly, as designed to manage the objection rather than answer it.

Those who want a settlement must accept that one-time means one-time. A regularization moment that functions as an opening rather than a closing repeats 1986. Future unauthorized entry operates under the new system, faces real consequences, and generates no future expectation of repetition. The bargain holds only if the people accepting it accept it as the last step, not the first.

Those who oppose any regularization on principle must state that position plainly. It requires mass removal of a population numbering in the millions, most of whom have lived here for years or decades, many of whose children are citizens, all of whom entered a labor market that was actively recruiting them. That is a coherent position. It should not hide behind procedural objections to the settlement mechanism.

## The questions worth asking

Rather than asking "amnesty yes or no," the questions that reveal where people actually stand are more specific:

Should people who were brought to the United States as children, who have grown up here, attended school here, and in many cases know no other country, receive a stable legal status rather than indefinite administrative limbo?

Should a parent of a childhood arrivals beneficiary or a U.S. citizen child be subject to removal in a way that fractures the household the law has chosen to protect?

Should non-criminal adults who have lived and worked in the United States for years or decades, who have established lives and raised families here, be eligible for a one-time conditional legal status paired with a genuine enforcement reset — or should the only option be either permanent shadow existence or mass removal?

Should the question of whether conditional status holders may eventually apply for permanent residence be decided separately from the question of whether they should receive conditional status at all?

Should all four questions be decided by separate national votes, so that the public's differentiated views receive differentiated democratic expression rather than being collapsed into a single amnesty referendum?

And should any settlement include an explicit, legally binding, one-time-only clause that makes clear the new rules are the new rules — and that future unauthorized entrants are under a different system with no expectation of a future repeat?

On most of these questions, the public is more differentiated than the binary suggests. That differentiation is not confusion. It is the raw material of a settlement the country has not yet had the institutional courage to pursue.

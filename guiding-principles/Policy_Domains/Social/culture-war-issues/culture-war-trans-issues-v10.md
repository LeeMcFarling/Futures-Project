# Trans Issues

## What the fight is actually about

The trans debate has been compressed into a single binary conflict that it has never actually been. What exists in reality is a stack of distinct legal, medical, developmental, institutional, and social questions that have been forced into one frame — and the forcing is doing serious damage to the country's ability to govern any of them well.

A child with gender dysphoria seeking therapy is not the same issue as a minor seeking irreversible surgery. Workplace anti-discrimination protections are not the same issue as the rules governing sex-segregated spaces — a question this framework defers to a subsequent round rather than collapsing into the civil dignity title. A parent who disagrees with a child's self-description is not the same situation as custodial abuse. A physician making high-stakes decisions about a developmentally significant intervention is not the same situation as a student being denied a seat at the lunch table. The current culture war treats all of these as one fight requiring one answer. The result is category collapse, and category collapse cannot be governed.

Once it happens, no domain gets addressed on its own terms. Sports becomes a referendum on whether trans people deserve to exist. Therapy becomes a referendum on schools. Anti-discrimination policy becomes a referendum on medical intervention. The whole field converts into symbolic warfare, and the people actually inside the institutions — the dysphoric teenager, the clinician trying to practice medicine responsibly, the female athlete, the teacher who just wants to know what she is and is not allowed to do — pay the cost of everyone else's certainty.

The institutional track record on this issue has not been good. Major medical bodies moved faster than the evidence warranted in some areas and failed to update adequately when the evidence began to shift. The United Kingdom's National Health Service commissioned an independent review — the Cass Review, completed in 2024 — that found the evidence base for pediatric gender medicine was far weaker than advocacy groups had claimed, that puberty blocker prescribing had outpaced what the data could support, and that the clinical culture around gender services had suppressed legitimate medical questioning in ways that are routine in every other specialty. Several Nordic countries that were early adopters of affirmative pediatric protocols subsequently narrowed them significantly based on their own evidence reviews — not as a result of political pressure, but because follow-up data showed weaker outcomes than the initial protocols anticipated.

None of that means the American conservative response has been calibrated or wise. Blanket bans that sweep in established care for adults, broad sports laws drafted without clinical input, and anti-trans rhetoric that conflates medical caution with denial of personhood have made careful governance harder, not easier. The political fight has been conducted in exactly the wrong direction: toward total victory and away from the narrower, evidence-grounded settlements that would actually reduce harm.

This framework is not designed to settle whether trans identity is real, fixed, chosen, good, bad, or in need of social celebration. Those are not questions the state is best equipped to answer. The state is better equipped to answer narrower things: what protections against harassment and humiliation should apply to trans people in employment and public life; how sex-segregated spaces should be governed in a way that protects both trans people and the integrity of those spaces; what professional obligations apply when minors present with gender dysphoria; what evidentiary and procedural threshold should govern interventions with significant or irreversible effects; and whether competitive physical sports can be governed through a category structure that reflects physiological reality without becoming an instrument of social exclusion. Those questions are difficult enough on their own. They are ungovernable when collapsed into one.

## The harms are not all the same problem

There is no single harm in this domain. There are several, and they cut in different directions.

Some trans and gender-nonconforming people experience genuine harm from state-sanctioned degradation — being forced to carry identification that does not match their presentation, being excluded from workplaces, being denied ordinary participation in public life on the basis of identity alone. A framework that treats every form of recognition as ideological capitulation will produce institutional cruelty and will fail the basic civil-dignity standard that a pluralistic society owes to people who are not harming anyone.

Some trans and gender-nonconforming minors experience genuine distress — depression, self-harm, family conflict, social isolation, bullying. A system that treats every dysphoric child as a political threat will produce cruelty, needless suffering, and a public-health failure among a genuinely vulnerable population.

Some clinicians, families, and policymakers also fear something real: that the institutional culture around gender services created conditions in which minors could move quickly toward irreversible medical pathways without a process proportionate to the stakes. That fear has now been validated by the Cass Review and by the Nordic course corrections. Dismissing it as moral panic is not an analysis. It is a refusal to look at the evidence.

There is a narrower harm in the therapy domain: coercive, predetermined, identity-directed treatment of minors — toward suppression or toward a preselected outcome — using shame, dependency, social pressure, scripting, or other coercive methods. That problem belongs with professional malpractice and child protection.

There is a legitimate concern about sex-segregated spaces — not trans presence in those spaces as such, but the integrity of spaces whose privacy and safety rationale depends on sex separation. That concern is not inherently anti-trans, and it deserves a real institutional answer rather than dismissal. It is also a question whose emotional salience is high enough, and whose policy complexity is real enough, that it warrants its own democratic deliberation rather than being folded into a civil dignity title where it would distort the vote on both questions. This framework defers it to a subsequent round.

And there is a separate question in competitive sports: whether a female physical category can be preserved in contests where sex-linked physiological differences materially affect performance and safety outcomes, while still providing a defined competitive home for trans athletes.

The purpose of this framework is to keep these harms from contaminating one another. Category discipline is not evasion. It is the minimum condition for actual governance.

## The five categories and proposed national votes

### 1. Civil dignity, anti-discrimination protections, and records

The first category governs the civil dignity protections that transgender people are owed in employment, public-facing institutional life, and official records — the domains where state and employer conduct can degrade a person through administrative machinery rather than through anything resembling a legitimate regulatory interest.

Transgender status should not be grounds for harassment, humiliation, or arbitrary exclusion from workplaces, classrooms, cafeterias, clubs, transportation, counseling, or other ordinary forms of public-facing participation. Employers and service providers should not have a general license to demean or exclude people in domains where no specific countervailing institutional interest exists. These protections attach to presentation — to the person in front of you — and do not require a medical credential to access.

A Yes vote on this category functions as a federal floor and preempts state laws that use administrative machinery to degrade rather than regulate. That includes requiring transgender people to carry identification documents that do not match their consistent public presentation, and blocking legal name and gender marker changes in public records for people who meet the recognition standard below.

#### The recognition standard

For purposes of legal name and gender marker changes in official records — where the state has a legitimate interest in accuracy and consistency — recognized transgender status requires two elements: consistent public presentation as a gender different from sex assigned at birth, sustained over a defined period established by the implementing statute; and written attestation from an accredited medical or licensed mental-health provider confirming that the presentation is genuine and ongoing. The attestation is not a diagnosis. It is a professional confirmation of an established, consistent presentation. The bar is real but not high, and it applies only to legal marker changes — not to the anti-harassment protections in ordinary public life, which attach to presentation alone.

#### Rules of construction

Nothing in this title shall be construed to establish or deny any entitlement to access sex-segregated spaces. That question is not governed here. It is a separate matter requiring its own democratic deliberation and is reserved for a subsequent round.

Nothing in this title creates immunity or reduced liability for any person who uses a fabricated claim of protected status or authorization to gain access for predatory purposes. The civil dignity protections this title establishes are not a shield for that conduct, and nothing in this title should be read as one.

**National vote:** Should a national civil-dignity and anti-harassment standard protect transgender people from humiliation and denial of ordinary public-facing participation — and should legal name and gender marker changes in official records be available to transgender people who meet a recognition standard of consistent presentation and clinical attestation? Yes or No.

### 2. Protected talk-based therapy and anti-coercion standards for minors

The second category governs talk-centered therapy and coercion in custodial settings. This is not the medical-intervention question and should not be governed as though it were.

A minor presenting with gender dysphoria or identity distress should be able to access open-ended, noncoercive, developmentally appropriate therapy — including distress management, treatment of depression and anxiety, trauma screening, family mediation, and watchful waiting where clinically indicated. Licensed providers should not face liability merely because gender comes up in treatment.

At the same time, minors should be protected from coercive, predetermined, identity-directed treatment in either direction. The professional obligation for a licensed provider working with a minor should never be outcome-seeking. A therapist who uses shame, social pressure, dependency, or ideological scripting to drive a child toward a predetermined identity outcome has departed from the same professional standard. The malpractice rule runs symmetrically. That symmetry is not a political concession. It is the thing that makes the rule honest.

Outside licensed settings, the same logic applies at a narrower abuse threshold. Parental disagreement, traditional religious teaching, and non-affirmation do not constitute abuse on their own. Forced attendance at abusive programs, confinement, threats, degrading treatment, and other coercive methods that would be unacceptable regardless of their ideological purpose do. The trigger throughout is documented coercion and harm — not identity disclosure, not family conflict, and not the mere fact that a child's stated sense of self differs from what their parents believe.

**National vote:** Should a national standard protect minors' access to exploratory, noncoercive talk therapy while prohibiting coercive identity-directed treatment in either direction — and establish abuse-triggered, not identity-triggered, child reporting and review protections for coercive conduct outside licensed settings? Yes or No.

### 3. Puberty blockers and hormone therapy for minors

Under this title, puberty blockers and cross-sex hormones may not be provided to minors as routine clinical care. They may be provided only through a formally approved clinical research program that meets the following federal requirements: independent ethics board approval; longitudinal outcome tracking maintained as a condition of continued participation; informed consent that accurately represents what the evidence does and does not show, including known fertility implications and current uncertainty about reversibility; screening for psychiatric and developmental comorbidities before enrollment; and parental consent, except in documented abuse situations. No prescribing outside that framework is permitted.

This is not a permanent ban. It is a research-gating requirement — the standard medicine applies in every other specialty when the evidence base for an intervention is genuinely weak. And in this domain, the evidence base is genuinely weak. The Cass Review found the quality of evidence underpinning puberty blocker use in gender-dysphoric minors was low to very low across the board. The claim that blockers are fully reversible — which drove much of the clinical and policy consensus in favor of their routine use — was not supported by the available data. Bone density effects, neurological development impacts, and the high rate of subsequent transition to cross-sex hormones among minors who received blockers all carried meaningful uncertainty that the clinical culture around gender services failed to surface honestly. Sweden, Finland, and Denmark restricted hormonal intervention to formal research settings after reviewing their own outcome data — not as a political response, but because the follow-up evidence was insufficient to justify the scale of prescribing that had developed. Cross-sex hormones carry an additional profile of fertility implications, cardiovascular effects, and developmental consequences that are not yet fully understood at the population level.

This restriction remains in place unless and until the evidence base is certified as sufficient for routine clinical use under standards defined by statute. If that threshold is met, the research-gating requirement lifts and routine prescribing under standard clinical governance becomes available. The rule is evidence-contingent, not permanent.

**National vote:** Should minors be permitted to receive puberty blockers or cross-sex hormone therapy for gender dysphoria outside of a formal, ethics-approved clinical research protocol? Yes or No.

### 4. Surgery for minors

The fourth category asks an equally direct question: should minors be permitted to undergo elective sex-characteristic-altering surgery?

The answer this framework arrives at is no.

The reasoning is developmental, evidentiary, and legal. Surgery on sex characteristics is irreversible in ways that blockers and hormones, whatever their risks, are not. A minor who later understands themselves differently cannot undo a surgical alteration. The long-term outcome literature for sex-characteristic surgery in adolescent populations specifically is thin — the procedure has not been performed at sufficient scale, over sufficient time, with sufficient follow-up, to produce the kind of evidence base that would normally support routine clinical offering. And the developmental reasoning that underlies every other domain of minor consent law — that adolescents lack the full cognitive and emotional architecture to make permanent, high-stakes decisions about their own bodies — applies here with particular force precisely because the decision is permanent.

The conservative criticism of this position will be that it does not go far enough. The progressive criticism will be that surgery bans strand a small number of minors for whom surgery is genuinely indicated. Both criticisms are worth taking seriously, and neither changes the answer. The evidentiary case for surgery in adolescence is the weakest in this entire domain. The irreversibility is real. The developmental concern is real. A serious framework names its conclusion.

**National vote:** Should elective sex-characteristic-altering surgery be permitted for minors? Yes or No.

### 5. Sex-classified sports participation

The fifth category is sports, and it should stay in sports.

The question here is not whether transgender people deserve dignity — they do, and that is settled by the first two categories. The question is whether competitive physical sports can be governed through a category structure that reflects physiological reality, preserves fairness in covered competitions, and provides a defined competitive home for trans athletes — without collapsing into a referendum on identity.

The answer is yes, through three categories with distinct definitions and distinct governing rationales.

#### The female category

The female category is reserved for competitors whose development was organized around female puberty. It exists because male puberty produces skeletal, muscular, cardiovascular, and neuromuscular adaptations — greater bone density, lung capacity, cardiac output, muscle fiber density, and skeletal leverage — that do not fully reverse under hormone therapy. Studies in military and athletic populations have consistently found that transgender women retain meaningful physiological advantages over female athletes even after multi-year hormone suppression, though the magnitude varies by sport and by the timing of transition relative to puberty. Those findings are contested at the margins, but the core result — that male puberty confers durable physical advantages that hormone therapy does not equalize — is consistent enough across the literature to warrant a protected category. Uniform anti-doping standards apply to every competitor in the female category without exception.

Covered competitions — those where the female category applies — are contests in which strength, speed, endurance, power, size, force production, contact tolerance, or comparable physiological attributes materially affect performance or safety. Track and field, swimming, rowing, volleyball, basketball, soccer, wrestling, boxing, weightlifting, cycling, and contact sports generally are covered. This list is illustrative; the governing principle is physiological materiality.

#### The male category

The male category is for competitors whose development was organized around male puberty, and it is open to trans athletes — both transgender men and transgender women who do not meet the female category definition — competing under the same uniform anti-doping standards that apply to every other competitor in that category. There is no separate standard, no special dispensation, and no declaration pathway that moves a competitor out of the category their development places them in. The male category is governed by competitive-integrity rules that apply to everyone within it equally.

#### The open category

The open category covers competitions where the physiological differences produced by male or female puberty are not material to outcomes — where the sex-classification rationale has no purchase. Chess, esports and competitive video gaming, debate, robotics, shooting sports where muscle output does not determine results, and comparable activities fall here. The open category is genuinely unrestricted: any competitor, regardless of sex, gender identity, or physiological category, may participate without restriction. No sex-classification rationale applies because no physiological materiality exists.

#### What the rule does not reach

This title governs competitive physical sports in covered categories. It does not touch ordinary classroom life, school clubs, performing arts, or any non-competitive domain. Students affected by category definitions retain full protection from harassment, humiliation, and exclusion in every other school domain. A student who is ineligible for the female volleyball team is still entitled to attend class, eat lunch, join clubs, and participate in school life without being degraded. The sports rule is a competitive-integrity rule. It is not a license for social cruelty.

#### Edge cases

Mixed martial arts weight classes, para-sport classifications, shooting sports with physical components, and similar competitions where the physiological question is genuinely ambiguous are handled by a defined sports review body applying the materiality standard — not by the statute itself. The principle is durable; an enumerated list in legislation is not.

**National vote:** Should competitive physical sports be governed through three categories — a female category reserved for competitors whose development was organized around female puberty, a male category open to trans athletes under uniform anti-doping standards, and an open category for competitions where physiological materiality does not apply — with category definitions applied consistently and without reclassification by declaration? Yes or No.

## Definitions and limiting principles

The framework only works if the definitions are narrow and title-specific, and if the titles are explicitly severable from one another.

Where a title requires a sex-based classification — the sports title being the primary example — the definition of sex should apply only to that title unless expressly stated otherwise. A sex definition written for competitive athletics should not silently rewrite anti-discrimination law, therapy standards, or civil dignity protections. That contamination is one of the ways category collapse happens at the drafting level rather than at the political level, and it is one of the things the law should explicitly prevent.

For purposes of the sports title, a functional biological definition — organized around the reproductive role of sperm or ova production — provides the precision the title requires. Congenital differences of sex development are handled through a separate review procedure specific to that title, because the edge cases in DSD are clinically complex in ways that a general statutory definition cannot resolve. The point of the definition is not to produce a universal philosophical settlement about human identity. It is to give the sports title the definitional precision it needs to function, without doing any other work.

"Gender identity" and "transgender status" should be separately defined and used where dignity and anti-discrimination protections are at issue. The separation matters because those terms are doing different conceptual work from the biological definition, and forcing one set of definitions to serve all purposes across the entire framework is another route to category collapse.

## Why decomposition matters here more than almost anywhere else

The trans issue is one of the clearest examples in American politics of a problem made ungovernable by structural compression. The harms are genuinely distinct. The institutions involved are different. The constitutional interests at stake differ across categories. The remedies differ. The evidentiary picture differs. And the public's actual views — which are considerably more differentiated than either side acknowledges — differ across the stack.

Most Americans hold differentiated views across this stack — supporting anti-harassment protections while skeptical of surgery for minors, or wanting a high evidentiary bar on medical intervention while still believing trans people deserve ordinary dignity. Those are not contradictory positions. They are what a differentiated public view of a differentiated set of questions looks like. The political debate treats those distinctions as incoherence because a collapsed debate requires collapsed positions. They are not incoherent. They are the raw material of actual governance.

A decomposed framework makes things possible that a collapsed framework cannot. It lets the country protect trans people from harassment without requiring consensus on every medical intervention. It lets the country preserve female physical sports while giving trans athletes a defined competitive home under the same rules as everyone else. It lets clinicians conduct exploratory therapy without collapsing into either speech bans or ideological scripting. It lets the country restrict surgery for minors without thereby foreclosing every form of support for gender-dysphoric young people. Each of those outcomes is achievable separately. None of them is achievable as part of one total fight.

## What we need to give up to get there

The progressive side has to give up the position that every part of this domain must be governed by the same moral vocabulary and the same outcome logic. The Cass Review is not a right-wing document. The Nordic reversals were not political backlash. The evidence moved, and a policy framework that cannot acknowledge that evidence has moved is not protecting trans people — it is protecting a political identity at the expense of the minors whose medical care is supposed to be the subject of the debate. The progressive side also has to give up the pure self-declaration standard in contexts where it does real harm to the framework's durability. A civil dignity protection that can be claimed by anyone in the moment, in every context, is not a durable civil rights protection. It is an invitation to collapse, and it has been used as one.

The conservative side has to give up the position that every accommodation, every dignity protection, and every therapeutic space for gender-questioning minors is part of one ideological project that must be defeated in total. That position makes cruelty easier to excuse, makes careful clinical governance harder to design, and leaves gender-dysphoric minors without the exploratory support they genuinely need while the political fight continues over their heads. The conservative side also has to give up using the bad-faith actor as the template for the entire framework. The existence of people who would exploit a protection does not mean the protection should not exist. It means the protection should be designed so it cannot be exploited — which is what the recognition standard and the rules of construction in title 1 are for.

Both sides have to give up the use of children as the terrain on which adults resolve disputes about identity, culture, and institutional authority. The law should protect minors from coercion, inadequate clinical process, humiliation, and ideological overreach. It should not use them to ratify a moral worldview.

And the country as a whole has to give up the expectation that one court ruling, one agency memo, or one sweeping legislative victory can settle this domain permanently. The issue is too decomposable for that, the evidence is still developing in some areas, and the public is too genuinely divided across too many distinct sub-questions for a single answer to hold.

## The questions worth asking

Rather than asking whether someone is simply for or against trans rights, the more useful questions are these:

Should transgender people be protected from harassment, humiliation, and state administrative degradation in employment and ordinary public-facing life — with legal name and gender marker changes available to those who meet a recognition standard of consistent presentation and clinical attestation?

Should minors with gender distress have access to exploratory, noncoercive therapy without institutional pressure toward a predetermined identity outcome in either direction?

Should puberty blockers and cross-sex hormone therapy for minors be restricted to formal, ethics-approved clinical research protocols until the evidence base is strong enough to support routine prescribing?

Should elective sex-characteristic-altering surgery be prohibited for minors, given its irreversibility, the weakness of the long-term outcome literature in adolescent populations, and the developmental reasoning that underlies every other domain of minor consent law?

Should competitive physical sports be governed through three categories — female, male open to trans athletes under uniform anti-doping standards, and genuinely open for non-physical competitions — with definitions applied consistently and without reclassification by declaration?

Should these questions be submitted to the country as distinct national votes rather than as one total referendum, so that the country's genuinely differentiated views can produce genuinely differentiated answers?

The country does not need one total settlement of the trans debate. It needs a set of narrower, clearer, more honest answers to narrower, clearer, more honest questions — answers that can be ratified democratically, that reduce harm across the relevant populations, and that are durable enough to survive the next change in administration without collapsing back into chaos.

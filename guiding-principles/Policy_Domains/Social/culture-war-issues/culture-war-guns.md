# Guns

## What the fight is actually about

Gun policy has been compressed into a binary conflict that hides two real harms operating at the same time. The first is obvious: people are suffering preventable harm at the hands of firearms, through homicide, suicide, domestic violence, impulsive crisis, and mass casualty events. The second is also real: many lawful gun owners have genuine reason to distrust a state that claims open-ended authority to monitor, seize, or indefinitely withhold weapons without clear standards, meaningful due process, or any credible path to restoration.

A durable framework has to be built to resist both failures simultaneously. That is not a both-sides hedge. It is a design requirement. A system that reduces gun violence by creating an open-ended seizure regime will not hold, because the political and constitutional resistance will eventually collapse it. A system that protects ownership rights by blocking every intervention mechanism will not hold, because the body count will eventually demand a response that bypasses due process entirely. The only stable architecture is one that takes both harms seriously at the level of institutional design — not as a rhetorical concession to the other side, but because both failure modes are real and the system has to be hardened against each of them.

The current fight treats these concerns as mutually exclusive. One side often speaks as though every new safeguard is the first step toward confiscation. The other often speaks as though every rights concern is merely an excuse for inaction. Neither framing governs well. The purpose of the framework proposed here is to separate the infrastructure question from the intervention question. The national government should establish the privacy-preserving standards, verification rails, due-process protections, and custody rules that any serious firearm regime requires. Then states can choose whether to adopt only a yellow-flag intervention layer or both yellow- and red-flag intervention layers. The country does not need fifty incompatible systems, and it does not need one maximal national regime. It needs shared institutional rails and bounded democratic choice over intensity.

---

## The harms are not all the same problem

Before getting to the framework, it is worth being honest about something the binary debate almost never acknowledges: the harms associated with firearms are distinct, they affect different populations through different mechanisms, and they respond to different policy interventions. Treating them as one undifferentiated mass is part of why the policy debate stays stuck.

Suicide accounts for the majority of gun deaths in the United States — consistently more than half in most years. It is primarily a means-restriction and crisis-intervention problem. When access to a lethal means is reduced during a period of acute crisis, many people survive and do not go on to die by suicide through other means. The yellow-flag and red-flag framework proposed here addresses this category most directly.

Domestic violence and intimate partner homicide represent a distinct harm in which a firearm in the household dramatically increases the likelihood that a threatening situation becomes fatal. The removal-during-danger function of the red-flag framework is one relevant intervention here, alongside stronger enforcement of existing prohibitions, relinquishment compliance, and closing the enforcement gaps that currently exist in that system.

Everyday community homicide — the shootings that account for most non-suicide gun deaths and that are heavily concentrated in specific communities — is largely a function of socioeconomic conditions, illegal market access, and policing and justice system failures that no background-check or flag system will adequately address on its own. This framework does not pretend otherwise. Reducing community gun violence requires interventions that go well beyond the scope of this piece.

Mass casualty events involving semi-automatic rifles and high-capacity magazines are a fourth distinct category. They are statistically rare relative to other gun deaths but disproportionately damaging to public trust and enormously disruptive to civic life. The policy debate about rifle classifications and magazine capacity is real and unresolved, and it sits largely outside the scope of the yellow-flag and red-flag architecture proposed here, which is focused on person-level dangerousness adjudication rather than weapon-class restriction. That is a separate debate that deserves its own honest treatment rather than being collapsed into the same framework.

The point is not to minimize any of these harms. It is to be honest about what a given policy tool can and cannot do, and to avoid the rhetorical trap in which every proposal is evaluated as though it must solve all four problems or it has failed.

## The two national referendums

### 1. National firearm standards, privacy-preserving background checks, and jurisdiction-aware compliance

The first national referendum establishes a standardized federal framework for firearm eligibility verification, background checks, and lawful transfer rules. The United States already has a national background-check backbone in the National Instant Criminal Background Check System, established under the Brady Act and used by federal firearms licensees to verify legal eligibility. That existing architecture should be modernized rather than replaced. The goal is not a national gun registry. The goal is a national trust and verification layer: standardized background-check rules, standardized disqualifier reporting, consistent appeals and correction pathways, and privacy-preserving proof of eligibility.

SSI and zero-knowledge proofs are well suited to this kind of problem. A seller does not need the buyer's full criminal, medical, or other protected personal records. The seller needs a legally meaningful answer to a narrower question: is this transfer lawful under the relevant federal and state rules? Under this framework, the system verifies status rather than exposing the underlying record. Lawful purchase and transfer would be supported by a privacy-preserving credential that proves eligibility without disclosing more than necessary. States would retain their own substantive firearm laws where permitted, but the verification and compliance architecture would become nationally standardized.

This referendum would also establish jurisdiction-aware transfer compliance. That does not mean continuous movement tracking of individual firearms or owners. It means that when a regulated transfer, declared relocation, licensed storage change, or other legally relevant event occurs, the system verifies whether possession or transfer is lawful in the receiving jurisdiction. The purpose is to reduce the current gap in which a weapon can be lawfully acquired under one jurisdiction's rules and then transferred or relocated into another legal regime without any coherent compliance check at the point where the law actually changes. No live location tracking, passive monitoring, or continuous possession surveillance is authorized under this framework.

**Proposed national referendum:** A standardized national firearm verification framework built on privacy-preserving background checks, shared reporting rules, interoperable appeals and correction pathways, and jurisdiction-aware compliance checks for regulated transfers and declared jurisdiction changes. The framework must be explicitly barred from becoming a live federal firearm registry.

### 2. A standardized yellow-flag and red-flag framework, available for state adoption

The second national referendum establishes a uniform dangerousness-adjudication framework that states may adopt. This referendum does not compel every state to implement yellow- or red-flag interventions. It creates a nationally standardized model — with fixed due-process protections, evidentiary standards, custody rules, and restoration pathways — that states may opt into as either a yellow-only regime or a yellow-plus-red regime.

This distinction matters. The current debate often jumps directly from "do nothing" to "seize the guns," which is part of why the issue is so politically unstable. A yellow-flag framework creates a middle category for acute concern without automatically converting concern into full deprivation. A red-flag framework is the stronger tool, reserved for higher-risk cases and backed by tighter judicial process.

The legal case for a narrow, court-centered red-flag structure is materially stronger than many critics suggest. In *United States v. Rahimi*, the Supreme Court held that when an individual has been found by a court to pose a credible threat to the physical safety of another, that individual may be temporarily disarmed consistent with the Second Amendment. The Court emphasized the importance of a judicial finding, the temporary nature of the restriction, and the fit between the law and a tradition of disarming those who pose a demonstrated threat of physical violence.

That does not mean any yellow- or red-flag law will survive scrutiny. It means a carefully drafted one can. The more discretionary, indefinite, or nonjudicial the system becomes, the more constitutionally vulnerable and politically unstable it becomes. This framework is therefore designed to be as tight as possible.

#### Yellow flag

Yellow flag is not confiscation by suspicion. It is a temporary risk-management status triggered by documented evidence of acute concern and governed by narrowly limited procedures. Yellow flag may authorize short-term purchase delay, short-term safe-storage requirements, temporary voluntary or ordered third-party custody, and mandatory prompt review. It should not authorize open-ended seizure, indefinite administrative holds, or informal blacklisting.

Yellow flag must require sworn evidence, a defined threshold for temporary concern, immediate notice where safety permits, a prompt hearing schedule, and a clear expiration date absent further judicial action. False or bad-faith petitions should carry real penalties. Yellow flag is designed to create friction and review in moments of credible concern, not to function as a softer name for rights deprivation.

#### Red flag

Red flag is the stronger intervention and should require a court order based on a higher evidentiary threshold. It may authorize temporary firearm removal and temporary purchase prohibition where a person has been shown to pose a credible and specific risk to self or others. The order must be time-limited, reviewable, and paired with a defined restoration path.

Red flag should be available only through judicial process, with prompt adversarial hearing rights, documented findings, and a burden of proof that is clear on the face of the statute. The system must not permit indefinite disarmament without renewed findings. Once the legal basis for dangerousness expires or is not sustained, restoration should be presumptive.

#### Custody and restoration

One of the deepest sources of distrust in gun policy is the belief that once the state physically takes possession of firearms, return becomes slow, politicized, or impossible. The framework should therefore separate adjudication from physical custody. The decision to impose yellow- or red-flag status must remain public and judicial. But physical custody of firearms during the restriction period may be carried out by approved third-party custodians operating under state law, including licensed dealers, bonded armories, or certified gun clubs and associations where authorized.

This preserves due process while reducing fear of permanent seizure through bureaucratic drift. If a third-party custodian returns firearms without legal clearance, that custodian becomes complicit in violating the legal hold and should face civil and criminal penalties. Adjudication remains public. Custody can be plural.

**Proposed national referendum:** A standardized yellow-flag and red-flag legal framework, with nationally fixed due-process and privacy protections, available for state adoption as either yellow-only or yellow-plus-red. States may choose the intensity of intervention, but any participating state must use the common national standards for hearings, evidence, custody, appeals, expiration, and restoration.

## Why the state opt-in structure matters

This framework is designed so that the civil-liberties architecture is national and the intervention intensity is state-level. That is the correct federalist balance.

It means the country shares one trusted legal and technical standard rather than fifty improvised systems. It means states that are unwilling to adopt a full red-flag regime can still adopt a narrower yellow-flag regime rather than rejecting the entire structure. It also creates a real learning system. Yellow-only states and yellow-plus-red states can be compared over time on suicides, domestic violence incidents, emergency removals, false-petition rates, restoration outcomes, and due-process performance.

The crucial point is that states may opt into intervention intensity, but they may not degrade the national rights protections once they participate. Privacy, notice, hearing rights, evidentiary standards, custody rules, penalties for abuse, and restoration pathways must remain fixed. States get democratic choice over how far to go, not permission to build lower-trust versions of the same system.

## What this framework is trying to solve

This framework is trying to solve several distinct problems that the binary debate flattens into one.

It is trying to reduce access to firearms by people who are in acute crisis, are making credible threats, or pose a demonstrated danger to others.

It is trying to do that without creating an open-ended confiscation regime or a backdoor national registry.

It is trying to make lawful ownership legible and defensible by building a privacy-preserving verification architecture rather than a surveillance architecture.

And it is trying to move the argument away from abstractions and toward governable questions: who is disqualified, by what standard, for how long, based on what evidence, with what hearing rights, with whose custody, and how does restoration occur?

Those are the questions a serious system has to answer. The culture war has mostly answered none of them.

## What we need to give up to get there

The gun-rights side has to accept that there are real, preventable harms associated with firearms and that a lawful society is allowed to build narrow systems for screening, delaying, and temporarily disarming people who pose a demonstrated threat. It also has to accept that privacy-preserving background checks and dangerousness adjudication are not the same thing as a universal confiscation regime.

The gun-control side has to accept that distrust of state seizure is not paranoia by definition. In a country with this constitutional tradition and this level of institutional mistrust, a durable framework has to be visibly hardened against abuse. That means judicial process, time limits, restoration rights, penalties for false claims, and no vague administrative authority to disarm people indefinitely because officials are uneasy.

Both sides have to accept that a sustainable firearms framework will look more procedural and less theatrical than the current debate. It will not satisfy maximalists. That is a sign that it may actually be built to last.

---

## The questions worth asking

Rather than asking whether someone is simply pro-gun or anti-gun, the more useful questions are these:

Should the country have one standardized, privacy-preserving background-check architecture rather than fifty partially incompatible systems?

Should the law distinguish between temporary acute concern and court-confirmed dangerousness instead of treating every case as all-or-nothing?

Should states be allowed to choose whether they adopt yellow-only or yellow-plus-red intervention authority once the national due-process framework exists?

Should physical custody during a temporary restriction be permitted to be carried out by approved third parties under strict legal rules, rather than automatically defaulting to direct state possession?

Should any such system be explicitly designed so that it cannot quietly become a federal registry or an indefinite confiscation regime?

Those questions get much closer to the real issue than the usual slogans do. The country is not choosing between total disarmament and total inaction. It is choosing whether to build a system that can respond to real danger without destroying trust in lawful ownership.

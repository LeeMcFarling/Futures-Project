# Executive Summary

The United States lacks a coherent, observable, and incentive-aligned system to direct talent toward solving large-scale public goods problems.

This policy establishes a **National Problem Portfolio (NPP)** and a **Competitive Public Impact Fund (CPF)** — a unified system that:

- Declares a small set of national "Big Asks"
- Enables open competition across sectors to solve them
- Measures progress transparently using standardized metrics
- Allocates funding based on validated outcomes
- Feeds directly into national prestige systems (ceremony + monument)

This is not a grant program. It is a **national problem-solving market** with enforced measurement, visible competition, and outcome-based rewards.

---

# The Operational Problem

Current U.S. innovation systems fail in three structural ways:

**1. Incentive Misalignment**
Markets underinvest in high-impact domains with diffuse or delayed returns (e.g., antibiotic resistance, public health systems, disaster resilience).

**2. Proposal-Centric Funding**
Grant systems reward institutional credibility and proposal writing rather than validated outcomes.

**3. Low Observability**
The public cannot see which problems matter, who is working on them, or what progress looks like.

The result is fragmented effort, weak coordination, and low trust in outcomes.

---

# Policy Overview

The system consists of four integrated components:

**1. National Problem Portfolio (NPP)** — Defines priority problems ("Big Asks")
**2. Competitive Ecosystem** — Open participation with visible progress tracking
**3. Competitive Public Impact Fund (CPF)** — Outcome-based financial rewards
**4. Emergent Impact Track (EIT)** — Parallel pathway for non-programmed contributions

Together, they create a closed loop:

> Problem Definition → Competition → Measurement → Reward → Prestige Integration

# I. National Problem Portfolio (Big Asks)

## Structure

Every 2–3 years, the federal system publishes **8–12 National Priority Problems**.

Each problem must satisfy:

- High national or civilizational relevance
- Clear, measurable outcome space
- Multiple viable solution pathways
- No prescribed method — outcomes are defined, approaches are not

## Core Design Principle

**Define outcomes, not methods.**

The state specifies what success looks like. Participants determine how to achieve it. A Big Ask that specifies a preferred technology or approach is not a Big Ask — it is a procurement specification, and it produces the incumbent-favoring, proposal-gaming dynamics the system is designed to replace.

A team that achieves the defined outcome through an approach that nobody anticipated is equally eligible for the prize as a team that achieves it through the approach that experts expected. The outcome threshold is the gate. The method is irrelevant to eligibility.

---

## Big Ask Selection Criteria

Selection of Big Asks is a high-stakes governance function. The portfolio must be genuinely difficult, genuinely important, and genuinely open — not a restatement of existing agency priorities, not a vehicle for incumbent organizations to collect prizes for work they were already doing, and not a domain captured by the best-organized advocacy coalition.

Each candidate Big Ask must satisfy all of the following criteria before inclusion:

**1. Market or coordination failure is documented.**
The problem must be one where private markets demonstrably underinvest, where returns are diffuse or delayed beyond private time horizons, or where coordination failure prevents investment that would be collectively rational. Problems where private incentives already adequately direct talent and capital are not Big Asks.

**2. The outcome is measurable and falsifiable.**
Before a problem enters the portfolio, the success condition must be defined in terms that DoDa can certify independently. "Improve education outcomes" is not a Big Ask. "Demonstrate a validated, open-source, privacy-preserving learning system that produces statistically significant mastery gains relative to control groups in three or more demographically diverse pilot districts, replicable by independent operators without the originating team" is a Big Ask.

**3. Multiple distinct solution pathways exist.**
A Big Ask must be genuinely open to competition. If there is only one credible approach and one credible organization capable of executing it, the NPP is not the right instrument — that is a procurement or a directed research grant. The Big Ask must be solvable in principle through approaches that have not yet been tried, by teams that do not yet exist.

**4. The problem is upstream of many other problems.**
Priority is given to problems whose solution unlocks progress across multiple domains simultaneously. A breakthrough in firm dispatchable clean energy reduces costs in manufacturing, computation, housing, and transportation simultaneously. A validated open-source education stack reduces inequality, improves workforce development, and reduces the cost of learning across the economy. Upstream problems with broad downstream leverage are systematically prioritized over narrower domain-specific problems.

**5. Civilizational timescale relevance.**
Big Asks are not short-cycle policy problems. They are structural challenges whose resolution — or non-resolution — shapes the trajectory of society over decades. The selection process explicitly asks: if this problem remains unsolved for thirty years, what does that cost? If it is solved in the next decade, what does that unlock?

**6. The problem cannot be gamed by incumbents.**
Before inclusion, the governing board must explicitly assess whether the outcome definition and measurement framework can be captured by incumbents — organizations with existing resources, relationships, and market position that allow them to win prizes for incremental work rather than genuine breakthrough. Problems where this risk is high require outcome definitions specific enough to prevent it, or they are excluded.

---

## Big Ask Selection Process

Big Ask candidates are sourced through multiple channels to prevent any single domain or interest from dominating the portfolio:

- Proposals from domain experts, agencies, and research institutions
- Signal from the Digital Democracy Platform mandate layer — stable national priorities identified through aggregated constituent signal over multiple reporting cycles
- Proposals from the public through a structured open submission process
- Nominations from the governing board's own independent assessment

All candidates undergo DoDa stress-testing before board consideration. DoDa assesses measurability, falsifiability, baseline data availability, and anti-gaming properties of the proposed outcome definition. Candidates that cannot be measured credibly are returned for redesign.

The governing board approves the final portfolio by supermajority. No single domain may represent more than three of the 8–12 active Big Asks at any time. No organization that has submitted a candidate Big Ask may receive a prize in that domain within the same portfolio cycle.

## Worked Examples: Three Illustrative Big Asks

The following examples are illustrative of the ambition level, specificity, and method-neutrality that Big Asks should achieve. They are not commitments to include these specific problems in the first portfolio cycle — they are design templates.

---

### Example 1 — Open-Source Personalized Learning Infrastructure

**Problem statement:**
The United States spends approximately $800 billion annually on K-12 education and produces outcomes that are uneven, geographically stratified, and poorly adapted to individual learning differences. Existing systems are designed around industrial-era assumptions — fixed schedules, cohort-based progression, teacher-as-primary-lecturer — that are poorly suited to the range of learning needs in a modern diverse population. AI-assisted personalization offers the potential to dramatically improve learning outcomes and reduce teacher workload, but existing commercial systems are extractive, proprietary, privacy-violating, and not independently validated.

**Outcome definition:**
Demonstrate a fully open-source, publicly governed, privacy-preserving AI-assisted learning system that:

- Produces statistically significant mastery gains relative to matched control groups across three or more demographically diverse pilot districts
- Compresses core academic instruction to a defined daily window while maintaining or improving long-term knowledge retention and transfer
- Reduces teacher administrative and grading burden by a defined threshold
- Is replicable by independent operators without the originating team
- Passes independent security, privacy, and algorithmic audit

**What this is not:**
This Big Ask does not specify whether the system uses multi-armed bandit algorithms, transformer-based models, knowledge graphs, or any other technical approach. It does not specify the curriculum, the interface design, or the deployment model. It specifies what success looks like from the outside — better learning outcomes, reduced teacher burden, open and auditable infrastructure — and leaves every technical and design decision to the competing teams.

The team that wins this prize may have used an approach that no expert predicted. That is the point.

---

### Example 2 — Direct Air Capture as a Self-Sustaining Commercial Operation

**Problem statement:**
Atmospheric carbon concentrations represent one of the most significant long-horizon civilizational risks currently documented. Existing approaches to carbon removal are either insufficient in scale, prohibitively expensive per ton, permanently subsidy-dependent, or all three. Markets cannot currently price the diffuse social benefit of carbon removal in a way that sustains private investment without public support. The goal is not a government carbon removal program — it is a pathway to carbon removal as a commercially self-sustaining operation that can scale without permanent public subsidy.

**Outcome definition:**
Demonstrate a direct air capture or equivalent atmospheric carbon removal process that:

- Achieves verified net removal at a defined cost-per-ton threshold that makes a credible commercial case without permanent subsidy
- Operates at a scale sufficient to demonstrate industrial replicability
- Produces a documented pathway to cost reduction at higher volumes
- Uses an independently auditable verification methodology for net removal claims
- Demonstrates a viable feedstock or product pathway that generates revenue sufficient to approach operational sustainability

**What this is not:**
This Big Ask does not specify the technology — DAC, enhanced weathering, ocean alkalinity enhancement, bioenergy with carbon capture, or any other approach. It does not specify the business model. It specifies the outcome: a verified, scalable, commercially plausible pathway to atmospheric carbon removal that does not depend on indefinite public subsidy to operate.

The team that wins this prize may have invented a process that does not currently exist in the literature. That is the point.

---

### Example 3 — Replicable Firm Dispatchable Clean Energy Below Defined Cost Threshold

**Problem statement:**
The transition to a clean energy system requires not only abundant intermittent generation but reliable firm dispatchable capacity — power that can be delivered on demand regardless of weather or time of day. Existing firm clean capacity (hydroelectric, nuclear) is geographically constrained and expensive to replicate. The absence of scalable, affordable firm clean power is the primary structural barrier to full decarbonization of compute, manufacturing, and transportation simultaneously. It is also a national security and industrial competitiveness issue of the first order.

**Outcome definition:**
Demonstrate a pathway to firm dispatchable clean power generation that:

- Achieves a defined cost-per-megawatt-hour threshold at scale, independently certified
- Operates without dependence on specific geographic features that limit replicability nationally
- Demonstrates a documented pathway to cost reduction at higher deployment volumes
- Passes independent reliability and environmental impact certification
- Is replicable by operators without the originating team in at least two distinct geographic contexts

**What this is not:**
This Big Ask does not specify the technology. Enhanced geothermal, advanced nuclear, long-duration storage paired with renewables, fusion, or an approach not yet on any roadmap — all are eligible. The outcome is firm clean power at a cost and scale that changes the economics of the transition. The method is irrelevant to eligibility.

---

## Connection to the Digital Democracy Mandate Layer

The National Problem Portfolio does not operate in isolation from the democratic signal infrastructure. The Digital Democracy Platform generates a continuously updated 10-year democratic mandate — stable national priorities aggregated from constituent signal across multiple reporting cycles. This mandate signal is a formal input to the Big Ask selection process.

Problems that appear consistently in the mandate signal — that are raised repeatedly by constituents in their own words, that persist across demographic groups and geographies, that show durable intensity rather than transient media-driven spikes — receive priority consideration for the portfolio. The NPP governing board reviews the mandate signal at each portfolio refresh cycle and must document its reasoning when it declines to include a problem that appears in the mandate signal's top tier.

This connection runs in both directions. The NPP dashboard is integrated into the Digital Democracy Platform as a publicly visible layer showing which Big Asks are active, which teams are competing, and what verified progress looks like. Constituents who raised housing affordability as a mandate priority can see which teams are working on housing construction time reduction, what their verified progress metrics show, and whether the fund has deployed prizes for demonstrated outcomes.

This bidirectional connection converts the Digital Democracy Platform from a signal aggregation system into a visible accountability loop: constituent priorities inform Big Ask selection, and constituent engagement with the NPP dashboard closes the feedback loop between what people said mattered and what the system is doing about it.

# II. Competitive Ecosystem

## Open Participation

Eligible participants include:

- Research institutions
- Startups and early-stage companies
- State and local governments
- Nonprofits and civic organizations
- Cross-sector coalitions
- Individual contributors where the outcome definition permits

No incumbent organization receives preference. Entry requirements are identical for all participants.

## Entry Requirements

- SSI-linked identity registration
- Defined approach and measurement plan
- Agreement to standardized reporting protocols
- Disclosure of funding sources and organizational affiliations

## Team Formation Dynamics

The system is designed to induce:

- Cross-sector collaboration — problems that require both technical and operational expertise favor coalition formation
- Regional competition — geographic variation in approaches generates natural comparison groups for evaluation
- Emergent coalition formation around problems that no single organization can solve alone

## Observability Layer

Each team maintains a public profile displaying:

- Problem targeted
- Approach summary (method disclosure at team's discretion during active competition; full disclosure required at prize claim)
- Interim verified metrics
- DoDa-certified progress updates

This creates a **national league table of problem-solving activity** — a public, continuously updated record of who is working on what, what progress looks like, and what the current frontier of each problem space is.

---

# III. Measurement & Verification (DoDa Integration)

The Department of Data and Accountability serves as the sole verification authority for the NPP. No prize is released without DoDa certification. No team may self-certify outcomes.

## Responsibilities

- Define canonical metrics per problem before the competition opens
- Validate reported outcomes against pre-registered measurement plans
- Audit methodologies and data integrity
- Detect manipulation and metric gaming
- Publish periodic evaluation reports accessible to all participants and the public

## Measurement Design Principles

- Baseline-adjusted comparisons — progress is measured against a certified pre-competition baseline, not against a self-reported starting point
- Longitudinal tracking — outcomes are tracked over time, not just at the point of prize claim, to detect solutions that produce results that do not persist
- Counterfactual-aware evaluation — where feasible, DoDa requires comparison groups to distinguish the team's contribution from background trends
- Cross-team benchmarking — teams competing on the same problem can see each other's verified progress metrics, creating competitive transparency

## Anti-Goodhart Mechanisms

- Rotating secondary metrics — the primary metric is published at competition launch; secondary validation metrics may be updated by DoDa to prevent teams from optimizing narrowly for the primary measure
- Randomized audits of methodology and data integrity at unpredictable intervals
- Full methodology transparency required at prize claim
- Independent replication required for prizes above defined thresholds

# IV. Competitive Public Impact Fund (CPF)

## Budget

Initial allocation: **$500M annually**

Expansion is performance-contingent and triggered by demonstrated system output — verified prizes awarded, talent reallocation evidence, and public engagement with the dashboard. Expansion mechanisms include increased federal appropriation and private and philanthropic co-investment against federal matching, allowing the fund to grow with demonstrated success rather than requiring upfront commitment to a larger number.

A portion of prize payouts is structured as revolving capital — recovered through clawback provisions where solutions are subsequently commercialized above defined thresholds — allowing the fund to partially self-sustain as the portfolio matures.

---

## Payout Structure and Allocation

The CPF deploys capital across three categories, allocated at approximately the following proportions. These are pilot parameters subject to adjustment based on observed team behavior and DoDa evaluation of incentive effects.

### Category 1 — Milestone Payments (approximately 25% of annual allocation)

Milestone payments sustain participation in long-cycle problem spaces where the outcome prize is years away. They are issued for verified intermediate progress against pre-registered milestones — specific, measurable intermediate states that demonstrate credible progress toward the outcome threshold.

**Design intent:** Milestone payments allow teams without large existing capital reserves to participate in problems that require sustained multi-year investment. Without them, the competitive ecosystem selects for well-capitalized incumbents — exactly the failure mode the system is designed to avoid.

**Guardrail:** Milestones must be pre-registered before the competition opens. Teams cannot define their own milestones retroactively. DoDa certifies milestone achievement using the same independence standards as outcome prizes.

**Scaling:** Milestone payment size scales with the difficulty and duration of the problem. A problem with a five-year expected solution horizon has larger milestone payments than a problem with a two-year horizon, calibrated to sustain participation without creating an incentive to collect milestones without pursuing the outcome.

---

### Category 2 — Outcome Prizes (approximately 50% of annual allocation)

Outcome prizes are the core incentive mechanism. They are large, released only after full DoDa outcome certification, and sized to be genuinely life-changing and career-defining for the winning team.

**Design intent:** The outcome prize must be large enough to compete with private sector exit valuations for talented people at career-defining moments. A prize that would represent a modest consulting contract does not redirect aspiration. A prize that would represent a significant liquidity event for a team that has spent years on a hard problem does. The prize redefines what "making it" looks like in the relevant domain.

**Scaling by impact magnitude:** Outcome prizes scale with the verified magnitude and reach of the solution. A team that demonstrates the education system in three pilot districts receives a base outcome prize. A team that demonstrates it in ten districts with diverse demographics and independent replication receives a larger prize. The scaling formula is pre-published before the competition opens — teams know in advance how the prize scales with outcome scope.

**Illustrative prize ranges:** For the three worked examples above, outcome prizes in the range of $20M to $100M would be appropriate given the scale and civilizational leverage of the problems. Final prize amounts are set by the governing board and published before competition opens. They are not negotiated after outcomes are achieved.

---

### Category 3 — Diffusion Rewards (approximately 25% of annual allocation)

Diffusion rewards incentivize the scaling and replication of proven solutions across regions and contexts. They activate after an outcome prize has been awarded and recognize the distinct and often undervalued work of taking a proven solution from its original context and making it work elsewhere.

**Design intent:** Invention and deployment are different problems requiring different expertise. The team that solves the education system in three urban districts in one region may have little capacity or interest in deploying it in rural districts in a different region. Diffusion rewards create a separate competitive track for organizations that take proven solutions and make them work at broader scale — rewarding implementation as a first-class contribution alongside invention.

**Scaling over cycles:** The diffusion reward allocation grows as the portfolio of proven solutions expands. Early cycles have few proven solutions to diffuse. Later cycles have many. The relative weight of diffusion rewards in the overall allocation therefore increases over time, reflecting the growing importance of scaling verified solutions relative to generating new ones.

---

## The Ratio Logic

The 50/25/25 allocation (outcome prizes / milestones / diffusion) reflects a specific set of behavioral incentives:

Outcome prizes at half the allocation signal that the primary purpose of the system is solving the problem, not sustaining participation. Milestone payments at a quarter of the allocation sustain participation in long-cycle problems without becoming the primary incentive — teams that collect milestones without pursuing the outcome are visible in the public dashboard and face reputational consequences. Diffusion rewards at a quarter of the allocation establish from the outset that scaling what works is valued as highly as discovering it, which is both true and culturally important to communicate.

These proportions are explicitly designated as pilot parameters. DoDa tracks behavioral indicators — the ratio of milestone-collecting teams to outcome-pursuing teams, the rate at which proven solutions are diffused, the demographic profile of participating teams — and reports annually on whether the allocation ratios are producing the intended incentive effects. The governing board may adjust proportions at each portfolio refresh cycle based on this evidence.

# V. Emergent Impact Track (EIT)

## Purpose

Capture high-impact contributions that fall outside predefined Big Asks — the breakthrough that nobody anticipated, the crisis response that changed outcomes, the local intervention that produced a replicable model.

## Eligible Contributions

- Crisis response and disaster mitigation with verified outcome impact
- Exceptional frontline action where counterfactual analysis confirms significant contribution
- Unanticipated breakthroughs that advance a domain materially
- Local or system-level interventions with clear, measured, generalizable impact

## Nomination and Initiation

EIT nominations may be initiated by:

- Federal, state, or local government agencies with relevant jurisdiction
- Research institutions with documented evidence of impact
- The NPP governing board based on its own assessment
- Public nomination with a defined evidence threshold for consideration

Self-nomination is permitted but requires a higher evidence threshold and independent institutional co-sponsorship.

## Validation Process

EIT validation follows a defined five-stage process:

1. **Nomination and evidence submission** — nominating party submits documented evidence package
2. **DoDa preliminary review** — DoDa assesses whether the evidence meets minimum threshold for full validation
3. **Independent institutional verification** — at least two independent institutions with relevant expertise verify the impact claim
4. **Counterfactual impact assessment** — DoDa constructs a counterfactual baseline and certifies the contribution's marginal impact
5. **Governing board final review** — board approves EIT designation by supermajority

Timeline from nomination to decision: maximum 180 days for routine cases, 60 days for crisis-context cases where speed matters.

## Integration

EIT participants:

- Qualify for CPF payouts in the EIT classification, with prize sizing calibrated to verified impact magnitude
- Feed into ceremony recognition on equal standing with NPP track contributors
- Enter the Acropolis registry pipeline immediately upon validation
- Are eligible for the Inner Canon on the same timeline and criteria as NPP contributors

---

# VI. Public Interface

## National Problem Dashboard

A publicly accessible platform displaying:

- Active Big Asks with full outcome definitions
- Mandate signal connection — which mandate priorities informed each Big Ask
- Participating teams and their verified progress metrics
- Milestone certifications and outcome prizes awarded
- Fund allocation flows by category and problem
- EIT nominations and validation status

The dashboard is integrated into the Digital Democracy Platform as a visible layer within the civic signal infrastructure. Constituent inputs that contributed to a Big Ask's selection are linked from the Big Ask's dashboard entry, making the mandate-to-problem pathway visible and traceable.

## Functional Role

- Enables public tracking of national progress against stated priorities
- Creates narrative continuity between democratic signal and resource deployment
- Anchors legitimacy in observable results rather than political assertion
- Provides the data substrate for the National Contribution Gala's category structure

---

# VII. Governance

## Big Ask Selection Body

A standing **NPP Selection Council** with the following structure:

- Nine members serving staggered four-year non-renewable terms
- Three appointed by the National Academy of Sciences
- Three appointed by the Government Accountability Office
- Three selected by the prior Council cohort from a public nominee pool

No member may have a financial interest in any organization participating in the NPP during their term or for three years following. No member may have been employed by a federal agency in a policy role within five years of appointment. No domain may be represented by more than two members simultaneously.

The Council's appointment logic is designed to prevent capture by any single domain, agency, or ideological constituency. The cross-branch appointment mechanism — NAS, GAO, and peer selection — ensures no single political actor can pack the body.

## Portfolio Anti-Capture Rules

- No domain may represent more than three of 8–12 active Big Asks
- No organization that proposed a Big Ask candidate may compete for a prize in that domain in the same cycle
- No two consecutive portfolio cycles may have the same domain composition
- All rejected candidates and rejection reasoning are published
- All accepted candidates and selection reasoning are published

## Fund Oversight

- Independent allocation authority, separate from the Selection Council
- Transparent payout rules published before each competition opens
- All prize awards, milestone certifications, and diffusion rewards published in real time
- Annual independent audit of fund allocation against stated criteria

## Review Cadence

- Portfolio refresh: every 2–3 years
- Payout ratio review: annually, based on DoDa behavioral evidence
- Full system audit: every portfolio cycle
- Mandate signal integration review: at each portfolio refresh, with published documentation of which mandate priorities were and were not incorporated and why

# VIII. System Integration

This system feeds directly into:

- **National Contribution Gala** — prestige assignment for validated contributors
- **Civic Acropolis** — permanence and canonization for durable impact
- **Civic Competence Pipeline** — NPP prize winners carry publicly certified track records of problem-solving competence that constitute legitimate credentials for democratic leadership

All recognition systems draw from the same validated dataset. No parallel scoring systems are permitted. The NPP dashboard is the evidentiary foundation for all downstream recognition.

# IX. Expected Effects

## First-Order

- Increased investment in neglected public goods problems
- Measurable progress on problems that markets systematically underinvest in
- Transparent, outcome-based funding allocation visible to the public

## Second-Order

- Talent reallocation toward high-impact domains as prize sizes create genuine career-defining incentives
- Formation of competitive cross-sector teams around specific outcome thresholds
- Regional competition generating natural evaluation opportunities for DoDA

## Third-Order

- Cultural shift toward contribution-based prestige as prize winners become culturally visible
- Emergence of a shared national progress narrative grounded in verified achievements
- Reduced reliance on political abstraction as progress becomes legible and measurable

---

# X. Failure Modes & Mitigations

## Metric Gaming
Mitigation: pre-registered milestones, rotating secondary metrics, randomized audits, independent replication requirements above prize thresholds

## Incumbent Capture of Selection
Mitigation: anti-capture rules, cross-branch appointment, domain diversity requirements, published rejection reasoning

## Milestone Collection Without Outcome Pursuit
Mitigation: public dashboard visibility of milestone-to-outcome ratio per team, payout ratio review, milestone scaling calibrated to outcome pursuit

## Over-Specification Creeping In
Mitigation: outcome-definition review at each cycle, explicit prohibition on method specification, DoDa challenge process for outcome definitions that effectively specify approach

## Public Disengagement
Mitigation: Digital Democracy Platform integration, narrative continuity through gala, dashboard visibility

## Mandate Capture of Portfolio
Mitigation: governing board independence, anti-domain-concentration rules, explicit documentation of mandate signal incorporation

# XI. Phase Logic

## Phase 1 (Pilot — 1–2 States, 2–3 Years)

- 3–5 Big Asks, including at least one from each of the worked example domains
- Limited CPF deployment ($100–150M in Phase 1)
- Prototype dashboard with Digital Democracy Platform integration
- Initial team onboarding and SSI registration
- DoDa measurement framework established and published
- Selection Council appointed and operational

## Phase 2 (Expansion — Multi-State, 3–5 Years)

- 6–8 Big Asks
- Full DoDa measurement stack operational
- Early ceremony integration — first gala cycle includes NPP track
- Payout ratio review based on Phase 1 behavioral evidence
- First EIT prizes awarded
- Dashboard fully integrated with Digital Democracy mandate layer

## Phase 3 (National Rollout)

- Full portfolio (8–12 problems)
- Integrated prestige systems — gala and Acropolis pipeline fully operational
- Scaled funding through co-investment and revolving capital mechanisms
- First Acropolis registry entries from NPP track
- Full mandate signal integration at each portfolio refresh

---

# Closing Principle

This system redefines how a nation allocates attention, capital, and prestige:

> The highest rewards and recognition accrue to those who solve the hardest, most important problems — with outcomes that can be seen, measured, and trusted.

The Big Ask is a bet on human ingenuity over institutional incumbency. It says: we do not know how this problem will be solved. We know what it looks like when it is. We will recognize and reward whoever gets there, regardless of their credentials, their organization, or their method. We will make that recognition visible to the country. And we will do it again, for the next problem, and the one after that.

That is the commitment this system makes.

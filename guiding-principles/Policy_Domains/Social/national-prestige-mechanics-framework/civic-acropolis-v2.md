# Civic Acropolis
## National Canon of Contribution

---

## Purpose

Every civilization encodes its values into permanent form. The monuments it builds, the figures it places in stone, the names it inscribes in lasting materials — these are not neutral historical records. They are statements about what the civilization believes mattered, and why, and for whom.

The United States has accumulated a monument tradition that over-represents political power, military victory, and inherited wealth. It has no durable, trusted mechanism for encoding into permanent national memory the people who actually solved the problems that made civilizational progress possible — the researchers, the teachers, the engineers, the practitioners, the discoverers whose contributions changed what was possible for millions of people who never knew their names.

The Civic Acropolis corrects this.

It is a permanent national institution — chartered by Congress, governed as an independent trust on the Smithsonian model, located in Washington DC — whose sole function is to encode into durable physical and digital form the contributions that have passed the most rigorous, time-tested, evidence-based recognition standard in American civic life.

It is not a hall of fame. It is not a museum. It is not a monument to political power.

It is a canon — a record of who was right, and what they changed, and that it lasted.

---

## The Two-Tier Architecture

The Acropolis operates on a dual-layer structure that reflects the difference between validated impact and canonized legacy. The two tiers are architecturally and institutionally distinct. Understanding the difference between them is essential to understanding what the Acropolis is.

---

### Tier 1 — The Outer Registry

The Outer Registry is the living record of validated contribution. It is expansive, growing, and continuously updated. Every individual or team whose contribution has been validated through the NPP or EIT systems and recognized through the National Contribution Gala enters the Outer Registry. Entry is automatic upon gala recognition. It requires no additional selection process.

The Registry is a national ledger of what worked — organized by problem, by domain, by time period, and by the scale of verified impact. It is publicly accessible in both physical and digital form. It is searchable, explorable, and linked to the original problem definitions, baseline data, outcome certifications, and validation records that established each contribution.

The physical Registry is designed as a continuous, expanding record — engraved or digitally augmented panels that can grow without limit, organized to allow navigation by domain and decade. The space feels like a library or an archive: dense with names and achievements, oriented toward exploration and discovery, alive with the accumulation of what has been done.

The digital Registry mirrors the physical installation and extends it — providing the full evidentiary record behind each entry, the DoDa-certified outcome data, the validation timeline, and where applicable the subsequent diffusion of the contribution into practice and policy. The digital layer is the access point for the majority of people who will encounter the Acropolis — educators, researchers, students, journalists — and it is designed to function as a genuine educational resource, not merely a list of names.

**The Registry's function** is to make the record of national contribution legible, searchable, and durable. It answers the question: who actually made things better, and what specifically did they do? It is the evidentiary foundation on which the Inner Canon rests.

---

### Tier 2 — The Inner Canon

The Inner Canon is something different in kind, not just degree.

It is a small collection of figures — anticipated to number in the dozens over decades, never in the hundreds — whose contributions have survived the most demanding test the system imposes: a full lifetime of scrutiny, longitudinal validation, and the judgment of successive Canon Councils operating decades after the original recognition.

Entry to the Inner Canon is not a larger prize. It is a different claim. The Registry says: this contribution was validated, it was real, it mattered. The Inner Canon says: across a full life, including everything we now know, the net contribution to human progress was of a kind and scale that warrants permanent physical inscription in the national canon.

That is a much harder claim to make. It is designed to be.

The physical Inner Canon is a separate, architecturally distinct space within the Acropolis complex — sparse, quiet, permanent in its materials and its proportions. Full statuary representations of canonized individuals, accompanied by inscribed contribution summaries, verified impact records, and honest accounts of the full arc of the contribution including its context, its limitations, and its human complexity. The space is designed to be entered deliberately, not encountered in passing. It communicates through its architecture that what it contains has survived tests that most recognition does not survive.

The contrast between the two tiers is legible without explanation. The Registry is where you learn what was done. The Canon is where you encounter what endured.

---

## The Lifetime Standard

The Inner Canon's most important design feature is its time requirement.

No individual may be considered for Inner Canon inclusion until a minimum of twenty-five years has elapsed since their initial NPP or EIT recognition, and no less than ten years since their death or confirmed retirement from active contribution in the relevant domain.

This is not a bureaucratic delay. It is the mechanism that makes the canon trustworthy.

Twenty-five years is long enough for:

- The full arc of a contribution to be visible — whether it produced durable change or temporary improvement, whether it scaled beyond its original context or remained local, whether it opened new possibilities or closed off alternatives that turned out to matter
- The person's full life to be substantially documented — their other contributions, their failures, their conduct, their human complexity — so that the Canon Council is evaluating a complete record rather than a moment
- The political and cultural context of the original recognition to have receded — so that the selection is made in a different moment than the one that produced the original enthusiasm, by people who did not participate in that enthusiasm
- Independent scholarship to have assessed the contribution — so that the Canon Council has access to the full range of critical and supportive analysis, not only the contemporaneous record

Ten years since death or retirement means the subject cannot lobby for their own inclusion, cannot benefit from the recognition in ways that compromise the selection process, and cannot be the subject of a recognition that serves their current interests rather than the historical record.

This standard means the Acropolis will not be culturally relevant in its early decades in the way the gala is culturally relevant. That is correct and intentional. The Inner Canon is not trying to be timely. It is trying to be right. Those are different objectives and they require different time horizons.

The first Inner Canon inductions are expected no earlier than the mid-2040s — at least a decade after the NPP and EIT systems produce their first recognized cohorts, and then the twenty-five year validation window on top of that. The Acropolis will exist for decades as a Registry before it has an Inner Canon. That is not a failure. It is the design working correctly.

---

## Human Complexity and the Imperfection Standard

The Civic Acropolis does not honor perfect people. It honors people whose contributions to human progress were real, durable, and significant — contributions made by humans who were fallible, contradictory, and imperfect in the ways that humans are.

This distinction matters because the alternative — honoring only people whose full lives meet an impossibly high moral standard — produces two failures simultaneously. It produces a canon that is artificially sparse, excluding people whose contributions genuinely deserve recognition because their personal lives included failures or contradictions. And it produces a canon that is implicitly dishonest, pretending that the people it does include were not also fallible — which sets up exactly the dynamic that produces statue-toppling when the full record eventually becomes visible.

The Acropolis addresses this by being explicit rather than pretending. Each Inner Canon inscription includes not only the contribution summary and impact record but a contextual account that acknowledges the full arc of the person's life — including failures, contradictions, and complexities that are part of the documented record. This is not a disclaimer. It is a commitment to honesty that makes the recognition more durable, not less, because it cannot be later invalidated by the revelation of information that was never hidden.

The standard for Inner Canon inclusion is not moral perfection. It is this: across the full documented record of this person's life and contribution, does the net contribution to human progress warrant permanent inscription in the national canon?

That question can be answered honestly even when the full record includes difficult elements. It cannot be answered honestly if the selection process pretends those elements do not exist.

---

## The Revisability Question

The Inner Canon is designed to be permanent. It is also designed to be honest. These two commitments create a tension that must be addressed directly.

The goal of extreme selectivity and long time horizons is to make the need for revision nearly nonexistent. By the time a contribution is being considered — twenty-five years after initial recognition, with the full arc of the person's life documented, with multiple decades of independent scholarship assessing the contribution — the information that would later require revision should already be part of the record. The Canon Council is not selecting based on incomplete information. It is selecting based on the most complete information that will ever be available.

Nevertheless, the Acropolis acknowledges three categories of post-canonization circumstances that require defined responses:

**Category 1 — New Information About the Contribution**
If subsequent evidence demonstrates that a canonized contribution caused significant harm that was not documented at the time of selection — a medical treatment that proved damaging at scale, a technical system whose negative externalities were not visible in the validation window — the Canon Council initiates a Contribution Reassessment. The reassessment evaluates whether the net impact, incorporating the newly documented harm, still meets the Inner Canon standard. If it does not, the inscription is modified to accurately reflect the full impact record, including both contributions and harms. The inscription is not removed. It is made more accurate.

**Category 2 — New Information About the Person**
If subsequent evidence reveals personal conduct by a canonized individual that was not part of the documented record at the time of selection — conduct that a reasonable Canon Council would have weighted heavily in its deliberations — the Council documents the new information in the contextual account accompanying the inscription. The inscription is updated to reflect the full record. The contribution is not removed.

**Category 3 — Conduct Incompatible with Continued Recognition**
In extreme cases — defined as conduct that constitutes crimes against humanity, systematic enslavement or exploitation of others, or other actions whose severity is of a kind that makes continued recognition indefensible regardless of the contribution — the Canon Council may initiate a removal process. Removal requires: a supermajority vote of the full Canon Council, independent review by a panel of three federal judges appointed by the Chief Justice, a public comment period of not less than 180 days, and a final vote of the Acropolis Board of Regents. The process takes a minimum of two years from initiation to conclusion. It is designed to be nearly impossible to trigger for anything less than the most extreme circumstances, and impossible to trigger politically.

The three-category framework is designed to make the Acropolis genuinely honest — able to update its record when the record requires updating — while making political manipulation of the canon structurally infeasible. The Confederate statue problem happened because those monuments were erected quickly and politically, without a durability standard, and the selection was never legitimate. A canon built on twenty-five years of validation, lifetime documentation, and a multi-decade selection process does not produce the same vulnerability — because the original claim was rigorous enough to survive scrutiny.

## Governance: The Smithsonian Model

The Civic Acropolis is chartered by Congress as an independent trust institution, governed on the Smithsonian model — federally established, independently operated, with its own Board of Regents, its own endowment, and statutory protection of its independence from executive branch control or political interference.

### Board of Regents

The Board of Regents consists of fifteen members:

- The Chief Justice of the United States (ex officio)
- Three members of Congress, one from each party represented and one independent or minority party, serving by rotation (ex officio, non-voting on canon matters)
- Eleven citizen regents serving staggered seven-year non-renewable terms, appointed by the Chief Justice from nominees proposed by the National Academy of Sciences, the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, the National Academy of Engineering, the National Academy of Medicine, and the American Historical Association — two from each institution, with the eleventh selected by the prior regent cohort from a public nominee pool

No regent may have a financial interest in any organization whose principals are under consideration for the Registry or Canon. No regent may be a current elected official.

The Board of Regents is responsible for the institutional governance of the Acropolis — its endowment, its physical maintenance, its operational leadership, and the protection of its independence. It is not responsible for selection decisions. Selection is the exclusive domain of the Canon Council.

### The Canon Council

The Canon Council is a standing body of nine members serving staggered ten-year non-renewable terms, appointed by the Board of Regents from nominees proposed by the same five institutions that nominate citizen regents — with the additional constraint that no domain may be represented by more than two members simultaneously and that the Council must at all times include members with expertise in the natural sciences, social sciences, engineering and technology, education, and public health.

The Canon Council is responsible for all Inner Canon selection decisions, Contribution Reassessments, and removal processes. Its decisions require supermajority votes. Dissenting opinions are published with all decisions.

The Canon Council operates with complete independence from the Board of Regents on selection matters. The Board may not instruct, pressure, or overrule the Council on any selection decision.

### DoDa's Role

The Department of Data and Accountability serves as the evidentiary authority for the Acropolis — providing, maintaining, and updating the certified outcome data that underlies each Registry entry and Canon inscription. DoDa does not make selection decisions. It ensures that the evidentiary record is accurate, current, and accessible to the Canon Council and the public.

DoDa's relationship to the Acropolis is the same as its relationship to the NPP and EIT: it measures and certifies reality. It does not interpret or evaluate.

## Physical Design

### Location and Site

The Civic Acropolis is located in Washington DC on a site selected to convey civic significance without political association. The National Mall is deliberately avoided — the Mall's political symbolism is inseparable from its monuments, and the Acropolis's independence from political power is best expressed through its location as well as its governance.

The preferred siting is in proximity to the Smithsonian campus — the natural institutional home given the governance model — but on a distinct site that gives the Acropolis its own identity and approach. The site selection is the responsibility of the Board of Regents in consultation with Congress and the National Capital Planning Commission.

The building is designed for longevity — materials, proportions, and maintenance standards that anticipate a useful life of centuries, not decades. The design brief explicitly states that the building should feel as appropriate in 2150 as it does at opening. This is a constraint on aesthetic choices that favor trend over permanence.

### The Registry Hall

The Registry Hall is the primary public space — expansive, well-lit, organized for navigation and exploration. The physical installation uses a combination of permanent engraving and digitally augmented surfaces that allow the Registry to expand continuously without architectural modification.

Entries are organized by domain and decade, allowing visitors to navigate by problem area (housing, health, energy, education, environment) or by time period. Each entry is accompanied by a QR-accessible link to the full digital record — the validation documentation, the outcome data, the gala recognition, and the subsequent diffusion record.

The Hall is designed to be inhabited — seating, study spaces, natural light, acoustic properties that support quiet engagement with the record. It is not a corridor to pass through. It is a space to spend time in.

### The Canon Chamber

The Canon Chamber is a separate, architecturally distinct space accessed through the Registry Hall but designed to be immediately recognizable as different in kind.

The Chamber is small by intention. It is designed to hold a limited number of figures — dozens over decades, not hundreds — and to feel complete rather than sparse at that scale. The proportions, materials, and light are calibrated to produce an atmosphere of deliberate permanence: you understand when you enter that what is here has survived tests that most things do not survive.

Full statuary representations — life scale, in durable stone or bronze — are the physical form of Inner Canon recognition. Each figure is accompanied by an inscribed panel containing:

- The contribution summary in plain language
- The verified impact record with key metrics and time horizon
- The full contextual account — the arc of the contribution, its context, its limitations, and the human complexity of the person who made it
- The date of gala recognition, the date of Inner Canon induction, and the names of the Canon Council members who voted for inclusion

The inscribed panel does not hide complexity. It presents it. The person's failures and contradictions are part of the record, honestly contextualized alongside the contribution. This is what makes the inscription durable — it cannot be later invalidated by the revelation of information that was always part of the honest record.

The Chamber is designed to be entered deliberately and quietly. No group tours without educational context. No commercial photography. The space is available for educational visits, civic ceremonies, and quiet individual reflection.

### The Digital Acropolis

The digital platform mirrors and extends the physical installation. It is the access point for the majority of people who will encounter the Acropolis — globally, across time zones, in languages other than English — and it is designed to function as a genuine educational resource.

Features include:

- Full searchable Registry with complete evidentiary records
- Timeline visualization of contributions by domain and decade
- Comparative impact visualization — the scale of contributions across domains made legible through consistent data presentation
- Educational pathways for different age groups and contexts
- Integration with the NPP dashboard showing active Big Asks alongside historical Registry entries in the same domain
- Submission portal for EIT nominations
- Public comment interface for Canon reassessment processes

The digital platform is governed by the same independence standards as the physical installation. It is publicly funded, open access, and never carries advertising or sponsored content.

## The Inclusion Pipeline

The pipeline from contribution to Inner Canon runs through the full prestige stack in sequence.

**Stage 1 — Validation**
Entry via NPP prize or EIT recognition. DoDa-verified outcomes. The evidentiary foundation is established.

**Stage 2 — Gala Recognition**
Public recognition at the National Contribution Gala. Cultural visibility assigned. The contribution enters the national narrative.

**Stage 3 — Registry Inclusion**
Automatic upon gala recognition. The permanent ledger entry is created. The digital record is published. The physical installation is updated.

**Stage 4 — Canon Eligibility**
A minimum of twenty-five years after Registry inclusion, and ten years after the individual's death or retirement from active contribution in the relevant domain, the Canon Council may initiate consideration. Eligibility is automatic at the time threshold. Consideration is not.

**Stage 5 — Canon Assessment**
The Canon Council commissions an independent scholarly assessment of the contribution — its durability, its generalizability, its net impact including any harms, and its relationship to subsequent work in the domain. The assessment is published and subject to public comment for 180 days before the Council votes.

**Stage 6 — Canon Selection**
The Council votes by supermajority. The vote, the reasoning, and any dissenting opinions are published. Selected contributions move to commissioning of the physical installation.

**Stage 7 — Induction**
A public induction ceremony installs the statuary and inscription in the Canon Chamber. The induction is a civic event — not a spectacle, not a media event, but a ceremony that reflects the gravity of what is being done: encoding permanently into the national canon the judgment that this contribution endured.

## Expected Effects

### Near-Term (Years 1–10)

The Outer Registry fills with the first cohort of validated contributors. The physical installation is inaugurated. The digital platform launches and begins functioning as an educational resource. The Canon Council is constituted and begins tracking potential long-term candidates without initiating formal assessment processes.

The Acropolis in this period is primarily a registry and an archive. Its cultural significance is modest — it is too new, and the Inner Canon is too distant, for it to function as the permanent canon it is designed to become. Its primary near-term function is to make the validated record durable and accessible.

### Medium-Term (Years 10–25)

The Registry grows substantially. The digital platform becomes a significant educational resource — used by schools, researchers, journalists, and policymakers as a trusted reference for the history of national problem-solving. The Acropolis establishes its institutional identity and independence through its first governance cycles.

The first Inner Canon candidates become eligible in the late years of this period. The Canon Council begins formal assessment processes. The anticipation of first inductions generates public attention that begins to communicate the institution's purpose and standards.

### Long-Term (Years 25+)

The first Inner Canon inductions occur. The Chamber begins to fill — slowly, deliberately, with contributions that have survived every available test. The physical space acquires the weight of accumulated time that makes permanent institutions meaningful.

Over generations, the Inner Canon becomes the reference point for the question: who changed what was possible, and what did it cost them, and does it endure? It becomes the answer the civilization gives to the question of what it values — not through declaration but through the accumulated, time-tested judgments of the Canon Councils that preceded the current one.

That is the long-horizon function of the Acropolis. It is not designed to be significant in the decade of its founding. It is designed to be significant in the century after.

## What the Acropolis Is Not

The Acropolis is not a hall of fame. Halls of fame recognize excellence within a domain — the best practitioners of a sport, the most accomplished performers in a genre. The Acropolis recognizes contributions that changed what was possible beyond their domain.

The Acropolis is not a museum of science or technology or medicine. Museums collect and display artifacts. The Acropolis encodes judgments — specific, evidence-based, time-tested judgments that this contribution endured.

The Acropolis is not a monument to political power. No elected official may be included in the Inner Canon for their political service. Political contribution to the conditions that made problem-solving possible may be recognized in the Registry. The Canon is for the people who solved the problems, not the people who created the political environment in which solving was possible.

The Acropolis is not a statement about the perfection of its honorees. It is a statement about the durability of their contributions — contributions made by people who were human, fallible, and imperfect, and whose contributions endured anyway.

---

## Closing Principle

The Acropolis defines what endures.

Not what was celebrated in the moment of achievement. Not what was recognized when the cultural enthusiasm was fresh. Not what a political administration wanted to honor, or what an industry wanted to commemorate, or what a media cycle briefly elevated.

What endured.

The Inner Canon is a judgment made across time — by successive Canon Councils operating decades after the original contribution, with the full record available, with the political moment of the original recognition long receded, with the contribution's actual durability visible rather than merely projected.

It says: we waited. We examined the full record, including the failures and the contradictions and the human complexity. We asked whether the net contribution to human progress warrants permanent inscription. And we concluded that it does.

That conclusion is not infallible. No human institution is. But it is as rigorous, as honest, and as durable as institutional design can make it.

The Acropolis earns its authority the same way the contributions it canonizes earn their recognition: slowly, through demonstrated judgment, over time, until the authority is no longer claimed but simply held.

# Emergent Impact Track (EIT)
## Recognition of Unsolicited Breakthrough Contribution

---

## Purpose

The National Problem Portfolio identifies problems in advance and rewards teams that solve them. It is the right instrument for problems we can define clearly enough to run a structured competition.

But some of the most important contributions to national and civilizational progress do not arrive through competitions. They arrive through a smoke jumper who improvises a novel approach under conditions the training manual never anticipated, and a town survives that would otherwise have burned. Through a teacher who develops a method that nobody asked for, proves it works in their classroom, watches it spread through their school, and produces results that should be spreading through every school in the country. Through a scientist who pursues a line of inquiry that no grant program prioritized, isolates a phage therapy that breaks antibiotic resistance in a specific pathogen class, and hands medicine a tool it desperately needed.

None of these people were competing for a prize. None of them were responding to a solicitation. They were doing their work — and in doing it, they produced something that changed outcomes in ways that matter at scale.

The Emergent Impact Track exists to recognize them.

EIT is not a blue ribbon. It is not a sympathy award. It is not a mechanism for institutions to recognize their own people for doing their jobs competently. It is a rigorous, evidence-first, counterfactual-grounded validation and recognition system for contributions that:

- Were not solicited or programmed by any institution
- Produced measurable impact that would not have occurred, or would have occurred less fully, without this specific contribution
- Have characteristics that make them worthy of national recognition and, where applicable, broader adoption

The bar is high by design. The recognition means something because it is hard to earn.

---

## The Three Tracks

EIT recognizes that breakthrough contributions arrive in structurally different forms that require different validation approaches. Three tracks address these differences explicitly.

---

### Track A — Crisis and Acute Action

**What this track recognizes:**
Contributions made in crisis, emergency, or high-stakes operational contexts where time pressure, uncertainty, and incomplete information required improvisation, judgment, or action that went beyond standard protocol — and where that improvisation, judgment, or action produced measurably better outcomes than protocol would have.

**The distinction that matters:**
A professional executing a textbook response to a crisis is doing their job well. Track A does not recognize competent execution. It recognizes the specific contribution that changed outcomes beyond what competent execution would have produced — the improvised approach, the split-second decision, the recognition of a pattern that training didn't cover, the action taken under conditions that no playbook anticipated.

**Illustrative example:**
A smoke jumper and their crew arrive at a wildfire with conditions that don't match the briefing. Standard protocol calls for a specific containment approach. The crew lead recognizes that the wind pattern has shifted in a way that will make the standard approach lethal and useless, deviates from protocol without authorization, improvises a novel containment line, and the town is saved. An after-action review subsequently confirms that the standard approach would have failed under those conditions and that the improvised approach is now being incorporated into training.

The crew lead is a Track A candidate. The rest of the crew, executing the improvised plan, may be recognized as part of the contributing team.

**What Track A is not:**
A firefighter who did their job bravely and effectively in a difficult situation is not automatically a Track A candidate. Courage and competence are honored elsewhere. Track A is specifically for the contribution that changed what was possible — the improvisation or judgment that produced outcomes beyond what the system would otherwise have produced.

**Validation approach:**
Track A validation is incident-based and moves on an accelerated timeline. The counterfactual is assessed against documented conditions and standard protocol. After-action evidence, witness accounts, and incident records form the primary evidence base. Independent domain experts assess whether the contribution exceeded what protocol would have produced and by how much.

**Timeline:** Maximum 120 days from nomination to decision for routine cases. 45 days for cases where recognition has time-sensitive significance (e.g., while the individual is still in active service in the relevant role).

---

### Track B — Practice Innovation

**What this track recognizes:**
Contributions developed within a practitioner's normal role over time — an approach, a method, a system, a practice — that was not solicited, not funded as a research project, and not developed through an institutional program, but that:

- Has been adopted in a local context
- Has demonstrated valid results in that context
- Has the structural characteristics that make it generalizable beyond that context
- Represents a genuine advance on prior practice, not merely a local variation

**The distinction that matters:**
A teacher who runs a well-organized classroom and gets good results is doing their job well. Track B does not recognize good teaching. It recognizes the specific methodological contribution — the developed approach that others could learn, adopt, and replicate, that produces results beyond what existing methods produce, and that deserves to spread.

**Illustrative example:**
A middle school teacher develops, over several years, a novel approach to teaching mathematical reasoning that treats formal proof as a narrative structure rather than a symbolic manipulation exercise. They refine it through their own classes, observe that students who go through it show markedly better performance in subsequent mathematics and also in formal argumentation across subjects. The approach spreads to other teachers in their school and one adjacent school. External assessment confirms the results. The approach has clear documentation, is teachable to other instructors, and produces results that existing curricula do not.

The teacher is a Track B candidate. The contribution is not just the idea — it is the developed, documented, locally validated practice that others can use.

**What Track B is not:**
An interesting classroom experiment that produced good results in one context but has no documentation, no evidence of adoptability by others, and no assessment of generalizability is not a Track B candidate. Local proof is necessary but not sufficient. The contribution must have the characteristics of something that should scale — not just something that worked once.

**Validation approach:**
Track B validation is longitudinal and practice-focused. Evidence includes documentation of the method, records of local adoption, outcome data from the contexts where it has been used, and expert assessment of generalizability. Independent domain experts assess whether the practice represents a genuine advance, whether the local results are likely to replicate in other contexts, and what barriers to broader adoption exist.

**Timeline:** 180 days from nomination to decision. The longitudinal nature of Practice Innovation contributions requires time for thorough evidence review.

---

### Track C — Discovery and Breakthrough

**What this track recognizes:**
Contributions in research, science, or technical domains that represent genuine discoveries or breakthroughs — findings, methods, or tools that advance the frontier of what is known or what is possible — that were not produced through a programmed research initiative, and that have demonstrated application pathway to real-world impact.

**The distinction that matters:**
Interesting research that advances a field is valuable but not automatically EIT-qualifying. Track C recognizes the specific contribution that crosses a threshold — the discovery that changes what medicine can do, the technical method that breaks a bottleneck that has existed for decades, the finding that opens a door that was previously closed. And it requires that the contribution has been independently replicated or is in active credible replication, and that a realistic application pathway exists.

**Illustrative example:**
A microbiologist pursuing a line of inquiry that no major grant program has prioritized isolates a bacteriophage cocktail that demonstrates efficacy against a specific antibiotic-resistant pathogen class in laboratory conditions. The finding is published. Three independent laboratories replicate the core result. Clinical trials begin. The pathway from discovery to therapeutic application is credible and documented.

The scientist is a Track C candidate. The contribution is the discovery plus the replication evidence plus the application pathway — not the discovery alone.

**What Track C is not:**
A promising finding that has not been independently replicated is not a Track C candidate. A discovery with no realistic application pathway within a defined time horizon is not a Track C candidate. Track C is not a mechanism for rewarding interesting science. It is a mechanism for recognizing the specific discoveries that change what is possible in ways that will matter in people's lives.

**Validation approach:**
Track C validation is replication-dependent and application-pathway-focused. Evidence includes the original research record, independent replication documentation, and a structured assessment of the application pathway. DoDa engages domain-specific expert panels for validation. Contributions that are in active replication but not yet fully replicated may be provisionally recognized pending replication completion, with full recognition contingent on confirmation.

**Timeline:** 240 days from nomination to decision for fully replicated contributions. Provisional recognition may be extended earlier where replication is in progress and the case for the discovery is strong.

## The Counterfactual Requirement

Across all three tracks, the counterfactual requirement is the structural mechanism that prevents EIT from becoming a blue ribbon.

Every EIT validation must answer one question with documented evidence:

**Would this outcome have occurred, or occurred as fully, without this specific contribution?**

If the answer is yes — if the town would have been saved by any competent crew lead following protocol, if the educational approach would have been developed by someone else within a year, if the discovery would have been made by another lab following the same research direction — then the contribution does not meet the EIT standard, regardless of how admirable the person is or how real the impact was.

This is not a harsh standard. It is the standard that gives the recognition meaning.

The counterfactual assessment is conducted by independent domain experts appointed by DoDa for each validation case. Their assessment is published as part of the validation record. Nominators and nominees may respond to the assessment before the final decision. The governing body reviews the counterfactual assessment as part of its final determination.

Where the counterfactual is genuinely uncertain — where it is plausible but not certain that the outcome would have occurred without this contribution — the validation body may issue a recognition with documented uncertainty acknowledgment. This preserves intellectual honesty while allowing recognition in cases where the contribution is clearly significant even if the counterfactual is not perfectly assessable.

## Nomination

### Who Can Nominate

Nominations may be initiated by:

- Any individual with direct knowledge of the contribution and access to supporting evidence
- Government agencies at federal, state, or local level with relevant jurisdiction
- Educational institutions, research institutions, or professional organizations with relevant domain expertise
- Journalists or documentary organizations with documented evidence
- Members of the public through the structured public nomination portal

Self-nomination is permitted for Track C (Discovery) contributions where the nominator is the contributing researcher. Self-nomination for Track A and Track B requires institutional co-sponsorship from an organization that can corroborate the evidence.

### The Nomination Threshold

Not every nomination triggers a full validation process. Before full review begins, nominations must pass a threshold assessment — sufficient to show that the counterfactual case is plausible and the evidence base is substantive enough to merit the investment of a full review.

The threshold assessment asks four questions:

1. **Who did the thing?** Clear identification of the contributing individual or team.
2. **What did they do?** A specific description of the contribution — the action taken, the method developed, the discovery made.
3. **What would have happened without them?** A plausible counterfactual account, even if preliminary.
4. **Who can corroborate?** At least two independent corroborating sources — witnesses, institutional records, published evidence, or other documentation.

Nominations that cannot answer these four questions with at least preliminary evidence are returned to the nominator with guidance on what additional documentation would be needed for reconsideration. They are not rejected — they are returned for development.

This threshold provides enough friction to prevent nomination avalanche while keeping the bar low enough that a first responder's incident commander, a teacher's principal, or a scientist's collaborator can complete it without institutional resources.

### The Nomination Experience

The nomination portal is designed to be usable by people who have never interacted with a federal recognition system. It asks the four threshold questions in plain language, allows document upload and link submission, and provides a structured template that guides nominators through what evidence is most useful for each track.

The portal is accessible through the Digital Democracy Platform and through a standalone public URL. No login is required to begin a nomination. SSI verification is required only to submit, to prevent spam and ensure accountability for the nomination record.

Nominators receive a confirmation within 72 hours of submission and a threshold assessment decision within 30 days. If the nomination passes threshold, the nominator is notified and the full validation process begins. If it does not pass threshold, the nominator receives specific guidance on what additional evidence would be needed.

## Validation Governance

### The EIT Validation Council

Each validation case is assessed by an independent **EIT Validation Panel** constituted specifically for that case.

The panel consists of:

- Three domain experts selected by DoDa from a standing pool of certified domain experts across all relevant fields
- One methodologist responsible for the counterfactual assessment
- One public interest representative selected by the NPP Selection Council

No panel member may have a prior professional relationship with the nominee or the nominating organization. No panel member may have a financial interest in the outcome. Panel composition is published as part of the validation record.

### DoDa's Role

DoDa manages the validation process but does not make the final recognition decision. DoDa's responsibilities are:

- Threshold assessment within 30 days of nomination
- Panel constitution within 14 days of threshold passage
- Evidence compilation and organization for panel review
- Counterfactual assessment framework development and quality control
- Publication of validation records
- Timeline enforcement

### The Governing Body

Final recognition decisions are made by the **EIT Governing Body** — a standing committee of five members with staggered three-year non-renewable terms, appointed by the NPP Selection Council from nominees proposed by the National Academy of Sciences, the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, and the National Academy of Public Administration.

The Governing Body reviews the panel's recommendation and the full validation record, may request additional evidence or panel clarification, and issues the final recognition decision by supermajority vote. Dissenting opinions are published.

The Governing Body may not reverse a panel's finding on evidentiary grounds without documented reasoning. It may decline to recognize a contribution that passes the evidentiary threshold on grounds of insufficient counterfactual significance — but that decision must be published with specific reasoning.

## Anti-Capture Architecture

The EIT is designed against several specific capture failure modes.

**Institutional self-nomination capture:**
Mitigated by self-nomination restrictions for Track A and B, and by the conflict-of-interest rules that prevent panel members from having prior relationships with nominees or nominating organizations. An institution cannot reliably nominate its own people and then populate the panel that reviews them.

**Political or cultural favoritism:**
Mitigated by the counterfactual requirement, which forces the validation to engage with evidence rather than narrative. A compelling story that does not survive counterfactual scrutiny does not receive recognition. The panel's counterfactual assessment is published, making the reasoning transparent and subject to public scrutiny.

**Domain capture of the Governing Body:**
Mitigated by the cross-institutional appointment mechanism and the prohibition on consecutive terms. No single domain can dominate the Governing Body over time.

**Sympathy inflation:**
Mitigated by the explicit distinction in each track between competent or courageous performance and the specific contribution that exceeded what the system would otherwise have produced. The bar is not heroism — it is counterfactual significance. These are different standards and the validation process is designed to maintain that distinction.

**Gaming through manufactured evidence:**
Mitigated by the requirement for corroboration from sources independent of the nominee and nominator, randomized audits of validation records, and the panel's authority to request additional evidence or challenge submitted documentation.

## Prize Structure

EIT prizes are drawn from the CPF allocation under a separate EIT classification.

Prize sizing reflects validated impact magnitude across three tiers:

**Tier 1 — Significant Contribution:**
Verified counterfactual significance, locally validated impact, clear contribution above baseline. Prize range: $500K–$2M per contribution.

**Tier 2 — Major Contribution:**
Verified counterfactual significance, impact demonstrated across multiple contexts or at meaningful scale, clear generalizability. Prize range: $2M–$10M per contribution.

**Tier 3 — Transformative Contribution:**
Verified counterfactual significance, demonstrated or highly probable impact at national or civilizational scale, fundamental advance on prior state. Prize range: $10M–$50M per contribution.

Tier classification is determined by the EIT Governing Body based on the panel's impact assessment. Tier criteria are published in advance. The Governing Body's tier determination is included in the published validation record with specific reasoning.

Team contributions are distributed according to a contribution weighting assessment conducted by the panel, with default equal distribution absent evidence of differentiated contribution.

Prize recipients retain full intellectual property rights to their contributions. Recognition of a contribution by EIT does not transfer any rights to the federal government.

## Integration with the Prestige Stack

EIT recognition feeds directly into the national prestige architecture on equal standing with NPP track contributions.

**National Contribution Gala:**
EIT recipients are eligible for gala recognition in the Emergent Impact categories. Gala categories for EIT are structured to reflect the three tracks — Crisis and Acute Action, Practice Innovation, and Discovery and Breakthrough — so the distinctive character of each type of contribution is visible rather than collapsed into a single award.

**Civic Acropolis:**
EIT recipients enter the Outer Registry upon validation. They are eligible for Inner Canon consideration on the same timeline and criteria as NPP contributors — minimum ten years, durability assessment, supermajority selection threshold.

**Civic Competence Pipeline:**
EIT recognition constitutes a publicly certified track record of demonstrated contribution that carries the same credential weight as NPP prize recognition for purposes of the competence pipeline. A smoke jumper who improvised an approach that is now incorporated into national training, a teacher whose method is being adopted across districts, and a scientist whose discovery is in clinical trials — all have demonstrated the specific kind of contribution that the competence pipeline is designed to recognize and reward.

## The Relationship to the NPP

EIT and the NPP are complementary, not competing, recognition systems. They recognize different kinds of contribution through different validation processes.

The NPP recognizes teams that competed for defined outcomes. EIT recognizes individuals and teams who produced outcomes without competing for them. The NPP is prospective — it defines what it wants and waits for someone to deliver it. EIT is retrospective — it identifies what was delivered and asks whether it meets the standard.

Both draw from the same validated dataset. Both feed into the same prestige architecture. Both are governed by the same independence and evidence-first principles.

The distinction between them is not a hierarchy. Neither is more important than the other. Some of the most significant contributions to human progress were produced by people who were not competing for prizes and were not responding to any solicitation. EIT ensures they are recognized with the same rigor and the same cultural weight as the people who were.

---

## What This System Is Not

EIT is not a mechanism for recognizing everyone who does difficult or important work. The vast majority of people who do difficult and important work every day — the nurses, the teachers, the engineers, the public servants — are not EIT candidates. Their contribution is real and it matters. It is not what EIT recognizes.

EIT recognizes the specific contributions where the counterfactual is compelling — where the evidence supports the claim that outcomes were materially different because of what this specific person or team did, in a way that goes beyond what the system would otherwise have produced.

That bar is high. It is high because the recognition means something. A system that recognizes everything recognizes nothing. The EIT is designed to recognize very few contributions very well — to make the recognition credible, durable, and culturally significant by ensuring it is genuinely hard to earn.

---

## Closing Principle

The smoke jumper who saved the town was not competing for a prize. The teacher whose method should be in every school was not responding to a grant solicitation. The scientist who found the phage therapy was not working on a programmed research initiative.

They were doing their work. And in doing it, they produced something that changed what was possible.

The Emergent Impact Track exists to say: we saw what you did, we verified that it mattered, and we are encoding it into the permanent record of what this country achieved and who achieved it.

That recognition is not a blue ribbon. It is a judgment — rigorous, evidence-based, counterfactually grounded — that what you contributed exceeded what the system would otherwise have produced, and that it deserves to be known.

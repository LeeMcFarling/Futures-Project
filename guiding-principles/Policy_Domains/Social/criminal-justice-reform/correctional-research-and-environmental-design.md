# Correctional Research and Environmental Design Framework

## Executive Summary

Correctional systems in the United States have historically been designed by tradition, managed by administrative convention, and reformed by crisis. The physical environments of correctional facilities — the architecture, lighting, acoustics, green space access, sleep conditions, air quality, and spatial organization — have received virtually no systematic empirical attention despite decades of evidence in adjacent fields demonstrating that these conditions powerfully shape human behavior, stress response, cognitive function, and social dynamics.

The result is a correctional environment that is, in many cases, actively hostile to the outcomes it is nominally designed to produce. Facilities built for maximum containment, with harsh lighting, high noise, minimal natural elements, and spatial designs that produce constant surveillance stress, are not neutral environments in which rehabilitation programming happens to occur. They are environments that systematically undermine the neurological and psychological conditions under which learning, behavior change, and social development are possible.

This framework establishes a structured program for testing, measuring, auditing, and scaling environmental and programmatic interventions within correctional institutions. Its purpose is to generate the highest-quality evidence available about which conditions — physical, social, clinical, and operational — produce measurable improvements in safety, wellbeing, capability development, and long-term reintegration. And its purpose is to translate that evidence, where appropriate, into the broader built environment: into the housing, neighborhoods, schools, and public spaces where the same conditions that produce harm inside correctional facilities are producing harm outside them.

This is the most operationally significant brief in the correctional stack. The Competitive Rehabilitation Framework establishes the incentive architecture. The Capability Development brief establishes the programmatic content. This brief determines which versions of those programs actually work — under what conditions, for which populations, with what design elements, at what scale — and generates the evidence base that makes scaling anything other than expensive guesswork.

It is also the most politically sensitive brief in the correctional stack. Research on incarcerated populations has a troubled history in the United States, including episodes of coercive participation, inadequate informed consent, and the use of incarcerated individuals as subjects for experiments that served researcher interests rather than participant interests. This framework is designed with that history explicitly in mind. The ethical architecture described below is not a procedural formality. It is the foundational constraint within which all research activity in this framework operates, and departures from it are treated as disqualifying rather than correctable.

## The Opportunity

Correctional facilities are, in one respect, among the most favorable environments for rigorous social research that exists in society.

Individuals in correctional facilities share the same built environment. They receive the same lighting, the same air quality, the same acoustic conditions, the same spatial organization, the same access to or absence of natural elements. They eat the same food, on the same schedule, in the same spaces. They sleep in the same conditions. They experience the same institutional architecture. This shared environment means that when a condition changes — when a wing of a facility installs circadian-supportive lighting, or when a yard receives a tree planting program, or when a housing unit redesigns its common space — the comparison between participants who experienced the change and those who did not is cleaner than almost any comparison achievable in the broader civilian environment, where countless confounding variables make causal inference difficult.

This research opportunity has been almost entirely unused by policy-relevant correctional research. The vast majority of correctional research has focused on programmatic interventions — treatment programs, educational programs, reentry support structures — and has largely ignored the physical and sensory environment within which those programs occur. The consequence is that policymakers have no reliable evidence about whether the environment itself is limiting the effectiveness of the programs, and whether environmental improvements would amplify program effects, substitute for some program costs, or produce independent safety benefits that no program generates.

Environmental psychology, neuroscience, and architecture research conducted outside correctional settings provides a substantial base of prior evidence. Access to natural light is associated with improved sleep quality, reduced stress biomarkers, and better cognitive performance. Green space access is associated with reduced aggression, improved mood regulation, and lower rates of violent incident in institutional settings. Acoustic noise exposure is associated with elevated cortisol, disrupted sleep, and impaired learning. Thermal discomfort is associated with elevated aggression rates across multiple species and in multiple institutional settings. These findings are robust, replicated, and biologically plausible. What is almost entirely absent is systematic evaluation of these environmental variables within correctional settings specifically, with outcome measurement that includes the reintegration variables that matter for public safety.

This framework generates that evidence.

## Research Domains

Research activity within the framework is organized across four domains. Interventions within each domain are piloted, measured, independently audited, and scaled only when they demonstrate improvement in defined outcomes.

### Physical and Environmental Design

The built environment of correctional facilities is a primary domain of investigation, because it is both highly variable across facilities and almost entirely unstudied with respect to rehabilitation outcomes.

Lighting is the highest-priority environmental variable. The evidence from non-correctional settings is sufficient to establish circadian-supportive lighting — light that tracks natural intensity and color temperature cycles — as a strong candidate intervention. Sleep disruption is ubiquitous in correctional settings, driven by artificial lighting that suppresses melatonin production at night and fails to provide the morning light exposure that calibrates circadian rhythms. The consequences of chronic sleep disruption on cognitive function, emotional regulation, impulse control, and learning capacity are well-established. A correctional environment that systematically disrupts sleep is systematically undermining the neurological conditions under which behavior change and skill development are possible. Piloting circadian-supportive lighting upgrades and measuring effects on sleep quality, institutional violence rates, educational participation, and treatment engagement is among the highest-priority research investments in the framework.

Green space and natural elements — access to outdoor space with vegetation, natural materials in interior spaces, views of natural environments — are the second priority. The literature on nature exposure and stress reduction, aggression reduction, and restorative attention recovery is substantial and biologically grounded. In correctional settings, the relevant outcomes include institutional violence rates, disciplinary incident rates, and self-reported wellbeing. The community stabilization literature — specifically the vacant lot remediation research, including work from Philadelphia demonstrating consistent reductions in gun violence and stress in areas surrounding greened lots — suggests that even modest natural element interventions produce measurable behavioral effects. The correctional research program tests these interventions under conditions where the comparison group is cleaner than the community-level studies and where the outcome measurement is more comprehensive.

Acoustic environment design — noise reduction through architectural treatment, schedule design to reduce ambient noise during sleep and learning periods, and sound management in high-density common spaces — is a third priority. High chronic noise exposure is both stressful in itself and destructive of the attentional conditions required for learning. Facilities that are structurally loud are facilities that are making their own educational programming less effective through an environmental variable that costs money to address but that the correctional system has no current incentive to measure or manage.

Spatial design variables — the layout of housing units, the size and character of common spaces, the presence and quality of visitation environments — are evaluated for effects on social dynamics, violence rates, and the quality of social interaction that contributes to rehabilitative outcomes. The Norwegian rehabilitation model's emphasis on small-scale, normalized living environments rather than large institutional housing blocks is a design hypothesis that the framework can test systematically in American conditions.

### Healthcare and Treatment Environment

The treatment environment — the conditions under which addiction treatment, mental health services, and behavioral health programming are delivered — is evaluated for factors that influence treatment engagement, completion, and durability.

Treatment settings that maintain clinical norms — private, quiet, designed for therapeutic interaction rather than institutional efficiency — produce better engagement than treatment settings that feel like adjuncts to institutional operations. This hypothesis is testable. The research program measures treatment completion rates, treatment engagement quality, and post-release treatment continuity as a function of the physical and operational characteristics of treatment delivery settings.

Nutritional interventions are evaluated as a component of the behavioral health environment. The evidence linking nutritional deficiencies — particularly omega-3 fatty acids, zinc, magnesium, and B vitamins — to aggression, depression, and cognitive impairment is sufficient to justify systematic investigation of whether nutritional improvements produce measurable behavioral outcomes in correctional populations. Several randomized controlled trials in correctional settings in the United Kingdom and elsewhere have found significant reductions in disciplinary incidents following nutritional supplementation. These findings warrant replication and extension within the framework.

Exercise and physical activity conditions are evaluated for effects on mental health, stress biomarkers, and violence rates. The physiological mechanisms linking physical activity to mood regulation, stress response, and impulse control are well-established. What is less clear is the optimal design of exercise opportunities in correctional settings — the frequency, duration, structure, and environmental conditions under which exercise produces the strongest behavioral effects. The research program generates that clarity.

### Education and Workforce Development Environment

The conditions under which education and workforce development programming occur are evaluated as independent variables alongside the programming content itself.

The personalized education infrastructure — adaptive learning systems, competency-based progression, mastery-based assessment — is evaluated in correctional settings for the same outcomes that drive its development in civilian educational contexts: learning rate, retention, engagement, and competency attainment. The correctional setting offers a useful research environment for evaluating adaptive learning systems because the population exhibits high educational heterogeneity and because the outcome measurement — credential attainment, post-release employment, post-release earnings — is comprehensive and longitudinal in a way that civilian educational research rarely achieves.

The timing and scheduling of educational programming within facility schedules is evaluated for effects on participation rates and learning outcomes. Programs that compete with recreation time, that are scheduled inconsistently, or that lack quiet and adequate lighting produce lower participation and worse learning outcomes than programs that are designed with the same seriousness as civilian educational infrastructure. The research program documents these effects and builds the evidence base for treating educational program design — not just content — as a performance variable.

Peer learning models, in which more advanced participants support less advanced participants in educational settings, are evaluated for effects on both teacher and learner outcomes. Peer teaching has consistent effects in civilian educational research. The correctional setting is a particularly appropriate environment to test peer learning models because the social dynamics — the importance of peer relationships, the sensitivity to hierarchy, the availability of individuals with relevant experience — are distinct from civilian classrooms in ways that may either amplify or dampen peer learning effects.

### Social and Institutional Environment

The social conditions of correctional facilities — the quality of relationships between staff and participants, the character of peer relationships among participants, the institutional culture governing daily interactions — are evaluated as primary determinants of facility safety and rehabilitation outcomes.

Staff-participant relationship quality is the most important social variable in the Norwegian rehabilitation model and the one most absent from American correctional research. Facilities in which staff build genuine relationships with participants — relationships characterized by mutual respect, professional investment in participant outcomes, and consistent expectations — produce safer environments and better reintegration outcomes than facilities in which staff-participant interaction is primarily adversarial. This finding is well-established in international comparative research. What is absent is systematic measurement of it in American correctional settings and rigorous evaluation of the staff training, compensation, and organizational culture interventions that produce it.

Peer support program design is evaluated for effects on violence rates and treatment engagement. Programs in which participants with established sobriety or behavioral stability support participants earlier in their recovery or behavioral change process have shown positive effects in multiple treatment contexts. The correctional setting is a high-stakes environment in which peer support can either accelerate positive change or reinforce negative dynamics, and the research program investigates what program structures produce the former rather than the latter.

Facility governance models — structures that give participants meaningful agency over their shared environment within appropriate security constraints — are evaluated for effects on institutional violence, engagement with programming, and self-reported wellbeing. The Facility Commons Fund established in the Capability Development brief provides one mechanism for participant agency. The research program evaluates whether different governance structures produce different outcomes on the relevant metrics.

## Ethical Architecture

This section establishes the non-negotiable constraints within which all research activity in the framework operates. These constraints are not subject to modification by facility operators, research contractors, or program administrators. Deviations from them are treated as disqualifying for individual researchers, contracting institutions, and participating facilities, not as findings to be assessed on a case-by-case basis.

### Voluntary Participation as an Absolute Requirement

Participation in any research activity — including data collection, environmental observation, program evaluation, and any form of physiological or behavioral measurement — requires genuine voluntary informed consent from the participant.

Genuine voluntary consent in a correctional setting requires specific institutional architecture. Declining to participate may not affect parole decisions, classification level, housing assignment, disciplinary status, program eligibility, commissary access, or any other condition of confinement. The institutional consequences of non-participation must be verifiably zero, and this must be verifiable by the participant through an independent channel — not merely asserted by the facility. Where the independence of that channel cannot be established, participation in research is not permitted.

Consent must be informed. Participants must understand what they are consenting to, what data will be collected, how it will be used, who will have access to it, what its limitations are, and what the relationship is between participation and any benefit they may be told they will receive. The language and literacy level of consent processes must be appropriate to the participant population. Consent cannot be obtained through a form that is inaccessible to individuals with limited literacy.

Consent may be withdrawn at any time and for any reason without adverse consequence. The withdrawal of consent applies to future data collection and to any use of previously collected data that requires ongoing consent. Participants who withdraw are not subject to any form of retaliation, pressure, or attempt to persuade them to reconsider.

### Independent Ethical Oversight

Research activities are reviewed by an independent Institutional Review Board with jurisdiction over all research within the framework. This IRB is constituted under federal human subjects research regulations applicable to research with prisoners, which impose heightened standards beyond those required for general human subjects research.

The independent IRB has the authority to halt any research activity it determines to present unacceptable risk, inadequate consent architecture, or violation of any provision of the ethical framework. Its authority cannot be overridden by facility operators, DoDa, or any administrative authority within the correctional system. Its decisions are final and are subject only to appeal through the federal research oversight process applicable to prisoner research.

Facility operators and research contractors may not self-certify compliance with the ethical framework. Every research activity requires independent IRB review regardless of its apparent scope, novelty, or resemblance to previously approved activities.

### Data Protection Architecture

Individual-level data generated through research activities is de-identified before any use outside the research protocol for which it was collected. De-identification meets the federal standard for de-identified research data, which requires that the data cannot reasonably be used to identify the individual without additional information that is not available to the researcher.

Research data may not be used for any law enforcement purpose, including investigation of past crimes, identification of criminal associates, prosecution of current or future offenses, parole decision-making, classification decisions, or disciplinary proceedings. This prohibition applies regardless of whether the data would otherwise be relevant to any of these purposes and regardless of any assertion by law enforcement that the data is necessary for a specific investigation.

Research data may not be used for commercial purposes, including sale to private entities, licensing for commercial product development, or inclusion in commercial databases. Data generated through research with incarcerated populations is a public research asset subject to public research data standards.

Access to individual-level research data is restricted to the research team for the specific protocol under which the data was collected, the independent IRB, and oversight bodies exercising their statutory review authority. Access is audit-logged through the IGL infrastructure. Unauthorized access is treated as a research misconduct violation and as a data protection violation subject to applicable legal consequences.

Data retention is time-limited. Individual-level data is retained only for the period required to complete the research protocol and the required audit of that protocol. Aggregate, de-identified findings are retained as part of the permanent research record. Individual-level data is destroyed on a defined schedule that is disclosed to participants as part of the consent process.

### Prohibited Research Categories

Certain research activities are prohibited regardless of the strength of the scientific rationale or the quality of the consent architecture. These categories are defined by the potential for harm to participants or to the institution's integrity that no benefit could justify.

Research that requires the administration of any substance — pharmaceutical, nutritional, or other — beyond what is clinically indicated for the participant's documented health condition is prohibited. Nutritional research, for example, must be based on adjustments to standard facility food service rather than administration of isolated supplements under research protocols, unless the participant's independent clinical provider prescribes the supplement for clinical reasons.

Research that involves any form of sensory deprivation, physical discomfort, or psychological stress induction as an experimental variable is prohibited.

Research that requires deception of participants is prohibited. Participants must know they are in a research study, what that study involves, and what data is being collected about them.

Research funded primarily by entities with a commercial interest in the research findings is prohibited without independent scientific review of potential bias and independent publication rights for all researchers involved.

### Transparency Requirements

Research protocols, methodologies, interim findings, and final outcomes are published in accessible formats. The transparency requirement is symmetric: negative findings — interventions that did not work, environmental changes that produced no measurable effect — are published with the same prominence and accessibility as positive findings. A research program that publishes only positive results is not a research program. It is a marketing program. The framework's credibility depends on its willingness to report failures honestly, because an evidence base composed only of successes is not trustworthy and should not be treated as such.

## Cross-Domain Translation — From Facilities to Communities

The most consequential secondary objective of the Correctional Research and Environmental Design program is identifying which correctional environmental interventions produce effects that are likely to replicate outside correctional settings — in housing, in schools, in blighted neighborhoods, in workforce stabilization sites, and in other environments where similar conditions of chronic stress, social disruption, and reduced exposure to natural and restorative elements are producing similar outcomes.

This is not automatic. An intervention that produces effects in a correctional setting does not necessarily produce effects in a radically different civilian setting. Translation requires separate validation. Findings from correctional research are treated as hypotheses about civilian applications, not as conclusions.

But the directional hypotheses are strong, and the Community Stabilization Framework has an explicit interest in them. The community stabilization literature establishes that lighting, green space, and activated public space consistently reduce crime and fear of crime in distressed urban neighborhoods. The mechanism proposed is the same mechanism operating in correctional facilities: these environmental conditions shape social behavior through well-documented physiological and psychological pathways. The correctional research program tests those pathways under conditions more rigorous than are generally achievable in community-level studies, because the comparison between treated and untreated populations is cleaner and the outcome measurement is more comprehensive.

Findings that circadian-supportive lighting reduces violence and improves sleep quality within correctional facilities become evidence for similar interventions in blighted neighborhoods where chronic sleep disruption and elevated stress are documented and where streetlighting is among the most cost-effective community safety interventions available. Findings that green space access reduces aggressive incidents within correctional facilities become evidence for the vacant lot remediation and green infrastructure investments the Community Stabilization Framework identifies as high-priority. Findings that acoustic noise reduction improves learning outcomes within correctional educational programs become evidence for acoustic management in schools serving high-noise environments.

The correctional facility is, in this respect, functioning as a research environment for questions that are not primarily about corrections. It is generating evidence about the relationship between environmental conditions and human behavior that has application across the full range of environments the Futures Project is designing. The fact that this evidence is generated in a correctional setting does not limit its applicability. It is generated there because correctional settings happen to offer a research environment with unusual methodological advantages. The findings are about humans and environments, not about prisoners and prisons.

The Healthcare-Enabled Housing Pilot and the Workforce Capable Stabilization Pilots are secondary translation environments. Residents of supportive housing and stabilization housing share many of the environmental conditions that correctional research is investigating — high-density shared living, limited access to natural elements, elevated ambient noise, disrupted sleep schedules. Environmental interventions that demonstrate effects in correctional settings are candidates for validation in these settings, which provides a staged translation pathway from controlled correctional research to broader community application.

## Evidence Generation and Scaling Protocol

All interventions in the framework follow a standard staged protocol.

Pilot deployment occurs in a limited number of facilities with defined start conditions, control conditions, and outcome measurement infrastructure established before the intervention begins. Pilot scale is sufficient to detect meaningful effect sizes on the primary outcome measures with statistical confidence, but limited enough that a failed intervention affects a small fraction of the overall population.

Independent measurement occurs throughout the pilot period and for a defined follow-up period after its conclusion. Measurement uses the DoDa data infrastructure for outcome tracking and is conducted by parties with no institutional interest in the findings. Measurement is pre-specified: the outcome measures, the analysis methods, and the decision rules for scaling are defined before the pilot begins and are published as part of the protocol. Post-hoc reanalysis to find favorable results is not permitted.

Independent audit reviews the measurement against the pre-specified protocol, assesses the quality of the research design, identifies limitations and potential confounds, and produces an audit report that is published alongside the measurement findings.

Replication requires that promising findings be replicated in at least two additional facilities before large-scale adoption is considered. Replication serves two functions. It distinguishes real effects from noise in a single-site study, and it tests whether findings generalize across different facility contexts, populations, and implementation conditions. A finding that replicates in facilities with different populations, different base conditions, and different implementation teams is a much stronger basis for scaling than a finding from a single flagship site.

Scaling occurs only when pilot and replication findings are positive, the effect sizes are meaningful at policy-relevant outcome measures, and the independent audit has identified no disqualifying design or implementation failures. Scaling is funded through the evidence-based allocation mechanism that governs Competitive Rehabilitation performance rewards: facilities that implement validated interventions earn performance credit for the outcome improvements the interventions produce.

Failures are documented and published. Programs that do not improve outcomes are sunsetted following the defined evaluation period. Resources are reallocated to higher-performing alternatives. The correctional system learns from this failure the same way any well-functioning learning system learns from failure: by treating negative results as information rather than embarrassment.

## Connection to Competitive Rehabilitation Performance Framework

The Correctional Research and Environmental Design program is the evidence engine of the Competitive Rehabilitation Framework.

The Competitive Rehabilitation Framework establishes that institutions compete on rehabilitation outcomes. The Research and Environmental Design Framework determines which institutional practices, physical conditions, and program designs actually produce those outcomes. The two are inseparable: a competitive rehabilitation market without reliable evidence about what works is a market that rewards luck rather than genuine effectiveness.

DoDa's performance measurement infrastructure tracks facility-level outcomes against the interventions each facility has implemented. This tracking serves two functions. For facility-level accountability, it connects performance ratings to specific practice choices, which allows the competitive framework to reward not just good outcomes but the practices that produce them — creating a learning loop in which effective practices spread through the competitive incentive structure rather than through administrative mandate. For system-level learning, it aggregates evidence across facilities and over time, identifying patterns that no single facility evaluation can reveal and building an evidence base that improves the quality of every subsequent research investment.

The Immutable Government Ledger records all research activity, all outcome data, and all audit findings in a permanent, tamper-proof record. This record serves the research program's transparency requirement and the competitive framework's accountability requirement simultaneously. There is no mechanism through which facility operators can revise, suppress, or otherwise alter the historical record of what they did and what happened.

## Meta Insight

American correctional policy has asked, generation after generation: what should we do to people who break the law?

The environmental design tradition asks a different question: under what conditions do people change?

The second question is not softer than the first. In many ways it is harder, because it requires honest confrontation with the reality that conditions shape behavior, that the conditions most correctional environments create are conditions hostile to behavioral change, and that the public interest in reducing future crime is therefore served by changing the conditions rather than by intensifying the coercive elements of the environment that has already failed to produce the desired outcome.

The research program does not assume that environmental improvement is sufficient for rehabilitation. It assumes that environmental conditions are a real variable with real effects on real outcomes, that those effects have been almost entirely ignored in the design of American correctional facilities, and that ignoring them has costs — in violence, in learning disruption, in treatment failure, in wasted program investment, in future recidivism — that the public bears without knowing why.

The purpose of this framework is to know why. And to use that knowledge to build correctional environments that actually work, and then to take what works in those environments and apply it where the same conditions are producing the same harm outside them.

That translation — from correctional facility to blighted neighborhood, from prison to school, from controlled institutional setting to the uncontrolled world where most lives are actually lived — is the long game this framework is playing. The facility is the laboratory. The community is the destination.

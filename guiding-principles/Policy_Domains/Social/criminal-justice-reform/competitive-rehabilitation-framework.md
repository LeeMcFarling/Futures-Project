# Competitive Rehabilitation Framework

## Executive Summary

The United States correctional system is organized around custody. The incentive structure governing most correctional institutions rewards occupancy, compliance management, and cost containment. It does not reward successful reintegration, because successful reintegration ends the revenue stream.

This framework establishes a different model.

Under Competitive Rehabilitation, confinement remains a public safety function. Custody is necessary for individuals who present a genuine threat, and this framework does not dispute that. What it disputes is the assumption that the custodial period should be structured primarily around containment rather than transformation. The two functions are not in conflict. A facility can be secure and simultaneously oriented toward producing human beings who, upon release, are healthier, more capable, and less likely to commit future crimes.

The mechanism is competition. Correctional institutions compete on independently verified rehabilitation outcomes rather than on occupancy rates. Custody itself operates on a break-even basis. No institution profits from keeping people confined longer. Institutions that demonstrate measurable improvement in educational attainment, treatment completion, credential acquisition, employment readiness, housing stability at reentry, and long-term reductions in recidivism earn recognition, expansion authority, and performance-based compensation. Institutions that fail to move those metrics do not.

The purpose of this framework is not to make incarceration more comfortable. The purpose is to make incarceration more effective. The standard by which effectiveness is judged is not institutional order. It is whether the person who walks out the door is more capable of staying out than the person who walked in.

Most incarcerated individuals will eventually be released. Public safety is best served when those individuals leave prison able to work, able to maintain housing, connected to treatment where needed, and oriented toward a life that does not require them to return. Every other outcome is a failure the public eventually pays for.

---

## The Problem with the Current Incentive Structure

American corrections does not fail because the people working within it are uniformly indifferent to rehabilitation. It fails in part because the system's incentive architecture does not reward rehabilitation outcomes even when practitioners pursue them.

Private correctional operators, where they exist, generate revenue from occupied beds. The longer individuals remain incarcerated, and the more individuals are incarcerated, the stronger the financial position of the operator. This is not a conspiracy. It is a rational response to the incentive structure as designed. The design is wrong.

Public correctional systems are not immune to the same distortion. Budget allocations in public corrections are typically driven by population counts. A facility that successfully reduces recidivism among its population shrinks the future justification for its own budget. Institutional self-interest and genuine public interest are structurally misaligned.

The result is a system that is very good at confinement and systematically underinvested in everything that determines what happens after confinement ends. Education, treatment, credential acquisition, mental health stabilization, housing coordination, reentry planning — these are functions that correctional institutions are authorized to perform but rarely financially rewarded for performing well. In many facilities, they are afterthoughts organized around volunteer programs and grant-funded initiatives rather than core institutional functions with dedicated resources and outcome accountability.

Competitive Rehabilitation reconfigures the incentive structure. Custody is funded as a public safety function, break-even, without financial upside from prolonged confinement. Rehabilitation outcomes become the primary performance objective, measured independently, verified against long-term reintegration data, and linked directly to institutional compensation and expansion authority. The system still requires secure facilities. It no longer rewards them for filling beds.

## Core Principle

No institution should profit from confinement. Institutions may only benefit from demonstrated rehabilitation.

This is not a statement about the moral status of incarceration. It is a statement about incentive design. A correctional system organized around occupancy produces occupancy. A correctional system organized around rehabilitation outcomes and funded accordingly produces something different. The evidence from Norway, Finland, the Netherlands, and other countries that have made this transition is available, and it is consistent: systems oriented toward rehabilitation produce lower recidivism, lower long-term costs, and fewer future victims.

The current debate in American correctional policy frequently assumes a binary choice between punishment and rehabilitation. This framework rejects that framing. Secure confinement remains necessary. Accountability to victims and to the rule of law remains central. The question is what happens during the period of confinement, and whether that period is used to increase or decrease the probability of future harm.

A correctional system that releases individuals with untreated addiction, untreated trauma, no employment history, no credentials, and no housing lined up is not protecting the public. It is deferring the cost of its own failure onto future victims, future communities, and future emergency systems. Competitive Rehabilitation treats that deferred cost as a present accounting problem and a present design problem.

## Strategic Premise

Most incarcerated individuals will eventually be released.

This is not a liberal premise. It is a numerical fact. Approximately 95 percent of people currently incarcerated in American prisons will at some point return to the communities from which they came. The correctional system therefore functions not only as a custody institution but as one of the largest human capability environments in society, whether or not it acknowledges that function.

Every individual passing through that system is moving toward one of two outcomes. They will leave with greater capability and a higher probability of successful reintegration. Or they will leave with greater dysfunction and a higher probability of future system involvement. The period of confinement does not leave outcomes unchanged. It shapes them. The question is whether the shaping is intentional and oriented toward the public interest, or incidental and oriented toward institutional convenience.

The evidence on what produces better reintegration outcomes is not ambiguous. Educational attainment during incarceration consistently reduces recidivism. Treatment for substance use and mental health conditions consistently reduces recidivism. Employment upon release consistently reduces recidivism. Housing stability upon release consistently reduces recidivism. These are not contested findings. They are the accumulated product of decades of correctional research. The gap between what the evidence supports and what most American facilities actually do is the gap this framework is designed to close.

## System Architecture

### Pillar 1 — Break-Even Custody

Confinement is a public safety function, not a profit center.

Correctional institutions, whether publicly or privately operated, may recover operational costs. They may not generate financial returns from incarceration volume or duration. This applies to private operators through contract structure and to public facilities through budget architecture. Neither the per-diem rate nor the appropriation model creates financial incentives for prolonged confinement.

This pillar does not eliminate private operation of correctional facilities. It eliminates the financial incentive for private operators to maximize custody duration or resist early release. A private operator under a break-even custody model competes by demonstrating better rehabilitation outcomes, not by lobbying against sentence reduction reforms that would reduce their population.

The separation of custody funding from rehabilitation funding is the foundational design choice. Custody is the floor. Rehabilitation is where the competitive returns are.

### Pillar 2 — Competitive Rehabilitation Incentives

Correctional institutions compete on measured rehabilitation outcomes rather than occupancy.

Performance metrics are defined independently, measured longitudinally, and linked to institutional compensation and expansion authority. Metrics include institutional violence reduction, treatment completion rates, educational attainment, credential acquisition under the National Competency Framework, workforce readiness assessments, employment outcomes at six and twelve months post-release, housing stability at twelve months post-release, and recidivism rates at one, three, and five years.

Institutions that demonstrate consistent improvement in these metrics across their population earn priority access to capital investment, expansion authority for new facility development or contract renewal, and performance-based compensation pools funded through the public savings generated by reduced recidivism. Institutions that fail to improve on these metrics are placed on improvement plans with independent oversight, and institutions that persistently underperform lose expansion authority and, in the case of private operators, contract renewal eligibility.

The mechanism is not primarily punitive toward poorly performing institutions. It is designed to create a genuine competitive dynamic in which demonstrating effective rehabilitation is the pathway to institutional growth. The same market logic that private correctional operators have used to argue for efficiency can be redirected toward the outcomes that actually matter.

### Pillar 3 — Capability Development as Core Function

Education, treatment, work experience, and credential acquisition are not supplementary services. They are primary institutional functions.

Capability development programming — described in detail in the Correctional Capability Development and Reentry Capital brief — is structured around the same competency framework that governs education and workforce development in the broader Futures Project. The National Competency Framework does not stop at the facility door. A person in a correctional facility pursuing a credential in welding, electrical work, culinary arts, digital systems, or healthcare support is pursuing the same verifiable competency record that a person in a community college or apprenticeship program is pursuing. The credential is issued through the same SSI-based infrastructure, is portable upon release, and is readable by employers and workforce systems without the prison origin being disclosed to parties with no legitimate need to know it.

This integration is structural rather than symbolic. Participants in correctional capability programs who achieve credential milestones are accumulating a professional record that transitions with them into the workforce stabilization and workforce coordination systems upon release. The Workforce Coordination Authority and Workforce Access Points that serve broader workforce populations are the same institutions that provide reentry pathway connectivity. Correctional capability development is not a separate pipeline. It is an on-ramp into the same system.

### Pillar 4 — Evidence and Continuous Improvement

Correctional systems should function as learning systems.

The Correctional Research and Environmental Design Framework provides the evidence engine. Programs, physical environments, treatment models, educational interventions, and operational practices are piloted, measured against defined outcomes, audited independently, and scaled when they demonstrate improvement. Practices that fail to move outcomes are revised or discontinued. This is the same continuous improvement logic that governs other public infrastructure investments under the Futures Project architecture — evidence gates expansion, and performance determines allocation.

The evidence generated within correctional facilities also has cross-domain value. Environmental interventions that improve outcomes in correctional settings — lighting design, green space access, acoustic management, sleep-supportive environments — are candidates for testing in supportive housing, workforce stabilization sites, schools, and other public environments. The facility is a research environment as well as a custodial one, governed by strict ethical constraints that are defined in the Research and Environmental Design brief.

### Pillar 5 — Reintegration as a Public Safety Objective

Correctional responsibility does not end at release.

Institutions are evaluated in part on post-release outcomes, which creates an institutional incentive to invest in reentry planning that begins at intake rather than at the gate. Housing arrangements, employment connections, treatment continuity, benefit activation, and community support networks are developed throughout the period of incarceration through coordination with the housing stabilization, workforce, and healthcare systems that operate across the broader Futures Project.

The warm handoff — the structured transition from correctional facility to workforce, housing, and healthcare systems — is a performance metric, not an aspiration. Facilities that achieve high warm-handoff completion rates with demonstrable downstream stability outcomes are rewarded. Facilities that release individuals into homelessness and unemployment without coordination are penalized.

## Cross-Domain Connections

Competitive Rehabilitation does not operate in isolation. Its effectiveness depends on the availability and functioning of connected systems across multiple domains.

**Workforce.** The Workforce Coordination Authority, Workforce Access Points, and Workforce Training and Education Alignment system collectively provide the post-release employment infrastructure that correctional capability development feeds into. Reentry workers entering this system through a warm handoff are not navigating an unfamiliar process. They are entering the same network of training pipelines, employer partnerships, and placement pathways available to any worker seeking to reenter the labor market after a disruption. The distinction between a worker emerging from a layoff and a worker emerging from incarceration is, within the workforce system, primarily a matter of which disruption occurred — not a separate category of person requiring separate infrastructure.

**Credentialing.** The Credential Portability and Competency Recognition Framework governs how credentials earned during incarceration translate into recognized occupational standing upon release. Verifiable occupational credentials stored in the individual's SSI wallet during incarceration become active workforce credentials upon release. Employers verifying credentials through the SSI infrastructure receive the same cryptographic confirmation of competency they would receive for any other worker's credential, without the facility of origin being disclosed as part of the standard verification. This design choice is deliberate: the credential should carry the weight of demonstrated competency, not the stigma of where it was demonstrated.

**Housing.** The Workforce Capable Stabilization Pilots and the Healthcare-Enabled Housing Pilot provide the reentry housing infrastructure. Individuals leaving correctional facilities whose primary barrier to stability is situational — the disruption of incarceration itself — are routed into workforce stabilization housing with time-bounded support and employment linkage. Individuals with higher clinical needs, particularly those with co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders, are routed into the healthcare-enabled housing infrastructure, which provides embedded clinical services alongside stable housing. The correctional facility's reentry planning process initiates these routing decisions well before release so housing is confirmed rather than searched for at the gate.

**Agriculture and Food Systems.** Correctional facilities represent a significant and stable institutional food purchasing base. Routing that purchasing through the regional processing infrastructure established by the Agriculture Supply Chain framework creates anchor demand that de-risks investment in regional capacity. Culinary and agricultural training programs within correctional facilities connect participants to regional food systems employment pathways. Where correctional capability programs include food production — institutional gardens, managed agricultural operations — those programs supply the same regional distribution networks, reinforcing the demand signal aggregation that the Agricultural Data and Market Signals framework identifies as a precondition for regional food system resilience.

**Research Translation.** Findings from the Correctional Research and Environmental Design program that demonstrate environmental interventions with measurable effects on human behavior and wellbeing become candidates for application in blighted neighborhoods through the Community Stabilization Framework. The same lighting conditions, green space interventions, and acoustic environments that demonstrably improve safety and reduce violence within correctional facilities are the same interventions the community stabilization literature identifies as effective in distressed urban environments. The evidence base built within controlled correctional settings is the highest-quality evidence available for scaling those interventions into the broader built environment.

## Relationship to Victims

Victims remain central stakeholders within the justice system.

This framework does not argue that the interests of victims are served by reduced accountability, shorter sentences, or lighter consequences for criminal behavior. It argues that the interests of victims include a significant interest in not being victimized again, and that interest is best served by a correctional system that actually reduces recidivism rather than one that maintains an appearance of severity while producing high rates of return.

The victim-centered argument for Competitive Rehabilitation is straightforward. A person who leaves prison healthier, employed, housed, and treated is less likely to produce new victims. A person who leaves prison sicker, unemployed, homeless, and untreated is more likely to produce new victims. The question of which correctional model better serves victims is not philosophically complex. It is an empirical question with a consistent answer.

Competitive Rehabilitation is therefore not in tension with victim-centered values. It is an extension of them.

## Governance Principles

The framework operates under five core governance requirements.

Rehabilitation outcomes must be independently audited. Institutions do not self-certify their outcomes. Outcome measurement is conducted through the DoDa infrastructure with independent verification against longitudinal reintegration data from workforce, housing, and healthcare systems that have no institutional interest in any particular correctional facility's performance rating.

Compensation mechanisms must be transparent. Performance-based compensation pools, expansion authority decisions, and institutional ratings are publicly reported through the DoDa accountability layer. The public can observe whether institutions are improving, whether rewards are flowing to performing facilities, and whether the system's output is changing over time.

Participation rights and civil liberties remain protected. No intervention within correctional facilities is conducted without informed consent where consent is required. Participation in capability development programming is voluntary except where individualized rehabilitation plans establish it as a component of a negotiated release pathway. The Research and Environmental Design brief establishes the ethical governance framework for all research activity within facilities, including strict protections against coercive participation and against the use of research participation in any classification or disciplinary decision.

Institutions may not manipulate classification systems to improve reported outcomes. Selection of lower-risk populations for flagship programs, reclassification of failures as transfers, or other techniques to improve reported metrics without improving actual outcomes are monitored through DoDa and treated as governance failures subject to penalty.

Expansion occurs only after demonstrated performance. No program, facility model, or intervention scales nationally without validated evidence from pilot-phase implementation. The evidence gate is not a bureaucratic delay. It is the mechanism that prevents scaling failed approaches while identifying and amplifying approaches that genuinely work.

## Political and Fiscal Architecture

The political design of this framework is deliberate.

The competitive structure converts rehabilitation from a public expenditure into a performance market. Institutions earn returns through demonstrated outcomes. The language of competition, performance measurement, and outcome-based compensation is the language of the private sector applied to a public safety function. That framing is not incidental. It is designed to make the framework legible to audiences who are skeptical of government programs and sympathetic to market mechanisms.

The fiscal case is available and should be made explicitly. Incarceration in the United States costs, on average, more than thirty-five thousand dollars per person per year, and substantially more in high-cost states. A single successful reintegration — one individual who leaves prison and does not return — saves the system the full cost of a future incarceration spell, which averages several years, plus the costs of investigation, prosecution, and incarceration of any crime that would have accompanied that return. Rehabilitation investments that produce meaningful reductions in recidivism generate fiscal returns that exceed their cost within a planning horizon that any serious budgetary framework can evaluate.

The Public Capital Authority instruments that fund infrastructure across the Futures Project are applicable here. PCA consortium financing for Rehabilitation Capability Center construction, energy efficiency retrofits of existing facilities, and technology infrastructure for credential delivery and outcome measurement can be structured as returns-based instruments that pay down through the documented fiscal savings of reduced recidivism. The financial architecture of the framework does not require treating rehabilitation as a cost. It treats it as an investment with a measurable return.

## Meta Insight

Correctional policy in the United States has been framed for decades around a single question: how severely should society punish crime?

This framework begins from a different question: who will this person be when they leave, and what does the answer mean for everyone who lives near them?

For individuals serving life sentences without the possibility of release, the custodial question dominates. For the vast majority of incarcerated individuals — those who will eventually return to their communities — the second question determines future victimization rates, future public expenditure, and the long-term safety of the communities they return to.

Competitive Rehabilitation aligns institutional incentives with the answer society actually wants.

A correctional system should not be judged solely by how securely it confines people. It should be judged by how reliably it produces people who do not need to be confined again.

# Non-Violent Crime — Rehabilitation Framework Overview

---

## Purpose of This Document

The non-violent crime rehabilitation domain is large enough and complex enough to require a set of dedicated briefs rather than a single policy document. This overview explains the strategic architecture — the problem, the model, the principles, and the sequencing — that the subsequent briefs will implement. It also maps the sub-brief structure so that the domain is legible as a coherent whole before the components are written in full.

The throughline across this domain is a single reorientation: for non-violent offenses, the purpose of the justice system is to reduce future harm, and the evidence is unambiguous that rehabilitation achieves this goal better than punishment. The question is not whether this is true. The question is how to build the institutions that make it real.

---

## The Problem with the Current Model

The United States incarcerates more people per capita than any other country in the developed world, and a substantial portion of that incarcerated population is serving sentences for non-violent offenses. This is not a natural outcome of American crime rates. American crime rates are not dramatically higher than those of peer countries. American incarceration rates are dramatically higher. The difference is policy: sentencing structures, mandatory minimums, prosecutorial incentives, and a cultural and political framework that has treated incarceration as the appropriate response to a wide range of behaviors for which other countries have developed more effective alternatives.

The outcomes of this model are well-documented. Recidivism rates in the United States are among the highest in the developed world. People who exit incarceration face a set of formal and informal barriers — to employment, to housing, to public benefits, to professional licensing, to civic participation — that are themselves predictors of reincarceration. The system releases people into conditions designed to fail them and then counts their failure as evidence that they were not rehabilitated. It is possible to build a system that produces different outcomes. Norway has done it. So have Finland, the Netherlands, and other countries that made a policy decision to treat non-violent offending as a problem with a solution rather than a transgression that requires suffering.

The suffering the American system inflicts is real. So is its failure to reduce the harm it is ostensibly designed to prevent. These two failures are connected. Incarceration that is organized around deprivation and humiliation does not produce people who are better equipped to function in society. It produces people who have been further destabilized, further disconnected from legitimate opportunity, and further marked as unfit for it. The recidivism data are the outcome of a system doing what its design produces.

The non-violent rehabilitation framework changes the design.

---

## The Model: Rehabilitation as the Primary Goal

The model this domain implements draws primarily from the Norwegian approach, piloted for youth in the violence interruption framework and extended here to adult non-violent offenders through a state opt-in structure. The Norwegian model is not borrowed wholesale without adaptation — American legal structures, federalism, and institutional contexts differ enough to require genuine translation rather than simple transplant. But the core principles are adopted because they are supported by the evidence, not because they are foreign or fashionable.

The core principles, stated plainly:

**Deprivation of liberty is the punishment.** The loss of freedom — the inability to move, to choose, to maintain the relationships and roles that constitute a life — is itself a profound sanction. It does not require additional degradation to constitute punishment. Conditions of confinement that add humiliation, danger, and further destabilization on top of the deprivation of liberty do not make the punishment more just. They make it less effective and more harmful.

**Rehabilitation is not what happens after punishment. It is the goal.** Every element of a correctional facility's design and programming — the physical environment, the daily schedule, the work available, the education offered, the relationships between staff and residents — should be oriented toward the question: what does this person need to be able to live a stable, law-abiding life upon release? This is the organizing principle. Everything else follows from it.

**Normality is preparation.** People who have never experienced stable housing, productive employment, ordinary social relationships, and the management of their own time are not prepared for those things upon release. Exposure to them during incarceration is not a reward or a luxury. It is preparation for the life the person is expected to maintain afterward. Facilities designed around the principle of normality — private spaces, communal life, real work, real education, real relationships with staff — produce better outcomes because they are actually preparing people for something.

**Reentry begins on day one.** Release planning that starts the week before release is not release planning. By that point, the window for meaningful preparation has passed. Housing arrangements, employment connections, support networks, benefit access, and community integration begin at intake and are developed throughout the period of incarceration so that the individual exits into a situation rather than into a void.

**Staff are practitioners, not guards.** The relationship between staff and residents is the central therapeutic mechanism of the Norwegian model, and it requires staff who are recruited, trained, and compensated as professionals rather than as custodians. Staff who build genuine relationships with the people in their facilities — who know their circumstances, understand their goals, and actively support their development — produce safer facilities and better outcomes than staff whose primary role is containment and control.

## Why State Opt-In Is the Right Structure

Federal criminal justice authority is constitutionally limited, and the variation in state legal systems, existing correctional infrastructure, and political conditions is large enough that a uniform national mandate would be both legally difficult and strategically counterproductive. The opt-in structure does several things that a mandate cannot.

It allows states that are ready to move quickly to do so without waiting for a national consensus that may never arrive. It creates competitive demonstration effects — when states that implement the model show better outcomes and lower costs than states that do not, the political case for adoption in holdout states becomes evidence-based rather than ideological. And it builds the institutional knowledge and practitioner capacity that national scaling will eventually require, in jurisdictions that have chosen to develop it rather than having it imposed on them.

The opt-in is not unconditional. States that choose to participate commit to the core principles, to the facility design standards, to the staff training and compensation requirements, and to the outcome measurement framework that allows DoDa to track whether the model is being implemented or merely nominally adopted. Participation is binary: the model, or not. A state that wants federal support for a program that calls itself rehabilitative but organizes around the principles of the current system is not participating in this framework.

## Sub-Brief Architecture

The non-violent rehabilitation domain requires detailed treatment across five sub-areas. This section maps what each brief addresses and how they connect.

### Sub-Brief 1: Sentencing Philosophy and Reform

The entry point of the rehabilitation system is sentencing: what outcomes judges are authorized to impose, what the governing logic of those outcomes is, and how the current structure of mandatory minimums, prosecutorial charging decisions, and sentencing guidelines has produced outcomes at variance with the stated goals of the system.

This brief addresses the philosophical and structural case for moving from punitive sentencing to rehabilitative sentencing for non-violent offenses. It covers the scope of mandatory minimum reform — which offenses, at what thresholds, with what replacement framework — and the incentive structures that currently push prosecutors toward maximum charging and judges toward maximum sentencing regardless of individual circumstances. It does not address sentencing for violent offenses, which is beyond the scope of this domain.

### Sub-Brief 2: Facility Design and the Rehabilitative Environment

The physical environment of a correctional facility is not incidental to its outcomes. It is one of the primary determinants of them. This brief addresses what rehabilitative facility design means in practice: the physical standards for private space, communal facilities, educational and vocational infrastructure, outdoor access, and the design choices that support normality rather than degradation.

It also addresses the staffing model — the professional framework, training requirements, compensation benchmarks, and ongoing development infrastructure that produces staff capable of the relationship-based work the model requires. And it addresses the transition pathway: what happens to existing correctional facilities, how the shift from custodial to rehabilitative design is phased, and what the capital and operational investment requirements look like.

### Sub-Brief 3: Education, Vocation, and Daily Life

A facility that is designed around rehabilitation but offers nothing to do during the day has not implemented the model. This brief addresses the substantive programming that fills the time that is not spent in structured work or rest: education connected to the national competency framework, vocational training connected to real labor market pathways, and the daily life programming — arts, athletics, community responsibility, peer support — that develops the capacities that employment and housing stability require.

The connection to the broader Futures Project education and workforce systems is explicit here. The learning spine does not stop at the facility door. A person in a rehabilitative facility has access to the same competency-based education infrastructure as a person in any other setting, earning credentials that are portable, verified, and meaningful to employers upon release.

### Sub-Brief 4: Reentry Infrastructure and Community Integration

The highest-risk period for reincarceration is the first 90 days following release. The primary predictors of reincarceration are housing instability, unemployment, and the absence of a support network. All three of these are addressable — not by the correctional system alone, but by the correctional system in active coordination with the housing, workforce, and healthcare stabilization systems that operate across the Futures Project.

This brief addresses the reentry pipeline: how housing stabilization intake is initiated before release, how workforce stabilization pathways are connected during the period of incarceration, how healthcare continuity — particularly for individuals with substance use and mental health needs — is maintained across the transition, and what the coordination mechanisms between the correctional system and the broader stabilization system look like in practice.

### Sub-Brief 5: Prosecutor Incentives and System Accountability

The best-designed rehabilitation system in the world will produce limited results if the incentive structure of the upstream system — prosecutorial charging decisions, plea bargaining, the organizational cultures of district attorney offices — continues to push toward maximum incarceration for non-violent offenses. This brief addresses the structural incentives that drive prosecutorial behavior, the accountability mechanisms that allow communities to understand and evaluate those decisions, and the reforms — prosecutorial performance metrics, charging transparency, plea bargain disclosure — that align prosecutor incentives with the rehabilitative goals of the system they feed.

It also addresses the measurement infrastructure that allows the system as a whole to be evaluated honestly: what recidivism means and how to measure it, what cost comparisons between rehabilitation and incarceration look like when they account for downstream social costs, and how DoDa's outcome tracking function provides the data that makes accountability real rather than nominal.

## Sequencing and Phasing

The five sub-briefs address components that are interdependent, and the sequencing of implementation reflects those interdependencies.

Sentencing reform and facility redesign are parallel tracks, not sequential ones. You cannot implement rehabilitative sentencing without rehabilitative facilities to send people to, and you cannot build rehabilitative facilities without the sentencing framework that determines who is in them and for how long. These two sub-briefs are written and implemented together.

Reentry infrastructure is developed in parallel with facility design — because reentry begins on day one — but comes online in stages as the facility model is implemented and the population that will use the reentry pipeline develops.

Education and vocation programming is implemented within the facility model and scales with it. The competency infrastructure connection is operational from the beginning of facility implementation; the vocational pathways develop as the facility model matures.

Prosecutor incentives reform is both upstream and downstream. Upstream, it affects who enters the rehabilitative system and with what charges. Downstream, it is the accountability mechanism that prevents the front end of the system from overwhelming the back end with a population that exceeds the rehabilitative system's capacity. It is addressed last in the sub-brief sequence because it requires the full system to be visible before the specific incentive reforms can be calibrated against it, but it is not less important.

---

## The Long Game

The non-violent rehabilitation framework is not designed to produce results in the first year. It is designed to produce results over a decade — in recidivism rates, in the social and economic outcomes of people who have been through the system, in the long-term costs of incarceration compared to the costs of rehabilitation, and in the communities that receive people back from a system that actually prepared them to return.

The Norwegian results did not emerge from a single policy change. They emerged from a sustained, coherent commitment to a set of principles applied consistently across decades of institutional practice. The United States has not made that commitment. This framework begins it — carefully, through state opt-in, pilot-validated, evidence-gated in its expansion, and explicitly designed to build the institutional knowledge and public trust required for the long-term project.

The goal is a system that actually does what it says: takes in people who have broken the rules, and returns people who are equipped not to break them again. That outcome is achievable. The infrastructure to achieve it is what the sub-briefs in this domain define.

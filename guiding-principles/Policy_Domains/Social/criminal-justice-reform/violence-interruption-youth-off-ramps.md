# Violence Interruption and Youth Off-Ramps

---

## Executive Summary

Some individuals are at genuine, elevated, near-term risk of entering or continuing cycles of violence. This is not an abstraction. It is a specific population — often small within any given community — whose circumstances, relationships, and current trajectories make violence a near-term probability rather than a distant possibility. The question this framework answers is not whether to intervene, but how to do it in a way that actually works.

The answer has two parts that must function together.

The first is violence interruption: direct, relationship-based engagement with individuals at highest risk, by people from the same communities who have the credibility, trust, and personal experience to reach them. This is not social work at arm's length. It is conflict mediation, risk reduction, and human connection delivered by credible messengers who have been where these individuals are and can speak to it honestly.

The second is immediate off-ramps: real alternatives that exist at the moment intervention happens. Credible messenger work is undermined when the only thing an interruption worker can offer is conversation. The conversation matters. So does having somewhere to go, something to do, and a pathway that does not require navigating a system designed for people with more resources and stability than most at-risk individuals possess.

This framework also introduces, for youth involved in non-violent crime, a structured pilot of the Norwegian rehabilitation model — the approach that has produced the lowest recidivism rates in the developed world and that rests on principles the United States has never systematically applied. The pilot begins with youth because the stakes of early justice system contact are highest at that age, because the evidence for rehabilitation over punishment is strongest for young people, and because a successful youth pilot is the most credible path to broader adoption.

## Violence Interruption

### The Evidence Base

Violence interruption as a method has been studied extensively across multiple American cities. The Chicago CRED program, Cure Violence's work in multiple jurisdictions, and the Group Violence Intervention model have all demonstrated that direct, relationship-based engagement by credible messengers — people who have been involved in violence themselves and have the standing to speak to it honestly — can measurably reduce shootings and retaliatory violence in high-risk networks. The mechanism is not mysterious: credible messengers can access conversations, relationships, and moments of escalation that no formal institution can reach, because they are trusted in ways that formal institutions are not.

The evidence is sufficient to scale. It is not sufficient to replace with something cheaper or more administratively convenient. The effectiveness of this method depends on the quality and authenticity of the relationships, and that requires investing in the people doing the work — training, compensation, organizational support, and the time required to build the trust that makes the work possible.

### How It Works

Violence interruption operates through trained credible messenger outreach workers who are embedded in the communities where elevated-risk individuals live. They are not deployed as extensions of law enforcement. They do not share information with police. They are not informants. Their effectiveness depends entirely on the trust they build with the people they work with, and that trust depends on the confidentiality of those relationships being real and known to be real.

The work itself involves three things that are harder to describe than to recognize in practice.

**Conflict mediation** is direct engagement in moments of active or imminent conflict — typically disputes that carry a real risk of escalating to violence — with the goal of de-escalating the situation before it reaches that point. Credible messengers can enter these moments because they are trusted by the people involved. They do not arrive as authorities. They arrive as people who understand the stakes from personal experience and can speak to them without pretense.

**Risk reduction** is ongoing relationship-based engagement with individuals identified as being at elevated risk — either of perpetrating violence, of being victimized, or of escalating their involvement in circumstances that lead to violence. This is not case management. It is human connection, sustained over time, by someone who genuinely understands the environment the person is navigating and can help them see and access alternatives they may not currently believe are real.

**Connection to off-ramps** is the moment where interruption becomes something more than de-escalation. A credible messenger who can offer nothing but conversation is limited in what they can achieve. A credible messenger who can walk someone to a workforce pathway, a crisis response team, a housing stabilization intake, or a youth program that has something real to offer — that is a different level of intervention. The connection has to happen in the moment, which means the off-ramps have to exist and be accessible when the moment arrives.

### What Interruption Workers Need

The people doing this work carry significant personal and professional burden. They are frequently working in environments where they have personal history, personal risk, and personal relationships that complicate their professional role. They are often the first call when something goes wrong and the last people supported when it does. The programs that work are the ones that take this seriously: competitive compensation, professional training and ongoing development, clinical support for the secondary trauma that accumulates in this work, and organizational cultures that treat interruption workers as professionals rather than as instruments.

Federal support for violence interruption programs includes funding for training and certification standards, compensation benchmarks, organizational capacity building, and the data infrastructure that allows programs to demonstrate their effectiveness and improve continuously. DoDa tracking of violence interruption activity — not surveillance of individual contacts, but aggregate program-level outcomes — provides the evidence base for scaling what works and correcting what does not.

## Immediate Paid Pathways and Structured Engagement

Violence interruption reduces the immediate probability of violence. It does not, on its own, change the circumstances that made violence a rational option in the first place. Those circumstances — the absence of legitimate economic opportunity, the instability of housing and income, the lack of social infrastructure — are addressed by the broader Futures Project systems. But they take time to build, and the people at highest risk of violence do not have time to wait for them.

Immediate paid pathways exist to close this gap. They are not token work. They are real employment and structured engagement with real wages, designed to connect individuals at risk to legitimate income at the moment they are most likely to make choices that foreclose other options.

**Credible messenger employment itself** is the first pathway. Individuals with lived experience of violence and justice system contact are the most effective interruption workers, and expanding the workforce of trained, compensated credible messengers serves both the program and the individuals who join it. This is not a workaround. It is the model. The people with the highest credibility in the highest-need communities should be employed, trained, and supported to use that credibility in service of their communities.

**Immediate on-ramps to workforce stabilization tracks** connect individuals at risk to the broader workforce system — not through a waiting list, but through a direct referral pathway that interruption workers can activate in real time. The housing workforce stabilization pilots and the National Capability Tour provide the downstream infrastructure; this framework provides the immediate connection that gets people into that infrastructure before the moment passes.

**Structured transitional employment** for individuals who are not immediately ready for the broader workforce system provides income stability and daily structure during a period that would otherwise be defined by their absence. Programs are sited in communities, pay real wages, and are connected to the training and credentialing pathways that lead to longer-term employment.

## Youth Off-Ramps and the Norwegian Rehabilitation Pilot

Young people who come into contact with the justice system face a fork in the road that most of them do not know exists. One path is the American model: prosecution, adjudication, detention or supervision, and a record that follows them into adulthood. This path has been studied extensively. Its outcomes are poor. Juvenile justice contact — particularly detention — is associated with increased likelihood of adult criminal justice involvement, disrupted education, and long-term economic disadvantage. The system is not rehabilitating the young people who pass through it. In many cases, it is producing the trajectories it claims to interrupt.

The other path — the one that Norway and the other Nordic countries have been taking for decades — produces dramatically different outcomes. Norwegian recidivism rates are among the lowest in the developed world. This is not an accident and it is not a cultural artifact that cannot travel. It is the result of a specific, coherent philosophy applied consistently across a set of specific institutional practices. Those practices can be studied, adapted, and piloted. That is what this framework does.

### The Norwegian Model: Core Pillars

The Norwegian rehabilitation model rests on a set of principles that are coherent enough to be stated plainly and radical enough, by American standards, to require it.

**The principle of normality** holds that life inside a correctional facility should resemble life outside it as closely as security requirements permit. This is not a luxury or a reward. It is a design requirement. People who have never experienced normal daily life — stable housing, productive work, ordinary social relationships, the management of their own time — are not prepared to maintain it upon release. Exposure to normality is preparation for it. Norwegian facilities are designed to provide that exposure: private rooms, shared communal spaces, kitchens, workshops, educational programs, and the daily rhythms of a functioning household.

**The principle of humanity** holds that individuals in the justice system retain their human dignity regardless of what they have done, and that the deprivation of liberty is itself the punishment — not the conditions of confinement. This means that treatment by staff is respectful, not contemptuous. That daily life is not organized around humiliation or submission. That individuals are addressed as people rather than managed as risks.

**The principle of rehabilitation as the primary goal** means that every aspect of the facility's design and programming is oriented toward the question: what does this person need to be able to live a stable, law-abiding life? The answer varies by individual — some need education, some need vocational training, some need mental health treatment, some need housing support — but the orientation is consistent. The goal is not punishment, though the deprivation of liberty is real. The goal is the person's capacity to function successfully outside.

**The principle of dynamic security** replaces the American reliance on physical restraint and staff-as-guards with an understanding that safety comes from relationships. Staff are trained not as correctional officers whose primary role is containment but as professionals who build relationships with the people in their facilities, understand their circumstances, and actively support their development. The evidence from Norway is that this approach produces safer facilities, not less safe ones. Relationships with staff who are invested in your success are a stabilizing force. Contempt and dehumanization are destabilizing ones.

**The principle of community integration** treats reentry not as an afterthought that happens at the moment of release but as a process that begins on the first day of incarceration. Release plans, community connections, housing arrangements, employment pathways, and support networks are developed throughout the period of incarceration so that the individual exits into a situation rather than into a void.

### The Youth Pilot

This framework establishes a pilot of the Norwegian model specifically for youth — individuals under 18 — involved in non-violent crime. Youth is the right starting point for three reasons.

First, the evidence for rehabilitation over punishment is strongest for young people. Adolescent brain development is still underway. The capacity for change is real and documented. The cost of foreclosing that capacity through early incarceration is enormous and largely irreversible.

Second, the stakes of justice system contact are highest at this age. A juvenile record, time in detention, and the disruption of educational continuity all compound over decades. Intervening with a genuinely rehabilitative approach before these trajectories are set is the highest-return intervention available.

Third, a successful youth pilot is the most credible path to broader adoption. Americans who might resist the Norwegian model as applied to adults are more likely to support it for young people, because the case for redemption and second chances is more intuitive when the person in question has not yet had a first chance. The pilot is designed to generate the evidence that makes the broader conversation possible.

**What the pilot looks like in practice:**

Youth participants are individuals adjudicated for non-violent offenses who are diverted into the pilot program rather than processed through the standard juvenile justice system. Participation is not coercive — it is a genuine alternative that the young person and, for minors, their family can accept or decline.

Facilities are purpose-built or purpose-adapted to reflect the normality principle: small-scale residential environments, not institutional detention facilities. Private rooms. Communal kitchens and living spaces. Access to education, vocational training, arts, athletics, and the ordinary activities of adolescent life. Staff recruited and trained specifically for this model, at compensation levels that attract people capable of building the relationships the model requires.

Education is continuous and not interrupted. Participants work toward their educational credentials as a primary activity, supported by the competency-based infrastructure that the education reform builds out. The learning spine does not stop because someone is in a residential facility — it is one of the places where it can most clearly demonstrate its value.

Mental health and substance use support is embedded, not referred out. Many young people who come into contact with the justice system have mental health needs that have never been addressed. The pilot treats those needs as primary rather than secondary.

Community integration begins immediately. Participants maintain contact with family, community connections, and — where appropriate — restorative justice processes with those affected by their actions. Release planning begins on the first day. Exit is into a warm handoff to the workforce, housing, and healthcare stabilization systems, not into a void.

**Advisory structure:**

The United States does not currently have the institutional knowledge to implement this model without external expertise. That is an honest assessment, not a criticism. Norway has been doing this for decades, has studied it rigorously, and has practitioners who understand what works, what fails, and what the common implementation errors are. This pilot brings in Norwegian correctional advisors — practitioners and researchers from the Norwegian Correctional Service and associated research institutions — as active participants in design, staff training, and ongoing program evaluation. This is not a junket. It is a knowledge transfer, structured to build American institutional capacity so that the pilot can eventually operate without ongoing external advising.

**Scaling pathway:**

The youth pilot, if successful, is subject to expansion in two directions. Geographically, it scales to additional jurisdictions through an opt-in framework — states that choose to adopt the model gain access to the federal support structure, the advisory network, and the evidence base the pilot generates. And programmatically, states may opt in to extend the model to adult non-violent crime. That extension is not automatic and is not the default. It is available to states that have demonstrated successful implementation with youth and choose to pursue the larger application. The pathway from youth pilot to adult system is evidence-gated, not mandate-driven.

## System Integration

Violence interruption and youth off-ramps connect to the broader criminal justice stabilization system at multiple points. Individuals diverted through the youth pilot exit into the same stabilization ecosystem — workforce pathways, housing stabilization, healthcare access — that crisis response teams route to. The warm handoff is the same mechanism; the entry point is different.

DoDa tracks both program-level outcomes — recidivism, educational attainment, employment at six and twelve months post-exit — and system-level indicators that reveal whether the connections between components are functioning. An individual who exits the youth pilot into a workforce stabilization track that then fails to place them has not been successfully served by the combined system. The measurement framework is designed to catch those failures at the connection point, not only at the component level.

## Performance Metrics

**Violence interruption:** Shooting and homicide rates in targeted corridors; retaliatory violence incidents following identified triggering events; crisis response co-dispatch rate; interruption worker retention and program stability.

**Immediate pathways:** Warm handoff completion rate to workforce, housing, and healthcare systems; employment and income stability at six months; re-contact with crisis response at 90 days.

**Youth pilot:** Recidivism rate at one year and three years post-exit; educational credential completion; employment at six and twelve months; housing stability at twelve months; cost per participant compared to standard juvenile justice processing.

**Equity:** Distribution of program access relative to need indicators; racial and demographic composition of program participants; outcomes disaggregated by demographic group.

## Failure Modes

**Net-widening** is the most common failure mode of diversion programs: expanding the reach of the justice system rather than providing a genuine alternative to it. The youth pilot is designed with explicit net-widening protections — it accepts only individuals who would otherwise be processed through the standard juvenile system, not individuals who would not have had any justice system contact without the program's existence. Intake criteria are public and auditable.

**Staff turnover** in violence interruption programs destroys the relationships that make the work effective. Competitive compensation, clinical support, and organizational cultures that treat interruption workers as professionals are operational requirements, not aspirational goals. Programs that cannot sustain their staff are not functioning programs, and the measurement framework tracks retention as a leading indicator of program health.

**Underfunded reentry** is the failure mode most likely to undermine the youth pilot specifically. A young person who exits a rehabilitative residential program into a community with no housing stability, no employment, and no ongoing support is likely to fail not because of the program but because of what comes after it. Reentry investment is funded explicitly and tracked explicitly. It is not treated as downstream from the program's responsibilities.

**Advisory without adoption** — bringing in Norwegian expertise without actually implementing what that expertise recommends — is a real risk in any knowledge transfer process. The advisory relationship is structured to include evaluation authority: Norwegian advisors assess whether the program being operated is actually consistent with the model being piloted, and their assessments are public. Deviation from the model is documented rather than obscured.

## Bottom Line

The people at highest risk of violence in American communities are not a mystery. The conditions that put them there are not a mystery. The interventions that work — credible messenger outreach, immediate legitimate alternatives, early and genuine rehabilitation for young people — are documented and available. What has been missing is the sustained commitment to fund them, staff them properly, connect them to a broader stabilization system, and measure them honestly.

This framework provides that commitment. It treats violence interruption as the skilled professional practice it is, provides real off-ramps rather than symbolic ones, and introduces — carefully, piloted with youth, advisory-supported, evidence-gated in its expansion — the rehabilitation model that has produced the best outcomes in the developed world.

The goal is not a more humane criminal justice system as an end in itself, though that matters. The goal is fewer people in violent situations, fewer young people whose life trajectories are foreclosed by early system contact, and a system that actually does what it claims to do: reduce harm, restore capacity, and return people to their communities better equipped to stay there.

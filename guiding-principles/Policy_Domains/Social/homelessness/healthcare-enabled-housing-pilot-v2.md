# Healthcare-Enabled Housing Infrastructure Pilot

## Executive Summary

The Healthcare-Enabled Housing Infrastructure Pilot establishes a limited, corridor-based demonstration model for integrating supportive healthcare services into purpose-built housing for individuals experiencing chronic or high-acuity homelessness.

This pilot is one of two parallel stabilization tracks for the unhoused population. It covers individuals whose primary barrier is **chronic clinical need** — co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders, disability-linked housing vulnerability, repeated institutional cycling — rather than situational instability. Individuals whose primary barrier is situational are routed to the **Workforce-Capable Stabilization Pilots** through the shared triage protocol defined below.

The separation is a systems design requirement, not a value judgment. High-acuity individuals achieve durable stability significantly more often when housed in environments with embedded clinical infrastructure. Workforce-capable individuals stabilize faster in non-clinical environments. Both populations deserve the infrastructure that actually works for them.

This pilot does not constitute a comprehensive homelessness strategy. Social services reform, behavioral health systems, addiction treatment, workforce reintegration, and prevention policy are addressed under separate domains. This pilot focuses narrowly on **infrastructure**: how housing and healthcare delivery can be co-located and structurally integrated to reduce return to homelessness and improve long-term stability.

---

## Core Principle

**Stability before throughput.**

Unlike the workforce stabilization track, this pilot does not optimize for rapid exit. It optimizes for **durable housing retention** — the condition under which clinical stabilization becomes possible and the chronic cycle of homelessness, emergency services, and institutional care is broken.

Throughput metrics are tracked but are not the primary success indicator. The primary indicator is whether individuals remain stably housed at 12, 24, and 36 months — and whether public expenditure on emergency services, inpatient care, and incarceration declines as a result.

---

## Triage and Intake Protocol
### Shared with Workforce-Capable Stabilization Pilots

All individuals entering the stabilization system pass through a standardized intake assessment before placement. The assessment determines which track — workforce stabilization or healthcare-enabled housing — is the appropriate entry point.

**Healthcare-Enabled Housing Track indicators:**
- Chronic homelessness: documented multi-year or repeated instability
- Co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders requiring clinical management
- Repeated emergency department utilization or inpatient psychiatric episodes
- Disability-linked housing vulnerability
- History of stabilization attempts without durable outcome in non-clinical settings
- Active need for medication management, behavioral health counseling, or peer support infrastructure

**Workforce Stabilization Track indicators:**
- Situational homelessness: job loss, eviction, relationship breakdown, short-term financial shock
- No active chronic behavioral health or substance use disorder requiring clinical management
- Able to engage with employment services, case management, and daily structure
- No documented pattern of repeated homelessness episodes linked to clinical condition
- Medical needs manageable through standard community health access, not embedded clinical staff

**Routing principles:**
- Intake assessment is conducted by trained staff, not administrative gatekeepers
- Individuals near the boundary default to whichever track has available capacity, with re-assessment at 60 days
- Movement between tracks is permitted and expected — a healthcare-track resident who achieves clinical stabilization may transition to the workforce stabilization track or directly to the arrival/transition stack co-living tier; a workforce-track resident who presents emerging clinical needs transfers here
- No individual is denied entry to the system because the appropriate track is at capacity — overflow protocols are defined at the metro cluster level

## Strategic Premise

Chronic homelessness intersects with mental health conditions, substance use disorders, disability, trauma exposure, and fragmented healthcare access. Traditional shelter models and scattered-site vouchers fail high-acuity cases because services remain episodic, disconnected, and inaccessible.

Housing-first models without integrated care infrastructure can achieve initial placement but struggle to maintain stability among the highest-need populations — the evidence base on housing-first outcomes is strongest when clinical infrastructure is embedded, not assumed to exist in the surrounding community.

This pilot is built on a simple principle: **certain populations require co-located, embedded healthcare access within residential infrastructure to achieve durable stabilization.** This is not a universal model. It is a targeted pilot for defined high-acuity cohorts.

---

## Target Population

Individuals meeting high-acuity criteria, which may include:

- Chronic homelessness with documented multi-year or repeated instability
- Repeated emergency department utilization — three or more visits in twelve months
- Co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders
- Disability-linked housing vulnerability
- History of failed stabilization in non-clinical settings

Eligibility standards are determined in coordination with local healthcare systems and social service agencies. This pilot does not redefine homelessness categorization nationally.

---

## Scope and Boundaries

This pilot applies to designated corridors and limited project counts. It is not a national mandate.

The framework does not replace:
- Emergency shelters
- Transitional housing programs
- Voucher-based housing assistance
- Broader social safety net reforms
- Workforce-capable stabilization infrastructure

It addresses a specific infrastructure gap: the absence of stable housing environments designed to support continuous, coordinated healthcare access for individuals whose clinical complexity makes standard housing placements fail.

Expansion beyond pilot scale requires outcome validation and legislative authorization.

---

## Physical Infrastructure Model

Healthcare-enabled housing differs from standard multifamily construction in several key ways:

**Residential units** are private and stable — this is not a shelter or a congregate facility. Residents have their own space. The building's clinical infrastructure is embedded within a residential environment, not the reverse.

**Clinical space** is dedicated and sufficient for primary care visits, behavioral health counseling, medication management, and care coordination. It is not a full medical facility — it is a primary care and behavioral health presence sufficient to eliminate the access barriers that cause clinical deterioration and housing instability.

**Common areas** support structured programming while maintaining dignity and autonomy. The design objective is stabilization, not institutionalization.

**Security design** balances resident safety with non-carceral operation. The building should feel like housing, not a facility.

**Mechanical, acoustic, and spatial planning** reflects long-term occupancy rather than short-term shelter turnover.

The building envelope remains consistent with corridor pattern-book standards where applicable, ensuring the building reads as residential rather than institutional from the street.

---

## Service Integration Layer

Healthcare-enabled housing requires formal partnerships with:
- Local hospital systems
- Community mental health providers
- Substance use treatment providers
- Medicaid and managed care entities
- Peer support organizations

Service delivery is structured through contractual agreements, not ad hoc referral pathways. The difference matters: ad hoc referrals fail when clinical needs are acute. Contractual embedded services remain present regardless of individual resident motivation or capacity to self-navigate.

**Embedded staff** may include case managers, licensed clinical professionals, and peer support specialists. Medical services are coordinated rather than permanently institutionalized — the goal is clinical support sufficient to maintain stability, not a replacement for community health infrastructure.

**Funding for services** is addressed under healthcare and social policy frameworks, not embedded in capital construction budgets. Medicaid reimbursement streams, managed care partnerships, and healthcare system cost-offset agreements are the primary operating funding mechanisms.

---

## Exit Pathways

Unlike the workforce stabilization track, this pilot does not have a defined maximum length of stay. Exit is driven by clinical stabilization and resident readiness, not administrative timers.

**Clinical stabilization exit — Workforce Stabilization Track:**
Residents who achieve sustained clinical stabilization and develop work capacity may transfer to the workforce stabilization track for employment linkage and market re-entry support. This is a genuine and expected pathway, not a theoretical one — it should be actively supported and celebrated.

**Clinical stabilization exit — Arrival/Transition Stack:**
Residents with sufficient stability to manage month-to-month co-living arrangements without embedded clinical support may transition directly to the co-living tier of the arrival/transition stack. Coordination with co-living operators in STZ corridors ensures the handoff is supported, not abrupt.

**Long-term housing — On-site:**
Some residents will remain in healthcare-enabled housing as their long-term housing. This is a legitimate outcome, not a failure. For the highest-acuity individuals, stable long-term housing with accessible clinical support is the durable solution — not a waypoint to somewhere else.

**Transfer — Inpatient or Higher-Level Care:**
Residents requiring inpatient psychiatric care, acute medical intervention, or higher-level clinical settings are transferred with active care coordination and a guaranteed return pathway. Loss of housing during clinical crisis is a primary driver of chronic homelessness — the return pathway is a structural commitment, not a best-effort.

---

## Financing Structure

Capital financing utilizes a consortium stack model with modifications reflecting service integration needs.

**Construction capital** may use PCA consortium financing with public subordinated equity participation under defined pilot envelopes. Participation is exposure-capped and time-bound.

**Operating costs** are structured differently from standard housing. Embedded clinical staffing, case management, and service coordination represent operating costs above standard multifamily operations. These are funded through:
- Medicaid reimbursement for eligible clinical services
- Managed care partnership agreements reflecting cost offsets from reduced ED utilization and inpatient days
- Healthcare system contributions reflecting documented avoided cost
- Targeted operating subsidy where reimbursement gaps exist, transparently identified and time-bounded

**Cost-offset validation** is a condition of expansion. The pilot must demonstrate that healthcare system savings, reduced emergency response costs, and reduced incarceration costs offset the operating cost premium above standard housing. This is not aspirational — the evidence base from comparable programs is strong enough that cost-offset validation is achievable with proper outcome measurement.

---

## Outcome Measurement

**Primary indicators — housing stability:**
- Housing retention at 12, 24, and 36 months
- Return to homelessness rate within 24 months of placement

**Healthcare utilization indicators:**
- Reduction in emergency department visits per resident per year
- Reduction in inpatient psychiatric days per resident per year
- Medication adherence rates where applicable

**Criminal justice indicators:**
- Reduction in jail bookings per resident per year

**Cost-offset indicators:**
- Net public cost per stably housed individual vs. baseline cost of cycling through emergency services, shelter, and incarceration
- Medicaid reimbursement coverage ratio for embedded clinical services

**Transition indicators:**
- Rate of clinical stabilization exit to workforce track or arrival/transition stack
- Employment and housing outcomes for residents who successfully transition out

Evaluation must occur over multi-year horizons. Short-term outcome measurement misses the stabilization effects that justify the model.

---

## Governance and Guardrails

- Limited project count during initial phase
- Independent outcome evaluation with published methodology
- Transparent reporting of cost, utilization, and housing stability metrics
- Defined sunset review after initial deployment period — expansion requires validated outcomes
- No permanent federal healthcare-housing authority created by this pilot
- No administrative conversion of standard co-living or market housing into healthcare-enabled housing
- Return pathway guarantee for residents transferred to inpatient settings

---

## Relationship to Workforce Stabilization Pilots

These two documents form a **paired stabilization system** — not a hierarchy.

The triage protocol shared between them is the operational mechanism that ensures each population receives the infrastructure designed for them. The transfer pathway between tracks, in both directions, is a functional requirement: a workforce-track resident who decompensates needs to be able to move here without losing housing; a healthcare-track resident who stabilizes needs to be able to move to the workforce track or the arrival/transition stack without administrative obstruction.

The paired system as a whole addresses the full spectrum of the currently unhoused population: those who fell on hard times and can return to stable independent living with structured support, and those whose clinical complexity requires embedded healthcare infrastructure to achieve the same goal.

Both deserve the infrastructure that actually works for them.

---

## Meta Insight

The chronic homelessness crisis in the United States is partly a housing supply problem, partly a healthcare access problem, and partly an infrastructure problem. This pilot addresses the third: the absence of a building typology that combines residential stability with embedded clinical access in a way that is dignified, non-carceral, and financially sustainable.

If successful, it demonstrates that a relatively small number of purpose-built units — sited thoughtfully, operated with embedded clinical partnerships, and measured rigorously — can break the cycle of homelessness for the most complex cases at a net cost to the public that is lower than the current cycle of emergency services, shelter, and incarceration.

If unsuccessful, the pilot sunsets without expanding into permanent structure.

The goal is simple: a person with complex clinical needs has stable housing, consistent care, and is no longer cycling through emergency rooms, jails, and encampments.

That is achievable. The infrastructure to achieve it is defined here.

## Executive Summary

This pilot portfolio deploys **workforce-capable stabilization housing** to bridge currently unhoused individuals who are able to work — or who can become work-ready with modest support — back into stable housing and labor market participation.

This is not a permanent housing system. It is a **throughput system**: structured, time-bounded, and explicitly oriented toward exit into the arrival/transition stack, market rental housing, or ownership pathways.

It operates as one of two parallel stabilization tracks for the unhoused population. This document covers individuals whose primary barrier is situational — job loss, eviction, relationship breakdown, short-term health disruption — rather than chronic clinical need. Individuals presenting with high-acuity health, behavioral health, or disability-linked barriers are routed to the **Healthcare-Enabled Housing Pilot** through the shared triage protocol defined below.

The separation is not a value judgment. It is a systems design requirement. Workforce-capable individuals stabilize faster and exit more reliably when they are not in clinical environments. High-acuity individuals stabilize better and stay housed longer when they have embedded healthcare infrastructure. Combining the populations in a single system underserves both.

---

## Core Principle

**Movement, not occupancy.**

System success is measured by flow — individuals moving through stabilization and into durable housing — not by beds filled. A site at 90% occupancy with low throughput is a failing site. A site at 90% occupancy with high throughput is a functioning one.

---

## Triage and Intake Protocol
### Shared with Healthcare-Enabled Housing Pilot

All individuals entering the stabilization system pass through a standardized intake assessment before placement. The assessment determines which track — workforce stabilization or healthcare-enabled housing — is the appropriate entry point.

**Workforce Stabilization Track indicators:**
- Situational homelessness: job loss, eviction, relationship breakdown, short-term financial shock
- No active chronic behavioral health or substance use disorder requiring clinical management
- Able to engage with employment services, case management, and daily structure
- No documented pattern of repeated homelessness episodes linked to clinical condition
- Medical needs manageable through standard community health access, not embedded clinical staff

**Healthcare-Enabled Housing Track indicators:**
- Chronic homelessness: documented multi-year or repeated instability
- Co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders requiring clinical management
- Repeated emergency department utilization or inpatient psychiatric episodes
- Disability-linked housing vulnerability
- History of stabilization attempts without durable outcome in non-clinical settings

**Routing principles:**
- Intake assessment is conducted by trained staff, not administrative gatekeepers
- Individuals near the boundary default to whichever track has available capacity, with re-assessment at 60 days
- Movement between tracks is permitted and expected — a workforce-track resident who decompensates may transfer to healthcare-enabled housing; a healthcare-track resident who stabilizes clinically may transition to workforce stabilization or directly to the arrival/transition stack
- No individual is denied entry to the system because the appropriate track is at capacity — overflow protocols are defined at the metro cluster level

## Target Population

Individuals experiencing homelessness whose primary barrier is **situational instability**, not chronic clinical need:

- Recently unemployed individuals with prior stable housing history
- Individuals exiting short-term institutional settings (hospital, jail) without housing to return to
- Individuals fleeing domestic instability with intact work capacity
- Workforce-ready individuals in geographic transition without local housing network
- Young adults aging out of systems (foster care, juvenile justice) with work capacity

This population can and does stabilize quickly with the right infrastructure. The failure mode is not clinical complexity — it is the absence of a structured bridge between crisis and normal housing.

## Pilot Architecture

Two pilot types operate as an integrated system:

1. **Tiny Home Stabilization Villages** — non-congregate structured stabilization
2. **Transitional Housing Clusters** — bridge housing before market re-entry

---

## Pilot 1 — Tiny Home Stabilization Villages

### Function

Provide non-congregate, structured stabilization for workforce-capable individuals who need privacy, safety, and consistent support engagement — but not clinical infrastructure.

### Design

- 40–80 units per site
- Shared hygiene, kitchen, and community facilities
- On-site case management and employment linkage
- Modular, relocatable construction aligned with future neighborhood buildout
- Sited within geographic clusters with access to transit and employment corridors

### Duration

- Typical stay: 3–9 months
- Maximum stay: 12 months, with documented review at 9 months
- Extensions require active case management justification and re-assessment for healthcare-enabled housing track eligibility

### Role in System

- Replaces encampments with stable, dignified micro-communities
- Increases service engagement and documentation recovery
- Creates predictable transition pipeline into downstream housing
- Generates employment stabilization data for labor market integration planning

---

## Pilot 2 — Transitional Housing Clusters

### Function

Provide bridge housing between stabilization village exit and full market re-entry.

### Design

Clustered acquisition and conversion of:
- Small apartment buildings
- Motels
- Modular infill on adjacent parcels

Includes:
- Light case management
- Employment linkage and workforce development access
- Proximity to transit and services
- Connection to co-living and arrival/transition stack operators for exit pathway

### Duration

- Typical stay: 3–18 months

### Role in System

- Prevents bottlenecks in the stabilization village layer
- Accelerates movement into market housing, co-living, or the arrival/transition stack
- Integrates residents into mixed neighborhoods without programmatic branding
- Serves as the penultimate step before full market re-entry

---

## Exit Pathways

Workforce stabilization exits route into three downstream systems:

**Primary exit — Arrival/Transition Stack (Co-Living Tier):**
Individuals ready for market housing but without the deposit, credit history, or local network to enter long-term leasing directly transition into the co-living tier of the arrival/transition stack. This is the natural handoff — month-to-month, furnished, no large upfront commitment. The transition stack's co-living tier is designed to receive exactly this population without programmatic stigma.

**Secondary exit — Market Rental:**
Individuals with stabilized employment and sufficient savings exit directly into standard rental housing. Case management supports lease navigation and deposit assistance where needed.

**Tertiary exit — Ownership Pathways:**
For individuals with sufficient tenure in transitional housing and employment stability, limited-equity ownership pathways through the tenure-convertible mid-rise typology may be available in STZ corridors with active PCA participation.

**Transfer — Healthcare-Enabled Housing:**
Individuals who do not stabilize within expected timeframes, or who present clinical needs that emerge during stabilization, are transferred to the healthcare-enabled housing track rather than extended indefinitely in workforce stabilization infrastructure.

---

## System Flow

```
unsheltered → triage intake → workforce stabilization track
                                      ↓
                          tiny home stabilization village
                                      ↓
                          transitional housing cluster
                                      ↓
                  co-living / market rental / ownership pathway

                          ↕ transfer at any point
                          
                          healthcare-enabled housing track
                          (if clinical needs emerge or are identified at intake)
```

## Site Strategy: Geographic Clusters

Pilots are deployed in geographic clusters, not isolated sites.

Each cluster includes:
- One stabilization village
- Nearby transitional housing inventory
- Access to transit and employment corridors
- Proximity to co-living and arrival/transition stack nodes for exit pathway continuity

Clustering enables:
- Operational efficiency and shared service infrastructure
- Case management coordination across the transition
- Future redevelopment alignment with STZ corridor buildout

Stabilization sites are sited in anticipation of STZ corridor development where feasible — modular and relocatable construction allows sites to be wound down as permanent mixed-use density arrives, rather than creating land use conflict.

---

## Integration with Housing Production Stack

Workforce stabilization capacity is explicitly linked to:
- Housing supply stabilization overlay trigger metrics (DoDa affordability stress indicators)
- STZ corridor buildout sequencing — stabilization clusters sited in pre-development nodes
- Tenure-convertible mid-rise typology as an exit pathway for stabilized residents
- PCA consortium financing for transitional housing cluster acquisition where private capital underperforms

Stabilization capacity scales with demonstrated housing supply stress. It does not operate as a substitute for housing production — it operates as a pressure valve while production scales.

---

## Separation from Mobility Housing Stack

Workforce stabilization is distinct from:
- Co-living
- Hostels
- Arrival and transition housing

Those systems serve broad-market mobility needs and must remain non-programmatic, non-stigmatized, and capital-viable.

The exit pathway from this system into the arrival/transition stack is a **one-way handoff**, not a bidirectional integration. Stabilization graduates enter the transition stack as market participants — they are not tracked, flagged, or administratively distinguished from any other co-living resident. This is required to preserve the product integrity of the transition stack and to prevent the co-living tier from being reclassified as crisis housing.

---

## Integration with IDP and National Readiness

Modular flex capacity provides:
- Surge housing during disasters and economic shocks
- Overflow capacity for workforce deployments
- Resilience infrastructure during regional migration or industrial corridor buildout

Supports:
- Execution Corps operations and temporary deployment housing
- Industrial corridor workforce absorption during STZ buildout phases
- Emergency response housing capacity

## Performance Metrics

### Throughput
- Percentage of residents exiting within target duration
- Average length of stay by pilot type
- Transfer rate to healthcare-enabled housing track

### Transition Success
- Percentage moving to co-living, rental, or ownership within 90 days of exit
- Employment stabilization rate at 6 months post-exit
- Housing retention rate at 12 months post-exit

### System Pressure
- Utilization rates (target: below 85%)
- Inflow vs. outflow balance
- Waitlist length as leading indicator of downstream housing production lag

### Cost Efficiency
- Cost per stabilized individual
- Avoided emergency service costs (ED, jail, crisis response)
- Cost per durable housing exit

---

## Guardrails

- Time limits on residency enforced with active case management — not administrative lock-out
- No permanent conversion of stabilization housing into long-term affordable housing
- No repurposing of market housing into stabilization inventory without explicit authorization
- Distributed siting to avoid burden concentration in single neighborhoods
- Transparent public reporting on throughput, outcomes, and costs
- No programmatic branding or stigma signaling on transitional housing cluster exteriors

---

## Failure Modes

System degrades if:
- Dwell times increase persistently without clinical explanation — indicates downstream housing production lag, not stabilization failure
- Sites become semi-permanent settlements — requires investigation of exit pathway blockage, not extension of stay limits
- Healthcare-enabled housing track is under-resourced and high-acuity individuals back up into workforce stabilization infrastructure
- Downstream housing production lags and the co-living/transition stack cannot absorb exits
- Public perception shifts to warehousing — addressed through throughput transparency and distributed siting, not population mixing

## Meta Insight

The American homelessness system has historically failed by conflating two distinct populations into a single undifferentiated crisis response. Workforce-capable individuals in situational crisis stabilize quickly with structure, dignity, and employment support. High-acuity individuals with chronic clinical needs require embedded healthcare infrastructure to achieve durable stability. Serving both populations in the same system underserves both.

This pilot separates the tracks not to create a hierarchy of worthiness — but because the right infrastructure for each population is genuinely different, and the right measure of success is genuinely different.

The goal for this track is simple: a person fell on hard times, the system caught them, and within a year they are housed, working, and no longer in contact with crisis services.

That is achievable. The infrastructure to achieve it is defined here.

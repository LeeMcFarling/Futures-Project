# UN Reform — Conflict Screening and Selective Recusal Architecture

## Purpose

This brief defines the conflict-screening and selective recusal rules required to prevent the reformed Security Council, Regional Security Councils, and Mediation Council from becoming venues for conflict-of-interest laundering. The core problem is not that states have interests. Every state has interests, and that is normal. The problem is that structurally entangled states can currently block, dilute, certify, or review outcomes in matters where their own strategic, economic, military, or infrastructural exposure is large enough to predictably distort judgment — and the current system has no formal mechanism to prevent this.

Formal equality among states is not sufficient when material entanglement reaches a scale that impairs the credibility of decisional authority. A state that holds major arms relationships, sovereign financing exposure, dominant trade dependence, or critical infrastructure stakes in a disputed matter is not positioned to judge that matter impartially. Allowing it to do so does not produce sovereign equality. It produces procedural cover for structural capture.

This brief answers design requirement four from the diagnosis: procedural impartiality and conflict screening are part of the core legitimacy architecture, not secondary ethics rules.

## Why This Layer Is Necessary

The prior briefs established three institutional layers: Regional Security Councils as first-order legitimacy generators, a reformed Security Council operating on a majority-veto model, and a Mediation Council for appellate review of contested procedural failures. Each layer is stronger than what it replaces. None of them is self-executing against capture.

The majority-veto model reduces unilateral blockage but can still be manipulated if materially conflicted states are permitted to count toward blocking coalitions. A state does not need to exercise a unilateral veto to distort collective action. It only needs to anchor a blocking majority in a matter where its own position is directly at stake. Regional Security Councils generate stronger local legitimacy but remain vulnerable to capture by the most powerful regional actor or by outside powers with deep economic or security penetration in the region. The Mediation Council cannot serve as a credible appellate chamber if the states assigned to panels hold material stakes in the outcome they are reviewing.

Without conflict screening, the reform architecture does not eliminate power politics. It relocates power politics into procedural form — where it is harder to see, harder to name, and therefore harder to contest.

## Core Design Principle

The governing rule is automaticity under published thresholds: where a state’s exposure to a matter crosses defined material parameters, the relevant recusal consequences apply without requiring discretionary political accusation as the initiating mechanism.

This principle distinguishes three conditions that must be treated differently. Having interests is normal and does not disqualify participation. Having disclosed interests is manageable and requires transparency but not exclusion. Having decision-distorting material entanglements is disqualifying for specific decisional functions — not for membership or speech in the system as a whole.

Recusal is therefore function-specific. A recused state does not lose its seat, its voice, or its broader standing in the institution. It is excluded from the specific decisional role — blocking, certifying, or reviewing — in the specific matter where its entanglement is large enough to compromise that function. The constitutional analogy is exact: judges recuse, directors recuse, procurement officials recuse. The point is not to punish states for having interests. The point is to prevent institutions from pretending those interests do not exist when they are large enough to distort judgment.

The reasonable-observer standard remains relevant, but as a secondary interpretive backstop for compound or edge cases rather than as the primary trigger. The system should begin from known parameters, not political accusation.

## Scope of Application

Conflict screening applies across all three institutional layers, with standards calibrated to the function being performed.

In the **Security Council**, screening applies primarily to blocking authority. A materially conflicted state may still participate in deliberation, negotiate, and cast votes — but under defined conflict conditions, its vote does not count toward a blocking coalition. The question is not whether a state should be heard. The question is whether a structurally implicated state should be empowered to stop collective action.

In **Regional Security Councils**, screening applies when the council is issuing formal findings: on external interference, on regional legitimacy, or on exhausted regional capacity. A state too entangled with the subject of a finding cannot credibly certify the finding’s impartiality.

In the **Mediation Council**, screening applies most strictly, because appellate review carries the highest claim to procedural neutrality. A reviewing panel that includes states materially affected by the outcome it is reviewing is not an appellate body. It is another venue for the same conflict.

The principle that governs calibration across these contexts is simple: the right to argue is broader than the right to block, and the right to block is broader than the right to review. The stricter the function’s claim to neutrality, the stricter the recusal standard.

## Categories of Conflict

The categories below move from the most direct forms of conflict to more structurally mediated forms. The question is always the same: is the specific entanglement large enough, and direct enough, that it predictably distorts judgment in the specific matter?

### Direct Conflict

Direct conflict exists where a state is a belligerent, is directly militarily involved, is actively sponsoring a proxy in the disputed matter, holds formal alliance obligations triggered by the specific situation, or has basing or force commitments that would be materially affected by the decision.

### Strategic-Economic Conflict

Strategic-economic conflict exists where a state holds major sovereign financing exposure to an affected party, faces significant sanctions exposure linked to the outcome, holds dominant trade dependence in a conflict-relevant commodity or sector, owns or controls critical infrastructure or port facilities in the affected region, or maintains major arms-transfer relationships with a principal party whose position would be materially altered by the decision.

### Institutional and Political Conflict

Institutional and political conflict exists where a state has intelligence dependence, active covert involvement, ongoing regime-support arrangements, major patron-client relationships, or pending negotiations whose material terms would be significantly affected by the decision.

### Compound Conflict

Compound conflict exists where multiple moderate exposures together create a level of dependence or stake equivalent in effect to a direct conflict, even if no single exposure would individually trigger recusal. The aggregate is assessed, not only the individual categories.

Alliance membership alone is not automatically disqualifying. Alliance-linked exposure in the specific matter may be. The question is always whether the specific entanglement is large enough to distort judgment in the specific matter, not whether a state belongs to a general alliance structure.

## Parameter Framework

Conflict screening should rely primarily on measurable and pre-published parameters rather than discretionary diplomatic argument. The architecture should be designed so that covered decisions automatically trigger a conflict screen against known exposure thresholds.

Core parameters should include, at minimum:

- trade concentration in conflict-relevant sectors
- sovereign financing exposure
- major arms-transfer dependence
- critical infrastructure ownership or operating control
- energy dependence
- sanctions exposure
- basing and force commitments
- intelligence dependency where lawfully established
- compound exposure scores that aggregate multiple moderate entanglements

The brief does not need to set final numeric thresholds at this stage, but the governing principle should be fixed: recusal is parameter-driven. Terms such as “major,” “dominant,” or “material” should ultimately be expressed through published threshold bands and scoring rules rather than left as entirely narrative judgment.

## Levels of Response

Conflict screening is tiered rather than binary. The same category of exposure may warrant different responses depending on its magnitude, specificity, and the function being performed.

**Level I — Disclosure Only.** Ordinary background interests that do not materially impair participation in the relevant function. The state discloses the interest in standardized form and participation continues without restriction.

**Level II — Heightened Scrutiny.** Meaningful but not automatically disqualifying exposure. Participation continues, but the state’s position is flagged, additional justification may be required, and other parties may formally challenge the classification. The state’s contributions remain part of the record with the conflict noted.

**Level III — Selective Recusal.** The state may participate in deliberation and maintain its broader role, but its vote does not count toward blocking authority in the Security Council, toward formal findings in a Regional Security Council, or toward a review panel in the Mediation Council. This is the most commonly applicable level for strategic-economic and institutional conflicts of significant magnitude.

**Level IV — Full Case Recusal.** The state is excluded from the relevant decisional role for the duration of the matter because the conflict is too direct or too large to be managed through selective exclusion from specific functions. This level is reserved for direct conflicts and the most severe compound conflicts.

This tiering is necessary because a binary disqualification standard would be either too rigid to govern real institutions or too easily gamed by states arguing that any screening creates an unacceptable exclusion. The goal is calibrated response, not symbolic gesture.

## Screening Mechanism

Conflict screening cannot rely on self-declaration alone. States will reliably discover theories of their own impartiality. The architecture therefore requires a structured, automatic, and independent process.

### Automatic Screening

Covered decisions automatically trigger a conflict screen. Screening does not begin because a rival state levels an accusation. It begins because the system requires it before covered decisional functions can be exercised.

### Mandatory Disclosure

States participating in covered decisions disclose relevant exposure categories in standardized form before the decisional process begins. Disclosure is a precondition for participation, not an optional transparency gesture.

### Independent Procedural Secretariat

A standing technical body applies pre-published screening criteria, compiles mandatory disclosures, cross-checks them against known data sources, and produces an initial tier assignment for each covered decision. The secretariat does not create political standards case by case. It applies standing thresholds and scoring rules.

### Default Application

The relevant institutional body applies the secretariat’s provisional classification by default unless formally challenged under the published procedures. This prevents the screen from becoming merely advisory.

### Challenge Mechanism

Affected parties, other member states, or designated institutional actors may challenge omissions, misclassification, hidden exposure, or disputed attribution. Challenge does not initiate screening in ordinary cases. Screening is automatic. Challenge exists to correct the system where the automatic screen is incomplete or contested.

### Review of Screening Decisions

Contested screening outcomes can themselves be reviewed under a narrow Mediation Council procedure. This prevents the screening mechanism from becoming a separate venue for abuse while ensuring that recusal determinations are not unreviewable.

## Evidentiary Standards and Burdens

Conflict screening does not require proof to a criminal standard. The governing standard is reasonable expectation of impaired judgment, operationalized through published exposure thresholds and scoring rules.

Evidence relevant to screening includes public treaties and alliance commitments, trade and finance concentration data, arms-transfer records, infrastructure ownership and concession agreements, sanctions regimes and their economic effects, energy dependence metrics, intelligence or basing agreements where lawfully established, and combined exposure metrics under the compound conflict standard.

The use of state-owned enterprises, sovereign wealth funds, national champion firms, and intermediary structures to hold exposure does not insulate against screening where the ultimate beneficial interest or strategic stake is attributable to the state. Opacity of structure is not a defense.

## Procedural Effects of Recusal

Recusal changes what a state can do in a specific matter. It does not change its broader membership status, its right to speak in deliberation, or its standing in the institution.

In the **Security Council**, a recused state’s vote does not count toward the blocking majority required under the majority-veto model. If recusal removes enough states from a blocking coalition that the coalition falls below threshold, the block fails. The resolution proceeds unless a valid blocking coalition can be assembled from non-recused members.

In **Regional Security Councils**, a recused state does not count toward the supermajority required for formal findings of external interference, regional legitimacy, or exhausted regional capacity.

In the **Mediation Council**, recused states are excluded from panel assignment at the point of conflict screening, before the panel is disclosed. If a state is found to be conflicted after assignment, it is removed from the panel before deliberation begins and replaced under the published assignment rules.

## Why This Is Not Disenfranchisement

Critics will argue that conflict screening is anti-sovereign — that it creates a mechanism for sidelining powerful states under procedural cover. That argument misunderstands what recusal is.

Recusal is not punishment. It is a procedural safeguard familiar in every serious governance context: courts, procurement, corporate boards, administrative review, and international arbitration all apply versions of it. The principle is not that interested parties should be excluded from all public life. The principle is that decisional functions with claims to impartiality cannot retain those claims when the decision-maker holds a material stake in the outcome.

Sovereign equality does not require pretending that a state can impartially judge a matter whose outcome materially affects its own strategic or economic position. It requires ensuring that all states have equal standing within a system of fair rules — and fair rules, properly applied, include recusal where entanglement is large enough to distort judgment. A system that ignores structural capture in the name of formal equality is not protecting sovereignty. It is protecting distortion.

## Risks and Failure Modes

No screening architecture is immune to failure. The most likely failure modes are worth naming directly.

**Over-recusal** occurs if screening standards are drawn too broadly, leaving institutions without enough unentangled participants to function. The tiered response model is designed to limit this: most conflicts produce disclosure or heightened scrutiny, not full recusal.

**Under-recusal** occurs if standards become symbolic through weak enforcement, narrow definitions, or institutional capture of the secretariat itself. The independent secretariat, mandatory disclosure, default application, and challenge mechanism are designed to reduce but not eliminate this risk.

**Strategic weaponization** occurs when states use conflict allegations opportunistically to sideline rivals rather than to protect institutional integrity. The pre-published criteria, parameter-based screening, and Mediation Council review of contested outcomes are designed to limit this while preserving correction pathways.

**Hidden exposure** occurs when states route interests through firms, funds, proxies, or intermediaries to avoid screening. The compound conflict standard and attribution rules for state-owned entities are designed to address this, though opacity remains a real implementation challenge.

**Bloc politics** occurs when screening criteria are drawn or applied to favor some categories of entanglement over others, effectively treating some types of structural conflict as invisible while policing others. The pre-published rules and independent secretariat are the primary safeguards, but political pressure on both will be ongoing.

These failure modes do not argue against screening. They argue for periodic recalibration, review of the secretariat’s performance, and honest recognition that the integrity layer must itself remain reviewable over time.

## Strategic Effect

Conflict screening and selective recusal change the operating logic of the reformed architecture in one fundamental way: they raise the legitimacy threshold for blocking, certifying, and reviewing contested matters by making it harder for states to convert material entanglement into procedural authority.

Without recusal, the majority-veto model can still be captured by a blocking coalition anchored by materially interested states. Regional findings can still be shaped by actors whose own position is at stake. Appellate review can still be distorted by reviewers who hold exposure in what they are reviewing. Each of those outcomes is a version of the same failure the diagnosis brief named: the system formally produces a result while substantively serving the interests of those with the most to gain from it.

With recusal, those pathways become harder. Not impossible, but harder. The system begins to discipline power rather than merely redistribute voice. That is the constitutional function conflict screening serves in the broader reform stack.

A reformed order without recusal improves the vocabulary of legitimacy. A reformed order with recusal begins to build the substance of it.

## Bottom Line

A reformed international order cannot rely on better voting rules alone. If materially conflicted states can still block, certify, or review contested decisions in which they hold direct strategic or economic stakes, the system will reproduce capture under a more sophisticated institutional vocabulary. The majority-veto model, regional legitimacy findings, and Mediation Council review are all more credible when the states exercising those functions have been screened for the conflicts that most predictably distort them.

Conflict screening and selective recusal are therefore not secondary ethics provisions. They are part of the core integrity architecture that makes the rest of the reformed system function as designed — and without which the rest of the system is, at best, a more elaborate version of what it was built to replace.

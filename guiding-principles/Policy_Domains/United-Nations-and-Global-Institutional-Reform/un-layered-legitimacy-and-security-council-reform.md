# UN Reform — Security Council Reform and Layered Legitimacy Architecture

## Purpose

This brief proposes two linked institutional reforms. First: replace the current unilateral permanent-member veto with a majority-veto model, in which a Security Council resolution is blocked only when a majority of the full Council — including both permanent and non-permanent members — votes to block it. Second: establish Regional Security Councils as standing legitimacy bodies capable of aggregating regional sovereign consent, issuing formal findings on illegitimate external interference or exhausted regional capacity, and triggering expedited review by broader international institutions.

These reforms answer design requirements one and two from the diagnosis: the international system must be able to act under conditions of major-power disagreement, and legitimacy must become more layered than a single global chamber can provide. Both reforms are jointly necessary, because solving only one reproduces the other. A majority-veto model without a regional legitimacy layer reduces paralysis but leaves the predation problem intact. A regional legitimacy layer without Security Council reform improves grounding but leaves collective action still hostage to unilateral blockage at the top.

The objective is not to abolish major-power reality or construct a world parliament detached from power. It is to reduce the degree to which the international system oscillates between top-level paralysis and informal great-power competition below.

---

## Why Neither Reform Alone Is Sufficient

The diagnosis established that the Security Council's core failure is structural: unilateral veto power embedded the cost-of-entry constraint directly into the enforcement mechanism. The same instrument that made the great powers' participation possible also made paralysis the automatic outcome of their disagreement. Reforming that mechanism is therefore necessary. But it is not sufficient on its own.

Even a less paralyzed Security Council remains distant from local conditions, vulnerable to abstraction, and susceptible to great-power bargaining over regions whose people are not meaningfully present in the decision environment. Numerical flexibility at the top does not close the legitimacy gap at the bottom. A reformed Council that acts more often but still acts without grounding in regional consent has solved the paralysis problem while leaving the predation problem largely intact.

The reverse is equally true. A purely regional answer is insufficient. Regional bodies can generate stronger local legitimacy, but they can also be intimidated, factionalized, or captured by the strongest local actors. Pushing all authority downward does not eliminate the failure mode — it relocates it. A region dominated by one powerful state or penetrated by an outside power cannot be its own sole judge of whether that domination or penetration is legitimate.

The necessary answer is therefore layered: regional bodies generate first-order legitimacy and consent, the Security Council provides wider authorization and scaling, and a separate review mechanism handles contested failures at either level. No single node in that architecture should be trusted absolutely. The system must be built to function through structured disagreement and cross-level review, not through assumed purity at any one point.

---

## Core Design Principle

A reformed international order should operate on a simple constitutional logic. Regional bodies are best positioned to interpret the legitimacy environment within a shared threat context — to say whether a crisis is real, whether external interference is wanted or unwanted, and whether the region is capable of managing the situation itself. Global bodies are best positioned to scale consequences, provide broader legal authorization, and prevent regional politics from becoming the sole judge of regional coercion. Review bodies are needed to prevent either level from becoming judge in its own cause.

This is not redundancy for its own sake. It is the minimum structural condition for an order that can degrade safely under stress rather than invert into paralysis above and predation below.

---

## Security Council Reform: From Unilateral Veto to Majority-Veto Model

The first reform is explicit: replace the current unilateral permanent-member veto with a majority-veto model. A Security Council resolution should be blocked only when a majority of the full Council votes to block it — and that majority must include both permanent and non-permanent members. No single permanent member retains the authority to freeze collective action alone.

This means that obstruction must be demonstrated rather than inherited. A proposed action should not fail because one powerful state objects. It should fail only if a broader cross-section of the Council can show that the action lacks sufficient legitimacy, prudence, or legal grounding to proceed. The burden shifts from the advocates of action to the opponents of it.

The governing rule:

> A resolution is blocked only when a majority of the full Security Council, including both permanent and non-permanent members, votes to block it.

The precise thresholds — exact numbers of permanent and non-permanent members required — are matters for negotiation. The constitutional principle is not. No unilateral veto. No closed permanent-member cartel veto. No blocking authority that excludes elected states from the threshold.

---

## Why the Majority-Veto Model Is More Legitimate

The old veto was designed for a world in which the price of keeping the strongest states inside the system was allowing each to stop the system from acting against its core interests. That model does not scale under modern rivalry. It produces an institution that functions only when the strongest states happen to agree — which is exactly the condition the reform agenda exists to escape.

The majority-veto model changes the burden of proof. Instead of requiring advocates of action to overcome a single great power's refusal, it requires opponents of action to assemble a wider case for restraint. Obstruction becomes something that must be justified across a broader political field rather than exercised from a privileged chair. That is a constitutional shift of real significance.

It also improves the signaling value of Security Council outcomes. A blocked resolution under coalition rules tells the world that a meaningful cross-section of states found the proposed action deficient or dangerous. A blocked resolution under unilateral veto tells the world only that one powerful state found it inconvenient. The former preserves some legitimacy even in disagreement. The latter corrodes it almost automatically.

## Layered Legitimacy: The Role of Regional Security Councils

Security Council reform reduces top-level paralysis, but it does not solve the legitimacy deficit created by distance from regional conditions. That requires a second institutional layer.

Regional Security Councils should serve as first-order legitimacy generators in their respective regions. Their function is not to replace the UN or to create supranational command authority over national forces. Their legitimacy comes not merely from geographic proximity, but from constituting regional sovereign consent into a standing institutional form. Their role is to interpret shared threat environments, register regional consent, issue formal findings regarding external interference, and sequence requests for broader multilateral action.

This matters because many of the most serious failures of the current order occur in the gap between abstract global authority and lived regional reality. Bilateral great-power intervention can be destabilizing and regionally unwanted while still being framed internationally as stabilization. Genuine regional crises can be discounted because they are filtered through distant geopolitical calculations before they are understood on their own terms. Regional Security Councils narrow that gap. They provide a formal layer through which a region can say: this is a legitimate security concern, this is illegitimate external interference, this action does or does not have regional consent, and this situation does or does not warrant escalation to broader international bodies.

That capacity to name, formally and institutionally, what is happening in a region — and who is responsible for it — is itself a form of power the current order largely denies to the people most directly affected.

---

## The Constitutional Function of Regional Findings

Regional findings should carry formal procedural weight in the reformed system, not merely advisory or political weight.

A Regional Security Council should be able to issue three categories of finding.

**A finding of legitimate regional concern** establishes that a developing crisis materially affects regional stability and warrants international attention. This finding triggers expedited review at the Security Council level and creates a stronger presumption in favor of investigation.

**A finding of illegitimate external interference** is a formal judgment that an outside power, proxy network, or transnational actor is shaping a regional outcome without meaningful regional consent and in a destabilizing manner. This finding raises the burden on the interfering party to justify its actions and provides grounds for graduated multilateral response.

**A finding of exhausted regional capacity** declares that the region cannot stabilize the matter through its own institutions and is formally requesting higher-level review, assistance, or enforcement authorization. This finding opens the enforcement ladder at the Security Council level.

These findings do not automatically trigger coercive action. But they change the procedural posture of the system in ways that matter: expedited review, stronger investigative presumptions, and a higher burden for blockage without articulated reason. A matter arriving with a formal regional finding is not the same as a matter arriving without one. That procedural difference is the mechanism through which regional legitimacy connects to global action.

---

## How the Layers Interact

The reformed system operates in sequence rather than in parallel.

In ordinary cases, regional bodies interpret the local legitimacy environment. Many situations will be managed at that level without requiring escalation.

In serious cases, a regional finding triggers formal Security Council consideration, with the finding itself shaping the procedural posture of the review.

In blocked or contested cases — where the Security Council cannot act or where either level is suspected of capture or coercion — the matter moves to a separate Mediation Council designed to assess procedural integrity, threshold compliance, and proportionality. That body is addressed in the next brief.

In extreme cases where thresholds are satisfied and reviews are exhausted, a graduated enforcement ladder opens under international authorization, moving through investigation, sanctions, financial and logistics restrictions, and in the most serious cases collective security measures.

The purpose of this sequencing is not procedural complexity. It is to prevent the two pathologies the current order most reliably produces: distant great-power improvisation without regional legitimacy, and regional paralysis or capture without any route upward. Each layer exists to catch what the layer below cannot handle and to refer what it cannot handle upward without collapsing into ad hoc crisis bargaining.

---

## What This Reform Does Not Do

This architecture does not abolish power politics. Major powers still matter and will continue to shape outcomes. The reform is aimed at making those outcomes more accountable and more resistant to unilateral manipulation — not at pretending power differentials do not exist.

It does not create automatic war powers for regional bodies or for the Security Council. The finding and enforcement architecture is designed to make legitimate collective action more credible and graduated. It is not a mechanism for easy or frictionless intervention.

It does not assume regional bodies are morally superior to global institutions. They are closer to local conditions, but they are also vulnerable to local dynamics that can distort their judgment. That is precisely why they are one layer in the system rather than the final word.

And it does not resolve every contested case. Some crises will still be blocked, delayed, or managed imperfectly. The goal is not a frictionless order. It is an order that fails more gracefully — one that degrades into procedural contest and cross-level review rather than immediate reversion to great-power improvisation and chessboard competition.

---

## Strategic Effect

Taken together, Security Council reform and layered legitimacy architecture change the operating logic of collective security in three ways.

First, obstruction becomes harder to exercise cheaply. Blocking action requires assembling a coalition and making a case, rather than exercising an inherited individual right. That raises the cost of purely strategic paralysis.

Second, regional legitimacy becomes institutionally legible. The international system gains a formal mechanism through which the consent or dissent of affected regions can be registered, recorded, and connected to global decision-making rather than ignored until a crisis forces improvisation.

Third, the system becomes more resistant to predatory interference dressed up as partnership. When a regional body has the formal authority to name illegitimate external interference and trigger review, the current diplomatic cover — "we were invited," "we are stabilizing" — becomes procedurally contestable in ways it currently is not.

None of this eliminates conflict. It reduces the degree to which the structure of the international system itself rewards and amplifies the worst forms of it.

---

## Bottom Line

The existing Security Council model was built for a world that assumed great-power convergence as the operating condition of collective security. That assumption failed almost immediately and has not recovered. A durable international order now requires two linked reforms: top-level blockage must become harder to exercise unilaterally, and legitimacy must become more layered than a single global chamber allows.

Security Council reform without regional legitimacy remains too distant and too brittle. Regional legitimacy without Security Council reform remains too vulnerable and too weak. The necessary answer is both together — a Security Council operating on a majority-veto model, and a layered legitimacy architecture in which Regional Security Councils generate first-order consent while broader international institutions authorize, scale, and review collective action.

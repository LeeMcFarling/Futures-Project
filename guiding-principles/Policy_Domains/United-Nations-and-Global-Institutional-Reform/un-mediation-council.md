# UN Reform — Mediation Council and Appellate Review Architecture

## Purpose

This brief proposes the establishment of a standing Mediation Council: a narrow-jurisdiction appellate body, periodically elected by the UN General Assembly, empowered to review contested decisions arising from Regional Security Councils and the reformed Security Council. It is not a shadow world government. It does not govern day-to-day security policy, direct military operations, or replace either regional or global institutions. It exists to review whether legitimacy-generating and enforcement-authorizing bodies are acting within agreed procedural, evidentiary, and anti-capture rules when a decision is formally contested.

The need follows directly from the prior briefs. A majority-veto Security Council reduces unilateral great-power blockage but does not eliminate coalition capture, conflict laundering, or politically distorted obstruction. Regional Security Councils provide first-order legitimacy but remain vulnerable to intimidation, factionalization, and regional asymmetry. If neither level can be trusted absolutely — and neither can — then a durable order requires a review layer capable of assessing contested breakdowns without collapsing back into the same incentives that produced the original failures. The Mediation Council is that layer. It is activated by contest, not by default.

---

## Why a Review Chamber Is Necessary

The bodies that act in the current international system are also, too often, the bodies that decide whether their own action or inaction was legitimate. That design invites deadlock, forum manipulation, and strategic self-justification. A regional body can fail to escalate a crisis because it is captured or intimidated, with no meaningful route upward. A Security Council can block action in a matter where key members hold direct interests in the outcome, with no formal mechanism to contest the blockage on procedural grounds. Under the current structure, those failures have nowhere to go except diplomatic improvisation and geopolitical bargaining among the same actors whose interests are already implicated.

A Mediation Council changes that logic. It does not promise perfect neutrality. It creates a standing review pathway for structured second-order judgment: a formal mechanism to ask not only whether a crisis exists, but whether the bodies empowered to interpret and respond to that crisis are doing so under legitimate conditions.

---

## Core Design Principle

The Mediation Council's jurisdiction is procedural, not open-endedly political. It reviews whether agreed thresholds were met, whether coercion or capture likely distorted a process, whether a blocking coalition was validly assembled, whether recusal rules were properly applied, whether a regional finding was procedurally sound, and whether a contested measure should continue, be narrowed, or be remanded. It does not determine the substantive content of security policy.

This distinction is load-bearing. A review body with unlimited substantive discretion would quickly become another arena for great-power struggle under different branding. A review body with narrow procedural jurisdiction can serve a genuinely constitutional function: it prevents the rest of the system from becoming judge in its own cause without substituting a new form of unchecked authority in its place.

---

## Institutional Structure

The Mediation Council is constituted through periodic election by the UN General Assembly from a standing pool of member states. Membership rotates on a fixed cadence. The purpose of periodic refresh is to reduce ossification, prevent review authority from becoming an entrenched geopolitical asset, and create lawful pathways for the composition of the review layer to evolve as the broader distribution of power and legitimacy shifts over time.

The Council does not sit as a single fixed body on every matter. The General Assembly elects a standing pool; individual review panels are drawn from that pool for specific cases through pre-agreed assignment rules rather than ad hoc political bargaining at the moment of crisis. This separates the legitimacy of institutional design from the politics of case-level assignment — two things the current system conflates constantly.

---

## Blind Assignment and Anti-Retaliation Logic

If states know in advance which reviewing members will hear a contested case, threatened or interested actors can pressure those members before the process begins. A review body designed to resist capture must therefore be procedurally insulated from that pressure.

Panel assignment should therefore be blind at the deliberative stage. The standing pool is elected openly. The assignment rules are published. Conflict-screening criteria are known in advance. But the specific panel selected for a given review is not disclosed until after conflict checks are completed and the formal process begins. This reduces the ability of powerful actors to pre-target reviewers, limits coercive pre-hearing bargaining, and gives smaller states greater confidence that participation in review will not immediately expose them to retaliation.

Blind assignment is not secrecy for its own sake. It is an anti-capture mechanism. The legitimacy of the review depends in part on the degree to which the reviewed party cannot predict and pressure its judges before they convene.

---

## Conflict Screening and Selective Recusal

The Mediation Council cannot be legitimate if materially conflicted states sit in review of matters where they hold direct strategic, financial, military, or infrastructural stakes. Formal equality among states is not sufficient where structural entanglement predictably distorts judgment.

Conflict screening should apply not only to direct belligerency or formal alliance ties, but to material entanglements whose full scope is addressed in the next brief: major arms relationships, sovereign financing exposure, critical commodity dependence, infrastructure ownership, sanctions exposure, or trade concentration significant enough that the outcome of a decision could plausibly affect the reviewer's own strategic or economic position. These criteria should be pre-published, rule-bound, and administered by an independent procedural secretariat rather than left to self-declaration.

States that fail screening are not excluded from the international system. They are recused from that review. The constitutional principle is simple: no state should sit in judgment where its own exposure is large enough that the outcome would reasonably affect its interests.

---

## Jurisdictional Triggers

The Mediation Council hears only defined classes of contested, system-significant cases. Narrow trigger conditions are necessary to keep the body governable and to preserve its constitutional role rather than converting it into a general-purpose appeals forum for every international disagreement.

The following trigger classes are illustrative.

A Regional Security Council issues a finding of illegitimate external interference and the Security Council blocks or dilutes response under contested procedural conditions.

A Regional Security Council fails to act despite pre-agreed evidentiary thresholds being met, and affected parties allege regional capture, coercion, or intimidation as the cause.

A Security Council blocking coalition is alleged to include materially conflicted states or to have been assembled through coercive leverage inconsistent with the system's procedural rules.

An emergency directive, sanctions package, or enforcement measure is challenged as exceeding the authorized procedural basis under the reformed architecture.

A matter satisfies objective escalation triggers but is stalled by unresolved disputes over threshold satisfaction, proportionality, or legitimacy of the reviewing body.

These triggers define the category, not an exhaustive list. The common element is contested procedural integrity in a system-significant case.

---

## Powers of the Mediation Council

The Mediation Council holds four core powers.

**Certification power.** It may affirm that the relevant body acted within agreed rules, allowing its decision to proceed with institutional legitimacy intact.

**Remand power.** It may return a matter to the originating body for renewed deliberation, narrower framing, additional evidence, or corrected application of the rules.

**Stay power.** It may impose a temporary pause on escalatory measures where credible claims of coercion, conflict violation, or procedural abuse require urgent review before the measure takes full effect.

**Override referral power.** Where a regional body is clearly captured or a higher body is procedurally blocked despite threshold satisfaction, it may refer the matter upward into the next review or enforcement track under pre-agreed escalation rules.

These powers are substantial but remain procedural. The Mediation Council does not itself become the permanent author of sanctions, military campaigns, or open-ended intervention. It governs whether the bodies that authorize those things are doing so under legitimate conditions.

---

## Relationship to Regional Councils and the Security Council

The Mediation Council does not supersede Regional Security Councils or the reformed Security Council. It sits between them as a constitutional review layer.

Regional Security Councils remain the generators of first-order regional legitimacy. The Security Council remains the body that authorizes scaled international response and opens the enforcement ladder. The Mediation Council intervenes only when one of those bodies is credibly alleged to be captured, conflicted, coercively distorted, or procedurally invalid in a way that threatens the integrity of the reformed architecture.

Activation by contest rather than by default is deliberate. If review becomes automatic in every serious case, the system becomes too slow to function. If review is unavailable in contested cases, the layered architecture collapses into trust in whatever body acted first. The Mediation Council exists to manage that middle space — available when legitimacy is genuinely contested, not required as a standing overhead on every decision.

---

## Why General Assembly Election Matters

The review function cannot be credible if it is monopolized by the same powers already dominant in the core enforcement architecture. General Assembly election broadens the source of review legitimacy, creates a lawful mechanism for periodic refresh, and prevents appellate authority from becoming another form of entrenched great-power privilege.

Periodic refresh matters for a separate reason. As established in the diagnosis brief, a stable international order must be revision-capable without being collapse-prone. Review authority, like representation more broadly, should not be frozen indefinitely. If the composition of the Mediation Council cannot evolve as new centers of power and legitimacy emerge, it will eventually be perceived — correctly — as a mechanism designed by incumbents to perpetuate their own interpretive authority. General Assembly election with fixed rotation is the structural answer to that risk.

---

## What This Body Does Not Do

The Mediation Council does not determine substantive foreign policy. It does not become a standing executive over force. It does not replace national sovereignty, regional consent, or Security Council authorization. It is not a shortcut around politics, and it cannot eliminate bad-faith action from the international system.

What it does is narrower and more durable: it creates a place where the procedural legitimacy of contested high-stakes decisions can be formally examined without immediately collapsing into the same actors, incentives, and pressures that produced the dispute. In a layered legitimacy architecture, that is not decorative. It is the mechanism that prevents each layer from becoming judge in its own cause — and therefore the mechanism that makes the other layers trustworthy enough to use.

---

## Strategic Effect

The Mediation Council changes the incentives of the broader system in three important ways.

First, it raises the cost of procedural manipulation. Bodies acting under capture, conflict, or coercion now face the prospect of formal review rather than assuming their decision is final because they spoke first or loudest.

Second, it reduces reliance on ad hoc diplomatic improvisation when institutional legitimacy breaks down. Instead of every contested case becoming a bespoke geopolitical bargain, the system has a standing review pathway that does not require creating new institutions under crisis pressure.

Third, it strengthens rather than weakens the authority of the regional and Security Council layers. Authority is easier to trust — and harder to dismiss — when it is reviewable rather than absolute.

Taken together, these effects change the central question the international system asks when a crisis becomes contested. Under the current order, that question collapses quickly into who is strongest in the moment. Under a system with a functioning review layer, it becomes whether the process can survive scrutiny. That is a different kind of politics — slower to abuse, harder to capture, and less rewarding of pure coercive leverage.

---

## Bottom Line

A layered legitimacy architecture without a review chamber is not a constitutional order. It is a set of institutions, each of which can become judge in its own cause when the political pressure is high enough. Regional bodies can be captured. Security Council coalitions can be conflicted. Enforcement measures can be procedurally abused. If those failures have nowhere to go except back into geopolitical bargaining among the same actors already implicated, the architecture will reproduce the failure modes it was built to escape.

The Mediation Council is the layer that prevents that reproduction from being automatic. Its value is not that it eliminates political struggle. It is that it changes where that struggle occurs — from raw bargaining among implicated actors to structured procedural review under published rules, blind assignment, and conflict screening. That is the difference between an international order that degrades gracefully under stress and one that inverts.

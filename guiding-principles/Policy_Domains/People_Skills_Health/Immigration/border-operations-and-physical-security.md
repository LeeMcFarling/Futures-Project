## Core principle

Border security exists to enforce lawful entry, disrupt criminal networks, and prevent
dangerous irregular migration. It is operational, evidence-based, and focused on
deterrence through certainty rather than symbolism.

The border is treated as a dynamic security environment, not a political gesture.

---

## Persistent monitoring and detection

The border is continuously monitored using layered surveillance systems.

Core components include:
- Persistent aerial monitoring using drones
- Ground-based sensor networks (motion, seismic, acoustic)
- Fixed and mobile camera systems
- Integrated command-and-control centers

This layered approach enables early detection, rapid response, and wide-area coverage
without relying solely on physical barriers.

---

## Evidence-based physical barriers

Physical barriers are deployed only where empirical evidence shows they reduce crossings
or channel movement into controlled interdiction zones.

Barriers are:
- Targeted, not universal
- Continuously evaluated
- Modified or removed if ineffective

Effectiveness is measured by reductions in illegal crossings, interdiction success, and
cost efficiency relative to technological alternatives.

---

## Predictive enforcement and dynamic deployment

Border enforcement uses predictive modeling to anticipate crossing attempts and
smuggling activity.

Models incorporate:
- Historical crossing data
- Terrain and weather patterns
- Intelligence on smuggling routes
- Cartel adaptation behavior

Personnel and equipment are surged dynamically to high-risk areas rather than being
fixed permanently in low-impact zones.

---

## Joint U.S.–Mexico enforcement coordination

Border security is strengthened through continued coordination between U.S. and Mexican
authorities.

This includes:
- Coordinated patrol operations
- Intelligence sharing on trafficking networks
- Deconfliction protocols to close enforcement gaps
- Joint targeting of smuggling corridors

The focus of cooperation is criminal organizations, not migrants.

---

## Counter-trafficking deterrence and penalties

Drug trafficking and human trafficking are treated as top-tier crimes with severe,
mandatory penalties.

Deterrence is reinforced through:
- Maximum federal sentences for trafficking offenses
- Asset seizure and permanent financial incapacitation
- Racketeering and conspiracy charges targeting leadership networks
- Extraterritorial prosecution authority where applicable

Penalties and consequences are communicated explicitly at ports of entry and border
crossings. Individuals entering the United States are clearly informed—through signage
and official documentation—that trafficking offenses carry the most severe penalties
under U.S. law.

This mirrors high-deterrence models used in other countries: The goal is clarity and deterrence.

---

## Processing and transfer

Individuals interdicted at the border are processed rapidly and consistently.

Processing includes:
- Identity verification and biometric collection
- Security screening
- Determination of lawful status
- Transfer to return, detention, or prosecution pathways

There is no release pending future adjudication outside lawful channels.

---

## Integration with asylum and immigration systems

Border operations do not function as intake for asylum or labor migration.

Individuals encountered at the border are routed according to system rules:
- Lawful entrants proceed through designated pathways
- Unauthorized entrants are processed for return
- Criminal activity is prosecuted

This preserves the integrity of upstream immigration and humanitarian systems.

---

## Plain-English explanation

The border is monitored constantly so dangerous crossings and criminal smuggling can be
stopped before people get hurt.

Instead of guessing where problems will occur, the system uses data and technology to
deploy resources where they actually work. Physical barriers are used only when they
help.

Traffickers are met with clear, severe consequences, and those consequences are made
explicit in advance. When rules are visible and enforcement is certain, deterrence
works.

This approach protects communities, supports lawful immigration, and reduces suffering
by making illegal crossings and trafficking both dangerous and pointless.

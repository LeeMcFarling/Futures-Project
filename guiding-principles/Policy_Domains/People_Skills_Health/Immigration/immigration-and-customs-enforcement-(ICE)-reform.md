## Purpose

Immigration enforcement is a necessary function of a sovereign state. It upholds lawful
entry, enforces removal where required, and dismantles criminal networks that exploit
people and undermine public safety.

However, enforcement must operate as **civil law enforcement**, not as a paramilitary
force. Recent practices have damaged public trust. These reforms preserve enforcement
capability while clearly bounding authority, conduct, and oversight.

---

## Core reform objectives

1. Restore civil-law enforcement norms  
2. Make enforcement activity visible and auditable  
3. Ensure humane treatment and due process  
4. Establish independent oversight and accountability  

---

## Operational transparency standards

All enforcement operations involving detention, search, or use of force are subject to
mandatory transparency requirements.

- Officers must wear clearly identifiable uniforms and visible badges  
- No face coverings during enforcement operations except for documented safety needs  
- No unmarked vehicles for routine enforcement actions  
- Body-worn cameras must be active during all operational interactions  

Failure to comply constitutes a disciplinary offense.

---

## Body-worn cameras and recording requirements

Body-worn cameras are mandatory for all officers engaged in enforcement actions.

Recorded footage is:
- Time-stamped and tamper-resistant  
- Securely stored and audited  
- Reviewed in misconduct investigations  
- Released in accordance with law and privacy standards  

Body cameras are treated as accountability tools, not discretionary equipment.

---

## Professionalization and training

All enforcement personnel undergo mandatory training and periodic recertification in:

- Constitutional and civil rights law  
- De-escalation and crisis response  
- Use-of-force standards  
- Cultural and language competency  
- Procedural justice  

Training completion is required for operational authorization.

---

## Detention standards and congressional oversight

Detention under immigration enforcement authority is administrative, not punitive.

All detention facilities must meet federally defined humane standards, including:
- Adequate medical and mental health care  
- Safe nutrition, sanitation, and temperature control  
- Access to legal counsel and communication  
- Clear maximum detention timelines tied to adjudication or removal  

Conditions may not be used as a deterrent or punishment.

All facilities are subject to regular, unannounced inspection. Members of Congress and
designated congressional staff are granted access for oversight and review, subject to
reasonable security protocols.

Independent oversight bodies are authorized to audit detention conditions, publish
findings, and mandate corrective action. Persistent violations trigger mandatory
intervention, facility decertification, or closure.

---

## Independent oversight and investigations

A permanent, independent oversight authority reviews:
- Use-of-force incidents  
- Allegations of misconduct  
- Systemic enforcement patterns  

The authority has subpoena power, reporting requirements, and public transparency
obligations.

---

## Structural clarification and agency reform

The enforcement agency is explicitly defined as a **civil immigration law enforcement
body**, not a paramilitary organization.

Rebranding and structural reform may be used to signal mission clarity and restore
legitimacy. Any renaming is paired with statutory role definition and oversight, not
cosmetic change alone.

---

## Integration with the immigration system

Enforcement operates separately from asylum adjudication and labor migration programs.

Its role is to:
- Enforce lawful entry requirements  
- Execute departures and removals following final decisions  
- Target criminal trafficking and smuggling networks  

Enforcement actions are logged in real time through SSI-based systems to support
accountability and lawful processing.

---

## Plain-English explanation

We need immigration enforcement for the system to work. But enforcement must follow the
law, be visible to the public, and treat people humanely.

These reforms require officers to show who they are, record what they do, and operate
under clear rules. Detention must meet humane standards and be open to congressional
oversight.

This keeps enforcement strong, lawful, and trusted—so the system can be firm without
being abusive.

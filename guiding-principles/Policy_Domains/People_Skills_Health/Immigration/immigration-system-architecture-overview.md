# System foundation

The system begins with the domestic workforce. People growing up in the United States must have clear visibility into which jobs will be in demand, where those jobs will be located, and what training or credentials are required to access them. The first obligation of immigration policy is therefore informational and preparatory: ensuring that citizens and lawful residents can make rational, informed choices about education and work before external labor is considered.

Labor demand is assessed continuously and transparently. The U.S. Department of the Treasury and the Federal Reserve work in coordination with DoDa and immigration services to model current and projected labor needs, stratified by sector, skill level, and region. These projections are published on a fixed cadence and form the baseline for all downstream immigration decisions.

Only after domestic pipelines are fully addressed does the system authorize immigration to fill verified gaps. Immigration under this framework is limited to specific skills, sectors, and locations where quantified shortages persist. No category is open-ended, and no admission is untethered from demonstrated demand.

Immigration and asylum are fully separated in law and administration. Labor and student migration are governed as economic instruments, with defined caps, timelines, and compliance requirements. Asylum is administered as a humanitarian and legal obligation, governed by service capacity and due process. The two systems are legally firewalled, processed by different departments, and evaluated on different metrics.

All design choices in the system incentivize lawful entry. Legal pathways are fast, transparent, and predictable. Entry outside those pathways reliably fails. When outcomes are clear in advance, compliance becomes the dominant behavior and enforcement becomes a function of system design rather than discretion.

# Border enforcement by design

Border control is strict, predictable, and non-negotiable. Entry into the United States occurs only through designated lawful channels. Enforcement prioritizes speed, clarity, and consistency rather than backlog management or discretionary exception-making. The objective is deterrence through certainty.

When lawful pathways are visible and reliable, and unlawful pathways consistently fail, smuggling activity collapses and compliance rises. Border enforcement under this framework is therefore a downstream consequence of system design, not a stand-alone political posture.

# Legal migration as labor protocols

Labor migration operates through standardized protocols tied to verified labor demand. Short-term and transitional visas are issued only where quantified shortages exist, with predefined wage floors, employer accountability requirements, and duration limits. Limited portability within approved sectors and regions prevents exploitation while preserving labor matching efficiency.

Workers are pre-vetted, background checked, and matched to verified employers before entry. Transport is coordinated directly to destination communities. Exit, renewal, and re-entry eligibility are tracked cleanly, restoring circular labor mobility as a lawful and enforceable feature of the system.

# Student-to-industry pathways

International students educated in the United States may remain temporarily to seek qualifying employment in shortage sectors under clear and time-bound rules. Failure to secure placement within the defined window triggers departure. Successful placement converts to lawful work status.

This pathway treats U.S.-educated talent as a high-signal labor pool while preserving enforcement credibility through predictable outcomes and strict timelines.

# Identity, speed, and compliance infrastructure

The immigration system is migrated over time onto secure SSI rails for identity verification, authorization, payroll integration, tax withholding, and exit confirmation. This infrastructure enables fast adjudication without sacrificing enforcement and makes compliance observable in real time.

Lawful participation is rewarded with priority processing and fast re-entry eligibility. Fraud, overstays, and employer violations result in immediate loss of access across programs.

# Transparency and oversight

Public legitimacy is maintained through mandatory transparency. Anonymized dashboards publish labor demand forecasts, visas issued by sector and region, compliance rates, removals for violation, wage effects, and re-entry utilization. Oversight focuses on outcomes rather than anecdotes.

# Scope and limits

This system is not amnesty. It is not open-ended. It does not rely on discretionary exception-making, and it does not conflate labor migration with humanitarian protection.

It is a closed-loop immigration system designed to align labor supply with demand, enforce borders through predictable rules, and deliver humane and lawful outcomes at scale.


# PUBLIC FACING DRAFT: Immigration and Asylum 

Under this system, immigration and asylum are handled as two different things, on purpose.

Immigration is about jobs, and it starts by looking inward. The system first assesses the people already living and growing up in the United States, identifies which jobs will be in demand, where those jobs will exist, and what training is required to access them. Only after domestic pathways are fully addressed does immigration come into play, and only to fill specific, verified gaps. Immigration allows people to enter the United States to work only when particular skills are needed, in defined sectors and locations, for a known period of time. These pathways are planned in advance, capped, enforced, and tied directly to labor demand.

Asylum is about safety. It exists to protect people who are facing serious harm and who meet legal criteria for protection. It is not a job program and it is not a substitute for immigration policy. It is limited by the capacity of the United States to provide housing, legal processing, and basic services. This is not an open-border policy. The border is closed. Entry into the United States occurs only through lawful channels, and asylum claims are initiated through legal processes in a person’s home country or designated legal intake pathways, not by arriving at the border.

Because these two systems serve different purposes, they are run separately and do not overlap. This separation protects both systems. Immigration can be enforced strictly without cutting off humanitarian protection, and asylum can be administered humanely without becoming an uncontrolled entry mechanism.

Clear rules reduce suffering. When people know in advance which legal paths exist and which do not, fewer people risk dangerous journeys or fall into the hands of smugglers. The goal is not ambiguity or discretion, but clarity and predictability.

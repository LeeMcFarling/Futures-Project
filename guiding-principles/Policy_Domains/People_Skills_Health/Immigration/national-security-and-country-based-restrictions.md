## Purpose

Immigration policy must account for national security realities. Certain states impose
legal, coercive obligations on their citizens that create unavoidable conflicts of
interest when individuals work in sensitive sectors of the U.S. economy.

This brief establishes a clear, categorical system for country-based restrictions that
protect national security without relying on ad hoc adjudication or discriminatory
case-by-case discretion.

## Core principle

Risk is assessed at the **state and sector level**, not through subjective judgments
about individuals.

Where foreign law, intelligence posture, or state behavior creates systemic risk,
restrictions apply predictably and transparently.

## Country-based risk classification

Countries are periodically classified into risk tiers based on factors including:

- Laws compelling citizens to act on behalf of the state or intelligence services
- Active cyber, economic, or industrial espionage campaigns
- Adversarial military or intelligence posture toward the United States
- Lack of judicial independence or exit protections for citizens abroad

Classifications are reviewed on a fixed cadence and published with clear criteria.

## Sector sensitivity tiers

Not all jobs carry the same level of risk. Immigration pathways are therefore stratified
by sector sensitivity.

High-sensitivity sectors include, but are not limited to:

- Defense and aerospace
- Semiconductors and advanced manufacturing
- Artificial intelligence and compute infrastructure
- Biotechnology and life sciences
- Energy systems and critical infrastructure
- Telecommunications and data networks

Lower-sensitivity sectors may allow broader access under standard labor rules.

## Restriction mechanics

For high-risk country and sector combinations:

- Student visas may be limited or excluded for sensitive fields
- Work visas are restricted or prohibited
- No discretionary waivers exist outside narrowly defined statutory exceptions

Restrictions apply at the program level, not through informal guidance.

## Interaction with student and labor pathways

Country-based restrictions override general eligibility under student-to-industry and
labor migration programs.

This ensures that retention and recruitment mechanisms do not become security
workarounds.

## Enforcement and administration

Restrictions are administered centrally and enforced automatically through SSI-based
systems. Eligibility determinations are binary and fast, reducing uncertainty for
applicants and institutions.

This approach favors clarity over false flexibility.

## Periodic review and adjustment

Risk classifications and sector definitions are reviewed regularly to reflect changes in
law, geopolitics, and technology.

Restrictions can be tightened or relaxed based on evidence, not political pressure.

## Plain-English explanation

Some countries require their citizens to help their government, even when they are
abroad. That creates real risks in sensitive jobs.

Instead of pretending those risks do not exist, this system deals with them directly.

If a country poses a security risk in certain industries, people from that country
cannot work in those fields here. The rules are clear, published, and applied the same
way every time.

This protects national security without shutting down legal immigration where it is
safe and appropriate.

## Purpose

The Futures Project fiscal architecture is not a spending program. It is a
structural redesign of how the federal government raises revenue, deploys
capital, and constrains costs across the three largest drivers of long-run
fiscal pressure: the tax code's structural escape hatches, the healthcare
cost curve, and defense procurement inefficiency.

This brief assembles the fiscal savings and revenue case in one place. Each
of the underlying mechanisms is established in detail in the relevant policy
briefs. This document makes the combined picture legible — what the system
produces, on what timeline, and where the honest uncertainties lie.

The argument is not that this platform eliminates the deficit through
accounting. It is that it addresses the structural causes of fiscal
deterioration rather than managing its symptoms, and that addressing causes
produces durable improvement where symptom management has produced decades
of drift.

## The Dinner Has Already Been Eaten

Before establishing what this platform produces, it is necessary to establish
what the current trajectory costs. The deficit does not exist because the
government has been too generous with ordinary Americans. It exists because
the government has been borrowing money in the name of ordinary Americans
and distributing the proceeds primarily to those who needed it least, without
being honest about what it was doing.

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 reduced federal revenue by approximately
$1.9 trillion over its first decade — a current-law estimate based on
CBO and JCT scoring at enactment. The individual provisions — the rate
reductions, the expanded standard deduction, the pass-through deduction —
were written as temporary. They expire at the end of 2025. The current
political debate refers to their extension colloquially as the "Big
Beautiful Bill" or TCJA extension; this platform refers to both simply
as the same underlying choice: extending deficit-financed tax provisions
or allowing current law to stand. Extending them costs approximately
$3.5 to $4 trillion over the subsequent ten years relative to current
law — a figure drawn from CBO's current-law baseline and broadly
consistent across independent scorekeepers. That extension is being
discussed not as new spending but as a tax cut, even though it represents
the continuation of deficit financing that has already occurred.

This is the restaurant check problem stated precisely. The dinner happened.
The people at the table ate. The bill is real and accumulating interest.
The debate now is not whether to pay it but who pays it and when. Extending
the individual provisions without offsetting revenue is a decision to hand
the check to the next generation with compounding interest attached —
a decision dressed up as a tax cut.

This platform does not extend the individual provisions on a deficit-financed
basis. It allows them to expire on current law and replaces the resulting
revenue baseline with a cleaner, broader, and more structurally sound
revenue architecture. The political claim that this represents a tax increase
is accurate only for the narrow slice of the population that benefited most
from the provisions and will no longer benefit from their extension. For
ordinary Americans — those below $50 million in total assets and below the
sustained corporate reciprocity threshold — the tax architecture this
platform establishes is not more burdensome than the current system. It is
simpler, more predictable, and more honest about what is actually happening.

## The Revenue Architecture

The four-document tax architecture does not produce revenue through a single
large rate increase. It produces revenue through a combination of mechanisms
that compound over time and that address the structural sources of base
erosion rather than simply taxing the base more aggressively.

**Base broadening through expiration and conversion.** Allowing the TCJA
individual provisions to expire on current law restores approximately
$300-400 billion in annual revenue relative to an extension baseline —
a current-law estimate consistent with CBO scoring. Converting tax
expenditures from off-budget hidden subsidies into explicit PCA
instruments does not produce immediate cash revenue, but it eliminates
the forgone revenue that currently flows to rent-seeking without
accountability. The Congressional Budget Office estimates that tax
expenditures cost approximately $1.7 trillion annually in forgone
revenue — a benchmark figure that has been relatively stable across
recent years. Not all of that is convertible, and conversion is phased
over years rather than immediate. But recovering 20-30% of that base
through the conversion doctrine — a conservative scenario given the
concentration of expenditures in a small number of sectors — produces
$300-500 billion annually at full operation.

**Corporate reciprocity floor.** The $100 million average book profit
threshold at 15% effective rate produces revenue from the population of
large profitable corporations that currently pay negligible federal income
tax through persistent engineering. The static estimate from a provision
of this structure is in the range of $200-300 billion over ten years —
a policy-range estimate based on the population of firms that would fall
within the dual-gate threshold. The behavioral response — corporations
investing genuinely in domestic capacity to reduce their reciprocity
liability through credits — is partially offsetting in direct revenue
terms but is the intended effect of the mechanism. Behavioral response
is not modeled at a specific figure because it depends on investment
decisions that are genuinely uncertain; the range above reflects a
post-behavioral conservative scenario.

**Realization parity at extreme scale.** The borrowing-triggered partial
realization mechanism and the dynasty tax framework together close the
primary structural paths through which extreme accumulated appreciation
has permanently escaped the tax system. Revenue from these mechanisms is
event-dependent and therefore lumpy year to year, but substantial in
aggregate over a decade. The Federal Reserve's distributional financial
accounts estimate the top 1% holds approximately $15-20 trillion in
unrealized capital gains — a benchmark reference figure, not a revenue
projection. Even modest behavioral shifts on that base — some fraction
choosing to sell rather than borrow, some fraction prepaying dynasty tax
obligations rather than waiting for estate settlement — produce recognition
events that would not otherwise have occurred. A conservative scenario
for the combined ten-year revenue effect from these mechanisms, accounting
for behavioral response, is in the range of $500 billion to $1 trillion.
The range is wide because the behavioral response is genuinely uncertain;
the figure should be read as an order-of-magnitude estimate rather than
a precise projection.

**Compliance and administrative recovery.** IRS modernization, the
immutable government ledger, and the automated income attestation
infrastructure together reduce leakage from the existing tax base. The
IRS has estimated the tax gap — the difference between taxes owed and
taxes collected — at approximately $600 billion annually, a figure the
agency itself treats as a benchmark estimate with meaningful uncertainty.
Administrative modernization cannot recover all of that, but recovering
20-30% through better reporting infrastructure, faster detection, and
lower compliance friction for ordinary taxpayers while increasing
detection capability for sophisticated evasion is an implementation-
sensitive range consistent with what peer tax authorities have achieved
with comparable infrastructure investments. That represents $120-180
billion annually at steady state.

The honest combined picture is this: the revenue architecture, fully
phased in over a decade and accounting for behavioral response, produces
structural annual revenue improvement in the range of $800 billion to $1.2
trillion compared to the extension baseline — which is the comparison that
matters politically, because the alternative being offered is not the status
quo but $3.5-4 trillion in additional deficit financing.

## The Healthcare Cost Curve

Healthcare is the largest single driver of long-run federal fiscal
deterioration. Federal healthcare spending — Medicare, Medicaid, ACA
subsidies, CHIP, and related programs — currently runs at approximately
$1.7 trillion annually and is projected to reach $3.5-4 trillion by 2040
on the current trajectory. The trajectory is not primarily a function of
aging population demographics, though that matters. It is primarily a
function of healthcare being structurally more expensive in the United
States than in every peer economy by a factor of roughly two, with no
commensurate improvement in outcomes.

The care delivery reforms, insurance architecture, and pharmaceutical cost
framework this platform establishes do not solve that problem completely
or immediately. But they attack its structural causes in ways that the
current system does not.

Removing insurance from primary and urgent care eliminates the
administrative overhead that currently runs at 25-30% of spending in
those segments. The McKinsey Global Institute estimated in 2019 that
administrative complexity accounts for approximately $265 billion in
annual healthcare waste — a figure that has grown since. Standardized
APIs, real-time payment settlement, and the elimination of prior
authorization and manual billing processes convert that overhead from a
structural feature of the system into an addressable inefficiency. This
does not happen in year one. The infrastructure phase-in is realistic
at a 5-7 year horizon. But at full operation, administrative cost
reduction alone is meaningful at the federal level because administrative
overhead is embedded in the prices Medicare and Medicaid pay.

Emergency utility regulation constrains pricing in the segment where
the gap between cost and charge is most extreme. Hospital emergency
departments currently charge on average 340% of Medicare rates for
the same services, according to RAND Corporation research. Regulated
rate bands tied to audited cost inputs do not eliminate that gap
immediately, but they change the structural logic of emergency pricing
from unconstrained monopoly markup to cost-plus utility return. A 15%
reduction in emergency and inpatient pricing relative to trend over
ten years, applied to the federal payer share of those services,
produces savings in the range of $150-250 billion over the decade.

The pharmaceutical cost architecture — recoupment-bound pricing, PBM
reform, and public capital drug discovery — attacks drug costs from the
access and pricing side simultaneously. The United States pays
approximately 2.5 times what peer countries pay for the same branded
drugs. Recoupment-bound pricing that transitions to cost-plus after
documented development costs are recovered does not eliminate innovation
incentives — it eliminates indefinite monopoly extraction after those
incentives have already produced their intended outcome. For the federal
government, which is the largest drug purchaser in the world, negotiating
from a cost-plus baseline rather than from a list-price negotiation
produces savings that independent estimates place at $150-400 billion
over ten years depending on the scope of implementation.

The public health and nutrition stack — ingredient disclosure, advertising
standards, demand-signal infrastructure, food research — is a longer
horizon investment. Chronic disease, driven primarily by diet and metabolic
dysfunction, accounts for approximately 90% of the $4.1 trillion in annual
U.S. healthcare spending. Shifting consumption patterns meaningfully takes
a generation, not a decade. But the fiscal case for the investment is
straightforward: a 1% reduction in the chronic disease burden over twenty
years produces fiscal savings an order of magnitude larger than the
investment required. This platform makes that investment. The fiscal
return is real but long-dated.

The honest combined healthcare picture is: structural cost reduction
mechanisms that produce $400-800 billion in federal savings over ten
years at realistic implementation pace, with a larger long-run trajectory
shift that compounds over the subsequent decade as the care delivery and
pharmaceutical reforms reach full operation.

## Defense Procurement Savings

Defense procurement is the third major driver of fiscal inefficiency,
and it is the one where the current system has the most thoroughly
documented and self-acknowledged failure record.

The Government Accountability Office has tracked major defense acquisition
programs continuously for decades. Its most recent annual report found
that the 33 largest programs in its portfolio show total cost growth of
$628 billion from their original estimates — a documented benchmark figure
from GAO's own portfolio analysis, not a policy-range estimate — not from
scope expansion or unanticipated technical challenges, but from the
structural features of single-prime lifecycle contracting, schedule
slippage, and the absence of ongoing competitive pressure to discipline
cost.

The continuous competitive procurement framework attacks that directly.
By replacing single-prime lifecycle contracts with contestable production
portfolios — multiple certified manufacturers producing subsystems
simultaneously, with production allocation adjusted on 24-36 month cycles
based on measured performance — the framework eliminates the structural
incentive for cost escalation that single-prime arrangements create.
When a contractor knows they retain production share regardless of cost
performance because there is no credible alternative source, the rational
behavior is to maximize revenue per unit, not minimize cost. The continuous
competitive model changes that incentive completely: underperforming on
cost, surge capacity, or reliability loses market share to a competitor
who is already producing the same subsystem.

A critical clarification belongs here: this is not a proposal to cut
defense capability. It is a proposal to buy the same or greater capability
at lower lifecycle cost through contestable production and modular
architecture. The competitive model produces more reliable systems,
better surge capacity, and greater industrial resilience than the
single-prime model — the capability argument and the fiscal argument point
in the same direction.

The N+2 industrial redundancy requirement and the modular open-architecture
mandate are the enabling conditions for ongoing competition. Without
standardized interfaces between subsystems and multiple certified
production nodes, the competitive model cannot be maintained. These
requirements impose transition costs — qualification of new vendors,
validation of modular interfaces, initial redundancy buildout — that
are real and should not be understated. But they are one-time transition
costs that purchase permanent structural discipline, rather than the
ongoing rent extraction that the current model produces.

Quantifying procurement savings with precision is genuinely difficult
because the comparison is against a counterfactual rather than a fixed
baseline. The $628 billion in documented GAO cost overruns is a floor for
what structural reform avoids on the next generation of programs. A
conservative scenario based on competitive procurement pilots — including
the Army's Stryker and helicopter programs, which showed 10-20% lifecycle
cost reduction under sustained competition — applied to the roughly
$300 billion in annual major defense acquisition spending, produces an
implementation-sensitive range of $400-500 billion in savings over ten
years. This range is weighted toward years 5-10 as legacy contracts roll
off and competitive discipline accumulates across new programs.

## The Capital Recycling Flywheel

The mechanism that makes the fiscal picture compound over time rather
than front-loading costs and back-loading returns is the Public Capital
Authority's recycling architecture.

Under the current system, public capital deployed through tax preferences,
grants, and appropriations is consumed rather than recycled. It leaves
the system. The $1.7 trillion in annual tax expenditures, the direct
spending on programs that produce no measurable return, the procurement
contracts that inflate costs without corresponding capability — none of
this comes back. It is spent once and gone.

Under the PCA model, capital deployed as ultra-low-interest loans,
revenue-linked repayments, equity-like stakes, and milestone-based grants
with clawbacks returns to the system when the intervention succeeds. It
is then redeployed. The fiscal effect compounds: not only does the initial
deployment produce its intended outcome, but the returned capital finances
the next round of deployment without requiring additional appropriations.

This is not a promise that all PCA investments will succeed. The framework
explicitly expects losses within approved risk parameters and treats
opacity as the real failure rather than loss itself. But even a recovery
rate of 60-70% on deployed instruments — conservative relative to what
development finance institutions have achieved at comparable scale —
produces a compounding capital base that grows over time rather than
requiring constant infusion from general revenue.

The fiscal implication is that the PCA architecture gradually reduces the
annual appropriations burden for public investment rather than increasing
it. Programs that currently require annual appropriations renewal because
they consume rather than recycle capital are replaced by instruments that
partially finance themselves. That shift does not happen overnight, and
it does not produce dramatic near-term budget savings. But at full
operation over a decade, the recycling effect meaningfully reduces the
gross federal spending required to achieve the same investment outcomes.

## The Honest Caveats

This brief would be dishonest if it did not name the genuine uncertainties
in the fiscal picture.

**Behavioral response reduces revenue.** Every mechanism in the tax
architecture is designed to change behavior, and behavior change reduces
the static revenue estimate. The borrowing trigger makes selling
genuinely competitive with borrowing — which is the point. When people
sell, that produces revenue, but less than the static estimate of what
the trigger itself would have collected if behavior had not changed.
The corporate reciprocity floor incentivizes genuine domestic investment
that reduces liability through credits — which is also the point. Revenue
projections that do not account for these behavioral responses overstate
the fiscal effect. Projections that do account for them show a smaller
but more durable revenue base — one that comes from broader, more genuine
participation in the tax system rather than from higher effective rates
on a shrinking base.

**Healthcare savings require implementation that will be contested.**
The care delivery reforms, insurance restructuring, and pharmaceutical
pricing mechanisms described here face concentrated opposition from
every incumbent stakeholder whose pricing power they constrain. The
savings case assumes successful implementation across a 5-10 year
horizon. Implementation that is partial, delayed, or captured by
incumbent interests produces proportionally smaller savings. The
transition authority, the pilot-first sequencing, and the DoDA
certification architecture are designed to make successful implementation
more likely — but they are not guarantees. The fiscal case is contingent
on execution.

**Defense procurement reform is a ten-year project, not a budget line.**
The savings from continuous competitive procurement accumulate over the
acquisition lifecycle of the systems that enter the new model. Legacy
contracts under the old model do not immediately convert. The first years
of the reform produce higher transition costs — vendor qualification,
interface standardization, industrial redundancy buildout — before the
competitive discipline begins to reduce unit costs. The ten-year savings
estimate is real, but it is weighted toward years five through ten rather
than years one through three.

**The capital recycling flywheel requires time to spin up.** The PCA
begins deploying capital before it begins receiving returns. The recycling
effect that compounds over time requires a ramp-up period of several
years before returned capital represents a meaningful share of annual
deployment capacity. The fiscal benefit is real but back-loaded.

## The Combined Picture

A fiscal architecture that addresses structural causes rather than
symptoms does not produce clean, auditable ten-year scores in the format
that Congressional Budget Office scoring requires. That is an honest
limitation of the presentation, not a weakness of the underlying case.
CBO's methodology captures some effects well — direct revenue changes,
first-order spending changes, and near-term behavioral responses are
estimated with reasonable confidence. It captures other effects only
partially — multi-year behavioral change, market restructuring, and
administrative modernization gains are harder to score with the same
precision. And it misses compounding institutional redesign almost
entirely — the effect of the PCA recycling architecture, the long-run
healthcare cost curve shift, and the structural discipline imposed by
continuous competitive procurement accumulate in ways that conventional
ten-year windows and static-to-dynamic scoring conventions are not
designed to measure. The same limitation applies symmetrically to the
alternative: the true fiscal cost of extending deficit-financed tax
cuts, allowing healthcare costs to continue inflating at twice the rate
of GDP, and continuing to procure defense systems through single-prime
contracts with no competitive discipline is also not fully captured by
conventional estimates that project current trends linearly.

The platform's fiscal case rests on three claims that are individually
modest and collectively substantial.

The first is that allowing the TCJA individual provisions to expire and
replacing the resulting baseline with a cleaner, broader revenue
architecture produces significantly better fiscal outcomes than extending
those provisions on a deficit-financed basis. The comparison is not
against a tax-free utopia. It is against $3.5-4 trillion in additional
debt.

The second is that structural cost reform in healthcare and defense
procurement — the two largest discretionary and mandatory spending
drivers — produces real savings that accumulate over the decade and
compound over the subsequent decade as reforms reach full operation.
The mechanisms are specific, the savings channels are identified, and
the estimates are conservative relative to the documented scale of
current inefficiency.

The third is that a capital deployment architecture that recycles public
investment rather than consuming it gradually reduces the gross annual
appropriations burden for achieving the same investment outcomes,
producing a long-run fiscal effect that conventional near-term scoring
cannot fully capture.

Together, these three claims describe a fiscal trajectory that is
materially better than the alternative — not because this platform
promises discipline by assertion, but because it changes the structural
architecture that has produced fiscal deterioration rather than
managing its surface manifestations.

## What This Is Not

This brief does not promise deficit elimination on a fixed schedule.
The structural causes of fiscal deterioration accumulated over decades
and will take a decade to address. Anyone who promises rapid elimination
of a deficit this large is either ignoring transition costs or proposing
austerity that would be economically and politically unsustainable.

This brief does not claim that every mechanism will work exactly as
designed. Implementation is contested, behavioral response is uncertain,
and some provisions will be modified in ways that reduce their fiscal
effect. The honest answer is that the platform is more durable than
alternatives because it addresses causes rather than symptoms, and that
durability compounds over time even when individual mechanisms underperform
their projections.

This brief does not pretend that the revenue from closing structural
escape hatches is equivalent to raising taxes on ordinary Americans.
The mechanisms that generate the most significant new revenue — realization
parity at extreme scale, corporate reciprocity, and the dynasty tax —
apply to populations and institutions that have systematically engineered
their contribution to near zero while continuing to draw on the public
systems that enabled their success. Closing those escape hatches is not
a tax increase on the median household. It is the end of a structural
subsidy that has flowed in the wrong direction for a generation.

The fiscal case for this platform is not that it is painless. It is
that the pain is distributed honestly, falls primarily on those most
able to bear it and most responsible for the structural problem, and
produces a system that is more durable, more legitimate, and more
capable of funding the public capacity that everyone — including
those who have benefited most from the current structure — depends on.

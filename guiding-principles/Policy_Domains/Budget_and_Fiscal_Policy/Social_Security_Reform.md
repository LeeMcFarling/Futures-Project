Problem Statement

Social Security retirement benefits are financed through a pay-as-you-go model that depends on stable worker-to-retiree ratios. Persistent below-replacement fertility, rising longevity, and slower labor-force growth have placed the system under long-term strain, requiring repeated political interventions to maintain solvency. At the same time, the U.S. supplemental retirement system is fragmented, voluntary, and unevenly adopted, leaving many workers underprepared for retirement despite decades of economic growth.

Incremental adjustments to Social Security can delay insolvency but do not address the structural mismatch between demographics and retirement financing.

⸻

Policy Objective

Replace Social Security retirement accruals for future cohorts with a universal, prefunded superannuation system that provides automatic, portable retirement savings through payroll, reduces long-term government liabilities, and simplifies the existing patchwork of employer-sponsored plans.

⸻

Core Design

Cohort-Based Transition
- All individuals born from a defined cutoff year onward (e.g., 2028) do not accrue Social Security retirement benefits.
- Current retirees and near-retirees remain fully covered under legacy Social Security rules.
- Social Security disability and survivor programs remain intact or are addressed separately.

This preserves the social contract while preventing new demographic liabilities from compounding.

Universal Superannuation Accounts
- Each eligible citizen receives a nationally standardized superannuation account.
- Accounts are individual, portable across jobs, and independent of employer plan design (like direct deposit).
- Assets are legally owned by the individual and protected from employer or government claims.

One-Time Government Seed Capital
- At birth or first eligibility, the government deposits a one-time seed contribution into each superannuation account.

**Purpose:**
- ensure early compounding,
- create universal buy-in,
- reduce dependence on voluntary savings behavior.
- The seed is capped and non-recurring, replacing decades of future Social Security liabilities with a finite, transparent cost.

Standardized Payroll Contributions
- Employers contribute a fixed percentage of wages directly into the worker’s superannuation account via payroll, analogous to direct deposit.
- This replaces the current system of discretionary 401(k) matches and plan-specific administration.
- Employees may optionally contribute additional amounts (percent match, etc.), but participation in the base system is automatic.

**Super-Account Investment Defaults and Guardrails**
- Default investment is a low-fee, diversified lifecycle (target-date) portfolio.
- Limited fund menu to prevent complexity and predatory behavior.
- Strong restrictions on early withdrawals to preserve retirement purpose.

**Retirement Conversion**
- At retirement age, a required portion of accumulated balances is converted into a regulated lifetime income product (public or tightly regulated private annuity, or pooled longevity fund).
- This preserves the core insurance function previously provided by Social Security: protection against outliving assets.

⸻

#### **Why This Is More Efficient**
- Fiscal clarity: replaces an open-ended demographic obligation with a capped seed and finite legacy costs.
- Coverage: automatic enrollment ensures near-universal participation.
- Portability and ease of use for different employers aligns with modern labor mobility.
- Equity: reduces reliance on financial literacy, which currently advantages higher-income workers.
- Administrative simplicity: This system implements one national standard instead of thousands of employer-specific plans.

Workers retain the freedom to use Roth IRAs and other private instruments, but the core retirement pillar becomes simple, universal, and predictable.

⸻

**Macroeconomic Effects**
- Raises national savings and long-term capital formation.
- Reduces future political pressure to raise payroll taxes or cut benefits.
- Converts wage growth directly into retirement wealth rather than future benefit obligations.
- Improves retirement outcomes for workers currently underserved by the 401(k) system.

⸻

**Transition Financing (High-Level)**

Legacy Social Security obligations for existing cohorts remain and are financed separately during the transition period. The superannuation system applies only to future cohorts and does not rely on ongoing Social Security payroll flows.

Supplemental revenue sources, including targeted visa-based programs (see short term labor in immigration), may be earmarked to support seed capital or transition costs and are addressed in separate immigration reform policy.

⸻

Conclusion

This reform modernizes retirement financing by aligning it with demographic reality, labor mobility, and capital formation. By shifting future cohorts to a universal superannuation system, the U.S. can stabilize retirement outcomes, simplify payroll retirement infrastructure, and reduce long-run fiscal risk—without breaking promises to current retirees.

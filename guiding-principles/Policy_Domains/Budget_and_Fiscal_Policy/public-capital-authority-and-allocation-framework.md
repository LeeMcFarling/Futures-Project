# Public Capital Authority and Capital Allocation Framework

## Executive Summary

The American fiscal system currently deploys public capital through two
mechanisms that share a common failure mode. The tax code deploys it
invisibly — through credits, deductions, exemptions, and preferential
rates that function economically as spending but are off-budget, difficult
to audit, and politically entrenched long after their original purpose
has expired. The appropriations process deploys it with so much
procedural overhead that execution becomes nearly impossible. Both
mechanisms obscure the true scale and performance of public investment.
Neither produces the accountability that would allow the system to
learn, correct, and improve.

This framework addresses both failures simultaneously through two
complementary moves.

The first is the conversion doctrine: tax expenditures that function as
sector-specific investment instruments are reclassified as implied
public capital and converted, prospectively, into explicit instruments
deployed through the Public Capital Authority. The tax code narrows to
its proper function — revenue collection — and stops serving as a
shadow investment system whose aggregate cost is measured in hundreds
of billions annually and whose aggregate benefit is largely unmeasured.

The second is the allocation doctrine: public capital is administered
through a legislative-executive execution instrument with a charter
explicitly authorizing it to deploy that capital as disciplined,
recyclable investments rather than as open-ended subsidies. The PCA
absorbs early-stage risk where markets underinvest, exits deliberately
as sectors mature, and recycles returned capital for redeployment.
Every instrument's performance is measured, reported, and subject to
mandatory sunset where outcomes do not materialize.

The end state is a public finance architecture in which the tax code
does one job well and the PCA does a different job well, rather than
one sprawling system failing at both simultaneously.

## The Problem: Two Broken Deployment Mechanisms

### The Tax Expenditure Shadow System

The Congressional Budget Office estimates tax expenditures at
approximately $1.7 trillion annually in forgone revenue — a figure
that has remained relatively stable across recent years and that
exceeds discretionary spending in most major categories. This is not
a minor inefficiency. It is the largest single use of public capital
in the federal system, operating almost entirely outside the scrutiny
applied to direct spending.

The structural failures of the tax expenditure system are not primarily
failures of intent. Many of the provisions that now constitute the
shadow system were enacted with genuine public purposes. The failures
are architectural.

Provisions accumulate without sunset. A temporary incentive enacted
to address a specific market failure becomes permanent through repeated
extension, long after the market failure has been resolved or the
incentive has been captured by actors it was never designed to benefit.
The political economy of sunset is deeply unfavorable: beneficiaries
of existing provisions are organized and motivated, while the public
interest in expiration is diffuse and unorganized. Without structural
sunset architecture, provisions persist by default.

Provisions are evaluated individually rather than at the sector level.
The relevant question is not whether any single credit is producing
marginal positive effects, but whether the aggregate public capital
committed to a sector is producing outcomes proportional to its cost.
Line-by-line evaluation, conducted when it is conducted at all, cannot
answer that question. It can only assess individual provisions in
isolation, missing the redundancy, concentration, and capture effects
that only become visible at the portfolio level.

Complexity becomes a subsidy for scale. A firm with sufficient
resources to maintain a team whose primary function is navigating and
exploiting the tax code extracts far more value from the system than
a firm without those resources. This is not a side effect of
complexity. At sufficient scale, it is complexity's primary product.
The tax expenditure system functions as a regressive subsidy disguised
as neutral policy.

The incentive and the outcome are structurally decoupled. A tax
incentive is paid whether or not the behavior it was designed to
incentivize occurs. A firm that would have made a qualifying investment
without the incentive collects it anyway. The public pays twice: once
in forgone revenue and once when that forgone revenue produces no
behavioral change. There is no clawback mechanism, no performance
condition, and no measurement architecture that would reveal the
difference between incentive-induced behavior and subsidized behavior
that would have occurred regardless.

### The Appropriations Micromanagement Failure

Direct appropriations have the opposite failure mode. Where tax
expenditures are too invisible, appropriations are often so heavily
proceduralized that execution becomes nearly impossible. Program
administrators operate under reporting requirements, procurement
rules, and congressional notification thresholds that were designed
to prevent abuse but that, in aggregate, prevent deployment at the
speed and scale that effective public investment requires.

The consequence is a government that is simultaneously large in
process and weak in outcomes. It allocates capital in both directions
— invisibly through the tax code and visibly through appropriations
— without being able to demonstrate, in either case, what that
capital produced. Because it cannot demonstrate what it produced, it
cannot build the political credibility required to invest more of what
works and stop investing in what does not.

## Core Principle

Public capital is a strategic asset to be deployed, not a consumable
entitlement or a passive subsidy mechanism.

The government's role in capital allocation is to absorb early-stage
risk where markets structurally underinvest, build the operating
conditions that make productive private enterprise possible, and exit
deliberately as sectors mature — rather than maintaining permanent
subsidies that create dependency, distort competition, and crowd out
the private investment they were meant to catalyze. The current
system does the opposite, at a cost of approximately $1.7 trillion
annually in opaque, unaccountable mechanisms.

Where government seeks to invest, stabilize, or accelerate development,
it should do so through accountable instruments rather than through
opaque tax preferences. Explicit instruments are visible, measurable,
time-bounded, and recoverable. Opaque preferences are none of these
things.

The discipline applied to the collection side of the fiscal system —
broad base, clean rules, enforceable obligations — applies equally
to the deployment side. A government that demands accountability from
taxpayers while exempting its own capital deployment from equivalent
accountability has not solved the legitimacy problem. It has moved it.

## The Conversion Doctrine

### The Tax Code as Implicit Capital Map

Every tax credit, deduction, exemption, and preferential rate functions
economically as public capital deployed into a sector. The provisions
differ in their legal form, but their economic effect is the same as
direct spending: they transfer value from the public to specific
activities and actors, with the public bearing the cost regardless of
whether the transfer was intentional, measured, or effective.

Recognizing tax expenditures as implicit capital commitments is not a
rhetorical move. It is the analytical precondition for applying to
them the same standards applied to any other public investment: defined
purpose, measurable outcome, time-bounded authority, and mandatory
review against performance.

Treasury maintains a comprehensive live inventory that assigns each
credit, deduction, exemption, and preferential rate to a primary
sector — energy, housing, healthcare, manufacturing, finance, labor,
R&D, agriculture — and a secondary objective — capacity expansion,
risk reduction, price stabilization, innovation, regional development.
The inventory is updated as provisions are enacted, modified, or
expired, and is available to congressional oversight and DoDA review.

Provisions that cannot be cleanly mapped to a sector and objective
fail the first threshold. They represent either genuine revenue-
structure decisions — which belong in the tax code proper — or legacy
preferences whose original purpose is no longer articulable, in which
case they are candidates for immediate sunset review.

Provisions that can be mapped are evaluated at the sector level rather
than individually. The relevant question is not whether a single
provision is producing marginal positive effects. It is whether the
aggregate public capital committed to a sector — across all provisions
that touch it — is producing outcomes proportional to cost, and
whether those outcomes could be achieved more efficiently through
explicit capital instruments.

### Conversion Criteria and Instrument Selection

Where the sector-level evaluation indicates that a tax expenditure
functions as risk capital, operating support, or capacity investment
— rather than as a genuine revenue structure adjustment or household
support mechanism — it is a candidate for conversion into a PCA
instrument.

The conversion default is set deliberately in favor of conversion for
sector-specific investment provisions. The burden of justification
runs toward retention in the tax code, not toward conversion. A
provision that can be replicated as an explicit instrument should be
replicated as an explicit instrument, because explicit instruments
are accountable and tax preferences are not.

Instrument selection follows the nature of the underlying activity.

Where the provision represents risk capital for activities that
markets underinvest in because the risk horizon exceeds private return
cycles — early-stage energy transition, novel pharmaceutical
development, foundational infrastructure — the appropriate instrument
is an ultra-low-interest loan, a revenue-linked repayment instrument,
or a first-loss guarantee. The state absorbs the risk premium that
prevents private capital from engaging at sufficient scale. As the
activity matures and private capital becomes available at market terms,
the public instrument steps back by statute.

Where the provision represents capacity investment in sectors where
scale requires a credible demand signal or supply-chain coordination
that no single private actor can provide — manufacturing corridors,
grid expansion, strategic logistics — the appropriate instrument is
a milestone-based grant with defined performance conditions, an
equity-like stake in a joint venture, or a revolving outcome-based
fund. Capital is deployed against measurable milestones. Successful
deployment triggers drawdown; failure to meet milestones triggers
restructuring or termination.

Where the provision represents long-duration industrial support in
sectors of permanent strategic importance — defense industrial base,
critical mineral supply, essential pharmaceutical manufacturing —
the appropriate instrument is a long-duration equity stake or
revolving fund with mandatory periodic reauthorization. No instrument
is permanent by design. Reauthorization requires demonstrated outcomes
against the capital committed.

### What Stays in the Tax Code

Not every tax preference is a misdirected capital instrument. The
conversion doctrine applies to sector-specific investment provisions,
not to the full range of tax structure decisions.

Revenue-structure decisions — rate schedules, depreciation timelines,
loss carryforward rules, international sourcing standards — are
properly located in the tax code and are not candidates for
conversion. They represent decisions about how income is measured
and when it is recognized, not decisions about where public capital
is deployed.

Household support mechanisms that primarily benefit ordinary families
are similarly not candidates for conversion. The mortgage interest
deduction, the charitable contribution deduction, and child and
dependent care credits are tax structure adjustments that reflect
decisions about the definition of taxable income or the appropriate
relationship between the tax system and essential household activity.
The distinction being drawn is between provisions that serve ordinary
household formation and provisions that function as planning tools
for sector-specific capital deployment or extreme wealth concentration.
The former remains in the code. The latter is what the conversion
doctrine addresses.

Household income-smoothing mechanisms that are most efficiently
delivered through the tax code — the Earned Income Tax Credit in its
current structure, for example — may remain in the code where the
administrative case for retention is stronger than the case for
migration to direct transfer rails. That assessment is made on the
merits of each provision, not by categorical rule.

### Transition Architecture

Conversion is prospective. Firms currently receiving sector-specific
tax preferences under existing law transition into the PCA framework
on defined timetables established by sector. New qualifying activity
in converted sectors defaults into PCA instruments from the effective
date of conversion. Legacy preferences are grandfathered through the
timetable period and then expire. There is no retroactive modification
of activity that already occurred under the legacy structure.

Timetables are set by sector readiness and PCA capacity, not by
political convenience. A sector converts when the PCA has developed
the instrument templates, performance measurement infrastructure,
and sector expertise necessary to administer the conversion without
creating a gap in genuine support for productive activity. Conversion
that outpaces PCA capacity produces a different dysfunction than the
one it was designed to correct.

The transition framework is published in advance. Firms know the
conversion schedule for their sector before it takes effect.

## The Public Capital Authority

### Institutional Design

The Public Capital Authority is established as a legislative-executive
execution instrument with a charter explicitly authorizing it to
administer public funds in pursuit of congressionally defined
objectives.

The PCA operates under the legislative branch and is fully subject
to congressional oversight. Its mandate is to deploy public capital
as disciplined, recyclable investments — as explicit, measurable,
time-bounded instruments — rather than as open-ended subsidies. It
functions as a specialized execution body with the technical,
financial, and operational expertise required to structure, evaluate,
and manage large-scale public investments across multiple sectors
simultaneously.

The PCA does not set tax policy. It does not determine national
priorities. It does not regulate industries. It translates
congressionally defined objectives and validated executive-branch
signals into structured financial instruments, deploys them against
defined performance expectations, and reports results. Outcomes are
monitored independently by the Department of Data and Accountability.
Policy direction comes from Congress. Execution is the PCA's
function. Those roles are not interchangeable.

The PCA's portfolio is organized at the sector level rather than by
individual investment. Each sector portfolio has defined objectives,
risk tolerance parameters, performance metrics, and sunset conditions.
The PCA manages a portfolio of investments within each sector against
those parameters, not a collection of independent deals evaluated in
isolation.

### Why This Is Not a Sovereign Wealth Fund

The PCA is not a sovereign wealth fund. A sovereign wealth fund
centralizes national wealth inside a state-managed portfolio,
accumulates a general national asset base for its own sake, and
makes investment decisions over an indefinite horizon. The PCA does
none of these things.

The PCA administers public capital instruments authorized by Congress:
loans, guarantees, revenue-linked repayment instruments, first-loss
layers, milestone grants, equity-like stakes, and revolving sector
funds. Its role is catalytic and time-bounded. It absorbs early-stage
risk where markets underinvest, builds capacity where public
objectives require coordination, and exits as sectors mature. It does
not accumulate a permanent national portfolio. It does not set
national investment priorities. It does not hold citizen retirement
wealth.

The distributed ownership layer is the universal superannuation
system. Citizens own their individual accounts. The PCA manages
public instruments. The PCA is the allocator of public catalytic
capital; the universal superannuation system is the distributed
ownership layer. The former builds capacity; the latter ensures
citizens own a stake in the capacity being built.

The architecture therefore does not create a sovereign wealth fund
controlled by the state. It creates a national capital-formation
system: disciplined public capital allocation through the PCA,
individually owned superannuation accounts as the citizen-held
counterpart.

### The Performance Architecture

Every PCA instrument is subject to a performance architecture that
applies uniform discipline regardless of sector, instrument type,
or political salience.

Defined objectives are established at deployment. An instrument that
cannot articulate what it is designed to produce — in measurable,
time-bounded terms — is not deployed. Objectives are not aspirational
language. They are the basis against which outcomes are subsequently
measured.

Performance measurement is conducted by the Department of Data and
Accountability, not by the PCA or the agencies whose programs the
PCA is funding. Independent measurement is the architectural
requirement. An institution that measures its own performance is not
accountable — it is narrating. DoDA certifies outcomes against defined
objectives on a cadence established at deployment. The PCA reports
against those certifications. It does not produce its own outcome
determinations.

Sunset is the default. No PCA instrument is permanent by design.
Every instrument has a defined end condition: either the objective
is achieved and the instrument exits, or the instrument reaches the
end of its authorized period and must be reauthorized against
demonstrated performance, or the instrument fails to meet its
performance conditions and is restructured or terminated.
Reauthorization requires affirmative demonstration of outcomes, not
merely the absence of documented failure. The political economy of
sunset is deliberately reversed from the tax expenditure model:
continuation requires justification, not the other way around.

Capital recycling is the expected outcome for recoverable instruments.
Loans are repaid. Equity stakes generate returns or are drawn down
on the defined schedule. Revenue-linked instruments return capital
as the underlying activity produces revenue. Recovered capital
re-enters the PCA portfolio for redeployment rather than reverting
to general revenue. This recycling effect is the mechanism through
which the PCA's capital base compounds over time without requiring
constant infusion from appropriations. A recovery rate of 60-70%
on deployed instruments — conservative relative to what development
finance institutions have achieved at comparable scale — produces
a self-reinforcing capital base that grows in deployment capacity
over time.

Loss is expected and budgeted. The PCA operates within approved risk
parameters that include an expected loss rate for early-stage and
high-risk instruments. Loss within approved parameters is a feature
of disciplined risk-taking, not a failure — it is what enables the
institutional learning that the current system structurally prevents.
Opacity about loss is the failure. The PCA publishes its loss rate,
recovery rate, return on deployed capital, and sector-level
performance against defined benchmarks. That visibility is what
distinguishes disciplined risk-taking from waste: both may produce
losses, but only disciplined risk-taking produces a public record
that improves subsequent deployment decisions.

### The Accountability Standard

The PCA's accountability architecture is the answer to the most
persistent political objection to public investment: that government
cannot deploy capital effectively and that public programs persist
regardless of performance.

That objection has been largely correct about the tax expenditure
system and about many direct spending programs. It is an objection
to opacity and to the absence of structural sunset, not to public
investment as a concept. The PCA is designed to make the objection
inapplicable by building the accountability architecture that has
historically been absent.

Every dollar deployed through the PCA is tracked end-to-end through
the immutable government ledger. Disbursements are attributed to
verified instruments, recorded with the performance conditions they
are subject to, and visible to congressional oversight and DoDA
monitoring. Records cannot be altered after writing. Fraud committed
today carries the risk of discovery not just during the next audit
cycle but indefinitely, as detection models improve against a record
that never changes.

The report card is not optional and not produced on the PCA's own
terms. DoDA publishes aggregate performance dashboards for each sector
portfolio — total capital deployed, recovery rate, loss rate, objective
achievement rate, and comparison against the counterfactual of the
tax expenditure structure it replaced — on a defined annual cadence.
Underperforming portfolios trigger mandatory restructuring review.
Portfolios that persistently fail to meet performance conditions are
wound down.

This is the political accountability claim stated precisely: public
capital deployed through the PCA comes with a report card that is
produced independently, published publicly, and triggers defined
consequences for underperformance. The question is not whether the
government will spend this money responsibly — it is whether the
architecture makes irresponsibility permanently visible and
structurally consequential. The PCA answers that question
affirmatively, not by trusting any particular administration, but
by building the measurement and sunset infrastructure that makes
sustained irresponsibility impossible to hide.

### Sector Portfolio Governance

PCA sector portfolios are the unit of governance, not individual
investments.

Each sector portfolio is established by congressional authorization
specifying the sector, the objectives, the authorized instrument
types, the total capital commitment, the performance metrics, and
the sunset condition. The PCA administers the portfolio within those
parameters. Individual investment decisions within an authorized
portfolio are PCA execution decisions, not congressional
appropriations decisions. This separation is the operational
innovation: Congress defines the portfolio mandate, the PCA executes
against it, and DoDA measures the result. None of these functions
collapse into each other.

The state absorbs early-stage risk where markets underinvest because
the risk horizon exceeds private return cycles — not because the
government is a better investor than private capital in general, but
because there is a specific category of risk that private capital
cannot price at the required scale and time horizon. Foundational
infrastructure, early-stage energy transition, novel pharmaceutical
development, and basic research share the characteristic that their
benefits are large, long-dated, and partly non-excludable — which
makes them systematically underfunded by private capital operating
on commercial return timescales. The PCA absorbs that specific risk
premium. As the risk resolves and private capital can engage at
market terms, the PCA steps back.

Deliberate exit is as important as disciplined entry. A PCA portfolio
that does not exit is a permanent subsidy with better documentation.
Sector maturity triggers a defined drawdown of public capital:
instruments are wound down, stakes are sold, guarantees expire, and
the sector operates on private capital terms. The exit schedule is
established at portfolio inception, not determined retrospectively
based on whether private stakeholders prefer continued public support.

## Relationship to the Tax Architecture

The PCA and the conversion doctrine are the deployment side of the
same fiscal architecture that the tax code brief defines on the
collection side.

The tax code collects revenue from a broad, clean, enforceable base.
The PCA deploys capital through explicit, accountable, recyclable
instruments. Separating these functions — rather than embedding
deployment strategy inside collection machinery — allows each to
specialize in the way that mixing them has prevented for decades.

The conversion doctrine is the bridge between the two. Tax
expenditures that function as investment instruments migrate into the
PCA framework where they can be measured, recycled, and sunset. The
tax code narrows to its proper function. The PCA expands its
deployment capacity as converted capital flows through it. The forgone
revenue that previously disappeared into unaccountable tax preferences
becomes explicit public capital subject to the PCA's performance
architecture.

This is not a net increase in the government's role in the economy.
The spending is already happening — at approximately $1.7 trillion
annually through the tax expenditure system alone, plus direct
appropriations. The change is whether that spending happens through
instruments that produce accountability, measurement, and capital
recovery, or through legacy structures that consume capital without
clear outcomes and persist by political inertia rather than
demonstrated performance.

## Failure Modes

The framework fails if the PCA acquires discretionary political
authority over which sectors and firms receive capital — if portfolio
mandates are set by executive preference rather than congressional
authorization, or if individual investment decisions are made on
political rather than performance grounds. The institutional design
separation between congressional mandate-setting, PCA execution, and
DoDA measurement is the structural safeguard against this failure.
It must be maintained.

It fails if DoDA outcome measurement is captured by the agencies
whose programs it is measuring. Independent measurement requires
institutional independence. An oversight body that reports to the
institution it oversees is not independent — it is embedded.

It fails if the sunset architecture is circumvented through
reauthorization that does not require demonstrated performance. If
continuation requires only the absence of documented failure —
rather than affirmative demonstration of outcomes against the capital
committed — then the PCA replicates the tax expenditure system's
persistence problem in a new institutional form.

It fails if conversion timetables outpace PCA sector expertise,
producing gaps in genuine support for productive activity that were
not present under the legacy tax preference structure. Conversion
that is faster than the PCA's capacity to administer it is not
reform. It is disruption without replacement.

It fails if the recycling architecture is disrupted by political
pressure to redirect returned capital to general revenue rather than
redeployment. The compounding effect that makes the PCA's capital
base grow over time depends on returned capital staying within the
portfolio system. Breaking that cycle for short-term budget optics
converts the PCA from a self-reinforcing investment vehicle into a
one-time spending program.

It fails if the report card is not genuinely independent, genuinely
public, and genuinely consequential. A performance report produced
by the PCA about itself, published in a form accessible only to
specialists, and triggering no defined response to underperformance
is not accountability. It is documentation.

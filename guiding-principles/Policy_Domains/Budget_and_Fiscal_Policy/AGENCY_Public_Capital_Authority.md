# Public Capital Authority (PCA): Agency Overview

Purpose
The Public Capital Authority is an executive-branch agency responsible for deploying public capital as disciplined, recyclable investment rather than opaque subsidy. Its mandate is to allocate capital in ways that expand national productive capacity, reduce systemic risk, and generate measurable long-term returns—financial, economic, or strategic—within parameters set by Congress.

The PCA does not set tax policy, define national priorities, or regulate industries. It converts legislatively authorized objectives into capital instruments and manages them as portfolios.

---

Core Functions
1.	Capital Instrument Design
    - Standardize and administer a limited set of capital tools: ultra-low-interest loans, repayable credits, revenue-linked repayment, equity-like stakes, revolving funds, equity stakes, and milestone-based grants with clawbacks.
2.	Sectoral Portfolio Management
    - Manage capital allocations at the sector level (energy, agriculture, housing, health, manufacturing, infrastructure, etc.), with explicit risk bands, time horizons, and expected outcomes.
3.	Tax Expenditure Conversion
    - Receive mapped tax expenditures from Treasury and design prospective conversion pathways into explicit capital instruments, with grandfathering and opt-in transition rules.
4.	Performance Measurement & Recycling
    - Track outcomes end-to-end, retire underperforming instruments, recycle returned capital, and publish standardized public reports.

⸻

Organizational Structure
	•	Central Authority
Small, technically strong core responsible for portfolio theory, risk modeling, capital standards, and reporting.
	•	Sectoral Capital Boards
Semi-autonomous boards aligned to major economic sectors. Boards allocate capital within congressionally defined envelopes and risk limits.
	•	Liaison Interfaces
Standing connections to line agencies (DOE, USDA, DOT, HHS, NSF, etc.) to translate research and planning needs into capital terms.

⸻

Appointment & Legitimacy Model (High Level)
	•	Technical vetting and interviews conducted through civil-service-style processes
	•	Qualified candidate slates reviewed through digital democracy mechanisms by verified sector participants
	•	Bounded veto power to block captured or conflicted candidates
	•	Final appointments made by the executive with documented justification
	•	Fixed, staggered terms with strict conflict-of-interest rules

⸻

Oversight & Accountability
	•	Congressional authorization of sector envelopes, objectives, and risk bands
	•	Mandatory quarterly and annual portfolio reporting
	•	Inspector General audits focused on process integrity and transparency
	•	Losses acceptable within approved risk parameters; opacity is not

⸻

First Steps (0–24 Months)

Step 1: Treasury Mapping Directive
Congress directs Treasury to inventory and classify all tax expenditures by sector, objective, cost, duration, and beneficiary concentration.

Step 2: PCA Stand-Up
Establish the PCA with an interim leadership team, core staff, and capital instrument framework. No large allocations yet.

Step 3: Pilot Sector Portfolios
Select 2–3 sectors with high tax-expenditure density (e.g., energy, housing, agriculture) and run parallel capital pilots alongside existing incentives.

Step 4: Prospective Conversion Rules
New incentives in pilot sectors default into PCA instruments. Legacy incentives are frozen and scheduled.

Step 5: Public Reporting Launch
Publish the first sectoral capital reports—explicitly tying public money to outcomes and lessons learned.

⸻

Long-Term Trajectory

As capital allocation migrates into the PCA framework, redundant funding offices within line agencies are absorbed or sunset. Departments retain regulation, science, and enforcement; capital becomes centralized, legible, and disciplined.

The end state is a government that knows where its money goes, why it goes there, and what it gets back—without collapsing expertise or concentrating unchecked power.

# Codetermination and Worker Board Representation Act

## The Problem This Solves

Every other instrument in the labor stack improves conditions around the edges of work. The wage floor raises the minimum. The benefits gradient ensures earning more always helps. Credential portability lets workers move where opportunity is. Real-time tax credit delivery smooths out the income volatility that makes low-wage work so precarious.

None of those instruments touch the decisions that actually determine whether the economy works for workers over the long run. Those decisions happen in boardrooms. Whether to automate a production line and how to use the resulting cost savings. Whether to restructure a workforce and how to sequence the transition. Whether to offshore a function and which workers absorb the disruption. Whether to deploy AI tools that eliminate entry-level roles that historically served as career on-ramps for millions of Americans.

For fifty years, those decisions have been made without the people most affected by them having any formal standing in the room. The results are visible in the data. Productivity has increased consistently. Compensation for the workers who produced that productivity has not kept pace. Automation has generated enormous shareholder value. The workers whose jobs it displaced have largely been left to navigate the consequences alone. Globalization expanded the corporate opportunity set dramatically. The communities built around the industries that moved have not recovered.

This is not primarily a story about bad actors. It is a story about a governance structure that gives one set of stakeholders — capital — formal standing in every major strategic decision, while giving another set of stakeholders — labor — no standing at all. Codetermination changes that structure. It does not hand workers control of the firm. It gives them a seat at the table when the decisions that determine their economic futures are made.

## Why Now

The AI transition makes this urgent in a way that previous waves of automation did not.

Prior automation waves were gradual and sector-specific. Manufacturing automation unfolded over decades and primarily affected blue-collar production roles. The adjustment was painful and poorly managed, but it was slow enough that policy responses, however inadequate, had time to develop. AI-enabled automation is different in two respects. It is moving faster than previous waves — capabilities that would have taken a decade to diffuse through the economy are now diffusing in years. And it is broad-spectrum rather than sector-specific — it affects knowledge work, professional services, and creative roles alongside production and logistics.

The specific concern for this Act is workforce entry. Automation historically hit the middle of the job ladder hardest — routine production and clerical work. AI is increasingly hitting the bottom of the job ladder: entry-level analysis, drafting, customer service, data processing, and administrative roles that have historically served as the first professional jobs for workers building careers. If those entry points are automated away before new ones are created, the career pipeline for the next generation of workers narrows in ways that are very difficult to reverse.

Firms making these deployment decisions today are not required to consider workforce entry effects, transition timelines, or the distribution of productivity gains. They are required to consider shareholder returns. Codetermination does not change what firms are allowed to do. It changes who is in the room when they decide to do it, and what information they are required to provide before they do.

## What This Act Does

This Act requires that covered firms seat worker representatives on their boards of directors, constituting one-third of the total board. Worker representatives have the same fiduciary standing, information access, and voting rights as other board members on all matters within the defined scope of codetermination.

The scope is deliberately bounded. This is not a general co-management regime. Worker board representatives have defined standing over four categories of decision:

**Automation and AI deployment** that materially affects workforce size, job function, or career pathway availability. Covered decisions include the deployment of AI systems that eliminate or substantially restructure roles affecting more than a defined threshold of the workforce, and the adoption of automation technologies that change the skill composition of the workforce in ways that disadvantage incumbent workers.

**Workforce restructuring** including layoffs, offshoring, outsourcing, and reorganizations that affect more than a defined threshold of the workforce. Worker representatives do not have veto power over these decisions. They have the right to advance notice, access to the supporting analysis, a defined deliberation period, and the ability to put an alternative proposal on the record before the board votes.

**Transition support design** when workforce reductions or role eliminations proceed. Worker representatives hold a limited consent right over transition-package adequacy — not over the restructuring decision itself, but over whether the terms offered to affected workers meet the statutory minimum and any negotiated improvements above it. This is a bounded consent right, not a general veto: the firm may proceed with restructuring, but transition terms cannot take effect without worker representative sign-off on adequacy. This distinction is intentional and should be legislatively explicit to prevent the consent right from being read as broader board power.

**Productivity gain distribution** when automation, AI deployment, or restructuring generates documented cost savings. Worker representatives have standing to propose how a portion of those savings is distributed — through wage increases, profit sharing, workforce development investment, or other mechanisms — and their proposal must receive a formal board response before the decision is finalized.

Worker representatives have standing over all covered strategic decisions, defined in the following section. This includes capital expenditure decisions whose primary or foreseeable effect is workforce automation, role elimination, career-pathway compression, outsourcing, offshoring, or relocation. Standing at the capital planning stage matters because automation and restructuring are capital-allocation decisions — if workers only see the deployment decision, the real strategic choice has already been made. On all other matters — product decisions, pricing, non-workforce capital allocation, and day-to-day operations — the board operates as it does today. Codetermination does not extend to operational management.

## Who Is Covered and When

Coverage applies to two categories of firm, with different timelines.

**Firms accessing PCA instruments or Execution Corps support** are covered immediately, regardless of size. The public capital stack — PCA financing, Execution Corps deployment, federal contracting preferences, industrial corridor co-investment — is not available without codetermination compliance. This condition applies at the point of application and is verified before capital or support is committed. This is the primary and immediate lever: it applies from the moment the Act is enacted, captures exactly the firms where public investment is most directly at stake, and is constitutionally the cleanest path.

**Firms above defined employee thresholds** are covered by a staged size-based mandate that phases in over five years, giving firms time to build governance capacity and giving regulators time to develop implementation infrastructure before the mandate reaches smaller firms.

The staged schedule is: firms above five thousand employees in year one of enactment; firms above one thousand employees in year three; firms above five hundred employees in year five. DoDA conducts an implementation review before each threshold activates, with authority to recommend a one-year delay if administrative capacity or legal infrastructure is materially unprepared. The statutory endpoint is five hundred employees — the threshold that matches the most common international precedent and captures the portion of the private sector where automation and restructuring decisions have the largest aggregate workforce impact.

Firms below five hundred employees are exempt from the mandate but may voluntarily adopt codetermination structures and receive recognition in PCA access negotiations. Firms that are covered by both the PCA condition and the size mandate comply once. There is no additional burden for firms that both exceed the threshold and access public instruments.

## How Worker Representatives Are Selected

Worker representatives are elected by direct workforce vote. Every employee of the covered firm is eligible to vote. Eligibility is verified through Self-Sovereign Identity (SSI) occupational credentials — workers prove their employment relationship cryptographically without requiring employer confirmation or union membership. This makes the election process independent of both employer cooperation and union density.

Candidates for worker representative seats may be nominated by any group of employees meeting a minimum signature threshold. They do not need to be union members. They do not need employer approval. The nomination and election process is administered by an independent election authority, not by the firm's human resources function.

Representatives serve defined terms with staggered renewal to provide continuity. They may be recalled by workforce vote under defined conditions. They receive the same liability protections as other board members and may not be retaliated against by the firm for their board service.

In firms with recognized unions, union bodies may nominate candidates through the same process as any other employee group — on equal footing, not with exclusive rights. This preserves union participation where it exists without making codetermination dependent on union density, which would exclude the majority of the private sector workforce.

## Interim Election Mechanism

Before SSI credential verification is fully available, elections are administered by an independent election authority using existing payroll records, tax filings, employment attestations, and third-party verification processes. The interim mechanism mirrors the process above in all substantive respects — direct workforce vote, independent administration, anti-retaliation protections — but relies on documentary verification rather than cryptographic credential proof.

Firms may not delay elections on the grounds that SSI infrastructure is not yet available. The interim mechanism is the operative standard until SSI is deployed, at which point elections transition to credential-based verification on a defined schedule. Anti-retaliation protections and audit rights apply equally to interim and SSI-based elections.

This mirrors the broader domain principle: instruments are designed to work before the full technology unlock is available, and are designed so the technology amplifies rather than enables them.

## Covered Strategic Decisions

Covered decisions under this Act include capital expenditures, AI deployments, automation investments, outsourcing arrangements, offshoring decisions, workforce restructuring, and reorganizations whose primary or foreseeable effect is material workforce reduction, role elimination, career-pathway compression, or substantial change to the skill composition of the workforce.

Coverage attaches at the decision stage, not the implementation stage. A capital expenditure plan that funds automation is a covered decision at the point the board approves the capital allocation — not six months later when the technology is deployed. This prevents firms from routing around codetermination by characterizing workforce-affecting decisions as capital or technology choices outside the defined scope.

Decisions that are not covered include product strategy, pricing, non-workforce capital allocation, executive hiring and compensation below board level, and day-to-day operational management. The scope is designed to give workers standing where it matters — when the strategic decisions that reshape the labor bargain are made — without extending into areas where firm governance is appropriately managed by directors and executives without codetermination constraints.

Given the particular urgency of the AI transition, this Act establishes a specific pre-deployment notice and deliberation requirement for AI systems that materially affect workforce entry pathways.

Before deploying any AI system that eliminates, substantially restructures, or automates functions that have historically served as entry-level roles — or that changes hiring criteria in ways that reduce access for workers without prior experience in the relevant function — the firm must provide worker representatives with a workforce entry impact assessment at least ninety days before deployment.

The assessment must include: the number and type of roles affected, the projected effect on hiring volume for entry-level positions over three years, the firm's proposal for how affected current workers will be transitioned, and the firm's proposal for how new entry pathways will be created or preserved.

Worker representatives may respond with a counter-proposal during the deliberation period. The board must formally consider and respond to the counter-proposal before deployment proceeds. Deployment may not be accelerated to avoid the deliberation window.

A formal response requires more than acknowledgment. The board's written response must address the following factors specifically: the projected workforce impact and the basis for that projection; the alternatives to the proposed deployment that were considered, including the worker counter-proposal, and why each was rejected; the estimated transition costs for affected workers and how those costs compare to the projected productivity gains from deployment; and any modifications to the original deployment plan made in response to the counter-proposal. A boilerplate rejection does not satisfy the response requirement.

The written response is filed with DoDA and becomes part of the firm's administrative record. DoDA publishes aggregate data on counter-proposal outcomes — how often counter-proposals result in deployment modifications, what categories of modification are most common, and what reasons are cited for rejections — so the system-level effect of the requirement is visible and evaluable. Individual firm responses are not published in full, but the administrative record is available for review in any subsequent legal challenge to whether the deliberation requirement was satisfied. This creates accountability for the quality of deliberation without converting the response requirement into a veto mechanism.

This requirement does not give worker representatives veto power over AI deployment. It requires that the workforce entry consequences be surfaced, documented, and formally considered before the decision is finalized. The DoDA regional wage heatmap provides the labor market context — what entry-level roles are available in the relevant sector and region, where wage floors stand, and where workforce entry gaps already exist — so the assessment is grounded in real data rather than firm-produced projections alone.

DoDA publishes aggregate measures of entry-level role compression by sector and region on a regular cadence, drawing on the same IRS income attestation and payroll data that powers the wage heatmap. Published metrics include entry-level job postings by sector and commuting zone, hiring volume for roles below defined experience thresholds, apprenticeship and on-ramp seat availability, internal mobility placements into entry-level and early-career positions, and wage progression rates for workers in their first three years of employment. These metrics make the workforce entry assessment requirement evaluable at the system level — not just procedurally correct firm by firm, but measurably effective or ineffective in preserving entry pathways across the economy. If entry-level role compression is accelerating in a sector despite assessment filings, that is a signal the assessment design or enforcement needs adjustment, not just that individual firms are complying.

## Transition Policies When Automation Proceeds

When automation or AI deployment proceeds and workforce reductions result, worker board representatives negotiate transition terms. Their approval is required for transition packages to take effect.

Minimum transition requirements — below which worker representatives may not agree — are set by statute and include: advance notice periods calibrated to tenure, retraining stipends tied to DoDA-certified credential programs, internal mobility priority for affected workers where equivalent roles exist, and wage continuation for a defined period during retraining.

Worker representatives may negotiate above these minimums. The statute sets the floor, not the ceiling. In firms where the productivity gains from automation are large and documented, representatives have standing to propose that a portion of those gains fund transition support above the statutory minimum — and the board is required to formally respond to that proposal.

The credential portability framework connects transition support to real labor market outcomes. Retraining programs funded through transition packages must be certified against DoDA's competency domain map, so workers emerge with credentials that are verifiable and portable rather than firm-specific training that has no value outside the firm that provided it.

### Dispute Resolution for Transition-Package Adequacy

If worker representatives withhold sign-off on the grounds that the proposed transition package fails to meet statutory minimums or negotiated terms, and the firm disputes that assessment, the disagreement is resolved through a specialized labor-governance arbitration panel rather than civil litigation.

The panel is constituted on an expedited basis — within fifteen days of a filed dispute — and is composed of one member selected by worker representatives, one selected by the firm, and one neutral selected by mutual agreement or, absent agreement, by an independent appointing authority. The panel's authority is strictly bounded: it may only determine whether the proposed transition terms meet the statutory minimum requirements and any negotiated terms that have been formally committed. It may not review the underlying restructuring decision, the automation or AI deployment decision, or any other board action outside transition-package adequacy. The panel issues a binding determination within thirty days of constitution.

If the panel finds the package meets the statutory minimum, the consent right is deemed satisfied and the transition terms take effect. If the panel finds the package falls short, the firm must revise and resubmit for worker representative review within fifteen days. A second dispute, if filed, follows the same process with the same timeline. The restructuring or deployment may proceed during arbitration — the dispute resolution process does not operate as a stay on the underlying business decision, only on the activation of transition terms for affected workers.

This design keeps the consent right operational without converting it into either a litigation trap for firms acting in good faith or a toothless procedural step that a determined firm can ignore. The timeline constraints are load-bearing: a dispute resolution process without fixed deadlines becomes a delay mechanism rather than a resolution mechanism.

One protection applies regardless of arbitration outcome or timeline: planning, procurement, and deployment may proceed during arbitration, but affected workers may not be separated and transition obligations may not be discharged until transition-package adequacy is resolved. The business decision can proceed; the worker-impact execution cannot outrun the transition protections it is supposed to fund. This preserves the no-veto principle — the firm is not blocked from making the decision or beginning implementation — while ensuring the consent right has real consequence for the workers it is designed to protect.

## Fiduciary Standing and Safe Harbor

Worker representatives owe fiduciary duties as directors of the corporation. Under traditional corporate law, this creates pressure toward shareholder-primacy interpretation — directors are fiduciaries to the corporation and, in most U.S. jurisdictions, are expected to prioritize shareholder value. Left unaddressed, this would create a structural conflict: worker representatives elected to consider workforce interests would face legal exposure for doing exactly what they were elected to do.

This Act resolves that conflict through an express statutory safe harbor. Within the defined scope of covered strategic decisions, worker representatives are expressly permitted — and expected — to consider workforce stability, transition adequacy, career-pathway preservation, credential program quality, regional labor-market effects, and long-run productive capacity of the firm. Consideration of these factors within covered decisions shall not constitute breach of fiduciary duty, regardless of whether those factors align with short-term shareholder return maximization.

For covered strategic decisions, the safe harbor applies to the board as a whole. Non-worker directors may also consider workforce stability, transition adequacy, career-pathway preservation, regional labor-market effects, and long-run productive capacity without violating fiduciary duties. This prevents codetermination from isolating worker representatives as the only directors legally permitted to weigh the workforce effects of covered decisions — a dynamic that would make the deliberation process structurally adversarial rather than genuinely deliberative. All directors voting on covered strategic decisions may give weight to these factors without personal liability exposure for doing so.

Outside covered decisions, worker representatives owe the same fiduciary duties as other board members and are subject to the same standards of care, loyalty, and good faith. The safe harbor is bounded to the codetermination scope — it does not create a general license to ignore shareholder interests or apply stakeholder-primacy standards to all board decisions.

This safe harbor is a necessary legal foundation. Without it, worker representatives face personal liability exposure that would either deter qualified candidates from serving or cause serving representatives to default to shareholder-aligned positions to avoid risk. The safe harbor makes the role legally viable without converting the entire board to a stakeholder-governance model.

## PCA Access Condition

Access to PCA financing instruments, Execution Corps deployment support, federal contracting preferences, and industrial corridor co-investment is conditioned on codetermination compliance. This condition applies at the point of application and is verified before capital or support is committed.

Firms that lose codetermination compliance after receiving PCA support — through workforce reductions that eliminate the representative workforce threshold, through interference with worker elections, or through failure to comply with notice and deliberation requirements — trigger a clawback review. Continued access to PCA instruments is suspended pending remediation.

This condition is not punitive. It reflects a straightforward principle: public capital deployed to support private production should not fund firms that exclude the workforce from governance standing over the strategic decisions that public investment makes possible. An industrial corridor development that receives PCA co-investment and then automates its workforce without worker representation in that decision is not consistent with the public interest rationale for the investment.

The condition also creates a market signal. Firms that establish codetermination structures before they need PCA capital gain faster access when they apply. Firms that build strong worker governance records — documented through DoDA reporting — receive favorable terms in PCA capital stack negotiations. Codetermination becomes a component of creditworthiness for public capital purposes, not merely a compliance checkbox.

## Size-Based Mandate: The Universal Large-Firm Backstop

The size-based mandate is the backstop. Firms above five hundred employees that do not access public capital are still required to comply.

Enforcement of the mandate for non-PCA firms runs through the existing labor law enforcement architecture, with civil penalties scaling with firm size and duration of non-compliance. The penalty structure is designed to make non-compliance more expensive than compliance for covered firms, while allowing good-faith implementation extensions where administrative barriers, rather than resistance, are the cause of delay.

The mandate is also the political and legal anchor. It establishes the principle that workers in large firms have a legal right to board representation, independent of whether the firm participates in public capital programs. That principle, once established, is much harder to repeal than a conditional access requirement, which can be quietly defunded or administratively narrowed. The mandate is the durable foundation. The PCA condition is the immediate and powerful enforcement lever.

## What This Is Not

This is not worker control of the firm. One-third board representation is a minority position. Worker representatives cannot unilaterally block board decisions. They can put alternatives on the record, require formal consideration of workforce impacts, and exercise a limited consent right over transition-package adequacy. They cannot override management on product decisions, pricing, non-workforce capital allocation, or day-to-day operations.

This is not a union recognition requirement. Worker representatives are elected by direct workforce vote regardless of union status. Unions may participate in the nomination process on equal footing with any other employee group. Codetermination does not require unionization and does not depend on it.

This is not a mandate to preserve jobs that technology has genuinely made obsolete. The Act does not give worker representatives the right to block automation that is economically sound and whose workforce consequences are responsibly managed. It requires that those consequences be surfaced, considered, and addressed — not that automation be prevented.

This is not a European import. The specific design — direct election verified through SSI credentials, bounded scope limited to automation, restructuring, and transition, PCA access as the primary enforcement lever, size-based mandate as the backstop — is built for the U.S. legal and political environment. It draws on international precedent for the basic concept while discarding the features of European models that don't translate: mandatory works councils, co-determination of operational decisions, and union-dependent nomination processes.

## The Three-Part Logic

To reiterate how Codetermination fits into the larger Labor and Economic Security framework: 

- Credentialing makes worker competence visible — what workers know, what they've demonstrated, what they're qualified for. 
- The DoDA heatmap makes labor market conditions visible — what workers earn by sector and region, where wages are rising, where workforce entry gaps are widening, where automation is concentrating. 
- Codetermination gives workers standing when firms make the strategic decisions that determine whether productivity gains are shared or extracted.

The three parts are designed to work together. Credentialing without labor market transparency produces workers who know their own qualifications but can't see how those qualifications fit into a shifting labor market. Transparency without governance standing produces workers who can see exactly how they're being affected but have no formal mechanism to influence it. Governance standing without credentialing produces board representation that is difficult to authenticate and easy to challenge. All three together produce a labor market where workers have the information, the credentials, and the institutional standing to participate in the decisions that shape their economic lives.

## What Success Looks Like

In the near term: covered firms have worker representatives seated on boards, pre-deployment AI impact assessments are being filed and formally considered, transition packages for automation-driven workforce reductions include certified retraining tied to portable credentials, and the PCA access condition is functioning as a market signal that makes strong worker governance a competitive advantage in accessing public capital.

In the medium term: the workforce entry assessment process produces a data record — filed with DoDA, published in aggregate — that makes visible for the first time how AI deployment is affecting entry-level role availability across the economy by sector and region. That data record becomes the input for workforce development policy, credential program design, and labor market planning in ways that are currently impossible because the information doesn't exist in any structured form.

In the long term: the principle that workers in large firms have legal standing in the decisions that determine their economic futures is established, durable, and has survived its first political cycle. The question is no longer whether workers should have board representation, but how to make it work better.

## The Political Frame

Codetermination is not about stopping technology. It is about making sure the workers who built the firm have standing when technology changes the firm.

If AI and automation increase productivity — and they will — the question is not whether firms may adopt them. They may. The question is whether the gains are shared, whether career pathways survive the transition, and whether workers are treated as participants in that transition rather than as collateral damage to be managed after the fact.

For fifty years, every wave of automation and globalization produced the same outcome: productivity went up, shareholder returns went up, and the workers who made both possible absorbed the displacement costs alone. That outcome was not inevitable. It was the result of a governance structure that gave capital formal standing in every strategic decision while giving labor none.

This Act changes that structure at the point where it matters most: the boardroom, when the decision is being made, before the consequences are locked in. It does not hand workers control of the firm. It gives them a seat at the table, a right to be heard, a structured process for raising alternatives, and legal standing to ensure that transition protections are real rather than nominal.

That is not a radical ask. It is the minimum condition for a labor market that working people can trust.

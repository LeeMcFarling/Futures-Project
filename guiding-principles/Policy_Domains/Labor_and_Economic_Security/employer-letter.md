# A Letter to Employers

*On the Labor and Economic Security Stack*

---

We are not asking you to pay more into a broken system. We are replacing the broken system.

If you currently offer employer-sponsored health insurance, you already know what that costs. Premiums that rise faster than inflation. Annual broker negotiations. Plan administration. Open enrollment. COBRA. And the constant pressure of managing hours below the threshold that triggers benefit obligations — which means your HR team spends time counting hours instead of building teams.

That system is not working well for employers any more than it is for workers. The Futures Project labor stack is designed to replace it with something more straightforward: predictable obligations, no hour cliffs, and far less private benefits bureaucracy to manage.

## What the Stack Does to Your Cost Structure

The reformed system separates three things that are currently bundled together in employer-sponsored insurance and handles each one differently.

Emergency and trauma care is covered universally through Severe Event Coverage at zero cost at point of service. This is not administered through employer-sponsored insurance — it is publicly backed and utility-regulated on the provider side. You are no longer the residual insurer for catastrophic medical events in your workforce.

Routine primary and urgent care operates as a transparent direct-pay market with posted prices. Lower-income workers receive income-graduated subsidies that are invisible to providers and to you. Your workers pay their share at the point of service. There is no claims processing on your end.

Routine care contributions are what the Portable Healthcare Contribution Floor covers. It is a modest, per-hour contribution into a portable worker-owned account. It phases in gradually alongside the healthcare reforms it is designed to complement. It runs through the same payroll infrastructure you already use for wages and tax withholding. Worker balances remain protected up to the reserve and stabilization levels, but excess balances above the statutory cap are automatically reconciled through reduced future contributions, payroll offsets, or rebates under transparent rules — so the account cannot become an accidental savings vehicle or an open-ended employer obligation. And because it is transitional by design, if the healthcare cost curve bends far enough that the contribution is no longer needed, future contribution rates step down on a published schedule. Where pilot data show contributions exceeded measured need, Congress may authorize prospective payroll credits or contribution offsets — not clawbacks of worker-owned balances, but forward reductions for employers.

The firms most likely to benefit from this transition are the ones already doing the right thing: currently paying for insurance, retirement administration, proper classification, and compliance while some competitors avoid those costs. If you are already bearing your fair share of labor costs, this system is designed to be neutral or better for you as it matures. The catastrophic insurance risk comes off your books. The administration simplifies. The cost structure becomes more predictable.

## The Cliff Is Gone

The current healthcare threshold creates a scheduling distortion that most employers recognize immediately. The difference between twenty-nine hours and thirty-one hours per week can mean owing or not owing substantial benefit obligations. So the rational response is to keep workers at twenty-nine. That decision has nothing to do with what the work requires and everything to do with what the benefits threshold triggers.

The per-hour contribution structure removes that incentive. Every hour generates a proportional contribution. There is no threshold to manage around. You schedule based on operational need.

The same logic applies to retirement. The superannuation contribution replaces discretionary retirement plan design — with all the compliance and administration that entails — with a statutory per-hour contribution that goes directly into a worker-owned account. No plan selection. No matching formula to design. No vesting schedule to track. No stranded accounts to manage when workers leave. The contribution follows the work, and when the work ends, the obligation ends.

To put it concretely: the tasks you stop doing are plan shopping, broker renegotiation, annual network changes, open enrollment administration, COBRA management, full-time eligibility tracking, and stranded retirement-plan administration for former employees. Those are replaced with standardized account routing through your existing payroll infrastructure — closer to direct deposit than benefits management.

## On Classification

The Worker Classification Parity Act does not change how independent contractors work. Genuinely independent businesses — workers who serve multiple clients, set their own rates, own their customer relationships, and run their own operations — are unaffected. The safe harbor is designed for exactly that relationship, and it is broad enough to cover legitimate independent contracting arrangements across industries.

What changes is the cost structure for arrangements where a firm controls a worker's schedule, pricing, and customer access but uses a contractor label to avoid employer obligations. In those situations, the employer-equivalent contribution applies. The contribution follows the economic reality of the relationship rather than the label on the contract.

## On Codetermination

Worker board representation is the part of this stack that understandably gets the most attention from employers. The scope is more bounded than the concept might suggest.

Worker representatives hold one-third of board seats — a minority position. They cannot block decisions on strategy, products, pricing, or day-to-day operations. Their defined standing covers a specific set of decisions: AI and automation deployments that materially affect workforce size or career pathways, restructuring above a defined scale, transition package design for affected workers, and how documented productivity gains are distributed.

On those decisions, worker representatives receive advance notice, access to the supporting analysis, and a defined period to put a formal counter-proposal on the record. The board must address that counter-proposal in writing — not with a boilerplate rejection, but with a substantive response that covers the workforce impact, the alternatives considered, and the reasoning. Worker representatives hold a limited consent right over the adequacy of transition terms, not over the underlying business decision itself.

An important legal point: the fiduciary safe harbor in the Act extends to the full board, not just worker representatives. For covered strategic decisions, all directors may consider workforce stability, transition adequacy, and long-run productive capacity without violating their fiduciary duties. The deliberation process is designed to be genuinely deliberative rather than structurally adversarial.

The AI workforce entry assessment is the provision most relevant to firms deploying AI over the next decade. It requires that before deploying systems that eliminate or substantially restructure entry-level roles, the firm provides worker representatives with a ninety-day advance assessment covering the projected workforce impact, the alternatives considered, and the firm's plan for transition and pathway preservation. The board must respond substantively. Deployment can proceed — the assessment is not a veto. It is a structured process for surfacing consequences that need to be formally considered before decisions are finalized.

## The Sequencing Is Deliberate

None of this arrives at once. The healthcare contribution floor activates only in pilot jurisdictions, phasing in alongside Severe Event Coverage deployment and the direct-pay routine care market. The superannuation contribution phases in by cohort, firm size, and regional conditions on a statutory schedule. The codetermination mandate phases in from firms above five thousand employees, then one thousand, then five hundred, over five years.

Small and mid-sized firms are not the first wave. The most governance-intensive requirements phase in through Public Capital Authority participation and large-firm thresholds first. Contribution systems are piloted before national scaling. If you run a firm below five hundred employees and are not accessing federal industrial capital programs, the codetermination mandate does not reach you until year five at the earliest — and the contribution systems will have been running in pilot regions long enough to demonstrate how they work in practice before they apply to you.

The intent is to replace the most expensive and volatile parts of the current system as the new infrastructure comes online — so that by the time mandatory contributions are fully in place, the healthcare cost curve has already been compressed. This is not a stack of new mandates layered on top of the current employer-insurance system. It is a replacement of that system, phased to give firms time to adapt as the underlying costs change.

## The Bottom Line

The labor stack is designed to make employer labor costs honest, proportional, and predictable — not simply larger.

Catastrophic insurance risk moves off your books. Benefits administration simplifies to standardized account routing. Hour cliffs disappear. And if the healthcare reforms perform as designed, transitional contributions step down rather than becoming permanent by inertia.

There is also a competitive case here that goes beyond cost. Right now, firms that misclassify workers, avoid benefits, and manage hours below thresholds have a structural cost advantage over firms that don't. That advantage is not efficiency — it is cost externalization onto workers and the public. This stack ends that arbitrage. Firms that have been doing the right thing stop competing against firms that have been gaming the rules. That is a market-fairness argument, not just a worker-protection argument.

For firms currently providing real benefits, offering decent wages, and classifying workers correctly: the goal is a system that is easier to manage and more predictable in cost than what you are running today.

For everyone: the obligations are more explicit, but the hidden costs — volatile insurance exposure, scheduling distortions, and the administrative overhead of a private benefits bureaucracy — are what this stack is designed to replace.

# Regional Wage Modernization Pilot

## Purpose

The Regional Wage Modernization Pilot establishes a controlled, evidence-gated pathway for raising wage floors without treating the national economy as a single uniform labor market. The pilot recognizes that a wage floor in Sedalia, Colorado operates inside a different price, rent, customer-volume, and business-margin environment than a wage floor in Boston, Massachusetts. The objective is therefore not to impose a blind national scalar, but to test regional wage restoration through calibrated labor-market areas, federal transition support, and pre-registered econometric evaluation.

The pilot is designed around a simple principle: workers should not be locked into poverty wages, but wage-floor policy should be implemented with enough measurement discipline to detect employment effects, hours effects, price pass-through, business stress, customer behavior, public-sector wage compression, regional inflation, and fiscal impacts before national scaling.

This moves minimum-wage policy out of the ideological binary of “raise it everywhere immediately” versus “do nothing,” and into a structured public experiment: raise the floor in controlled regions, support viable firms and public-service institutions through the transition, measure the effects against comparable regions, and expand only when the evidence clears statutory gates.

## Core Architecture

The wage floor should be regional and universal within participating labor-market areas. The transition support should be targeted. The evaluation should be sector-specific. The scaling decision should be evidence-gated.

Participating jurisdictions would opt into a federally authorized wage restoration pathway. The wage target would be calibrated by region using a certified wage-and-price matrix, rather than a single national number. The relevant unit should be a commuting zone, metro area, county cluster, or labor-market region rather than a state average, because state-level aggregates hide too much variation. Denver, Sedalia, Pueblo, and Vail do not share the same labor market. Boston and rural western Massachusetts do not share the same price structure. The policy unit should approximate the region in which workers actually commute, firms compete, and consumers spend.

The pilot would begin with a baseline wage restoration target, such as restoring the 1970 federal minimum wage to today’s purchasing power. That places the first-stage restoration benchmark roughly in the low-to-mid teens in current dollars. After the first-stage restoration target is reached, the wage floor would be indexed to a blended measure, such as a combination of CPI and regional median wage growth. CPI preserves purchasing power, while regional median wage growth prevents the wage floor from permanently falling behind the broader labor market.

A second-stage productivity-sharing pathway could be authorized only after Phase I clears the evaluation gates. This would allow regions that demonstrate strong absorption capacity to move toward a broader labor-share benchmark over time, while avoiding a national shock in regions where the evidence shows stress.

## Theory of Intervention

The pilot treats the minimum wage as a local labor-market repricing event. A wage floor does not only affect workers who receive a direct raise. It changes the outside option for low-wage labor, alters business cost structures, shifts recruitment dynamics, affects public-sector wage ladders, and may change prices, customer behavior, and firm survival.

The central policy question is therefore not simply whether wages rise. The question is whether regional wage restoration produces broad-based gains without unacceptable damage to employment, hours, business dynamism, public-service capacity, price stability, or local fiscal health.

The pilot should test whether a higher wage floor can:

* increase earnings for low-wage workers;
* reduce poverty and benefit dependence;
* improve retention and reduce churn;
* strengthen household demand;
* preserve or improve labor-force participation;
* avoid excessive employment or hours losses;
* avoid excessive price pass-through;
* avoid abnormal business closures relative to matched regions;
* avoid destabilizing public-sector recruitment and wage ladders;
* improve net fiscal outcomes through higher tax receipts and lower benefit reliance.

The intervention is not a claim that every region can absorb the same wage floor at the same speed. It is a test of which regions can absorb which wage path under which institutional supports.

## Regional Calibration

Each participating region would receive a certified wage-floor target based on a regional adjustment factor. The adjustment factor should account for local cost of living, local median wages, rent burden, industry mix, labor-force participation, customer density, small-business density, and sectoral exposure to low-wage labor.

A simplified version would look like:

```text
Regional wage floor = baseline federal restoration floor × regional adjustment factor
```

The adjustment factor should not be purely cost-of-living based. A region with high costs but weak business density may need a slower onramp than a high-cost region with strong consumer demand. Similarly, a lower-cost region may still require a stronger wage floor if workers face high transportation costs, housing scarcity, childcare costs, or concentrated employer power.

The pilot should therefore distinguish between the wage target and the onramp speed. The target reflects the desired labor-market floor. The onramp speed reflects local absorption capacity.

This distinction is central. A poorer or thinner-margin region should not be permanently assigned to lower wages simply because its current economy is weaker. But it may need a slower transition, more federal support, and stronger small-business productivity assistance.

## Onramp and Indexing

The wage increase should occur through a predictable multi-year onramp. A plausible first-stage pathway would raise the regional wage floor over three to five years toward the CPI-restored benchmark. The timeline could vary based on regional wage shock intensity, local business density, sectoral stress exposure, baseline wage distribution, and local inflation conditions.

After reaching the first-stage benchmark, the wage floor would index automatically to a blended metric. A strong default would be a weighted index combining CPI and regional median wage growth. Pure CPI indexing preserves purchasing power but allows the wage floor to drift away from the economy workers helped build. Pure productivity indexing may be too volatile and politically vulnerable. A blended wage-price index is more defensible and more stable.

The statute should also include an inflation circuit breaker. Scheduled real increases would pause automatically if inflation exceeds a defined threshold, such as 3.5 to 4 percent annualized CPI, or if certified regional price acceleration exceeds a separate threshold. Nominal wages would not fall, but the next scheduled increase would wait until inflation returns within tolerance. This lower threshold is intentionally conservative. The wage pilot should not be designed in a way that allows opponents to plausibly frame it as pro-cyclical during an inflationary period.

DoDA shall certify both the pause and the restart. Once national and regional inflation return within tolerance, DoDA shall determine whether the onramp may resume based on pre-registered conditions. Missed increases should not snap back all at once. Any catch-up should be spread across multiple years and conditioned on employment, hours, business formation, consumer traffic, public-service staffing, firm-survival metrics, and price-pass-through remaining within acceptable bands relative to matched comparison regions.

This restart certification is essential because the circuit breaker will become a political pressure point. Some actors will try to restart the onramp before the region has stabilized; others will try to convert a temporary inflation pause into a permanent freeze. The statute should therefore specify that DoDA, subject to independent peer-review scrutiny where required, certifies when restart conditions are met.

## Federal Backstop and PCA Support

The federal government would provide transition support through PCA-certified instruments. This support should be temporary, conditional, and targeted toward firms and public-service employers facing high adjustment exposure.

Within a participating labor-market area, the legal wage restoration pathway should apply across covered employers rather than creating permanent firm-size exemptions. If small firms require a slower effective transition, the better mechanism is temporary passthrough support and PCA backstop protection, not a separate permanent wage schedule. This avoids creating a statutory incentive for firms to remain small, split entities, franchise around thresholds, or subcontract labor to evade the wage floor.

The goal is not to permanently subsidize low-wage business models. The goal is to help viable firms cross the adjustment bridge while workers move toward a more dignified wage floor.

Eligible support could include working-capital loans, payroll smoothing facilities, revenue-share transition finance, pooled procurement support, energy-efficiency upgrades, bookkeeping and compliance assistance, scheduling and productivity software, kitchen or equipment modernization, local foot-traffic improvements, and technical assistance for firms facing high wage-shock exposure.

For restaurants and small local service firms, PCA support should focus on productivity and margin resilience rather than simple wage reimbursement. Public support should help firms become capable of paying higher wages, not preserve business models whose only competitive advantage is sub-living-wage labor.

Before implementation, participating regions should establish a business-level measurement baseline wherever administratively feasible. This baseline should include payroll, employment, hours, sales, visits, prices, tax receipts, firm age, sector, ownership structure, lease exposure, and prior growth or distress indicators. In a modest metro area, this will be complex, but the complexity is part of the intervention. Without a pre-treatment business baseline, the pilot will struggle to distinguish wage effects from ordinary churn, local demand shifts, landlord pressure, or sector-specific shocks.

For covered firms inside the target area, the preferred approach is a full legal passthrough of the wage schedule with a temporary federal backstop for firms that experience severe distress. A firm that goes under, faces imminent closure, or shows sharp deterioration relative to its own baseline and matched peers could trigger review for temporary stabilization finance, restructuring assistance, acquisition support, worker transition support, or orderly wind-down support. The backstop should protect workers, preserve viable local capacity, and identify avoidable failures without freezing the wage floor for the entire region.

Firms receiving support should meet basic conditions: payroll compliance, participation in certified data reporting, no artificial subcontracting to evade the wage floor, no artificial restructuring to qualify for support, no excessive owner extraction during the support period, and no use of public support for buybacks, dividends, or financial engineering.

Public-sector employers should also be monitored. A regional wage floor can create compression pressure for school districts, fire departments, EMS, municipal services, childcare providers, eldercare providers, cafeteria workers, bus drivers, aides, custodians, and other essential service workers. The pilot should include a public-service wage compression adjustment channel so that private-sector wage restoration does not destabilize local public services.

## Sectoral Exposure and Spillovers

The intervention should be regional, but the evaluation must be sector-specific. A wage floor affects different workers and institutions through different channels.

Restaurant workers are directly exposed. Their hourly wages, tips, hours, turnover, and household earnings may change immediately. Restaurants face direct labor-cost pressure and may respond through menu prices, staffing models, service charges, scheduling changes, automation, reduced hours, productivity improvements, or closure.

Teachers are usually affected indirectly. Most certified teachers earn above the minimum wage, but school support staff may not. As the wage floor rises, paraprofessionals, cafeteria workers, bus drivers, custodians, aides, and early-childhood workers may require upward adjustment. That creates compression pressure on teacher pay scales and district budgets.

Firefighters are also mostly indirectly affected, especially in career departments with civil-service or union compensation. The relevant effects are recruitment, overtime, EMT pipelines, dispatch staffing, volunteer-to-career transitions, municipal payroll pressure, and public-safety response capacity.

The pilot should explicitly measure direct wage gains, compression effects, and public-budget effects. Spillovers should be treated as policy signal, not statistical noise. If restaurant wages rise and childcare centers lose staff, that is part of the intervention’s real labor-market effect. If school districts face new wage compression, that is not an externality to ignore. It is a measurable implementation cost.

## Business Visits, Prices, and Firm Survival

The evaluation should include a dedicated business-demand and price-pass-through module. Employment alone is too narrow. A region may preserve jobs while damaging business visits, or maintain revenue only because prices rose while customer volume declined. The pilot must distinguish nominal revenue from real consumer activity.

For restaurants and local services, the evaluation should track foot traffic, reservations, point-of-sale transactions, card spending, delivery-app orders, average ticket size, menu prices, tips, service charges, payroll hours, staffing ratios, turnover, closures, new openings, business-license churn, bankruptcy filings, rent delinquency, tax receipts, and consumer substitution across jurisdictional borders.

The key question is not whether some businesses close. Businesses close constantly. The key question is whether closure rates, traffic declines, or price increases exceed expected levels relative to statistically similar non-pilot regions.

This distinction should be written directly into the pilot. Opponents should not be able to point to ordinary churn and declare failure. Supporters should not be able to point to higher nominal revenue and declare success if visits fell and prices spiked. The pilot should measure the full adjustment pathway.

## Wage Shock Intensity

The pilot should construct a wage shock intensity score for each firm, sector, and region. A two-dollar wage increase has different meaning depending on baseline wages, labor share, firm margins, and local purchasing power.

A useful exposure score would include:

```text
Wage shock intensity =
share of workers below the new floor
× average mandated wage increase
× labor share of operating costs
```

This allows DoDA to compare high-exposure and low-exposure firms within the same region, rather than relying only on region-to-region comparisons. It also supports more precise PCA targeting. A large firm with strong margins and low exposure should receive little or no transition support. A small, labor-intensive firm with high exposure and strong compliance history may qualify for temporary support.

The wage shock intensity score also improves the causal design. It allows the evaluation to test whether outcomes vary with actual exposure to the policy, rather than simply comparing pilot and non-pilot regions.

## Econometric Evaluation

The primary evaluation method should be standardized Euclidean nearest-neighbor matching. Each pilot region would be matched to statistically similar non-pilot regions based on pre-treatment characteristics. These characteristics should include median wage, wage distribution, rent burden, population density, industry mix, unemployment, labor-force participation, business density, restaurant share of employment, baseline firm churn, local CPI proxies, sales-tax base, household income, tourism exposure, public-sector employment share, and prior wage growth.

All matching variables should be standardized before distance is calculated. In plain terms, each variable is converted into comparable units so that one variable does not dominate the matching process simply because it is measured on a larger scale.

A simplified Euclidean distance formula would be:

```text
distance(A, B) = sqrt(sum((z_Ak - z_Bk)^2))
```

Where `z_Ak` and `z_Bk` are standardized values for region A and region B on variable `k`.

The nearest-neighbor comparison asks the right question:

Did the pilot region change more than statistically similar non-pilot regions over the same period?

This is much stronger than asking whether restaurants closed, prices rose, or employment changed after the wage floor increased. Those things may have happened anyway. The pilot should estimate whether the change exceeded what would have been expected in comparable regions.

The primary model should combine nearest-neighbor matching with difference-in-differences and event-study estimation. Region fixed effects would account for stable differences between places. Time fixed effects would account for national shocks. The treatment-by-post coefficient would estimate the effect of the wage intervention relative to matched controls.

The evaluation should test statistical significance for worker earnings, hours, employment, labor-force participation, business visits, price pass-through, firm survival, business formation, public-sector wage compression, municipal budget pressure, and net fiscal effects.

DoDA should publish balance diagnostics before treatment begins. If the nearest-neighbor regions do not actually resemble the pilot region, the match should be rejected or revised. Robustness checks may include Mahalanobis distance, propensity-score matching, synthetic controls, border discontinuity analysis, and firm-level exposure models, but standardized Euclidean nearest-neighbor matching should remain the primary public-facing design because it is explainable and politically legible.

The public message is simple: compare Sedalia-like places to Sedalia-like places, Boston-like places to Boston-like places, restaurant-heavy regions to restaurant-heavy regions, and high-exposure firms to similar high-exposure firms.

## Regression Discontinuity and Border Analysis

Where feasible, the pilot should also use regression discontinuity designs around jurisdictional borders. If one region participates and an adjacent region does not, firms and workers near the boundary provide a useful comparison group. This is especially valuable for restaurants, retail, hospitality, local services, and other sectors where customers can cross borders.

Border analysis should track whether customers shift spending into nearby non-pilot jurisdictions, whether workers commute into pilot jurisdictions for higher wages, whether firms relocate, and whether price pass-through differs near the boundary.

The pilot may also use threshold-based discontinuities if temporary support intensity varies by firm size, payroll exposure, or sector. However, firm-size thresholds should be treated carefully because they can invite manipulation. Firms may avoid growth, split entities, increase subcontracting, or restructure to remain below compliance thresholds. For that reason, the pilot should avoid permanent small-firm exemptions and should rely instead on universal regional coverage, business-level baselines, temporary PCA passthrough support, and audit rules against artificial restructuring.

Where firm size is used administratively, it should be defined through a rolling multi-year measure of payroll, receipts, related-party entities, franchise relationships, and beneficial ownership rather than a single headcount snapshot. DoDA and the relevant enforcement agency should audit discontinuities around eligibility thresholds to detect bunching, subcontracting, entity-splitting, or suspicious changes in reported payroll structure.

## Higher Education Research and Independent Peer Review Layer

The Regional Wage Modernization Pilot shall include a higher-education research and independent peer-review layer to ensure that pilot findings are credible, replicable, and not dependent on a single government evaluator.

DoDA shall retain responsibility for pre-registration, official data certification, baseline evaluation, statistical publication, and statutory gate recommendations. However, DoDA shall not be the only institution assessing whether the pilot worked. Participating universities, business schools, economics departments, public-policy schools, and nonpartisan research institutions shall be authorized to review, replicate, publish, and stress-test the pilot’s findings using privacy-preserving research datasets.

The purpose of this layer is to create a dual-key evidence system. This is one of the pilot’s core institutional innovations. Government can act, but it cannot grade itself in isolation. Academic and independent reviewers can challenge the evidence, but they cannot substitute their own policy discretion for democratic authorization. DoDA provides the official measurement spine. Independent researchers provide external methodological certification before major policy gates activate.

This protects the pilot from the predictable critique that the government designed the intervention, measured the intervention, and certified its own success. It also protects the system from academic veto-by-vibes by limiting the outside review function to methodological certification rather than broad political discretion.

### University Research Role

Participating universities and research institutions may produce companion studies, replication reports, sectoral analyses, business-survivorship studies, labor-market analyses, and regional case studies.

Business schools should be encouraged to study firm adaptation, price pass-through, customer behavior, restaurant customer visits, business visits, productivity changes, management practices, payroll compliance, business formation, and firm survivorship.

Economics and public-policy departments should be encouraged to study employment, hours, wage compression, spillovers, public-sector effects, municipal budget pressure, labor-force participation, household income, benefit usage, and net fiscal impact.

These studies shall not replace DoDA’s official evaluation. They shall serve as a transparency, replication, and learning mechanism. The official statutory gate shall remain tied to DoDA-certified findings, subject to independent peer-review certification.

All publicly funded studies shall disclose methods, funding sources, conflicts of interest, data limitations, model specifications, and whether their findings replicate or materially diverge from DoDA’s baseline estimates.

### Independent Research Review Panel

The pilot shall establish an Independent Research Review Panel composed of qualified external researchers from accredited universities, public research institutions, nonpartisan statistical organizations, and relevant methodological disciplines. The panel should include labor economists, regional economists, business-school researchers, public-finance scholars, statisticians, econometricians, and experts in small-business dynamics.

The panel shall not decide wage policy. Its role is to certify whether the evidence supports the statutory claim being made.

The panel shall review DoDA’s pre-registered evaluation design, matching procedures, balance diagnostics, treatment definitions, statistical models, robustness checks, data limitations, spillover strategy, and gate findings. Its review shall assess whether the evaluation complied with the pre-registered design; whether standardized Euclidean nearest-neighbor matches were valid; whether treatment and comparison regions were balanced on pre-treatment characteristics; whether estimated effects were statistically significant; whether findings were robust across reasonable alternative specifications; and whether material effects on business visits, price pass-through, public-sector wage compression, restaurant customer visits, firm survival, and regional inflation were adequately measured.

### Two-Stage Peer Review

The peer-review process shall occur in two stages.

Before pilot implementation, the Independent Research Review Panel shall review the evaluation design. This includes the matching variables, regional definitions, wage-shock exposure measures, treatment timing, outcome metrics, event-study windows, regression discontinuity assumptions, statistical power, spillover strategy, and pre-registered thresholds.

After pilot results are published, the panel shall review the findings. This includes model execution, statistical significance, robustness, attrition, missing data, measurement error, alternative specifications, subgroup effects, and interpretation of results.

This two-stage structure is essential. Pre-treatment review prevents post-hoc manipulation. Post-treatment review prevents overclaiming, cherry-picking, or selective interpretation after the results are known.

### Peer Review Gate

A pilot region may proceed to expansion, second-stage wage increases, or national scaling only if two conditions are met.

First, DoDA must determine that the region cleared the statutory performance thresholds.

Second, the Independent Research Review Panel must certify that DoDA’s finding is methodologically sound, replicable, and not materially contradicted by independent analysis.

The panel may classify findings as sufficient, insufficient, mixed, non-replicable, or requiring additional observation. A mixed finding shall not automatically terminate a pilot, but it shall prevent automatic expansion until the disputed metrics are resolved or the statute authorizes a modified pathway.

All panel reports shall be public. Dissenting methodological opinions shall be published alongside majority findings. Panel members shall disclose institutional affiliations, funding sources, paid consulting relationships, donor conflicts, and any financial interest in affected sectors or jurisdictions.

## Evaluation Gates

The pilot should continue, pause, modify, or expand based on pre-registered gates. At this stage, terms such as material, moderate, acceptable bands, and statistically meaningful margins should be understood as placeholders for numerical tolerances to be finalized during pilot design. Before implementation, DoDA shall pre-register specific threshold ranges for each gate, including employment, hours, customer visits, price pass-through, firm survival, business formation, public-service vacancies, municipal budget stress, and net fiscal effects. Those thresholds should be published before treatment begins so that neither the implementing agency nor political opponents can redefine success after the results are known.

A region should clear the first-stage gate if worker earnings rise, poverty indicators improve, employment and hours remain within acceptable bands, price pass-through remains moderate relative to matched controls, business closures do not exceed expected churn by a statistically meaningful margin, and public-sector compression effects remain manageable or are offset through adjustment support.

A region should trigger review if business visits fall materially relative to matched controls, price increases accelerate beyond threshold, firm closures exceed expected churn, hours decline sharply, public-service vacancies rise, or municipal payroll stress threatens core services.

A region should trigger pause if multiple stress indicators exceed threshold for a sustained period. A pause should not repeal the wage floor already reached, but it should delay further scheduled increases while DoDA evaluates whether the problem is inflation, weak demand, sector-specific exposure, public-budget pressure, or a broader regional mismatch.

A region should qualify for second-stage productivity-sharing only if the first-stage restoration path is absorbed without material harm to employment, business dynamism, public-service capacity, or price stability.

## Governance Logic

The pilot’s institutional structure should be understood as a dual-key evidence system and divided accountability architecture.

DoDA is the official evaluator.

Universities and research institutions are the replication and publication layer.

The Independent Research Review Panel is the methodological certification gate.

PCA is the transition-finance instrument.

Congress, states, and participating jurisdictions remain the policy authorizers.

This structure strengthens the legitimacy of the pilot. It allows government to act, but not to grade itself in isolation. It allows universities to test and challenge the findings, but not to substitute academic discretion for democratic authorization. It gives workers, firms, local governments, and the public a clearer answer to the central question: did the wage increase produce broad-based gains without unacceptable regional, sectoral, or fiscal damage?

## Statutory Draft Language

The pilot could be written in legislative-style language as follows:

“The Regional Wage Modernization Pilot shall authorize participating jurisdictions to adopt a scheduled wage-floor restoration pathway calibrated to certified regional labor-market areas. Certified regional labor-market areas shall be based presumptively on commuting zones, metropolitan labor markets, county clusters, or other empirically validated labor-market geographies rather than statewide averages, unless DoDA certifies that a statewide unit accurately reflects the relevant labor market. The restoration pathway shall be based on a blended wage-and-price matrix incorporating cost of living, median wages, rent burden, industry composition, and sectoral exposure to low-wage labor.

Scheduled increases shall proceed through a multi-year onramp and shall be indexed after attainment to a blended measure of consumer prices and regional median wage growth. Scheduled real increases shall pause automatically when national or regional inflation exceeds statutory thresholds, or when sectoral stress indicators exceed pre-registered tolerance bands relative to matched comparison jurisdictions. DoDA shall certify both pause conditions and restart conditions. A paused onramp may resume only after DoDA determines that national and regional inflation have returned within tolerance and that employment, hours, customer visits, firm survival, public-service staffing, and price-pass-through metrics remain within pre-registered acceptable bands relative to matched comparison jurisdictions. Any catch-up after a pause shall be spread across multiple years and may not occur through an immediate snapback increase.

The Public Capital Authority may provide temporary, conditional transition finance to qualifying employers and public-service institutions facing high wage-shock exposure. Such support shall be limited to payroll smoothing, working-capital stabilization, productivity-enhancing investment, pooled procurement, compliance modernization, and other approved transition uses. Support shall require payroll compliance, participation in certified evaluation telemetry, and restrictions against evasion, artificial subcontracting, excessive owner extraction, or financial engineering.

The Department of Data and Accountability shall conduct pre-registered evaluation using standardized Euclidean nearest-neighbor matching, difference-in-differences estimation, event-study models, and, where feasible, border discontinuity analysis. Evaluation shall include worker earnings, hours, employment, labor-force participation, business visits, consumer prices, firm survival, business formation, public-sector wage compression, municipal budget effects, and net fiscal impact.

The pilot shall establish an Independent Research Review Panel composed of qualified external researchers from accredited universities, public research institutions, nonpartisan statistical organizations, and relevant methodological disciplines. The panel shall review DoDA’s pre-registered evaluation design, balance diagnostics, treatment definitions, statistical models, robustness checks, data limitations, spillover strategy, and gate findings.

A participating region may proceed to expansion, second-stage wage increases, productivity-sharing experiments, or national scaling only if DoDA determines that statutory performance thresholds have been met and the Independent Research Review Panel certifies that the finding is methodologically sound, replicable, and not materially contradicted by independent analysis. Second-stage wage increases shall be treated as a nested experiment within the pilot rather than as an automatic continuation of first-stage restoration. DoDA shall pre-register the second-stage treatment design, outcome metrics, regional eligibility conditions, and pause triggers before implementation.

All peer-review reports, dissenting methodological opinions, data limitations, and conflict disclosures shall be published. Continuation, modification, expansion, or termination of the pilot shall be governed by published statistical findings relative to matched comparison regions and statutory evaluation gates.”

## Political Frame

This is not a one-size-fits-all federal wage mandate. It is a regional wage restoration pilot with federal transition support, statistical controls, inflation circuit breakers, independent peer review, and clear expansion gates. It should be framed affirmatively as the most rigorous wage policy ever proposed at the federal level: a wage restoration system that tests effects region by region, measures business and worker outcomes directly, and scales only when the evidence clears the gate.

It acknowledges the worker-side truth that the federal wage floor has badly eroded. It acknowledges the business-side truth that local firms operate under different price matrices and cannot all absorb wage shocks the same way. It acknowledges the public-sector truth that teachers, firefighters, school aides, EMS workers, and municipal services may experience wage compression and budget pressure even when they are not directly paid minimum wage.

The offer is disciplined:

Raise wages where the evidence supports it. Support the transition where the adjustment is real. Pause when inflation or sectoral stress exceeds tolerance. Scale when matched comparisons and independent reviewers show the policy is working.

The reform is pro-worker, pro-measurement, pro-local variation, and institutionally serious. It treats wage policy as something important enough to test, support, peer review, and scale only when the evidence clears the gate.

## Problem Statement

The current labor classification framework allows firms to reduce labor costs by designating workers as independent contractors rather than employees.

In dependent working relationships — where firms control schedules, pricing, branding, or customer access — misclassification shifts payroll taxes, healthcare obligations, and benefit burdens onto workers and the public system.

This creates competitive distortion, erodes worker protections, and incentivizes regulatory arbitrage rather than productive efficiency.

## Policy Objective

Establish cost parity and accountability in labor classification by:

1. Neutralizing the financial incentive to misclassify dependent workers.
2. Preserving legitimate independent business activity.
3. Ensuring that economically dependent labor relationships carry equivalent social-cost obligations.

## Core Structural Reform

### Employer-Equivalent Contribution Requirement

For workers classified as independent contractors but meeting statutory “dependent contractor” criteria, the hiring entity must remit an employer-equivalent contribution.

The contribution equals:

- Employer payroll tax equivalents.
- Healthcare transition contributions (where applicable).
- Statutory labor-cost obligations otherwise borne under W-2 classification.

This removes cost arbitrage between W-2 and dependent 1099 arrangements.

### Dependent Contractor Test

Classification is determined by economic substance, not contractual label.

Indicators of dependence include:

- Firm control over schedule or routing.
- Firm control over pricing or compensation structure.
- Branding or uniform requirements.
- Restriction on outside clients.
- Majority income derived from a single firm.
- Firm control of customer relationships.

Statutory safe harbors apply to genuine business-to-business relationships characterized by:

- Multiple clients.
- Pricing autonomy.
- Meaningful capital investment.
- Ability to subcontract.
- Independent marketing and branding.

### Joint-and-Several Liability

When misclassification occurs within a labor supply chain, any major economic beneficiary of the labor relationship may be held jointly and severally liable for unpaid payroll taxes, benefit contributions, and statutory penalties.

This prevents the use of undercapitalized intermediaries to shield liability.

Firms may privately seek indemnification from contracting entities after worker restitution is satisfied.

### Reporting and Aggregation

Large firms must provide standardized reporting for contractor payments sufficient to evaluate dependent contractor criteria.

Affiliated entities under common control are aggregated for classification analysis to prevent payroll fragmentation.

## Interaction with Healthcare Transition

Where the Portable Per-Hour Healthcare Benefits Floor Act is active, dependent contractors are subject to employer-equivalent healthcare transition contributions.

This ensures parity during healthcare system migration and prevents classification-based avoidance.

## Economic Effects

Expected outcomes:

- Reduction in dependent 1099 arbitrage.
- Stabilization of labor standards across sectors.
- Preservation of legitimate independent contracting.
- Improved transparency in labor cost structures.

## Fiscal Considerations

Increased payroll tax and contribution compliance may increase federal receipts.

Enforcement requires enhanced auditing and data integration capacity.

## Evaluation Metrics

- Share of labor income routed through dependent contractors.
- Reclassification case volume.
- Payroll tax compliance rates.
- Contractor-to-employee ratios in large firms.
- Litigation incidence and resolution timelines.

## Strategic Framing

This reform aligns legal classification with economic reality.

It protects legitimate independent business activity while eliminating cost arbitrage in dependent labor relationships.

The objective is structural neutrality: firms choose classification based on operational need, not social-cost avoidance.

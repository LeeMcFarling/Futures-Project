# Worker Classification Parity and Dependent Contractor Accountability Act

## The Problem in Plain Terms

There is a well-established way for large companies to reduce their labor costs without reducing their workforce: classify workers as independent contractors instead of employees. On paper, this means the worker is running their own business and the company is just a client. In practice, it often means the company controls when the worker shows up, what they charge, what they wear, which customers they serve, and whether they can work for anyone else — while the worker pays their own payroll taxes, buys their own health insurance, and receives none of the protections that employment law provides.

This is misclassification. It is not a gray area in most cases where it occurs at scale. A driver who can only work through one platform, whose pay rate is set by that platform, whose customer relationships are controlled by that platform, and who cannot negotiate their own terms is not running an independent business. They are an employee whose employer has found a legal structure to avoid the obligations that come with that relationship.

The cost of this arrangement is not absorbed by the company. It is shifted onto the worker, who pays the employer's share of payroll taxes themselves, and onto the public, which funds the healthcare, income support, and retirement benefits that misclassified workers can't access through their work. It is also shifted onto competing businesses that play by the rules — firms that properly classify their workers face higher labor costs than competitors who don't, which creates pressure across entire industries to misclassify rather than compete on actual efficiency.

This Act does not ban independent contracting. It defines when an employment relationship is economically real regardless of what the contract says, and it requires that real employment relationships carry the obligations that go with them.

## Why This Is Getting Worse

Misclassification is not a new problem, but it is an accelerating one. Platform-enabled gig work has made it possible for firms to coordinate very large dependent workforces while maintaining the legal fiction of independent contractor relationships at scale. AI-enabled task management is extending this model into new sectors — professional services, creative work, customer support, data processing — that previously had little exposure to classification arbitrage.

The pattern is consistent: technology that makes it easier to coordinate and supervise workers at scale also makes it easier for companies to claim those workers are autonomous agents rather than employees. The legal tests that determine classification were developed before these technologies existed and are being outpaced by business models designed to exploit the gap between economic reality and legal category.

This Act modernizes the classification framework for the current environment. It does not restrict the genuine flexibility that legitimate independent contracting provides. It closes the gap between what classification law was designed to prevent and what firms are actually doing.

## What This Act Does

### The Dependent Contractor Test

Classification is determined by economic substance, not contractual label. A worker is a dependent contractor — and the hiring firm owes employer-equivalent obligations — when the economic reality of the relationship resembles employment regardless of what the contract says.

Indicators of economic dependence include: firm control over the worker's schedule or routing; firm control over pricing or compensation structure; branding, uniform, or equipment requirements imposed by the firm; restrictions on the worker's ability to take clients or jobs outside the relationship; majority of the worker's income derived from a single hiring entity; and firm control of customer relationships so that the worker cannot maintain those relationships independently if they leave.

No single indicator is determinative. The test looks at the relationship as a whole. A worker who controls their own schedule but derives ninety percent of their income from one firm under that firm's pricing structure and branding is economically dependent regardless of the scheduling flexibility.

### Legal Status and Scope of Obligations

Dependent contractor status does not automatically convert every covered worker into a W-2 employee for all legal purposes. It triggers employer-equivalent obligations for the parts of the labor stack that depend on accurate cost allocation: payroll tax equivalents, healthcare transition contributions under the Employer Benefits Floor where active, real-time income reporting under the IRS modernization program, wage-floor coverage under the Regional Wage Modernization Pilot where applicable, and aggregation for threshold-based governance obligations including codetermination.

This distinction preserves legitimate flexibility while eliminating cost arbitrage. A platform may allow a worker to choose their hours and still owe employer-equivalent contributions if the platform controls pricing, customer access, branding, and the economic terms of the relationship. The flexibility a worker retains over their schedule does not license the hiring entity to shift employer costs onto the worker and the public. Flexibility and cost externalization are separate questions, and this Act treats them separately.

### Rebuttable Presumption for Platform-Controlled Work

A rebuttable presumption of dependent contractor status applies when a hiring entity controls pricing, controls access to customers, and accounts for a majority of the worker's income over a defined measurement period. The firm may rebut the presumption by demonstrating that the worker maintains genuine pricing autonomy, meaningful independent customer relationships, diversified client income across multiple hiring entities, and independent business capacity that would survive the loss of the presumed hiring entity as a client.

The presumption is designed to make enforcement administrable rather than purely case-by-case. Platform structures that control the three key economic levers — price, customer access, and income share — are the highest-risk misclassification environments. Placing the burden of rebuttal on the firm in those circumstances reflects where the relevant information sits: the firm knows its pricing and customer-access structures; the worker often does not have access to the documentation needed to prove economic dependence from their position.

Statutory safe harbors protect genuinely independent businesses. A worker or firm qualifies for the safe harbor when the relationship is characterized by: multiple clients across which income is reasonably distributed; pricing autonomy — the worker sets their own rates rather than accepting a rate determined by the hiring entity; meaningful capital investment in tools, equipment, or intellectual property the worker owns; the ability to subcontract or delegate work; and independent marketing and branding not controlled by the hiring entity. These are not technical checkboxes. They are the substantive markers of a business that could survive without any particular client.

### The Employer-Equivalent Contribution Requirement

For workers who meet the dependent contractor test, the hiring entity must remit an employer-equivalent contribution covering: employer payroll tax equivalents; healthcare transition contributions under the Employer Benefits Floor where it is active; and other statutory labor-cost obligations that would otherwise be borne by a W-2 employer.

This is not a penalty. It is cost parity. The contribution requirement means that the decision to classify a worker as a contractor rather than an employee is based on the genuine nature of the work relationship — not on the cost savings from avoiding employer obligations. A firm that classifies a worker as a contractor in a genuinely independent business relationship faces no additional obligation. A firm that classifies a worker as a contractor to avoid the costs of an employment relationship it actually controls pays those costs regardless of the label.

The requirement connects directly to the IRS Modernization and Automated Income Attestation program. Near-real-time income reporting from employers and payroll processors makes it possible to identify misclassification patterns at scale — firms paying large numbers of 1099 contractors in categories that historically had low independent contractor rates, workers deriving most of their income from a single platform, and income flows inconsistent with genuine business-to-business relationships. The Department of Data and Accountability (DoDA) publishes aggregate data on classification patterns by sector and region, giving enforcement agencies and policymakers a system-level view of where arbitrage is concentrated.

### Joint-and-Several Liability

Misclassification is frequently structured through intermediaries — staffing agencies, subcontractors, and platform entities that sit between the economic beneficiary of the labor and the worker. The economic beneficiary — the firm that actually directs and benefits from the work — often claims no responsibility for classification decisions made by the intermediary.

This Act closes that structure. Any major economic beneficiary of a labor relationship in which misclassification has occurred may be held jointly and severally liable for unpaid payroll taxes, benefit contributions, and statutory penalties. Liability flows to where the economic benefit actually went, not just to whichever entity signed the contract.

Firms held jointly liable may privately seek indemnification from contracting intermediaries after worker restitution is satisfied. The indemnification right is preserved; it simply cannot be used to shield the economic beneficiary from initial accountability to workers and the public.

### Reporting and Aggregation

Large firms must provide standardized reporting for contractor payments sufficient to evaluate dependent contractor criteria. This reporting flows through the same IRS modernization infrastructure that supports the Real-Time Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) and Child Tax Credit (CTC) Delivery Act and the benefits gradient compliance regime — it is not a new reporting burden but an extension of payroll reporting to contractor relationships.

Affiliated entities under common control are aggregated for classification analysis. A firm cannot avoid the threshold effects of the dependent contractor test by splitting a workforce across subsidiaries, related entities, or franchising arrangements. The aggregation rule looks through corporate structure to the economic reality of the workforce relationship.

## What Workers Gain When Classification Is Accurate

Accurate classification is not just a compliance question. It determines which workers can access the full set of labor market protections the broader stack provides.

A dependent contractor whose relationship is correctly classified gains access to the same labor market infrastructure as a W-2 worker in an equivalent role. Their income is reported accurately and in near-real time, which means the Benefits Gradient Modernization Act's income-support calculations reflect what they actually earn — and the guarantee that every additional dollar earned leaves them better off applies to them as fully as it does to any other worker. Their tax credit advances under the Real-Time EITC and CTC Delivery Act are calibrated to their real income rather than to estimates that miss how they actually earn. Their employer contributes to their Healthcare Transition Account at the same per-hour rate as for a comparable W-2 worker, so their portable healthcare financing accumulates as they work. Their earnings count toward the wage floor analysis in regions covered by the Regional Wage Modernization Pilot, so the floor extends to them. And their working relationship counts toward the headcount thresholds that determine whether their economic employer is subject to codetermination obligations — which means the workers who build large platform businesses have formal standing in the governance decisions those businesses make.

Accurate classification does not change what work looks like day to day. It changes what workers are entitled to from that work.

## Legitimate Independent Contracting Is Protected

The Act is designed to produce a specific outcome: classification decisions are made on the basis of the genuine nature of the work relationship, not the cost savings from avoiding employer obligations. That means legitimate independent businesses — freelancers with multiple clients, consultants with pricing autonomy, skilled tradespeople who own their tools and run their own customer relationships — face no additional obligation and no new compliance burden.

The safe harbor criteria are calibrated to distinguish genuine independent business activity from dependent labor with an independent contractor label. A graphic designer with ten clients, a plumber who owns their van and sets their own rates, a software developer who contracts with multiple firms simultaneously — these are independent contractors in economic reality as well as legal form. The Act leaves them where they are.

What changes is the cost structure for firms that want the operational control of employment without the legal obligations of employment. That combination is no longer available at the employer's unilateral option.

### Worker Choice and Portability

Some workers genuinely value the flexibility that platform and contractor arrangements provide, and platform firms routinely invoke worker preference as a defense against classification reform. This Act does not eliminate flexible scheduling or require that all gig workers become full-time employees. It eliminates cost externalization.

A worker may retain the ability to set their own hours, decline jobs, and work across multiple platforms — and the hiring entity may still owe employer-equivalent contributions if the economic relationship is one of dependence. Scheduling flexibility and cost allocation are separate questions. A platform that controls pricing and customer access is an economic employer regardless of how much scheduling latitude it extends. Workers keep the flexibility they want. Firms bear the costs they owe. Those two outcomes are not in conflict.

## Enforcement and Data Infrastructure

Enforcement is most effective when it operates on patterns rather than individual cases. The IRS modernization data stream, combined with DoDA's classification analysis by sector and region, makes it possible to identify industries and firms where the gap between economic reality and legal classification is widest — and to concentrate enforcement resources there rather than relying on individual worker complaints, which are costly to file and frequently suppressed by retaliation risk.

DoDA publishes annual data on dependent contractor patterns: the share of labor income flowing through 1099 arrangements by sector and commuting zone, contractor-to-employee ratios in large firms, and changes in classification patterns over time. This data serves two functions. It gives enforcement agencies a system-level picture of where misclassification is concentrated. And it gives workers, researchers, and the public visibility into how classification patterns are changing — whether the Act is working or whether firms are finding new structures to achieve the same result.

Enhanced auditing capacity is required to make enforcement effective. The Act authorizes DoDA to flag high-probability misclassification patterns for enforcement agency review, reducing the investigative burden on agencies that currently must identify misclassification case by case.

## What This Costs and What It Returns

Increased payroll tax and benefit contribution compliance produces additional federal revenue — the scale depends on the current extent of misclassification, which is difficult to measure precisely because misclassification is designed to be invisible to standard reporting. Available estimates from independent researchers and government audit findings suggest the fiscal cost of misclassification runs to tens of billions of dollars annually in lost payroll tax revenue and public benefit expenditures that substitute for employer obligations firms are avoiding, though a definitive system-level figure requires the kind of real-time income data the IRS modernization program is designed to produce.

The competitive correction is also a fiscal benefit. When misclassifying firms are required to bear the actual labor costs of their workforce, the cost advantage that drives misclassification shrinks or disappears. This reduces the pressure on properly classifying firms to follow suit and stabilizes labor cost structures across sectors where misclassification has become widespread.

Enforcement infrastructure is a real cost. Building the data integration capacity to identify misclassification at scale requires investment in the IRS modernization and DoDA systems that the broader stack also depends on. This is a shared cost across multiple instruments, not a cost borne solely by this Act.

## The Political Frame

Worker classification parity is not anti-business, but rather pro-competition. 

The firms harmed most by misclassification are not workers — though workers bear the direct costs in lost benefits, higher taxes, and weaker protections. The firms harmed most are the businesses that properly classify their workers and compete against firms that don't. A restaurant that pays employer payroll taxes, provides workers' compensation, and offers benefits is competing against a platform-based delivery service that has shifted all of those costs onto its "contractors." The restaurant is not losing because it is less efficient. It is losing because its competitor has found a legal structure to externalize costs that the law says employers should bear.

This Act levels that playing field. Classification decisions are made on the basis of what the work relationship actually is — not on which legal structure allows a firm to avoid the most cost. Firms that genuinely use independent contractors keep doing what they're doing. Firms that use independent contractor labels to avoid the obligations of employment relationships they actually control start bearing those obligations.

The objective is structural neutrality: firms choose how to organize their workforce based on what works operationally, not based on which classification minimizes what they owe the workers who make their business run.

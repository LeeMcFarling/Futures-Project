## A Note on Nomenclature

Three terms carry specific and consistent meanings across all documents in the Framework Foundations layer.

**Threat salience** is the umbrella term for the aggregate experienced state of heightened perception that survival and meaningful life are under threat. When threat salience rises, people shorten their time horizons, harden in-group identity, become hostile toward perceived out-groups, and are drawn toward authoritarian leadership that promises to defend what they feel is at risk.

**Mortality salience** is the material channel — the perception that physical survival systems are unreliable or failing. Economic insecurity, housing instability, healthcare inaccessibility, and exposure to violence all raise mortality salience. This is the channel that terror management theory's experimental paradigm primarily activates and that the social epidemiology literature has most thoroughly documented.

**Existential salience** is the symbolic channel — the perception that one's meaning structures, identity anchors, or ways of life are under threat. Cultural attack on anchor communities, political delegitimization of valued beliefs, identity negation, and manufactured threat amplification all raise existential salience. This channel operates independently of material conditions and responds to different reduction strategies than mortality salience.

## Core Axiom

Sustained cooperation within and between societies depends on credible belief in survivable and meaningful futures.

Governance systems that fail to maintain this belief — whether by allowing material conditions to deteriorate, by permitting the symbolic legitimacy of valued ways of life to be systematically attacked, or by allowing the information environment to amplify perceived threats beyond what conditions warrant — increase the probability of political instability, economic volatility, conflict escalation, and institutional breakdown.

The Futures Project treats preservation of existential stability as a foundational design objective across domestic policy, economic architecture, security frameworks, and international governance.

## The Two Channels of Threat Salience

Threat salience arises through two distinct channels that must be understood separately because they are generated by different conditions and reduced through different mechanisms.

### The Mortality Channel

Mortality salience rises when the material and physical systems that people depend on for survival are perceived as unreliable, failing, or under threat. The relevant conditions include:

- direct exposure to violence, physical insecurity, or health crisis
- economic instability, job loss, housing insecurity, or collapse of long-term income prospects
- infrastructure failure reducing access to basic necessities
- institutional incompetence in crisis response, eroding confidence in survival systems

The behavioral consequences of elevated mortality salience are well-documented in the terror management theory literature and the social epidemiology record: shortened time horizons, hardened in-group boundaries, increased demand for strong central authority, reduced empathy across group lines, and withdrawal from the cooperative behaviors that democratic institutions depend on.

### The Existential Channel

Existential salience rises when the symbolic and meaning systems that people invest in — their identities, their communities, their beliefs, their ways of life — are perceived as under threat. The relevant conditions include:

- sustained polarization and identity threat framing in the political and media environment
- erosion of institutional legitimacy and perceived fairness
- cultural displacement narratives that frame valued ways of life as morally disqualified
- breakdown of shared civic frameworks and national narratives
- manufactured threat amplification by actors who profit from elevated salience

The behavioral consequences of elevated existential salience are largely indistinguishable from those of elevated mortality salience — worldview hardening, in-group/out-group polarization, time horizon compression, and authoritarian attraction — but the mechanisms producing them are different, and so are the interventions that address them.

### Why the Distinction Matters for Policy

Treating threat salience as a unified construct — as the original TMT literature tended to do — obscures the most important policy implication: the two channels require different responses, and addressing one while ignoring the other will produce incomplete or counterproductive results.

A government that improves material conditions without addressing the symbolic threat environment may find that mortality salience falls while existential salience remains high — leaving the behavioral profile of elevated threat salience largely intact. Conversely, a political movement that addresses symbolic recognition without improving material conditions may produce symbolic gains that fail to hold because the material drivers of instability were never addressed.

The Futures Project's instrument design reflects this: material outcome improvement operates on the mortality channel, while settlement architecture, epistemic infrastructure, and community anchor construction operate primarily on the existential channel. Both must move.

## Material and Symbolic Protection Systems

Existential stability — the sustained belief in survivable and meaningful futures — depends on perceived reliability in both the material and symbolic domains.

### Material protection systems

- physical security and deterrence credibility
- economic stability and access to meaningful work
- housing availability and community continuity
- infrastructure reliability and crisis-response competence
- predictable alliance commitments
- rule of law

### Symbolic protection systems

- belief in fair opportunity and upward mobility
- civic identity and belonging
- institutional legitimacy and perceived fairness
- cultural, religious, and philosophical meaning frameworks
- future-oriented national and civilizational narratives
- dignity pathways linked to contribution rather than only to wealth or status

Failure in either domain produces elevated threat salience with similar behavioral consequences. The domains are not substitutable — symbolic protection does not compensate for material failure, and material security does not compensate for systematic symbolic threat. Both must be maintained for existential stability to hold.

## Intra-Country Causality Chain

Structural shocks → elevated threat salience → political hardening → institutional degradation.

The pathway from material failure:

economic stagnation or housing scarcity
→ mortality salience rises as survival systems become less reliable
→ symbolic contract erosion (belief in fair opportunity weakens)
→ existential salience rises as the meaning of participation in the social order degrades
→ polarization and grievance politics intensify
→ demand for strong central authority increases
→ democratic norms and institutional trust weaken
→ governance volatility rises
→ investment confidence and long-term growth decline

The pathway from symbolic failure:

sustained political or media attack on valued anchor communities
→ existential salience rises for the targeted anchor class
→ threat response mobilizes: in-group hardening, out-group hostility, shortened time horizons
→ antagonism between anchor communities rises
→ cooperative political engagement degrades
→ institutional legitimacy erodes
→ governance capacity to address material conditions weakens
→ mortality salience begins to rise as material systems degrade

Both pathways can initiate the spiral. Once initiated, the two channels reinforce each other: material deterioration raises existential salience by eroding the symbolic contract of fair participation; elevated existential salience degrades the cooperative capacity needed to address material conditions. The spiral is self-reinforcing and increasingly difficult to reverse as it deepens.

## Inter-Country Causality Chain

Regional instability → elevated threat salience → alliance fragility → escalation risk.

The same two-channel structure operates at the interstate level. Nations hold continuity portfolios in exactly the sense individuals do — their political cohesion and long-horizon investment capacity depend on the perceived reliability of their security, economic, and institutional systems. When those systems are threatened — materially through conflict, economically through trade disruption, or symbolically through perceived civilizational attack — the behavioral response mirrors the individual one: shortened time horizons, hardened national identity, reduced cooperative capacity, and increased risk of conflict escalation.

The pathway from material interstate threat:

persistent gray-zone attacks or conflict exposure
→ perception that security protection systems may fail
→ mortality salience rises in the affected population
→ capital flight and fiscal stress reduce material resilience
→ political decision horizons shorten
→ hedging behavior and unilateral security moves increase
→ cooperative security frameworks weaken
→ adversary coercive leverage grows
→ probability of escalation or fragmentation rises

The pathway from symbolic interstate threat:

sustained civilizational or ideological attack framing between nations
→ existential salience rises for the targeted national or cultural identity anchor
→ political hardening and nationalism intensify
→ multilateral institutions lose legitimacy as shared reference points
→ bilateral competition escalates into perceived existential conflict
→ the range of acceptable negotiated outcomes narrows
→ conflict becomes more likely as cooperative pathways close

Alliance architecture that is concentrated in a single security guarantee — rather than distributed across multiple overlapping institutions — faces the same portfolio concentration risk at the interstate level that individuals face with single-anchor portfolios. Any perceived threat to the single guarantee produces a disproportionate existential salience response, because the entire security meaning of the alliance is at stake.

## Design Implication

Modern governance architectures must be evaluated not only on efficiency, growth, or deterrence outcomes, but on their ability to reduce background threat salience across both channels.

Systems that:

- extend decision time horizons by making material conditions stable and predictable
- stabilize access to meaning and dignity pathways across diverse anchor communities
- increase predictability of security and economic outcomes
- distribute risk across cooperative networks rather than concentrating it in single dependencies
- resist political and media incentives to manufacture existential threat for mobilization purposes

are structurally more likely to sustain democratic legitimacy, alliance durability, and long-term peace.

Threat salience is therefore treated as a cross-domain performance variable — not a purely psychological or sociological concern but a governance design parameter with measurable consequences for democratic stability, economic productivity, and international security.

## Relationship to Futures Project Instruments

The full instrument set is documented across the domain stacks of this corpus. The following summarizes the primary connections to the threat salience framework.

**Mortality channel — reducing material threats:**
Housing supply stabilization, counter-cyclical capital deployment through the Public Capital Authority, healthcare access expansion (Health Security Accounts, HETA reform), labor and economic security stack (wage modernization, benefits portability, codetermination), criminal justice reform (reducing exposure to violence), and integrated defense partnerships (distributed deterrence credibility).

**Existential channel — reducing symbolic threats:**
Culture war settlement architecture (disaggregating active anchor conflicts into durable, democratically ratified arrangements), electoral reform (removing institutional incentives for threat amplification through duopoly competition), epistemic infrastructure (digital nutrition label, algorithmic attention initiative, synthetic media provenance, CDE enforcement), and civic prestige architecture (creating positive-sum meaning pathways through contribution recognition).

**Anchor diversification — building portfolio resilience:**
Built environment and community anchor infrastructure (neighborhood civic overlay, modular reurbanization, national seam infrastructure), IDP outbound mobility scaffold (international anchor formation through direct cross-national experience), National Capability Tour (domestic cross-community civic contribution experience), and prestige architecture redesign (expanding the cultural legitimacy of civic contribution as a meaning pathway).

**Institutional legitimacy — anchoring the symbolic protection system:**
CPQA Civic Build Mark (visible demonstration of institutional competence through built infrastructure), Department of Data and Accountability (independent verified performance measurement), and the adaptive governance and evidence protocol (commitment to honest evaluation and revision).

**Interstate stability — extending the framework internationally:**
UN Security Council reform, Regional Security Councils, Mediation Council, and the alliance capability investment architecture collectively constitute a threat salience management architecture at the interstate level — designed to structure competition between nations without allowing it to produce the correlated material and symbolic threat escalation that makes conflict the rational response.

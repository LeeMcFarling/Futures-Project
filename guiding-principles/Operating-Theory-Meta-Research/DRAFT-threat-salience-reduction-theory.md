# Threat Salience Reduction
## A Constructive Theory of Civilizational Stabilization

---

## Prefatory Note

This document is addressed to researchers in terror management theory, existential psychology, political sociology, and governance theory. It is the constructive complement to the Existential Stability Axiom — which establishes what threat salience is and what its elevation produces — and to the Central Axioms of Civilizational Stability — which formalize the continuity portfolio model and the civilizational cost function.

The existing framework documents establish the diagnostic half of the theoretical architecture with considerable rigor. The constructive half — a systematic theory of what *lowers* threat salience at the individual and population level, through what mechanisms, and how institutional design can deliberately activate those mechanisms — has been implicit across the platform's instruments without being made explicit as a unified theory.

This document attempts that synthesis.

The argument proceeds in four stages. First, it identifies the gap in the existing TMT literature — the asymmetry between the extensive account of what elevated threat salience produces and the underdeveloped account of what reduces it. Second, it proposes a taxonomy of threat salience reduction mechanisms drawn from multiple theoretical traditions. Third, it maps those mechanisms onto the platform's instruments, making explicit the reduction logic that is currently implicit in the operational design. Fourth, it identifies the measurement and pilot design implications of treating threat salience reduction as a primary performance variable.

---

## I. The Asymmetry in Terror Management Theory

Terror management theory, as developed by Solomon, Greenberg, and Pyszczynski from Becker's foundational synthesis, has produced one of the most empirically robust research programs in social psychology. The experimental literature on mortality salience induction is now four decades deep and covers an extraordinary range of phenomena: worldview defense, in-group favoritism, out-group hostility, authoritarian attraction, self-esteem striving, creativity suppression, time horizon compression, and the full behavioral profile of a organism under existential threat.

What the literature has not developed with comparable rigor is the constructive question: what *lowers* threat salience, through what mechanisms, and with what population-level effects?

This asymmetry is not accidental. It reflects the experimental method's affordances — it is easier to induce threat salience in a laboratory setting than to reduce it, and easier to measure the acute behavioral responses to induction than to measure the gradual behavioral changes produced by sustained reduction. The clinical literature on existential anxiety — Yalom's existential psychotherapy, the meaning-centered approaches of Frankl and Wong, the acceptance-based frameworks of contemporary clinical psychology — addresses individual-level reduction mechanisms with some precision. But the translation from individual clinical intervention to population-level institutional design has not been systematically attempted.

This framework attempts that translation. It is necessarily more speculative than the diagnostic half of the theory, because it is drawing on a less developed literature and extending it into institutional design territory that the original researchers did not address. The appropriate epistemic posture is therefore one of testable hypothesis rather than established theory — which is precisely why the pilot design implications at the end of this document matter as much as the theoretical synthesis.

## II. The Framework's Definitional Extension

The existing framework documents make one significant extension to the standard TMT vocabulary that is foundational to the reduction theory developed here.

Standard TMT focuses on *mortality salience* — the specific heightening of death awareness and its behavioral consequences. The framework's Central Axioms split this into two distinct channels:

**Mortality salience** — the perceived likelihood of physical threat to survival, arising from violence, economic collapse, health crisis, or environmental catastrophe. This is the channel TMT's experimental paradigm primarily activates.

**Existential salience** — the perceived threat to identity, meaning, and symbolic continuity, arising from cultural displacement, status loss, institutional illegitimacy, and the collapse of the meaning systems through which people understand their lives as significant.

**Threat salience** is defined as the composite perception arising from both channels simultaneously. This extension is theoretically significant because it identifies existential salience as a distinct driver of the destabilizing behavioral profile that TMT documents — one that operates through different mechanisms and requires different reduction strategies than mortality salience proper.

The reduction theory must therefore address both channels separately and their interaction jointly.

## III. A Taxonomy of Threat Salience Reduction Mechanisms

The following taxonomy proposes six distinct mechanisms through which threat salience — in both its mortality and existential channels — can be reduced at the individual and population level. These mechanisms are not mutually exclusive; they interact and reinforce each other. But they are analytically distinct and operate through different pathways, which matters for institutional design.

---

### Mechanism 1: Material Security — Reducing Mortality Channel Threat Load

The most direct mechanism for reducing mortality salience is the most obvious: reducing the actual material threats that generate it. If mortality salience is elevated because survival systems are perceived as unreliable or failing, then making those systems more reliable and credible directly addresses the source of the salience rather than its psychological consequences.

This mechanism operates through what the Existential Stability Axiom calls the material protection systems: physical security and deterrence credibility, economic stability and access to meaningful work, housing availability and community continuity, infrastructure reliability, predictable alliance commitments, and rule of law.

The theoretical relationship is straightforward: when material protection systems are credible — when people have reasonable confidence that they will have housing, healthcare, economic security, and physical safety — the mortality channel of threat salience loses its primary activation source. The background hum of survival anxiety that generates the behavioral profile TMT documents — the worldview defense, the in-group/out-group hardening, the time horizon compression — quiets when the material conditions that activate it are stabilized.

This is not a novel claim. The social epidemiology literature has documented the relationship between material security and psychological wellbeing extensively. What is less well appreciated is the directional specificity: it is not primarily wealth that lowers mortality salience, but security — the predictability and reliability of the material systems one depends on. A person with moderate income and high security may have lower mortality salience than a person with high income and low security. The policy implication is that stability and predictability of material systems matter as much as their absolute level — which changes the design criteria for economic and housing policy in ways that standard welfare economics does not capture.

**Platform instruments operating through this mechanism**: housing supply stabilization, counter-cyclical capital deployment through the PCA, healthcare access expansion, infrastructure investment, integrated defense partnerships, and the adaptive governance model's explicit commitment to systems that extend decision time horizons.

---

### Mechanism 2: Anchor Diversification — Reducing Portfolio Concentration Risk

The continuity portfolio model identifies anchor concentration — the condition in which a disproportionate weight of a person's identity and future investment is placed on a single anchor — as a primary driver of fragility. A person whose entire continuity portfolio is concentrated in a single anchor (economic status, national identity, a specific cultural framework) is vulnerable to catastrophic portfolio collapse if that anchor is threatened.

The behavioral consequence of portfolio concentration under threat is precisely the profile TMT documents: when the single overweighted anchor is threatened, the full force of the organism's existential defense system is activated. The worldview defense is intense because there is nothing else to defend. The in-group favoritism is extreme because the in-group is defined by the single anchor. The out-group hostility is severe because the out-group represents the threat to the only anchor available.

Anchor diversification — expanding the credible meaning pathways available to a person, so that their continuity portfolio contains multiple medium-weight anchors rather than one dominant anchor — reduces threat salience through portfolio resilience. When any single anchor is threatened, the diversified portfolio absorbs the shock without catastrophic fragility, because alternative anchors maintain the person's sense of meaningful future investment.

This mechanism operates at the population level through the cultural and institutional infrastructure that makes multiple anchors credible simultaneously. A society in which economic achievement, civic contribution, community belonging, family, faith, and creative expression are all recognized as legitimate and valued pathways to a meaningful life has a more diversified aggregate continuity portfolio than one in which only wealth accumulation and celebrity are treated as serious achievements.

The reduction in threat salience operates through reduced fragility to any single shock — not because any particular anchor is strengthened, but because the overall portfolio is more resilient to disruption.

**Platform instruments operating through this mechanism**: the prestige architecture redesign (expanding the cultural recognition of civic contribution as a legitimate achievement pathway), the National Capability Tour (creating a formative experience of civic contribution as meaningful work), the National Problem Portfolio (creating a competitive arena for contribution-based achievement), and the Meaning in Society framework's explicit orientation toward supporting all four foundational dimensions of meaningful life.

---

### Mechanism 3: Bridging Social Capital — Reducing Existential Channel Threat Load Through Familiarity

TMT research has consistently documented that elevated mortality salience increases hostility toward those who hold different worldviews, belong to different groups, or threaten the symbolic systems through which the self derives meaning. This is the worldview defense mechanism: when death awareness is heightened, the presence of people who live differently or believe differently feels threatening rather than merely different, because their existence implicitly challenges the validity of one's own meaning system.

The inverse relationship is less documented but theoretically grounded and empirically supported: familiarity with people from different groups, prior to threat activation, significantly reduces the worldview defense response. People who have ongoing, casual, repeated contact with individuals from different backgrounds — through the weak-tie networks that form in functional third place environments — experience the presence of difference as ordinary rather than threatening.

This is not tolerance in the sense of deliberate cognitive effort to overcome prejudice. It is something more fundamental: the automatic categorization of different others as familiar rather than strange, which precedes and shapes the threat response. A person who regularly encounters their neighbor from a different political tradition at the local café, who knows their name and their children's names and has shared complaints about the weather, does not respond to political threat with the same intensity of worldview defense as a person for whom that neighbor is a stranger and a representative of an enemy category.

The mechanism through which this operates is the normalization of difference through repeated low-stakes encounter — exactly the function that functional third places provide. The built environment instruments are therefore operating, at one level of description, as existential salience reduction mechanisms: by creating the spatial conditions for bridging social capital formation, they reduce the perceived strangeness of different others, which reduces the existential threat load that different others represent, which reduces existential salience.

This is a longer-horizon mechanism than material security — it operates over years and generations rather than months — and its effects are more diffuse and harder to measure. But it addresses the existential channel of threat salience at its primary source: the perception that difference is threatening.

**Platform instruments operating through this mechanism**: the neighborhood civic overlay, the seam and spine infrastructure, the National Capability Tour's cross-regional deployment design, and the built environment instruments designed to create third place conditions for weak-tie formation.

### Mechanism 4: Institutional Credibility — Reducing Existential Salience Through Navigable Futures

A significant driver of existential salience in the contemporary developed world is not cultural displacement or status loss in the traditional sense, but something more specifically institutional: the perception that the systems one depends on to navigate life — democratic government, economic markets, legal institutions, public health infrastructure — are failing, captured, or beyond redemption.

This perception generates what might be called *institutional existential salience* — a specific form of existential threat arising not from one's personal meaning system being challenged but from the collective meaning system (the shared belief that the society's institutions can produce fair and livable outcomes) collapsing. When institutions lose legitimacy, the future becomes unnavigable in a specific sense: not because one lacks personal resources or meaning, but because the shared framework within which planning, investment, and cooperation occur has become unreliable.

The behavioral consequences are predictable from the TMT framework and visible in contemporary data: withdrawal from civic participation (if institutions don't work, why engage with them?), attraction to authoritarian alternatives (if the system is broken, a strong leader who can bypass it is appealing), short-termism in investment and family formation (if the future is unnavigable, why invest in it?), and the generalized cynicism that degrades cooperative capacity at every level.

The reduction mechanism is the restoration of institutional credibility — specifically through demonstrated competence rather than rhetorical assertion. This is why the adaptive governance model's commitment to empirical evaluation, transparent failure acknowledgment, and visible course correction matters for threat salience reduction beyond its direct policy efficiency effects. An institution that demonstrably learns from failure and corrects itself lowers existential salience by making the future more navigable — by showing that the system can respond to reality rather than only to political incentives.

This mechanism is explicitly named in the democratic legitimacy guiding principles: trust is earned through competence, not through messaging. What is less explicitly named is the psychological mechanism through which demonstrated competence reduces threat salience — it restores the perception of navigable futures that institutional failure destroys.

**Platform instruments operating through this mechanism**: the adaptive governance and evidence protocol, the Department of Data and Accountability, the digital democracy platform's accountability architecture, the congressional operating system interface, and the democratic integrity reforms that reduce capture of the institutions on which navigable futures depend.

### Mechanism 5: Time Horizon Extension — The Feedback Loop Between Low Salience and Future Investment

Threat salience and time horizon are in a bidirectional relationship that creates both a vicious cycle under elevation and a virtuous cycle under reduction.

When threat salience is elevated, time horizons compress: investment in long-horizon activities (education, family formation, infrastructure, institutional building) declines because the future feels too uncertain or too threatening to invest in. This compression is both a symptom of elevated salience and a driver of further elevation — because the withdrawal of long-horizon investment makes the future genuinely less hospitable, which confirms the threat perception that generated the withdrawal.

Conversely, when threat salience is reduced and time horizons extend, long-horizon investment increases: people have children, plant trees, build institutions, invest in education and community — all of which generate the conditions that make the future more hospitable, which further reduces threat salience and further extends time horizons. This is the virtuous cycle of low threat salience.

Time horizon extension is therefore both a consequence of the other reduction mechanisms and an independent amplifier of them. Institutions that deliberately extend time horizons — through housing security that makes roots possible, through community formation that creates long-term local investment, through prestige systems that valorize decade-long commitment to hard problems — are not merely doing good policy in a narrow sense. They are activating the virtuous cycle feedback mechanism that compounds threat salience reduction over time.

The specific indicators of time horizon compression identified in the Central Axioms — declining fertility rates, declining long-term investment, increasing short-term financial behavior, declining civic participation — are therefore not only symptoms of elevated threat salience but also diagnostic signals of whether the reduction mechanisms are working. As reduction takes hold, these indicators should move in predictable directions: fertility stabilizes or increases, long-term investment rises, civic participation grows.

**Platform instruments operating through this mechanism**: all of the above, with particular intensity for housing stability (which enables long-term local investment), the National Capability Tour (which creates a formative experience of long-horizon contribution), and the prestige architecture (which valorizes sustained commitment over short-term accumulation).

### Mechanism 6: Narrative Coherence — Reducing Existential Salience Through Shared Progress Frameworks

A dimension of existential salience that the framework's formal model captures but that the policy instruments address less directly is what might be called *narrative fragmentation* — the condition in which a society lacks shared frameworks for understanding its history, its present situation, and its trajectory forward.

Narrative coherence is not consensus on values or ideology — diverse societies can and should have vigorous disagreement on both. It is something more specific: a shared understanding that there is a story one is part of, that the story has continuity from past to present to future, and that one's own contribution to it is potentially meaningful. The opposite — narrative fragmentation — is the condition in which the shared story has been contested to the point of dissolution, where the past is only a site of grievance, the present only a struggle for power, and the future only a threat.

Narrative fragmentation elevates existential salience because it removes one of the primary mechanisms through which individuals extend their continuity beyond their biological lifespan — participation in a larger story that will continue after them. When that story is contested to the point of incoherence, the existential function it provides disappears.

The reduction mechanism is not the imposition of a single authoritative narrative — that is the authoritarian response to narrative fragmentation and it fails for reasons that the democratic legitimacy framework makes clear. It is the creation of shared reference points within which disagreement can occur: demonstrated achievements that are independently verified and therefore resistant to partisan contestation, visible national progress on concrete problems that crosses ideological lines, institutional competence that provides common ground for civic pride regardless of political differences.

This is the deepest function of the National Problem Portfolio and the prestige architecture — not only redirecting aspiration, but creating the shared reference points for a national narrative of progress that is grounded in verified achievement rather than ideological assertion. A country that can point to a measurably reduced opioid mortality rate, a measurably increased housing supply, a measurably improved antibiotic treatment — and that has an independent, trusted institution certifying those measurements — has something that contested ideological narratives cannot provide: a shared, verifiable story of what was done and whether it worked.

That is a modest contribution to narrative coherence. But modest contributions to narrative coherence, sustained over time, compound into the shared frameworks within which genuine disagreement can occur without collapsing into the fragmentation that elevates existential salience.

**Platform instruments operating through this mechanism**: the National Problem Portfolio and competitive fund, the Department of Data and Accountability (as the verification authority whose independence makes shared reference points credible), the prestige architecture (as the canonization system that encodes shared achievements into durable cultural memory), and the digital democracy platform (as the infrastructure through which citizens can see national progress in real time, across partisan lines).

## IV. The Interaction Structure

The six mechanisms do not operate independently. They interact in ways that create both reinforcing loops and potential substitution effects that the institutional design must account for.

**Reinforcing loops** between mechanisms include: material security enabling long-term investment (Mechanism 1 → Mechanism 5), anchor diversification reducing worldview defense intensity (Mechanism 2 → Mechanism 3), institutional credibility extending time horizons (Mechanism 4 → Mechanism 5), and narrative coherence supporting anchor diversification by providing shared frameworks within which multiple anchors are legitimate (Mechanism 6 → Mechanism 2).

**Potential substitution effects** include: cases where material security is achieved through mechanisms that damage anchor diversification (economic growth that increases inequality and therefore portfolio concentration), cases where institutional credibility is restored through mechanisms that reduce bridging social capital (technocratic competence that excludes public participation), and cases where narrative coherence is pursued in ways that damage bridging social capital (national pride narratives that define the nation by exclusion rather than contribution).

These interaction effects and substitution risks are why the platform's instruments must be evaluated not only for their direct effects on the specific mechanism they primarily address, but for their effects on the full interaction structure. This is the role of the civilizational cost function — it captures the aggregate effect across all mechanisms simultaneously, preventing local optimization that produces global degradation.

The formal model's three cost function terms — mean fragility, variance in fragility, and anchor antagonism — map onto the reduction mechanisms as follows: Mechanisms 1 and 4 primarily address mean fragility by reducing the average threat load. Mechanisms 2 and 5 primarily address variance by diversifying and extending portfolio resilience across the population. Mechanisms 3 and 6 primarily address anchor antagonism by reducing the perceived threat of different others and providing shared reference points that cross anchor boundaries.

A platform that operates all six mechanisms simultaneously — even imperfectly — addresses all three terms of the cost function in a coherent and mutually reinforcing way.

## V. The Crystal of Density as Spatial Nucleation Theory

The built environment doc establishes the theoretical case for spatial design as community anchor infrastructure. This section adds a specific design principle that follows from the threat salience reduction theory: the **crystal of density** as the primary spatial mechanism for Mechanism 3 (bridging social capital formation).

The crystal metaphor is precise and worth unpacking. In materials science, crystallization does not occur uniformly across a supersaturated medium. It nucleates at specific points — seed crystals, surface irregularities, impurity sites — and then the surrounding medium grows toward and around the nucleus. The crystal does not spread uniformly; it grows in a specific directional pattern determined by the structure of the seed and the conditions of the medium.

The spatial analog is this: community does not form uniformly across a residential fabric. It nucleates at specific places — the café with the good corner table, the park with the bench that faces the path, the market where people linger, the library room with the comfortable chairs — and the surrounding neighborhood gradually orients toward those nuclei. People develop walking routes that pass the nucleation point. They encounter each other there repeatedly. Weak ties form. The surrounding area gradually becomes more community-oriented not because it was planned that way but because the nucleus created the conditions for orientation.

The design implication is that the primary spatial intervention is not comprehensive neighborhood redesign but strategic nucleation: identifying the right sites for density seeds — the low-resistance, high-leverage locations where a café or a small market or a shared workspace can become the nucleus around which community crystallizes — and removing the regulatory obstacles that prevent them from forming.

This is precisely what the Neighborhood Civic Overlay does, though it does not frame itself in these terms. By allowing a small set of civic uses by right in existing residential neighborhoods, it creates the legal conditions for nucleation without prescribing where or how the crystals form. The crystals form where people want them — where the foot traffic is, where the spatial relationships are right, where the community is already reaching for the thing the overlay makes possible.

The seam infrastructure extends this logic to the corridor scale: the seam is not a neighborhood, it is a nucleation spine — a linear sequence of conditions favorable to community crystallization, which allows nuclei to form along its length and the surrounding fabric to orient toward them over time.

The STZ framework and the node typology work extend it to the metropolitan scale: the nodes are the primary nucleation sites at which the crystals of density and community form, and the surrounding single-family neighborhoods are the medium in which they crystallize without being required to transform.

This spatial nucleation theory has a direct implication for pilot design. You do not measure whether a neighborhood has transformed. You measure whether crystals are forming — whether the nucleation sites are attracting repeated use, whether weak ties are forming around them, whether the surrounding area is orienting toward them — and you use those leading indicators to predict the longer-horizon community formation that the theory predicts. Crystal formation is measurable years before the community it generates is fully visible.

## VI. Measurement Implications and Pilot Design

The threat salience reduction theory makes specific and testable predictions about what should change when the platform's instruments are deployed. These predictions generate a measurement agenda that extends beyond the standard outcome metrics (housing units built, healthcare access improved) to include the intermediate indicators of threat salience reduction that the theory specifies.

**Mortality channel indicators** — measurable through existing data sources and the DoDA infrastructure — include: housing stability rates, healthcare access latency, economic precarity measures, long-term investment rates, fertility rates, and the time horizon metrics identified in the Central Axioms (long-term bond yields, R&D investment share, average debt maturity).

**Existential channel indicators** require more methodological development. Candidate measures include: institutional trust indices (Edelman, Gallup, WVS), affective polarization measures (ANES-style affect thermometer gaps), cross-group social contact frequency (adapted from social capital survey instruments), narrative fragmentation measures (media sentiment analysis, keyword frequency in threat-themed discourse), and civic participation rates.

**Crystal formation indicators** at the neighborhood scale require the most methodological innovation, because the standard urban outcome metrics (tax productivity per acre, housing absorption) are lagging indicators of community formation rather than leading ones. Candidate leading indicators include: foot traffic at nucleation sites (measurable through mobile device data with appropriate privacy protections), small business formation rates and survival rates (administrative data), resident-reported social contact frequency (lightweight survey instrument), walking route analysis (measuring whether people's movement patterns orient toward nucleation sites), and the radius within which residents conduct daily activities on foot (a direct measure of the neighborhood's community crystallization progress).

The Digital Democracy Platform's free-text signal aggregation is a natural instrument for tracking existential channel indicators at scale — constituent inputs about loneliness, community disconnection, institutional distrust, and loss of meaning are precisely the signal that the threat salience reduction theory predicts should change as the platform's instruments take effect. That signal, aggregated and tracked over time through the DoDA processing layer, becomes a population-level longitudinal measure of existential salience — the kind of measurement that the TMT experimental literature has never been able to achieve at scale.

This is the deepest methodological contribution the platform can make to the theoretical literature: a longitudinal, population-level, naturally-occurring dataset on threat salience dynamics and their relationship to institutional interventions. Nothing in the existing TMT or social capital literature approaches this measurement opportunity in scope or ecological validity.

## VII. Honest Uncertainties and the Limits of the Theory

The threat salience reduction theory presented here is a synthesis of several well-developed theoretical traditions applied to an institutional design problem that none of those traditions has directly addressed. Several significant uncertainties warrant explicit acknowledgment.

**The translation from individual to population level** is the primary theoretical uncertainty. TMT's mechanisms are established at the individual level through experimental methods. The population-level dynamics — how individual threat salience aggregates, how it is transmitted socially, how institutional interventions propagate through a population — are theorized but not well established empirically. The state-space model in the Central Axioms provides the formal framework for this translation, but its parameters are currently estimated rather than measured.

**The timescale question** is genuinely uncertain. Some mechanisms operate on relatively short timescales — material security improvements should produce measurable mortality salience reduction within months to a few years. Others operate on generational timescales — narrative coherence formation, bridging social capital density, the full effects of anchor diversification. The platform's ability to demonstrate threat salience reduction within a political cycle is therefore limited to the faster-acting mechanisms, while the deeper and more durable effects will only be visible across decades.

**The measurement gap for existential salience** remains significant. Mortality salience has relatively well-validated proxy measures at the population level (health outcomes, economic precarity measures, physical security data). Existential salience at the population level is harder to measure validly — the instruments that exist (trust surveys, polarization measures) capture some dimensions but not the full construct. The Digital Democracy Platform's free-text signal is potentially the best available instrument, but its validity requires calibration against external benchmarks.

**The interaction effects between mechanisms** are theorized but not empirically established at the population level. The claim that all six mechanisms are mutually reinforcing is plausible from the theoretical literature but could be wrong in specific configurations — particularly the substitution risks identified above. Pilot design should include explicit tests of these interaction effects rather than assuming they operate as predicted.

These uncertainties are not reasons for theoretical retreat. They are the agenda for the empirical work that the platform's pilot architecture is positioned to conduct. The theory is specific enough to generate testable predictions. The measurement infrastructure is being built to test them. The adaptive governance model creates the institutional conditions under which tests can be conducted, results published honestly, and theory revised based on evidence.

That is the appropriate relationship between theory and practice in a domain as complex as civilizational stabilization.

## VIII. The Relationship to the Full Meta Layer

This document completes the theoretical arc of the framework foundations layer.

The Central Axioms establish the formal model: the continuity portfolio, the continuity fragility measure, the civilizational cost function with its three terms.

The Existential Stability Axiom establishes the diagnostic: what threat salience is, how it arises, and what its elevation produces in terms of individual behavior and systemic dynamics.

The Incentive-Meaning Coherence document establishes the failure loop: how misaligned incentive structures elevate threat salience through the destruction of symbolic meaning systems.

The Prestige Architecture and Meaning document establishes the cultural design response: how deliberate prestige architecture redesign addresses anchor diversification and narrative coherence at the civilizational level.

The Built Environment and Community Anchors document establishes the spatial design response: how deliberate architectural and urban design restores the conditions for community anchor formation and bridging social capital at the neighborhood level.

This document — the Threat Salience Reduction Theory — synthesizes those responses into a coherent theoretical framework: six mechanisms, their interaction structure, their relationship to the cost function terms, and the measurement and pilot design implications that follow from treating threat salience reduction as a primary performance variable.

Together, these documents constitute a theoretical framework for civilizational stabilization that is, to the authors' knowledge, without direct precedent in the existing literature. The constituent theories — TMT, social capital theory, place-based community formation, institutional credibility, narrative coherence — are all well-developed. Their synthesis into a unified design framework for democratic governance has not, to our knowledge, been previously attempted at this level of theoretical integration and institutional specificity.

The framework makes predictions that are falsifiable. The platform's instruments are designed to test them. The measurement infrastructure is being built to track the results. The adaptive governance model creates the conditions under which honest evaluation and theoretical revision are institutionally possible.

The theory is offered in that spirit: not as settled knowledge but as a structured set of hypotheses about what makes societies stable, what makes them fragile, and what governance systems can do — and cannot do — to push the balance toward stability.

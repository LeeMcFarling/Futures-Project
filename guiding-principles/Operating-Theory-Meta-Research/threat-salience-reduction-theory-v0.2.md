# Threat Salience Reduction
## A Constructive Theory of Civilizational Stabilization

---

## Prefatory Note

This document is the third in the Framework Foundations reading sequence, following the Civilizational Stability Foundations document and the Model Definitions. Nomenclature — threat salience, mortality salience, existential salience — is established in the Foundations document and used here without re-definition.

This document is the constructive complement to the diagnostic architecture established in the first two documents. The Foundations document establishes what threat salience is, how it arises, and what its elevation produces. The Model Definitions document formalizes the individual stability score and the mechanisms of anchor inversion, radicalization, and correlated class-level shock. This document asks the question those documents do not directly answer: what *lowers* threat salience, through what mechanisms, and how can institutional design deliberately activate those mechanisms?

This document is addressed to researchers in terror management theory, existential psychology, political sociology, and governance theory. Its applied companion — covering the same terrain for policymakers and practitioners — is the Political Salience Management brief.

---

## I. The Asymmetry in Terror Management Theory

Terror management theory has produced one of the most empirically robust research programs in social psychology. The experimental literature on mortality salience induction is now four decades deep and covers an extraordinary range of phenomena: worldview defense, in-group favoritism, out-group hostility, authoritarian attraction, self-esteem striving, creativity suppression, time horizon compression, and the full behavioral profile of an organism under existential threat.

What the literature has not developed with comparable rigor is the constructive question: what *lowers* threat salience, through what mechanisms, and with what population-level effects?

This asymmetry is not accidental. It reflects the experimental method's affordances — inducing threat salience in a laboratory is straightforward; reducing it is not an experimental operation in the same sense. The clinical literature on existential anxiety — Yalom's existential psychotherapy, the meaning-centered approaches of Frankl and Wong, the acceptance-based frameworks of contemporary clinical psychology — addresses individual-level reduction mechanisms with some precision. But the translation from individual clinical intervention to population-level institutional design has not been systematically attempted.

This framework attempts that translation. It is necessarily more speculative than the diagnostic half of the theory, because it is drawing on a less developed literature and extending it into institutional design territory the original researchers did not address. The appropriate epistemic posture is one of testable hypothesis rather than established theory — which is precisely why the measurement and pilot design implications matter as much as the theoretical synthesis.

## II. The Theoretical Extension: Two Channels, Two Reduction Strategies

Standard TMT focuses on mortality salience as a unified construct. The framework's Central Axioms and Model Definitions split this into two distinct channels — mortality salience and existential salience — each driven by different threat conditions and requiring different reduction strategies.

This split is theoretically significant because the behavioral consequences of elevated threat salience look similar across both channels — worldview defense, in-group hardening, out-group hostility, time horizon compression — but the conditions producing them are different, and the interventions that address them are different.

**Mortality channel threats** are material and measurable: job loss, housing insecurity, healthcare inaccessibility, physical violence, economic precarity. Mortality salience falls when these conditions improve. The mechanism is direct: when survival systems become more reliable, the background activation of existential defense mechanisms quiets. This is not primarily about wealth — it is about security and predictability. A person with moderate income and stable housing may have lower mortality salience than a person with high income and volatile employment, because predictability of the material conditions one depends on matters more than their absolute level.

**Existential channel threats** are symbolic and perceptual: attack on anchor classes, delegitimization of valued ways of life, identity negation, manufactured threat amplification. Existential salience can rise without any change in material conditions — indeed, the manufactured component of existential salience operates almost entirely in the information environment, decoupled from the physical world. This makes existential salience in some respects harder to reduce than mortality salience: you cannot build your way to lower existential salience the way you can build your way to lower housing insecurity. The information environment, the political incentive structure, the institutional legitimacy of valued meaning systems, and the availability of alternative anchors all matter.

The reduction theory must address both channels and their interaction. Improving material conditions without addressing the symbolic threat environment may fail to lower overall threat salience if existential salience remains high. Addressing symbolic threats without improving material conditions will fail for the same reason in the other direction. The two channels are distinct levers that must be pulled simultaneously.

## III. A Taxonomy of Threat Salience Reduction Mechanisms

Six distinct mechanisms lower threat salience at the individual and population level. They are analytically distinct — operating through different pathways — but mutually reinforcing when deployed together. They map onto the three dimensions of population-level stability identified in the Model Definitions: average stability across the population, inequality of stability between groups, and antagonism between anchor communities.

---

### Mechanism 1: Material Security — Addressing the Mortality Channel Directly

The most direct mechanism for reducing mortality salience is reducing the actual material threats that generate it. When survival systems — housing, healthcare, economic security, physical safety — are credible and predictable, the mortality channel of threat salience loses its primary activation source.

What the social epidemiology literature has established, and what standard welfare economics does not fully capture, is that it is the *stability and predictability* of material systems that matters most, not their absolute level. Policy design that produces stable, predictable material security reduces mortality salience more effectively than policy that produces higher average outcomes with greater volatility.

This mechanism operates through what the Existential Stability Axiom calls material protection systems: physical security and deterrence credibility, economic stability and meaningful work access, housing availability, infrastructure reliability, and rule of law. When these systems are functional and perceived as reliable, the background hum of survival anxiety that activates the TMT behavioral profile quiets.

**Platform instruments**: housing supply stabilization, counter-cyclical capital deployment, healthcare access expansion, infrastructure investment, integrated defense partnerships, and the labor and economic security stack.

---

### Mechanism 2: Anchor Diversification — Reducing Portfolio Concentration Risk

The continuity portfolio model identifies anchor concentration — a disproportionate weight of identity and future investment placed on a single anchor or anchor class — as the primary structural driver of fragility. A person whose entire portfolio runs through one anchor is vulnerable to catastrophic collapse if that anchor is threatened, and the behavioral response to that threat is the most intense version of the TMT profile: worldview defense is severe because there is nothing else to defend; in-group identification is extreme because it is organized around the single anchor; out-group hostility is acute because the out-group represents the threat source.

Anchor diversification — expanding the credible meaning pathways available to people, so that portfolios contain multiple anchors across different dimensions and classes — reduces threat salience through portfolio resilience. When any single anchor is threatened, the diversified portfolio absorbs the shock without catastrophic destabilization. Alternative anchors maintain the person's sense of meaningful future investment while the threatened anchor recovers or is replaced.

Crucially, this mechanism requires not just that multiple anchors exist in principle, but that they are genuinely available to specific people in specific conditions. An economic achievement anchor that is structurally unavailable due to credential barriers, geographic isolation, or discrimination does not diversify the portfolio of the people who cannot access it. A community anchor that requires physical infrastructure that does not exist in someone's neighborhood is not available to them regardless of their desire for it. Anchor diversification is therefore partly a policy problem — expanding the structural availability of anchors across the population — not only a cultural or psychological one.

The anchor class framework developed in the Model Definitions matters here because correlated shocks to anchor classes — threats that hit all anchors of a particular type simultaneously — produce the most destabilizing failures. Portfolio resilience requires not just multiple anchors but anchors drawn from different classes with non-overlapping vulnerability profiles: a religious community anchor and a neighborhood civic anchor are both community-dimension anchors, but they fail through different mechanisms and are resistant to different threats. True diversification requires distributing across classes, not just across anchors within a single class.

**Platform instruments**: prestige architecture redesign (civic contribution as a legitimate achievement pathway), National Capability Tour (formative cross-regional civic contribution experience), National Problem Portfolio (competitive arena for contribution-based achievement), IDP outbound mobility scaffold (international anchor formation), built environment instruments (community anchor infrastructure), and the civic acropolis and prestige architecture stack.

---

### Mechanism 3: Bridging Social Capital — Reducing Existential Salience Through Familiarity

The primary mechanism through which existential salience falls in the relationship between anchor communities is familiarity — the direct personal experience of specific individuals from other communities as human beings rather than as abstract threats.

The political science and social psychology literature on contact theory has established this extensively: direct, personal, repeated contact between members of groups in perceived conflict reduces out-group hostility and existential threat perception. The mechanism is not primarily cognitive — it is not that people are persuaded by arguments to update their threat assessments. It is that familiarity with specific individuals makes the abstract threat narrative harder to maintain. The person who shares a coffee shop with their ideologically different neighbor has personal evidence that contradicts the worst version of the threat narrative, and that personal evidence is more resistant to political manipulation than propositional belief.

This mechanism is uniquely dependent on physical and spatial conditions, because the familiarity that reduces existential salience between anchor communities requires repeated, incidental, low-stakes encounter — the kind that occurs in shared physical spaces rather than in organized dialogues or formal settings. This is why the built environment instruments are, in model terms, a direct intervention in anchor antagonism: they create the spatial conditions within which the familiarity-formation mechanism can operate naturally.

The mechanism also operates through the IDP outbound mobility scaffold at the international level. A person who has lived and worked alongside people from partner nations has personal evidence that contradicts threat narratives about those countries — evidence that operates at the level of identity anchors rather than propositional belief, and is therefore more durable than counter-argument.

**Platform instruments**: neighborhood civic overlay, national seam infrastructure, modular reurbanization, special transit zones (all creating physical third-place conditions), IDP outbound mobility scaffold, and National Capability Tour (domestic cross-community shared work experience).

---

### Mechanism 4: Institutional Credibility — Anchoring Legitimacy as a Meaning Source

Institutional credibility — the perceived reliability, competence, and fairness of the institutions through which collective life is organized — is a direct determinant of existential salience through the legitimacy anchor dimension. When institutions are perceived as competent and fair, they provide a shared reference point that reduces the sense that the social order is arbitrary, captured, or collapsing. When they are perceived as corrupt, incompetent, or systematically unfair, they elevate existential salience by removing the symbolic protection they would otherwise provide.

The reduction mechanism operates in two directions. First, direct institutional performance improvement — building things that work, delivering services reliably, demonstrating competence through verifiable outcomes — provides the material for legitimacy reconstruction. Second, the institutional design of governance systems to be transparent, adaptive, and accountable provides the structural conditions under which legitimacy can be sustained even when specific outcomes are disappointing.

The CPQA Civic Build Mark is a specific instrument through which the republic makes its authorship of working infrastructure visible — the mark on the transit station, the civic room, the neighborhood market is evidence of institutional competence that counteracts the "government only destroys" existential threat narrative without requiring any rhetorical claim. The evidence is the building.

The Department of Data and Accountability serves a parallel function in the epistemic domain: it provides the verified, independent measurement infrastructure through which institutional claims about performance can be evaluated against reality, reducing the existential salience produced by contested or unverifiable claims about whether things are getting better or worse.

**Platform instruments**: CPQA Civic Build Mark, Department of Data and Accountability, adaptive governance and evidence protocol, the pilot-first deployment model (which demonstrates competence before scaling), and transparent performance reporting across all domain stacks.

### Mechanism 5: Time Horizon Extension — Reversing the Temporal Compression of Elevated Threat Salience

One of the most robust behavioral consequences of elevated threat salience is time horizon compression: the shortening of investment horizons, planning horizons, and future-oriented commitment that occurs when perceived threat makes the future feel unreliable or inaccessible. This compression is both a consequence and a cause of elevated threat salience — it reduces the investments that would improve material conditions, which in turn maintains or worsens the material threat load.

Time horizon extension — the expansion of the planning and investment horizons within which individuals, firms, and governments operate — is therefore a reduction mechanism in its own right. When people feel sufficiently secure to plan and invest for the long term, they produce the conditions that make the future more secure, creating a virtuous cycle that is the mirror of the vicious cycle threat salience produces.

The indicators of time horizon compression identified in the Central Axioms — declining fertility rates, declining long-term investment, increasing short-horizon financial behavior, declining civic participation — are therefore not only symptoms of elevated threat salience but leading indicators of whether reduction mechanisms are working. As reduction takes hold, these indicators should move in predictable directions.

**Platform instruments**: housing stability (enabling long-term local investment), superannuation and long-term savings architecture, counter-cyclical capital mechanisms (reducing the economic volatility that compresses horizons), National Capability Tour (formative long-horizon contribution experience), and prestige architecture (valorizing sustained commitment over short-term accumulation).

### Mechanism 6: Narrative Coherence — Reducing Existential Salience Through Shared Progress Frameworks

A dimension of existential salience that the formal model captures but that policy instruments address less directly is narrative fragmentation — the condition in which a society lacks shared frameworks for understanding its history, present situation, and trajectory forward.

Narrative coherence is not ideological consensus. Diverse societies can and should have vigorous disagreement on values and policy. Narrative coherence is something more specific: a shared understanding that there is a story one is part of, that the story has continuity from past to present to future, and that one's own contribution to it is potentially meaningful. The opposite — narrative fragmentation — is the condition in which the shared story has been contested to the point of dissolution, where the past is only a site of grievance, the present only a struggle for power, and the future only a threat.

Narrative fragmentation elevates existential salience because it removes one of the primary mechanisms through which individuals extend their continuity beyond their biological lifespan: participation in a larger story that will continue after them. When that story is contested to the point of incoherence, the existential function it provides disappears.

The reduction mechanism is not the imposition of a single authoritative narrative — that is the authoritarian response to fragmentation and it fails, at scale, because it delegitimizes the experiences of those whose stories it excludes. The reduction mechanism is the creation of shared reference points within which disagreement can occur: demonstrated achievements that are independently verified and therefore resistant to partisan contestation, visible national progress on concrete problems that crosses ideological lines, institutional competence that provides common ground for civic identity regardless of political differences.

This is the deepest function of the National Problem Portfolio and the prestige architecture: not only redirecting aspiration toward contribution, but creating the shared reference points for a national narrative of progress grounded in verified achievement rather than ideological assertion. A country that can point to measurably reduced opioid mortality, measurably increased housing supply, measurably improved infrastructure performance — verified by an institution whose independence from political interference is structural — has something that contested ideological narratives cannot provide: a shared, verifiable story of what was done and whether it worked.

**Platform instruments**: National Problem Portfolio and competitive fund, Department of Data and Accountability (as the verification authority), prestige architecture and Civic Acropolis (as the canonization system), and the CPQA Civic Build Mark (as the visible inscription of shared civic authorship on the built environment).

## IV. The Interaction Structure

The six mechanisms do not operate independently. They interact in ways that create both reinforcing loops and potential substitution effects that institutional design must account for.

**Reinforcing loops** between mechanisms include: material security enabling long-term investment (Mechanism 1 enabling Mechanism 5); anchor diversification reducing the intensity of worldview defense when any single anchor is threatened (Mechanism 2 enabling Mechanism 3); institutional credibility extending time horizons by making the future feel more reliable (Mechanism 4 enabling Mechanism 5); and narrative coherence supporting anchor diversification by providing shared frameworks within which multiple anchors are simultaneously legitimate (Mechanism 6 enabling Mechanism 2).

**Substitution risks** — cases where pursuing one mechanism can damage another — deserve explicit attention because they represent the primary failure mode of well-intentioned reform. Material security pursued through mechanisms that increase inequality and portfolio concentration may improve average stability while worsening the distribution (Mechanism 1 damaging Mechanism 2's population-level effect). Institutional credibility pursued through technocratic competence that excludes public participation may restore trust among some populations while deepening alienation among others (Mechanism 4 damaging Mechanism 3). Narrative coherence pursued through national pride frameworks that define the nation by exclusion rather than contribution elevates existential salience for excluded communities while reducing it for included ones (Mechanism 6 damaging Mechanism 3).

These substitution risks are why the platform's instruments must be evaluated not only for their direct effects on the mechanism they primarily address, but for their effects across the full interaction structure. The anti-anchor-trigger design discipline described in the Political Salience Management brief is the applied expression of this theoretical requirement: every instrument is assessed for whether it reduces threat salience for its target population without raising it for other anchor communities.

Mapping the mechanisms to the three dimensions of population-level stability from the Model Definitions:

Mechanisms 1 and 4 primarily address **average stability** — reducing the baseline threat load across the population by improving material conditions and institutional legitimacy.

Mechanisms 2 and 5 primarily address **inequality of stability** — diversifying portfolio resilience and extending time horizons across the population rather than only for the already-anchored and already-secure.

Mechanisms 3 and 6 primarily address **anchor antagonism** — reducing the perceived existential threat between anchor communities and providing shared reference points that cross anchor boundaries.

A platform that operates all six mechanisms simultaneously — even imperfectly — addresses all three dimensions in a coherent and mutually reinforcing way. No single mechanism is sufficient. The combination is what produces durable stabilization rather than localized improvement with compensating deterioration elsewhere.

## V. The Crystal of Density as Spatial Nucleation Theory

The Built Environment and Community Anchors document establishes the theoretical case for spatial design as community anchor infrastructure. This section adds a specific design principle that follows from the threat salience reduction theory: the **crystal of density** as the primary spatial mechanism for Mechanism 3.

The crystal metaphor is precise. In materials science, crystallization does not occur uniformly across a supersaturated medium. It nucleates at specific points — seed crystals, surface irregularities, impurity sites — and then the surrounding medium grows toward and around the nucleus. The crystal does not spread uniformly; it grows in a specific directional pattern determined by the structure of the seed and the conditions of the medium.

The spatial analog: community does not form uniformly across a residential fabric. It nucleates at specific places — the café with the good corner table, the park with the bench that faces the path, the market where people linger, the library room with the comfortable chairs — and the surrounding neighborhood gradually orients toward those nuclei. People develop walking routes that pass the nucleation point. They encounter each other there repeatedly. Weak ties form. The surrounding area gradually becomes more community-oriented not because it was planned that way but because the nucleus created the conditions for orientation.

The design implication is that the primary spatial intervention is not comprehensive neighborhood redesign but strategic nucleation: identifying the right sites for density seeds — the low-resistance, high-leverage locations where a café or a small market or a shared workspace can become the nucleus around which community crystallizes — and removing the regulatory obstacles that prevent them from forming.

This is precisely what the Neighborhood Civic Overlay does. By allowing a small set of civic uses by right in existing residential neighborhoods, it creates the legal conditions for nucleation without prescribing where or how the crystals form. The crystals form where people want them — where the foot traffic is, where the spatial relationships are right, where the community is already reaching for the thing the overlay makes possible.

The seam infrastructure extends this logic to the corridor scale: the seam is a nucleation spine — a linear sequence of conditions favorable to community crystallization, which allows nuclei to form along its length and the surrounding fabric to orient toward them over time.

The STZ framework and the node typology work extend it to the metropolitan scale: the nodes are the primary nucleation sites at which the crystals of density and community form, and the surrounding neighborhoods are the medium in which they crystallize without being required to transform uniformly.

**The implication for pilot design**: you do not measure whether a neighborhood has transformed. You measure whether crystals are forming — whether the nucleation sites are attracting repeated use, whether weak ties are forming around them, whether the surrounding area is orienting toward them — and you use those leading indicators to predict the longer-horizon community formation the theory predicts. Crystal formation is measurable years before the community it generates is fully visible.

## VI. Measurement Implications and Pilot Design

The threat salience reduction theory makes specific and testable predictions about what should change when the platform's instruments are deployed. These predictions generate a measurement agenda that extends beyond standard outcome metrics to include intermediate indicators of threat salience reduction.

**Mortality channel indicators** — measurable through existing data sources and the DoDA infrastructure — include: housing stability rates, healthcare access latency, economic precarity measures, long-term investment rates, fertility rates, and the time horizon metrics identified in the Central Axioms (long-term bond yields, R&D investment share, average debt maturity).

**Existential channel indicators** require more methodological development. Candidate measures include: institutional trust indices (Edelman, Gallup, WVS), affective polarization measures (ANES-style affect thermometer gaps), cross-group social contact frequency (adapted from social capital survey instruments), narrative fragmentation measures (media sentiment analysis, keyword frequency in threat-themed discourse), and civic participation rates.

**Crystal formation indicators** at the neighborhood scale require the most methodological innovation, because standard urban outcome metrics are lagging indicators of community formation rather than leading ones. Candidate leading indicators include: foot traffic at nucleation sites (measurable through mobile device data with appropriate privacy protections), small business formation and survival rates (administrative data), resident-reported social contact frequency (lightweight survey instrument), and the radius within which residents conduct daily activities on foot — a direct measure of neighborhood community crystallization progress.

The Digital Democracy Platform's free-text signal aggregation is a natural instrument for tracking existential channel indicators at scale — constituent inputs about loneliness, community disconnection, institutional distrust, and loss of meaning are precisely the signal that the threat salience reduction theory predicts should change as the platform's instruments take effect. That signal, aggregated and tracked over time through the DoDA processing layer, becomes a population-level longitudinal measure of existential salience — the kind of measurement that the TMT experimental literature has never been able to achieve at scale.

This is the deepest methodological contribution the platform can make to the theoretical literature: a longitudinal, population-level, naturally-occurring dataset on threat salience dynamics and their relationship to institutional interventions. Nothing in the existing TMT or social capital literature approaches this measurement opportunity in scope or ecological validity.

## VII. Honest Uncertainties and the Limits of the Theory

The threat salience reduction theory presented here is a synthesis of several well-developed theoretical traditions applied to an institutional design problem that none of those traditions has directly addressed. Several significant uncertainties warrant explicit acknowledgment.

**The translation from individual to population level** is the primary theoretical uncertainty. TMT's mechanisms are established at the individual level through experimental methods. The population-level dynamics — how individual threat salience aggregates, how it is transmitted socially, how institutional interventions propagate through a population — are theorized but not well established empirically. The Civilizational Stability Foundations document provides the conceptual framework for this translation, and the Model Definitions formalizes it, but the parameters are currently estimated rather than measured.

**The timescale question** is genuinely uncertain. Some mechanisms operate on relatively short timescales — material security improvements should produce measurable mortality salience reduction within months to a few years. Others operate on generational timescales — narrative coherence formation, bridging social capital density, the full effects of anchor diversification. The platform's ability to demonstrate threat salience reduction within a political cycle is therefore limited to the faster-acting mechanisms, while the deeper and more durable effects will only be visible across decades.

**The measurement gap for existential salience** remains significant. Mortality salience has relatively well-validated proxy measures at the population level. Existential salience at the population level is harder to measure validly — the instruments that exist capture some dimensions but not the full construct. The Digital Democracy Platform's free-text signal is potentially the best available instrument, but its validity requires calibration against external benchmarks.

**The interaction effects between mechanisms** are theorized but not empirically established at the population level. The claim that all six mechanisms are mutually reinforcing is plausible from the theoretical literature but could be wrong in specific configurations — particularly the substitution risks identified above. Pilot design should include explicit tests of these interaction effects rather than assuming they operate as predicted.

**The radicalization pathway and its reversibility** deserve specific acknowledgment. The model predicts that anchor concentration under sustained threat produces radicalization as a predictable structural response rather than a pathological individual one. What is less theoretically settled is how reversible that radicalization is once it has become organized and institutionally embedded. Portfolio diversification should reduce new radicalization. Whether it reverses existing radicalization at scale, and on what timescale, is an empirical question the theory cannot currently answer with confidence.

These uncertainties are not reasons for theoretical retreat. They are the agenda for the empirical work the platform's pilot architecture is positioned to conduct. The theory is specific enough to generate testable predictions. The measurement infrastructure is being built to test them. The adaptive governance model creates the institutional conditions under which tests can be conducted, results published honestly, and theory revised based on evidence.

## VIII. The Relationship to the Full Meta Layer

This document completes the theoretical arc of the Framework Foundations layer on the constructive side.

The **Civilizational Stability Foundations** document establishes the core axiom, the two-channel threat salience framework, the continuity portfolio concept, the incentive-meaning failure loop, the three dimensions of population-level stability, the domestic and international causality chains, and the measurement architecture.

The **Model Definitions** document formalizes the individual stability score with full precision: anchor dimensions, anchor classes, person-anchor fit coefficients, material and symbolic threat terms, the negative anchor and radicalization mechanisms, and the international scaling of the framework.

The **Prestige Architecture and Meaning** document establishes the cultural design response: how deliberate prestige architecture redesign addresses anchor diversification and narrative coherence at the civilizational level.

The **Built Environment and Community Anchors** document establishes the spatial design response: how deliberate architectural and urban design restores the conditions for community anchor formation and bridging social capital.

This document — the **Threat Salience Reduction Theory** — synthesizes those responses into a coherent theoretical framework: six mechanisms, their interaction structure, their measurement implications, and the honest uncertainties that the empirical program must address.

The **Political Salience Management** brief is the applied companion to this document and to the layer as a whole. It covers the same terrain for policymakers and practitioners, without the formal theoretical apparatus, and with explicit attention to the political failure modes — particularly the symbolic turn — that the reduction theory is designed to avoid repeating.

Together, these documents constitute a theoretical framework for civilizational stabilization grounded in the empirical literature on terror management, social capital, place-based community formation, institutional credibility, and narrative coherence. The framework makes predictions that are falsifiable. The platform's instruments are designed to test them. The measurement infrastructure is being built to track the results. The adaptive governance model creates the conditions under which honest evaluation and theoretical revision are institutionally possible.

The theory is offered in that spirit: not as settled knowledge but as a structured set of hypotheses about what makes societies stable, what makes them fragile, and what governance systems can do — and cannot do — to push the balance toward stability.
